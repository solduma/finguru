## Why start here

Every tool in this guide — moving averages, RSI, Elliott Waves, candlesticks —
ultimately rests on one idea: **markets move in trends, and those trends can be
read.** That idea has a source. It comes from a quiet financial journalist who
died in 1902 and never once called his work a "theory," let alone a trading
system.

Understand Charles Dow and you understand the grammar of everything that
follows.

## Who he was

**Charles Henry Dow** (November 6, 1851 – December 4, 1902) was an American
financial reporter — a Connecticut farm boy who became one of the most
influential figures in market history, all by the age of 51. In 1882 he
co-founded **Dow Jones & Company** with Edward Jones and Charles Bergstresser.
The firm began humbly — hand-delivering two-page financial bulletins, the
*Customer's Afternoon Letter*, to Wall Street subscribers. That bulletin grew
into **_The Wall Street Journal_**, which Dow co-founded and first edited in
1889. A spell as a partner at the NYSE brokerage **Goodbody, Glynn and Dow**
(from 1885) gave him a trader's-eye view of the market he wrote about.

Along the way he built something we still talk about every single day: the
**stock market average**.

- **July 3, 1884** — Dow published his first average: 11 companies, mostly
  railroads (railroads were the era's dominant public companies).
- **May 26, 1896** — the **Dow Jones Industrial Average** debuted with 12
  industrial stocks. (None of those original 12 remain in it today.)

From 1899 until his death in 1902, Dow wrote a market column for the *Journal*.
Those roughly 250 editorials are the entire raw material of what we now call Dow
Theory.

<Callout type="key">
Charles Dow never wrote a book and never used the phrase "Dow Theory." He
observed markets and wrote down what he saw. Dow supplied the raw insights; the
"theory" is a **posthumous synthesis** — named, organized, and extended by
others *after he died*.
</Callout>

### The people who built "Dow Theory" from his notes

- **S.A. Nelson** — in *The ABC of Stock Speculation* (1902), he compiled Dow's
  editorials and is credited as the first to actually use the term "Dow Theory."
- **William Peter Hamilton** (1867–1929) — Dow's successor and the *WSJ*'s
  fourth editor (1908–1929). He refined the ideas over 27 years of editorials
  and wrote *The Stock Market Barometer* (1922), the first systematic
  exposition. His editorial **"A Turn in the Tide"** (October 25, 1929) called
  the end of the bull market just before the Crash — and just weeks before his
  own death.
- **Robert Rhea** (1896–1939) — a bedridden invalid who studied the market
  intensively. His *The Dow Theory* (1932) organized Dow's and Hamilton's
  writings into the explicit tenets we use today; he used it to forecast the
  1932 bottom and 1937 top.
- Later popularizers like **Richard Russell** (*The Dow Theory Today*, 1958) and
  **Jack Schannep** (*Dow Theory for the 21st Century*, 2008) carried it forward.

## The core idea in one breath

The stock market, taken as a whole, forecasts the health of business better than
any single expert can. Watch the **broad averages** (not individual stocks) and
they reveal an underlying tide — a *primary trend* — that runs for months or
years. Your job is **not** to predict tops and bottoms, but to identify which way
the tide is currently flowing and stay with it until the market itself proves the
tide has turned. The market is a **barometer**: it already reflects all known
information, so its own price action is the best forecasting tool you have.

## The six tenets of Dow Theory

These are the heart of the lesson. Read them slowly — they look simple, and that
simplicity is exactly why they've survived for over a century.

### 1. The averages discount (already price in) everything

Here "discount" means price-in, not mark down: price already reflects all known
information — every hope, fear, earnings report, and rumor (the one exception
being genuine acts of God). Don't try to out-research the market on news — read
the price action. Dow anticipated the "efficient market" idea by decades.

> **Example:** A company reports weak earnings and the stock barely moves —
> because the market already *expected* weakness and priced it in. Only genuine
> surprises move price, and even those rarely shift the *primary* trend.

### 2. The market has three trends

Think of the ocean: **tide, waves, and ripples.**

| Trend | Analogy | Rough duration | What you do with it |
|-------|---------|----------------|---------------------|
| **Primary** | The tide | Months to years | This is what you trade with |
| **Secondary** | The waves | ~10 days to 3 months | A counter-move; opportunity, not reversal |
| **Minor** | The ripples | Hours up to ~3 weeks | Noise — largely ignore |

A **secondary** reaction typically retraces **one-third to two-thirds** (often
about half) of the prior primary move. It shakes out weak hands but does not turn
the tide. **Minor** trends are essentially noise.

### 3. Primary trends move through three phases

A **bull market**:

1. **Accumulation** — informed "smart money" buys quietly while the public is
   still pessimistic and price barely moves.
2. **Public participation** — the trend becomes obvious, trend-followers pile in,
   and the largest, fastest price gains occur.
3. **Excess / distribution** — euphoria and rampant speculation; the public is
   all-in, and smart money quietly sells into their enthusiasm.

A **bear market** runs the same psychology in reverse: distribution → decline →
panic/capitulation (a final burst of give-up selling where the last holders bail
out).

> **Example:** In early 2009, contrarians bought stocks while headlines were
> apocalyptic (accumulation). The years that followed saw the broad public
> return (participation).

### 4. The averages must confirm each other

This is Dow's signature contribution. A signal is only trustworthy when **two
different averages agree** — originally the Industrials and the Railroads (today
the Transports).

The logic is beautifully concrete: companies that **make** goods (Industrials)
and companies that **ship** them (Transports) are two ends of the same economy.
If factories are truly producing more, the railroads and trucks must be busy
hauling the output — so the two averages should rise together.

- A new high (or low) in one average is only valid when the **other average also
  makes a new high (or low)**. That is **confirmation**.
- When one makes a new high but the other fails to, that is a
  **non-confirmation** (a divergence) — a warning that the trend may be
  weakening. "Change is in the air." It does not reverse the trend by itself; it
  *withholds* a fresh buy/sell signal.

### 5. Volume must confirm the trend

Volume should swell in the direction of the primary trend — heavier on rallies in
a bull market, heavier on declines in a bear market. But volume is only a
*secondary, confirming* clue. It **never** initiates a signal. **Price is always
the final word.**

> **Example:** A breakout on shrinking volume is suspect — fewer and fewer buyers
> are carrying it.

### 6. A trend stays in effect until it definitively reverses

Trends get the benefit of the doubt. A sharp two-week drop inside an uptrend is
presumed to be a secondary reaction — *not* a reversal — until the market proves
otherwise. **Don't anticipate reversals; wait for them to be confirmed.** This
single principle is the ancestor of "the trend is your friend."

## How Dow Theory defines a trend (and a trend change)

This is the practical core you'll reuse constantly:

- An **uptrend** is a series of **higher highs and higher lows.**
- A **downtrend** is a series of **lower highs and lower lows.**

A trend change is **sequential**, not instant:

1. First, a rally **fails to make a new higher high** — and a **non-confirmation**
   often appears (one average fails to match the other's new extreme). This is a
   *warning* only.
2. The reversal is **confirmed** when price then **breaks below the prior
   secondary-reaction low** (bull→bear) — or above the prior reaction high
   (bear→bull) — and **both** averages agree.

<Callout type="warning">
The exact "secondary-reaction reference point" is the genuinely ambiguous part of
Dow Theory — even experts disagree on which prior low or high counts. Treat the
rule above as the standard interpretation, not a mechanical formula.
</Callout>

## A worked example

*(Stylized numbers, built to show the mechanics — not historical prints.)*

A bull market is underway. Watch both the **Industrials** and the **Transports.**

1. **March:** Industrials peak at **34,000**; Transports peak at **16,000.**
2. **April (secondary reaction):** Industrials pull back to **32,000** (a
   secondary low); Transports pull back to **15,000.** That's roughly a one-third
   to one-half retracement — normal. The tide is intact.
3. **May:** Industrials rally to a **new high of 35,000.** *But* the Transports
   reach only **15,800 — short of their March peak of 16,000.**
   - → **Non-confirmation.** Industrials made a higher high; Transports made a
     *lower* high. The averages disagree. No new buy signal; raise caution.
4. **June:** Industrials roll over to **31,500 — below the April secondary low of
   32,000** — and the Transports also break below **15,000.**
   - → **Confirmed reversal to a bear market.** The sequence of higher lows is
     broken (31,500 &lt; 32,000), *and both averages confirm* by breaking their
     prior reaction lows. The May non-confirmation was the early warning; the June
     break is the actionable signal.

**Contrast — a confirmed continuation:** if in May the Transports had instead
pushed to a *new* high (say 16,300) on rising volume, the two averages would have
agreed on a fresh high. That confirms the bull trend continues — you stay long.

Notice the trade-off: the reversal signal fires near **31,500 on the way down**,
not at the **35,000 top.** Dow Theory deliberately sacrifices the exact top and
bottom in exchange for avoiding false alarms. A real example: Hamilton's famous
**"A Turn in the Tide"** (October 25, 1929) declared the bull market over — a
celebrated call that nonetheless came *after* the crash had begun. That lag is
the theory working as designed, not failing.

## A beginner checklist to apply Dow Theory

<Callout type="key">
1. **Pull charts of both averages** — the DJIA *and* the DJ Transportation
   Average. (Dow Theory is about the broad averages, never a single stock.)
2. **Identify the current primary trend on each:** higher highs + higher lows =
   up; lower highs + lower lows = down.
3. **Check that they agree.** If one is trending and the other diverges → that's
   a non-confirmation → **wait.**
4. **Look for the next confirmed new high (bull) or new low (bear) in *both*
   averages**, ideally on expanding volume → the trend is confirmed to continue.
5. **Stay positioned with the primary trend.** Treat secondary reactions as noise
   or opportunities, not reversals.
6. **Only conclude a reversal** when an average fails to confirm a new extreme
   *and* then breaks the prior secondary swing point — confirmed by the other
   average.
</Callout>

## Legacy and modern implementation

Dow Theory is the **conceptual bedrock of technical analysis.** Trendlines,
support and resistance, trend-following systems, and intermarket analysis
(reading how different markets — stocks, bonds, commodities, currencies — move
together to confirm a signal) all descend directly from it.

- **Still in use:** the Dow Jones Transportation Average is still published for
  confirmation, and the CMT Association grants the **Charles H. Dow Award.**
- **An academic debate worth knowing:** Alfred Cowles, in "Can Stock Market
  Forecasters Forecast?" (*Econometrica*, 1934), tested Hamilton's actual
  Dow-Theory editorials (1902–1929) and found they *underperformed* buy-and-hold
  — roughly **12% annualized vs. ~15.5%** for a diversified buy-and-hold
  portfolio. (Buy-and-hold means simply holding through the ups and downs instead
  of trading in and out.) For decades that was treated as decisive. But **Brown,
  Goetzmann & Kumar**, in *The Journal of Finance* (1998), re-examined the same
  editorials and argued Cowles' method was flawed: Hamilton's timing produced
  **positive risk-adjusted returns** (a higher Sharpe ratio — return per unit of
  risk — and *lower* volatility). The raw return trailed by ~2%, but at less
  risk. The verdict is genuinely mixed — which is itself a lesson about technical
  analysis.

### Criticisms (be honest about these)

- **Lag** — by design, you never buy the bottom or sell the top; confirmation
  requires *both* averages to break key levels, so signals always arrive late.
- **Ambiguity** — defining a "secondary reaction" vs. a true reversal is
  subjective; the theory is a set of guidelines, not a science, and theorists
  routinely disagree on whether a signal has fired.
- **Dated averages** — the industrial/rail link made sense in a 1900s goods
  economy. In a modern services, digital, and intangibles economy, shipping
  volumes correlate far less with overall output, weakening the confirmation
  logic.
- **Only two indices** — relying on two narrow averages ignores breadth, sectors,
  and the rest of the market.
- **Tension with the efficient-market hypothesis** — tenet 1 ("the market
  discounts everything") echoes the EMH, which, if true, implies the *predictive*
  trend-following part shouldn't reliably add value.

## Common pitfalls and misconceptions

<Callout type="warning">
1. **Trading individual stocks with it.** Dow Theory is about the *broad
   averages*, not single names.
2. **Mistaking a secondary reaction for a reversal** — panic-selling a normal
   33–66% pullback and getting whipsawed.
3. **Ignoring the confirmation requirement** — acting on a new high/low in *one*
   average alone. A non-confirmation is a *warning to wait*, not a signal.
4. **Anticipating reversals** instead of waiting for them to be confirmed
   (violates tenet 6 — give the trend the benefit of the doubt).
5. **Using volume as the primary trigger.** Volume only *confirms*; it never
   initiates.
6. **Expecting precision or price targets.** Dow Theory identifies *direction*,
   never how far or how long — and it lags.
7. **Treating it as a complete, mechanical system.** It's a framework of
   principles requiring judgment — and it was never a formal system even to Dow
   himself.
8. **Confusing "Dow Theory" with "the Dow" (the DJIA).** The theory is a body of
   principles; the index is just one tool within it.
</Callout>

## Putting it to work

You don't "trade Dow Theory" with a single button. You absorb its mindset: think
in trends, demand confirmation before trusting a move, respect that a trend
persists until proven otherwise, and never confuse daily noise with the tide.
Every later chapter in this guide is a refinement of these instincts.
