## Why start here

Every tool in this guide — moving averages, RSI, Elliott Waves, candlesticks —
ultimately rests on one idea: **markets move in trends, and those trends can be
read.** That idea has a source. It comes from a quiet financial journalist who
died in 1902 and never once called his work a "theory," let alone a trading
system.

Understand Charles Dow and you understand the grammar of everything that
follows.

## Who he was

**Charles Henry Dow** (November 6, 1851 – December 4, 1902) was an American
financial reporter. In 1882 he co-founded **Dow Jones & Company** with Edward
Jones and Charles Bergstresser. The firm began humbly — hand-delivering
two-page financial bulletins, the *Customer's Afternoon Letter*, to Wall Street
subscribers. That bulletin grew into **_The Wall Street Journal_**, which Dow
founded and first edited in 1889.

Along the way he built something we still talk about every single day: the
**stock market average**.

- **July 3, 1884** — Dow published his first average: 11 companies, 9 of them
  railroads (railroads were the era's dominant public companies).
- **May 26, 1896** — the **Dow Jones Industrial Average** debuted with 12
  industrial stocks. (None of those original 12 remain in it today.)

From 1899 until his death in 1902, Dow wrote a market column for the *Journal*.
Those roughly 250 editorials are the entire raw material of what we now call Dow
Theory.

<Callout type="key">
Charles Dow never wrote a book and never used the phrase "Dow Theory." He
observed markets and wrote down what he saw. Others assembled his scattered
observations into a coherent body of thought **after he died**.
</Callout>

### The people who built "Dow Theory" from his notes

- **S.A. Nelson** — in *The ABC of Stock Speculation* (1902), he is credited as
  the first to actually use the term "Dow Theory."
- **William Peter Hamilton** — Dow's successor at the *WSJ*, who refined the
  ideas over 27 years of editorials and wrote *The Stock Market Barometer*
  (1922).
- **Robert Rhea** — whose *The Dow Theory* (1932) studied ~252 Dow and Hamilton
  editorials and remains the standard codification.
- Later popularizers like **E. George Schaefer** and **Richard Russell** carried
  it into the modern era.

## The six tenets of Dow Theory

These are the heart of the lesson. Read them slowly — they look simple, and that
simplicity is exactly why they've survived for over a century.

### 1. The averages discount everything

Price already reflects all known information: every hope, fear, earnings report,
and rumor. Dow anticipated the "efficient market" idea by decades.

> **Example:** A company reports weak earnings and the stock barely moves —
> because the market already *expected* weakness and priced it in. Only genuine
> surprises move price, and even those rarely shift the *primary* trend.

### 2. The market has three trends

Think of the ocean: **tide, waves, and ripples.**

| Trend | Analogy | Rough duration |
|-------|---------|----------------|
| **Primary** | The tide | Months to years |
| **Secondary** | The waves | Weeks to a few months |
| **Minor** | The ripples | Days up to ~3 weeks |

A **secondary** reaction typically retraces **one-third to two-thirds** (often
about half) of the prior primary move. **Minor** trends are essentially noise.

<Callout type="note">
Sources disagree on the exact durations — treat them as approximate ranges, not
hard rules. The *concept* (a big trend, counter-trend reactions within it, and
daily noise) is what matters.
</Callout>

### 3. Primary trends move through three phases

A **bull market**:

1. **Accumulation** — informed "smart money" buys while the public is still
   pessimistic.
2. **Public participation** — the trend becomes obvious, trend-followers pile
   in, and the largest price gains occur.
3. **Excess / distribution** — euphoria; the public is all-in, and smart money
   begins quietly selling.

A **bear market** runs the same psychology in reverse: distribution → decline →
panic/capitulation.

> **Example:** In early 2009, contrarians bought stocks while headlines were
> apocalyptic (accumulation). The years that followed saw the broad public
> return (participation).

### 4. The averages must confirm each other

This is Dow's signature contribution. A signal is only trustworthy when **two
different averages agree** — originally the Industrials and the Railroads (today
the Transports).

The logic is beautifully concrete: if factories (Industrials) are truly
producing more, then more goods must be **shipped** (Transports). The two should
rise together. When one average makes a new high and the other does **not**,
that **non-confirmation** is a warning.

### 5. Volume must confirm the trend

Volume should swell in the direction of the primary trend — heavier on rallies
in a bull market, heavier on declines in a bear market. But volume is only a
*secondary* clue. **Price is always the final word.**

> **Example:** A rally on shrinking volume is suspect — fewer and fewer buyers
> are carrying it.

### 6. A trend stays in effect until it definitively reverses

Trends get the benefit of the doubt. A sharp two-week drop inside an uptrend is
presumed to be a secondary reaction — *not* a reversal — until the market proves
otherwise. This single principle is the ancestor of "the trend is your friend."

## How Dow Theory defines a trend (and a trend change)

This is the practical core you'll reuse constantly:

- An **uptrend** is a series of **higher highs and higher lows.**
- A **downtrend** is a series of **lower highs and lower lows.**

A trend change is **sequential**, not instant:

1. First, a **non-confirmation** appears (one average fails to match the other's
   new extreme) — a *warning* only.
2. The reversal is **confirmed** when **both** averages then break the prior
   secondary-reaction low (bull→bear) or high (bear→bull).

<Callout type="warning">
The exact "secondary-reaction reference point" is the genuinely ambiguous part
of Dow Theory — even experts disagree on which prior low or high counts. Treat
the rule above as the standard interpretation, not a mechanical formula.
</Callout>

## A worked example

*(Stylized numbers, to show the mechanics.)*

1. The **Industrials** rally to a new high of **35,000**. The prior
   secondary-reaction low was **32,000**.
2. The **Transports** rally but **fail to exceed their own prior high** — a
   **non-confirmation.** Warning flag raised; the trend is *not yet* broken.
3. Both averages sell off, then bounce. The Industrials' next rally tops at
   **34,000** — a **lower high** — and the Transports also make a lower high.
4. Both averages then **break below 32,000** (the prior reaction low). When
   *both* confirm, the primary trend is judged to have **reversed from bull to
   bear.**

Notice the trade-off: the signal fires near **34,000 on the way down**, not at
the **35,000 top.** Dow Theory deliberately sacrifices the top and bottom in
exchange for avoiding false alarms.

A real example: Hamilton's famous editorial **"A Turn in the Tide"** (October
25, 1929) declared the bull market over — a celebrated call that nonetheless
came *after* the crash had already begun. That lag is the theory working as
designed, not failing.

## Legacy and modern implementation

Dow Theory is the **conceptual bedrock of technical analysis.** Trendlines,
support and resistance, trend-following systems, and intermarket analysis all
descend directly from it.

- **Still in use:** the Dow Jones Transportation Average is still published for
  confirmation, and the CMT Association grants the **Charles H. Dow Award.**
- **An academic debate worth knowing:** Alfred Cowles (1934) argued Hamilton's
  Dow Theory calls *underperformed* buy-and-hold. But Brown, Goetzmann & Kumar,
  in *The Journal of Finance* (1998), re-examined the same editorials and found
  Hamilton's timing produced **better risk-adjusted returns** (higher Sharpe
  ratios, lower volatility). Reasonable people still argue about it — which is
  itself a lesson about technical analysis.

### Criticisms (be honest about these)

- **Lag** — by design, you never buy the bottom or sell the top.
- **Ambiguity** — defining a "secondary reaction" is subjective; the theory is a
  set of guidelines, not a science.
- **Dated averages** — the Transports may represent a service/tech economy less
  faithfully than rails represented the 1890s.

## Common pitfalls and misconceptions

<Callout type="warning">
1. **Treating it as a precise mechanical system.** It is interpretive —
   reasonable analysts read the same chart differently.
2. **Confusing "Dow Theory" with "the Dow" (the DJIA).** The theory is a body of
   principles; the index is just one tool within it.
3. **Expecting price targets.** Dow Theory identifies trend *direction*, never
   *how far* or *how long.*
4. **Trading the minor trend.** Daily wiggles are explicitly noise.
5. **Treating volume as the primary signal.** Volume only confirms — price
   decides.
</Callout>

## Putting it to work

You don't "trade Dow Theory" with a single button. You absorb its mindset:
think in trends, demand confirmation before trusting a move, respect that a
trend persists until proven otherwise, and never confuse daily noise with the
tide. Every later chapter in this guide is a refinement of these instincts.
