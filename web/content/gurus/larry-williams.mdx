## The one-sentence version

In 1987, a Montana journalist-turned-trader named Larry Williams took $10,000 in a real-money futures contest and grew it past $1 million in twelve months — and then spent the next three decades teaching, selling, and occasionally being honest about why almost nobody (including him) has done it again.

Larry Williams matters not because he found a magic indicator, but because he built a complete, repeatable *style*: short-term futures timing that fuses a momentum oscillator, volatility-based entries, named gap/reversal patterns, calendar and seasonal tendencies, the positioning of professional hedgers, and — crucially — an aggressive, mathematically explicit way of *sizing* each bet. His career is also a useful case study in survivorship, marketing, and the gap between a spectacular result and a reliable method. We will take all of it seriously — the genuinely good ideas and the honest caveats — and at every step say exactly what a beginner should *do* differently from what he did.

## Who he was

Larry R. Williams was born **October 6, 1942**, in Miles City, Montana, and graduated from the University of Oregon's **School of Journalism** in **1964** ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Larry_R._Williams)). Note the degree: journalism, not finance. He is **self-taught as a trader**, active in the markets since **1962**, and the author of roughly a dozen books — most famously *How I Made One Million Dollars Last Year Trading Commodities* (1979) and *Long-Term Secrets to Short-Term Trading* (1998), the canonical text for this lesson. He created the **Williams %R** oscillator and the **Ultimate Oscillator**, plus COT-based indices; his indicators are licensed into platforms like TradeStation, NinjaTrader, eSignal, and StockCharts.

He also ran twice as the Republican U.S. Senate nominee from Montana — losing to Max Baucus in **1978** and to John Melcher in **1982** — and he is the father of actress **Michelle Williams**. There is a financial footnote: Michelle won the same World Cup Trading Championship about a decade after he did, a fact that cuts both ways — suggesting either a teachable edge or a family well-versed in how the contest's math works.

He is not without controversy. Williams faced a trial on three counts of tax evasion; on **February 5, 2010**, the felony charges were dropped and he **pleaded guilty to three misdemeanor counts of failing to file income-tax returns on time** for tax years **1999–2001** ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Larry_R._Williams)). He later wrote *Confessions of a Radical Tax Protestor* (2011). Worth knowing when weighing how much to trust any single narrative about him — and separate from his trading.

<Callout type="warning">

**A self-promotion / impostor caveat, up front.** Williams sells courses, seminars, and indicators, and a great deal of the "Larry Williams strategy" content online is marketing-flavored or written by third parties. His own official site prominently warns that many people falsely claim to have been "personally mentored" by him. Read his published *method* on its merits; treat self-reported and contest-sponsor performance numbers with skepticism.

</Callout>

<Callout type="note">

"Futures" are standardized contracts to buy or sell something (oil, wheat, an index) at a set date. They are *leveraged*: you control a large contract with a small deposit ("margin"), so gains and losses are amplified. This leverage is the engine behind both Williams' contest result and the risk that comes with copying him.

</Callout>

## The core idea in one breath

Over short horizons, markets are driven by **emotion and over-reaction, not value.** Williams' edge is to **buy when the crowd is fearful and sell when it's greedy** — but only with **objective, pre-defined triggers**, never gut-feel discretion. He looks through four lenses, and a signal is strongest when several **agree**:

1. **Momentum / over-extension** (Williams %R) — is price stretched too far?
2. **Volatility & range structure** (breakouts, the Oops! gap reversal) — has price moved far enough to signal a real move, or gapped far enough to snap back?
3. **Who is positioned where** (the COT report) — are the smart-money commercials buying while the public sells?
4. **Calendar tendencies** (seasonality, day-of-week) — does this date historically favor up or down?

The fuel that turned $10k into $1.1M was *not* a magic indicator — it was **aggressive but defined position sizing** layered on top of a modest edge. Keep that order in mind for the whole lesson.

## The 1987 World Cup Trading Championship

The World Cup Championship of Futures Trading (run by Robbins Trading Company) is scored on *percentage return* in a real, audited brokerage account. In 1987 Williams turned **$10,000 into $1,137,600 — an 11,376% return** for the year ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Larry_R._Williams)).

That number is real in the sense that it happened in a tracked account. But you must understand *how* it happened before you treat it as a target.

Here is the math that makes it both famous *and* unrepeatable. $10,000 → $1,137,600 is a multiple of **×113.76** over roughly **250 trading days.** Compounded, that is only about **1.9% growth per day** — which sounds modest until you realize it is *only* achievable with heavy leverage on a positive-expectancy system, run on a tiny, all-in account. The same percentage on $10M would move the markets he traded and is simply not realistic.

<Callout type="warning">

A contest scored on percentage return rewards extreme, concentrated leverage. To grow an account 100x in a year, you must repeatedly risk a very large fraction of capital and be right. The same approach that produces an 11,000% winner also produces a long tail of competitors who blow up to zero — and we mostly only hear about the winner. This is **survivorship bias**, and Williams himself has acknowledged he traded far larger position sizes in the contest than he would recommend for a normal account.

</Callout>

So the honest reading: the *method* may have an edge; the *result* is an inseparable mix of skill, aggressive sizing, and luck. Replicating the method is plausible. Replicating the 11,376% is, for almost everyone, a path to ruin.

## Core idea 1 — Williams %R

This is his best-known indicator and the place to anchor the technical work. **%R** is a momentum *oscillator* — a tool that measures where today's close sits inside the recent high-low range, to gauge whether price is stretched.

The intuition: in an uptrend, closes tend to cluster near the *top* of the recent range; in a downtrend, near the *bottom*. %R simply quantifies that position.

```
%R = (Highest High over N − Close today) / (Highest High over N − Lowest Low over N) × −100

where:
  Highest High over N = the highest high of the last N bars
  Lowest Low over N   = the lowest low of the last N bars
  Close today         = the most recent closing price
```

In the worked example below we use N = 14, so "last N bars" means the last 14 daily bars — i.e. the last 14 trading days (on a daily chart, one bar is one day).

The result ranges from **0 to −100**:
- **0 to −20** = overbought (close near the top of the range)
- **−80 to −100** = oversold (close near the bottom)

The default lookback is commonly **N = 14**, though Williams originally used a **10-trading-day** period ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Williams_%25R)).

### Worked example (illustrative)

Suppose over the last 14 days a market's highest high is **110**, its lowest low is **90**, and today it closes at **104**.

```
%R = (110 − 104) / (110 − 90) × −100
   = (6 / 20) × −100
   = 0.30 × −100
   = −30
```

A reading of −30 says the close is in the upper-middle of its range: firm, but not yet "overbought." If tomorrow it closed at 108 with the same range, %R = (2 / 20) × −100 = **−10**, now in overbought territory.

<Callout type="key">

%R is *arithmetically equivalent to the %K line of the stochastic oscillator* (%K is stochastics' raw range-position line, scored 0–100), *just shifted onto a negative scale* (%R = %K − 100). If you already know stochastics, you know %R — it is the same range-position math, on a 0-to-−100 scale instead of 0-to-100. Do not run both and think you are getting independent confirmation; you are not.

</Callout>

<Callout type="note">

A sign-convention quirk: Williams' original book multiplied by **+100** (giving a 0-to-100 scale), but a widely-copied **−100** variant — possibly spread by a printing error — is now the default in most software. The interpretation is identical; just check which scale your platform uses.

</Callout>

**How to read it without getting whipsawed (repeatedly stopped out by false signals in a choppy market):** Williams explicitly *warned against* mechanically buying every oversold reading and selling every overbought one — in a strong trend, %R can pin near an extreme for a long time. His refinement was *confirmation by the turn*: in an uptrend, wait for %R to reach oversold (−80 to −100), let several days pass, and act only when it **climbs back up through** a threshold such as −85% or −95% — a timed pullback-buy. A **divergence** — price makes a new extreme but %R does not — is a stronger signal than any single reading. (A *failure swing*, where %R refuses to confirm a new price extreme, is one form of divergence.)

## Core idea 2 — Volatility breakout entries

Williams popularized **volatility breakout** entries: instead of guessing a top or bottom, you let the market prove momentum by moving a set distance beyond today's open.

The classic recipe:

```
Buy stop   = Today's open + (k × yesterday's range)
Sell stop  = Today's open − (k × yesterday's range)

where range = (High − Low) of the prior day
      k     = a fraction of that range
```

**What is k?** Williams' own published examples typically used **about 0.25 to 0.5** of the prior day's range (some variants run higher, up to ~0.8; a full 1.0× is a more aggressive, trend-following version). A **buy stop** is an order that sits above the current price and only executes if price climbs up to it — so it fires automatically the moment a thrust confirms upward momentum (a **sell stop** is the mirror, sitting below price and firing if price drops to it). The logic: once price moves a meaningful fraction of yesterday's range beyond today's open, a directional move is likely underway, so you join it. The stop is **recomputed every morning** from that day's open and the prior day's range — it is not a fixed standing level.

### Worked example (entry math)

Yesterday: High **5,050**, Low **5,000** → range = **50 points.** Today opens at **5,020.** With k = 0.4:

```
Buy stop  = 5,020 + (0.4 × 50) = 5,040
Sell stop = 5,020 − (0.4 × 50) = 5,000
```

If price rises to **5,040** first, you go long, place a hard stop near 5,000, and ride the range expansion. Because the trigger distance scales with the market's *own* recent volatility, the system stays adaptive across calm and wild markets rather than using a fixed dollar amount.

## Core idea 3 — Short-term timing patterns

The book that gives this lesson its subtitle, *Long-Term Secrets to Short-Term Trading* (1998), is mostly a catalog of **specific, named entry patterns**. %R and the volatility breakout are two of them; here are three more that Williams used heavily. None is a standalone system — each is a *trigger* you take only when the bigger picture (sentiment, seasonality, trend) already leans your way.

### "Oops!" — the open-gap reversal (his most famous setup)

The market **gaps** (opens with a jump beyond yesterday's range) in one direction on fear or news, then *reverses* — and Williams' reaction was the word "Oops!", as in *oops, I was wrong to gap.*

- **Bullish Oops!:** today **opens below yesterday's low** (a down-gap). Place a **buy stop at yesterday's low**. If price climbs back up and trades through that low, you're long.
- **Bearish Oops! (mirror):** today opens **above yesterday's high**; place a **sell stop at yesterday's high.**
- **Filter:** require a minimum gap size (roughly **15–20 ticks**) so you are fading a real gap, not noise.
- **Exit:** Williams' original rule was the **"first profitable open"** — exit on the next session open that shows a profit; modern intraday users close at session end. Always use a hard stop.

The idea: an opening gap driven by overnight panic often gets faded once the regular session opens, and the snap back through the prior extreme is the tell.

<Callout type="warning">

**Oops! was built for the overnight-close era.** It needs a real session break so that genuine *opening gaps* can form. On modern 24-hour electronic markets (FX, many futures), clean gaps are rare, so raw versions backtest worse post-2000. It works best on instruments with a true session break — e.g. index futures or the DAX. Don't run Oops! on a market that never really "opens."

</Callout>

### Trade Day of Week (TDW) — the calendar inside the week

Williams was an early, persistent proponent of the idea that **the day of the week carries a statistical bias.** Through decades of backtests he argued certain markets had a recurring tendency to bottom or top on particular weekdays — for example, buying an index future on a weak **Monday or Tuesday** close and exiting later in the week. He generalized this into **TDW** (and a finer "Trade Day of Month") filters: only take the setup if today is a historically favorable weekday for that market.

### Smash-day reversals

A **smash day** is a bar that makes a dramatic close in one direction — a down close below the prior day's low (a "downside smash"), seemingly confirming a breakdown. The reversal pattern fires when **the very next day trades back above the smash day's high** (for a long): the breakdown failed, trapped sellers must cover, and that short-covering fuels the bounce. The mirror (an "upside smash" that fails the next day) sets up a short. The common thread across Oops! and smash days is the same: **a failed move in one direction becomes fuel for a move in the other.**

<Callout type="warning">

These patterns are seductive because they are concrete and easy to backtest — which is exactly the danger. Williams' own published win rates come from his marketing materials, and weekday/calendar edges in particular are notorious for **decaying once widely known** (and for being products of *overfitting* — see criticisms). Treat them as triggers to layer on top of real context, not as money machines, and demand that any edge survive out-of-sample testing before you size up.

</Callout>

## Core idea 4 — Commercials and the COT report

This is arguably his most durable contribution to *sentiment* analysis. The **Commitments of Traders (COT)** report is published weekly by the **U.S. Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC)** — the U.S. federal regulator for futures markets — reflecting positions as of the preceding **Tuesday's close** and released **Fridays at 3:30 p.m. Eastern** ([CFTC](https://www.cftc.gov/MarketReports/CommitmentsofTraders/index.htm)). In its legacy format it splits futures open interest into the categories below. (**Open interest** is the total number of futures contracts still open — entered into but not yet closed out or settled — at a given time; the COT report sorts that total by who is holding it.)

| Category | Who | Reputation |
|---|---|---|
| **Commercials** (hedgers) | Producers/users with real exposure (a farmer, an airline) | "Smart money" / informed |
| **Non-commercials** (large speculators) | Funds trading for profit | Trend-following crowd |
| **Non-reportable** (small speculators) | Positions below reporting size | Often wrong at extremes |

Williams built **COT indices** that normalize commercial positioning over a lookback window (often about three years) onto a **0–100% scale**. **Net-long** means a group holds more buy (long) contracts than sell (short) ones after the two offset; **net-short** is the reverse. The thesis: commercials understand their own market best, so when hedgers swing to an *extreme net-long* (index near 100%), it can mark a bottom; an extreme net-short (near 0%) can mark a top. It is a *contrarian sentiment bias*, not a precise trigger.

<Callout type="warning">

COT is **lagging and incomplete**: it is released three days stale (Tuesday data, Friday release), and reportable positions cover only roughly 70–90% of total open interest. It tells you *who is positioned how*, not *when* to act. Treat it as a slow-moving backdrop — a multi-week tailwind or headwind — **never** as a same-day entry signal.

</Callout>

## Core idea 5 — Seasonality

A *seasonal* tendency is a recurring calendar pattern — a market that has historically tended to rise or fall during a specific window of the year, usually for a structural reason. The classic Williams example is **heating oil**, which has often firmed heading into the Northern-Hemisphere winter as demand for heat rises, then eased in spring. Grains can soften around harvest when new supply floods in; gasoline can firm into the summer driving season. Williams would check whether the date sat inside such a historically favorable window and let it *tilt* his bias — taking his A/B/C-grade entry signals only in the direction of the seasonal lean, never acting on the seasonal alone.

## Core idea 6 — Aggressive money management

This is the piece the contest result actually turns on, and the one beginners most often skip. Williams is explicit in his books that **how much you bet matters more than what you buy.** Two named approaches sit at opposite ends of the aggressiveness scale.

### The 0.5–2% risk rule (the conservative anchor — use this)

The standard, survivable approach: on any single trade, risk no more than about **0.5–2% of account equity** — where "risk" is the distance from entry to your stop, times position size. If your stop is far away, you trade fewer contracts; if it's tight, you can hold more. Lose ten in a row at 2% and you're down ~18%, bruised but alive. This is the rule we recommend for almost everyone, and it is emphatically *not* how Williams won the 1987 contest.

### Optimal-f, fixed-fractional & Kelly sizing (the aggressive engine)

Williams's contest sizing was built on **fixed-fractional position sizing**: bet a *fixed fraction* of current equity each time, so position size **compounds** as the account grows — win, and the next bet is bigger in dollars; lose, and it shrinks. That compounding is exactly what bends an equity curve from linear into the near-vertical 1987 climb.

The sharp edge of this idea is **optimal-f**, popularized by **Ralph Vince** (in *Portfolio Management Formulas*, 1990), whose work Williams championed and used. It answers: *what single fixed fraction of capital, risked per trade, maximizes long-run geometric (compounded) growth, given this system's historical wins and losses?* It is closely related to the **Kelly criterion**:

```
f = (b · p − q) / b

where:
  p = probability of a win
  q = 1 − p (probability of a loss)
  b = win/loss payoff ratio (size of a win ÷ size of a loss)
```

**Worked example.** Suppose a system wins **55%** of the time (p = 0.55, q = 0.45) at even money (b = 1):

```
f = (1 × 0.55 − 0.45) / 1 = 0.10  →  bet 10% of equity per trade
```

Risking 10% of a growing account per trade is explosive on the upside — and brutal on the downside. A short losing streak at 10% risk halves the account fast. This is the engine behind 1.9%-per-day compounding; it is also why it nearly killed the account along the way (Williams reportedly rode through a 50%+ drawdown during the run).

<Callout type="key">

**Why "optimal" is a trap for beginners.** Optimal-f and full Kelly maximize *growth rate*, not survival. At the true optimal fraction, the expected **drawdowns are brutal** — routinely 50–90% peak-to-trough — and betting *above* it grows the account *more slowly* while sharply raising the **risk of ruin** (going broke). Worse, the fraction is computed from *past* trades; if the future is even slightly worse than the backtest, sizing at optimal-f marches you toward zero. This is precisely why practitioners trade a small slice of it — **half- or quarter-Kelly** — and why the plain **0.5–2% rule** exists as the safe default. A beginner should risk 0.5–2%, full stop.

</Callout>

So the honest summary: **fixed-fractional / Kelly-style compounding is the real "secret" of the 11,376%** — not %R, not the breakout. It is also why copying his contest sizing in a live account is the fastest route to zero. The transferable lesson is to size by a *fraction of equity* with a defined per-trade risk, and to keep that fraction small.

## Honest criticisms

- **The contest result oversells the method.** Extreme leverage (fixed-fractional sizing near optimal-f) plus survivorship bias inflate the headline, and contest accounts are tiny and all-in. Williams' *audited* returns in following years were far lower. He deserves credit for often saying so himself.
- **It doesn't scale.** The aggressive sizing that produced 11,376% on $10k is mathematically and practically impossible on large capital and guarantees catastrophic drawdowns at full size.
- **Regime decay.** Oops! depends on overnight session gaps that have largely vanished in 24-hour electronic markets; raw versions backtest worse post-2000.
- **%R is not unique.** It is a relabeled stochastic %K; it reads "overbought/oversold" *because* a trend is strong, and in trends it stays pinned. It is a *timing* tool, not a standalone system.
- **Seasonality and calendar patterns are fragile.** TDW and seasonals are notoriously prone to *overfitting* and decay once widely known.
- **Self-promotion conflict.** Much bio and performance data flows through promotional channels; many published "Williams strategies" are marketing-flavored, and his own site warns of impostors.
- **Optimal-f is dangerous as stated.** It maximizes growth, not survival, and is computed from the past; used full-size it courts ruin. Its honest role is conceptual.
- **Personal credibility caveat.** The 2010 misdemeanor tax-filing guilty pleas (tax years 1999–2001) are a fair thing to flag, separate from his trading.

## Common pitfalls

- **Trading %R extremes blindly** in trends — the single biggest beginner error, and one Williams explicitly warned against. Wait for the *turn back through* the level.
- **Copying contest position sizing** (full optimal-f / Kelly at contest size) in a real account — the fastest route to a zeroed account. Use 0.5–2% risk per trade.
- **Trading Oops! on 24-hour markets** with no real session break — there are no clean gaps to fade.
- **Stacking redundant tools** (%R + stochastics + another momentum gauge) and mistaking correlated readings for independent confirmation.
- **Treating COT, seasonality, or TDW as a timing trigger** rather than as slow context or a bias filter (COT data is three days stale at release).
- **Curve-fitting** the breakout fraction k and the lookback N to past data, then expecting them to hold live.
- **No hard stop / no defined risk** — Williams' entire edge is *defined* risk. "Aggressive money management" is not the same as "no stop."
- **Chasing the $1.1M dream** — expecting contest-style returns leads to over-leverage and ruin.

## How to apply it today

Start in a *simulator* or with tiny size on a liquid market with a real session break. Build the stack in order:

1. Check the latest COT for commercial extremes.
2. Note the seasonal (and TDW) lean.
3. Wait for a %R divergence or extreme — and the *turn back through* the threshold — in that direction.
4. Enter on a volatility breakout or a short-term pattern (Oops!, smash-day) that fires in the same direction; place the entry stop and a hard protective stop *before* you're filled.
5. **Size by a small fixed fraction with that hard stop — risk 0.5–2% per trade, not contest-style leverage.**

Journal every trade. The goal is a *process* with positive expectancy (the average trade makes money once you weight wins and losses by their size and frequency) over many trades — not one heroic year.
