## The one-sentence version

Joel Greenblatt's entire philosophy fits in a single sentence: **buy good
companies at cheap prices — and do it by the numbers, not by gut feeling.**

The rest of this lesson is learning what "good" and "cheap" mean precisely enough
that you could rank a thousand companies on a Saturday morning and act on the list
on Monday — and, just as important, why staying with that list through bad years
is the part almost nobody manages.

## Who he is

**Joel Greenblatt** (born December 13, 1957, in Great Neck, New York) is an
American hedge-fund manager, author, and longtime teacher who did something
unusual: he took the hands-on, judgment-heavy craft of value investing and
distilled its core into a **mechanical, rules-based formula** that an ordinary
person can follow without a finance degree.

- He earned a B.S. (*summa cum laude*, 1979) and an M.B.A. (1980) from the
  **Wharton School** at the University of Pennsylvania. He started Stanford Law
  School, then left after a year for investing.

<Callout type="note">
A common mix-up: Greenblatt's *degrees* are from **Wharton**, not Columbia.
Columbia is where he has *taught* value investing to MBA students for 20-plus
years — he studied at Penn, he teaches at Columbia.
</Callout>

- In **1985 he launched Gotham Capital with about $7 million** (seed capital
  substantially from Michael Milken). Over **1985–1994 the fund reportedly
  compounded at roughly 50% a year before fees** (about 30% net of all fees) — an
  extraordinary record, though it was a small, concentrated fund leaning heavily
  on "special situations" (more on those below). In 1995 he returned most outside
  capital and ran mostly internal money.
- He **co-founded the Value Investors Club** (with John Petry), an
  invitation-only, idea-driven community capped at ~250 members, and later formed
  **Gotham Asset Management** (2008, with longtime partner Robert Goldstein),
  which runs systematic long/short value funds.
- A fun footnote: in 2000 he helped seed Michael Burry's Scion Capital — the same
  Burry of *The Big Short*, who credits *You Can Be a Stock Market Genius* as
  foundational.

<Callout type="key">
Why Greenblatt matters as a "master": he is the clearest **bridge from
discretionary value investing to systematic, quant-style factor investing.** He
began as a Graham-and-Buffett-style stock picker doing deep, case-by-case work,
then packaged the core of that judgment into two numbers anyone can compute.
</Callout>

## The core idea in plain language

Most investors get only *half* of value investing right: they buy good companies
that are expensive, or cheap companies that are bad. Greenblatt's "Magic Formula"
(his own slightly tongue-in-cheek name) insists on **both at once** — a borrowing
straight from Warren Buffett and Charlie Munger's "a wonderful business at a fair
price."

- **"Cheap"** means the company earns a lot relative to its price — a **high
  earnings yield.** You're getting more operating profit per dollar you pay.
- **"Good"** means the company earns a lot relative to the capital it actually
  needs to run the business — a **high return on capital.** That signals an
  efficient, often competitively-advantaged business.

His insight: don't try to find the single perfect stock. Instead, **rank every
company on both dimensions, combine the ranks, and mechanically buy the
best-scoring names.** Doing it by rule removes emotion — and emotion is exactly
what makes most investors buy high and sell low. The deeper bet is that markets
misprice businesses in the short run, but over the long run price converges to
value, so a patient, rules-based buyer of cheap-and-good companies wins.

<Callout type="key">
The strategy works *because* it sometimes underperforms for uncomfortable
stretches. Those painful periods scare most people out of it — which is precisely
why the edge survives for the patient few who stay in their seats.
</Callout>

## The two metrics (use these exact definitions)

This is the part beginners most often get wrong. Greenblatt's two metrics look
like the familiar P/E ratio and return on equity (ROE) — but they are
**deliberately different**, and the differences are the whole point.

### Earnings Yield = EBIT ÷ Enterprise Value

- **EBIT** = Earnings Before Interest and Taxes — the raw operating profit, before
  the company's debt load and tax situation muddy the picture.
- **Enterprise Value (EV)** = market value of the stock **plus** net
  interest-bearing debt. It's the true "all-in" cost of buying the whole
  business, debt and all.

Why not just use the everyday **P/E** (price ÷ earnings)? Because P/E ignores
debt. Two companies can earn the same profit, but one is loaded with debt and one
is debt-free — and EBIT/EV reflects that, while P/E doesn't. EBIT/EV puts a
debt-heavy and a debt-free company on the same footing, and normalizes for
different tax rates too.

### Return on Capital = EBIT ÷ (Net Working Capital + Net Fixed Assets)

- **Net Working Capital** = the short-term money tied up running the business
  (inventory and receivables, minus what suppliers extend you).
- **Net Fixed Assets** = the long-term physical capital (factories, equipment)
  net of depreciation.

Why not just use **ROE** (return on equity)? Because ROE can be inflated by debt
and is distorted by accounting quirks of book equity. Greenblatt's version asks a
cleaner question: *for every dollar of real, tangible capital this business needs
to operate, how much operating profit does it throw off?* The denominator
deliberately excludes goodwill, so it measures the capital the business actually
needs. A high number means a genuinely efficient, high-quality business.

## The full method — concrete steps a beginner can follow

Here is the complete recipe. Steps 1–5 come straight from Greenblatt's official
free screener (magicformulainvesting.com); steps 6–9 add the portfolio-management
detail from the book.

1. **Filter the universe by size.** Set a minimum market capitalization. A common
   floor is **$50M+**, but beginners are often better served by a higher floor
   (say `>$100M`, even `>$1B`) for liquidity and cleaner data.
2. **Exclude financials and utilities** (banks, insurers, utilities) — the
   EBIT-and-capital math distorts for them — and **exclude foreign ADRs.**
3. **Rank every remaining company by earnings yield** (1 = cheapest) and
   **separately by return on capital** (1 = highest quality).
4. **Add the two ranks together.** The lowest combined score is the best
   "cheap AND good" company. (A stock ranked 20th-cheapest and 5th-best-quality
   scores 25.)
5. **Buy a diversified basket of the top-ranked names — typically 20–30 stocks.**
   Concentration in just a few names turns a factor strategy into a gamble.
6. **Build the portfolio gradually.** Greenblatt's book suggests buying roughly
   **5–7 stocks every 2–3 months over a 12-month period**, so you're not betting
   everything on one entry date. (The site confirms the 12-month purchase window;
   the precise cadence is book-sourced and approximate.)
7. **Hold each stock about one year**, then rebalance into a fresh top-ranked
   list.
8. **Sell tax-smart:** sell **losers just *before*** the one-year mark (to book a
   short-term loss) and **winners just *after*** one year (to qualify for
   lower long-term capital-gains tax). A beginner pitfall is getting this
   backwards.
9. **Repeat for the long haul** — a minimum of **3–5 years, ideally 5–10+.** This
   is non-negotiable: the edge only shows up over long periods and through
   genuinely painful drawdowns.

## A worked example

*(Illustrative numbers, to show how the ranking actually decides.)*

Compare three companies. Watch how being cheapest alone, or highest-quality alone,
does **not** win — the formula rewards the best *combination.*

**Company A — strong on both (cheap AND good):**

- EBIT = $200M; Enterprise Value = $1,000M → **Earnings Yield = 200 / 1,000 = 20%**
- Net Working Capital + Net Fixed Assets = $500M → **Return on Capital = 200 / 500 = 40%**

**Company B — superb quality, but expensive:**

- EBIT = $150M; Enterprise Value = $1,500M → **Earnings Yield = 150 / 1,500 = 10%**
- Capital = $300M → **Return on Capital = 150 / 300 = 50%**

**Company C — very cheap, but low quality:**

- EBIT = $100M; Enterprise Value = $400M → **Earnings Yield = 100 / 400 = 25%**
- Capital = $1,000M → **Return on Capital = 100 / 1,000 = 10%**

Now rank each metric across the three (1 = best) and add the ranks:

| Company | Earn. yield (rank) | Ret. on capital (rank) | **Combined** |
|---------|:-:|:-:|:-:|
| A — cheap **and** good | 20% (#2) | 40% (#2) | **4** |
| B — good but pricey | 10% (#3) | 50% (#1) | **4** |
| C — cheap but weak | 25% (#1) | 10% (#3) | **4** |

In this tiny three-company world all three tie at 4 — and that is itself the
lesson. **C wins the cheapness contest but is a poor business; B wins quality but
is expensive; only A scores near the top on *both*.** In a real universe of ~3,500
companies the combined-rank distribution spreads out, the top ~30 names are the
rare stocks that score strongly on both axes — and a balanced name like A floats
to the top while the one-dimensional names like B and C drift down the list.

You would never buy three names in isolation — you'd compute these two metrics for
the whole eligible market, add the ranks, and **buy the 20–30 names with the
lowest combined scores.**

In a real universe the spread is what does the work. Picture ~3,500 eligible
companies, each given a rank on *cheapness* (1–3,500) and a rank on *quality*
(1–3,500), with the two ranks added. Most companies are mediocre on at least one
axis, so their combined scores pile up in the middle and high end. Only a thin
sliver scores low on **both** — and those are the names that "float up" to the top
of the list. The chart below shows how few companies actually land in that
buy-worthy bucket.

## You Can Be a Stock Market Genius — the other Greenblatt

Before the Magic Formula made him famous, Greenblatt wrote a book for a very
different audience. **You Can Be a Stock Market Genius** (1997, despite the cheesy
title) is his playbook for **special situations** — corporate events that
temporarily misprice a stock and that few analysts bother to study. This is a
*separate discipline* from the formula: not a mechanical screen, but case-by-case
detective work.

- **Spin-offs** (his favorite) — when a company carves off a division as a new
  standalone stock.
- **Merger securities / risk arbitrage** — capturing the small, defined spread
  between a deal price and the current price.
- **Bankruptcies, restructurings, recapitalizations, "stub" stocks, rights
  offerings, and LEAPS** (long-dated options to lever a thesis).

**Why spin-offs tend to be mispriced** (and why he loved them):

- Index funds and big institutions that hold the parent receive the small spinoff
  shares and **dump them indiscriminately** — not because the business is bad, but
  because it's too small, off-mandate, or not in their index. That creates
  artificial selling pressure.
- The new company gets **little analyst coverage** at first, so information is
  underdigested.
- Freed from the conglomerate, management often gets **direct equity incentives**
  and runs the business better; a clean sum-of-the-parts value finally becomes
  visible. Greenblatt's tell: watch for insiders loading up on the new shares.

This is the **deep, hands-on, case-by-case** end of Greenblatt — the opposite of
the mechanical formula. It demands real work per situation and does **not** scale
to large capital, but the payoff is hunting where the crowd isn't looking. The
Magic Formula was Greenblatt's later answer to "what can someone do who *can't* do
all that homework?"

## The key books

- **"How the Small Investor Can Beat the Market"** — *Journal of Portfolio
  Management* (1981), co-authored with Rich Pzena and Bruce Newberg. The earliest
  statement of his lifelong theme: ordinary investors can systematically
  outperform.
- **You Can Be a Stock Market Genius** (1997) — the special-situations playbook
  described above. Despite the title, the content is advanced, not get-rich-quick.
- **The Little Book That Beats the Market** (2005) — introduces the Magic Formula;
  a bestseller (300,000+ copies). Updated as **The Little Book That *Still* Beats
  the Market** (2010) with refreshed data. *This is the primary text for the
  formula.*
- **The Big Secret for the Small Investor** (2011) — argues for value-weighted
  indexing over standard cap-weighted index funds for ordinary investors.
- **Common Sense** (2020) — a broader book on the economy and opportunity, less
  about stock selection.

## Honest criticisms and limitations

Be skeptical — Greenblatt himself would want you to be.

<Callout type="warning">
**The real-world returns are far below the book's headline.** The 2005 book
reported about **30.8% annualized for 1988–2004 vs. ~12.4% for the S&P 500** in
backtests. But that is Greenblatt's *own in-sample backtest* over a chosen window.
**Independent replications find far less.** Backtester **Robert Andrew Martin**,
re-running the formula on U.S. stocks, found only roughly **~3% annualized
outperformance** over the market — genuine, but a *fraction* of the book's claim.
The gap reflects a different period, real-world trading costs, and likely
look-ahead/survivorship bias in the original backtest.
</Callout>

- **Long stretches of underperformance.** Even in Greenblatt's own data the
  formula lagged the market in a large share of individual years and could trail
  for **2–3 consecutive years** — exactly the underperformance that causes
  investors to quit at the worst moment. The strategy's real risk is behavioral,
  not statistical.
- **It may not be "magic" so much as early, clean factor packaging.** Academic
  work (à la Fama-French and Novy-Marx) suggests much of the return is explained
  by the well-known **value** and **quality/profitability** factors. It's
  correlated with small-cap and value exposure, which suffer in growth-led markets
  (e.g., much of the 2010s).
- **Small-cap and liquidity bias.** Top-ranked names skew toward smaller companies
  where the cited returns are hardest to capture (illiquidity, trading costs,
  market impact). Raising the market-cap floor for safety *reduces* the returns.
- **Data quality and value traps.** Trailing EBIT can be inflated by one-time
  gains; a cheap-and-good screen can surface declining businesses or accounting
  distortions. The formula doesn't read the business qualitatively.
- **Crowding and capacity.** A famous, free formula gets arbitraged away in liquid
  large caps; "what's known doesn't pay as well."
- **Concentration and volatility.** Holding only 20–30 names is more volatile —
  with sharper drawdowns — than a broad index fund.

## Common beginner pitfalls

<Callout type="warning">
1. **Quitting during underperformance.** The single biggest failure mode. The
   strategy is *designed* to test your patience; bailing after one or two bad
   years guarantees you capture the downside without the recovery.
2. **Not holding enough stocks.** Buying 3–5 names instead of 20–30 reinvites the
   stock-specific risk the formula is built to diversify away.
3. **Cherry-picking from the screen.** The method is *mechanical.* Skipping the
   "scary-looking" names — often exactly where the returns come from — breaks it.
4. **Mis-defining the metrics.** Confusing earnings yield (EBIT/EV) with P/E, or
   return on capital with ROE. Greenblatt's definitions are intentionally
   different.
5. **Including financials, utilities, or ADRs** in the ranking — the EBIT/capital
   math misfires for them, producing garbage rankings.
6. **Getting the tax rule backwards.** Sell losers *before* one year, winners
   *after.*
7. **Expecting the book's ~30%.** Anchor to a modest single-digit *outperformance*
   (if any), not 30%.
8. **Confusing the two strategies.** *You Can Be a Stock Market Genius* (hands-on
   special situations) is **not** a mechanical screen like the Magic Formula.
   They're separate disciplines requiring different skills.
</Callout>

## Putting it to work

You don't need a Bloomberg terminal. A motivated beginner can:

1. **Use Greenblatt's own free screener** (magicformulainvesting.com) to generate
   a ranked list — enter a minimum market cap and choose 30 or 50 stocks — or
   replicate the two metrics in a spreadsheet from financial statements.
2. **Pick a market-cap floor** you're comfortable with (`>$1B` is a safe
   beginner choice for liquidity).
3. **Buy 20–30 of the top names**, building the basket in over ~12 months.
4. **Hold each ~a year, rebalance, harvest taxes**, and — the hard part —
   **keep going through the bad stretches.**

The deepest lesson isn't the arithmetic; it's the temperament. Greenblatt proved
that a *simple* value-and-quality rule, followed with *uncommon discipline*, can
beat the market. The formula is easy. Sitting still while it underperforms for two
years is the part almost nobody can do — and that difficulty is, paradoxically,
the source of the reward.

<Callout type="key">
Remember the trade Greenblatt is really offering: a method that is **simple to
understand, simple to run, and emotionally brutal to stick with.** Master the last
part and you've understood the master.
</Callout>
