## The one-sentence version

Mark Minervini built a career on a deceptively simple idea: **buy the strongest companies, in the strongest part of their trend, at the exact moment the market is offering you the least risk — and cut the trade fast if you're wrong.**

Most beginners do almost the opposite. They buy weak stocks because they're "cheap," they buy them while they're still falling, and they hold losers hoping to break even. Minervini's framework — **SEPA**, the **Trend Template**, and the **Volatility Contraction Pattern (VCP)** — is a disciplined inversion of those instincts. This lesson unpacks it precisely, with worked numbers you can follow, and is honest about where it can mislead you.

<Callout type="note">

This is an advanced lesson. It assumes you already know what a moving average is, what relative strength means, and how a stop-loss works. If those are fuzzy, review the [RSI](/indicators/rsi) and moving-average lessons first — and note that Minervini's "RS" is **not** the RSI oscillator (more on that below).

</Callout>

## Who he was

Mark Minervini is an American growth-stock trader, author, and educator who rose to prominence in the 1990s. He is **largely self-taught** — he did not come up through a Wall Street training program — and by his own account he lost money for years in the 1980s before "cracking the code" by studying earlier traders, especially **Jesse Livermore, Nicolas Darvas, and William O'Neil**. He was later profiled in Jack Schwager's *Stock Market Wizards* lineage of interviews.

<Callout type="warning">

**Be careful with the headline returns.** Minervini's most-quoted claim is a **~33,554% compounded return over roughly five years (1994–1999), about 220% a year with no losing year.** That figure is **self-reported and not independently audited** — it traces back to Minervini's own reporting (popularized via *Business Insider*) and has been examined skeptically (Skeptics Stack Exchange). I could not confirm it appears in Schwager's book. Treat it as a *remarkable claim*, not an established fact, and never as a return you should expect.

</Callout>

His more verifiable credentials come from the **U.S. Investing Championship**, a real-money trading contest with audited results:

- **1997:** won the stock division with an audited **+155% annual return** — reportedly while only about 50% invested on average (he beat the field while holding a lot of cash). This is his most consistently corroborated number.
- **2021:** won the $1M+ stock division with a **+334.8% return.** (Sources conflict on a second, earlier win sometimes cited as 2008/+218%; treat the 2021 figure as the better-supported "second win.")

His core books are ***Trade Like a Stock Market Wizard: How to Achieve Super Performance in Stocks in Any Market*** (McGraw-Hill, 2013) and ***Think & Trade Like a Champion*** (2017), plus *Mindset Secrets for Winning* (2019).

<Callout type="note">

Biographical details are fuzzy and partly promotional. Sources give his birth year as either 1965 (Queens, NY) or 1960 — hence "early 1960s" here. Claims like a specific net worth or "left school in 8th grade" are single-source; don't lean on them.

</Callout>

### His lineage

Minervini's method is a **synthesis**, and he's open about it:

- From **William O'Neil** (creator of CAN SLIM, founder of *Investor's Business Daily*): the focus on high-growth leaders with accelerating earnings, relative-strength ranking, and basing patterns before breakouts. CAN SLIM is the clearest intellectual ancestor of SEPA.
- From **Stan Weinstein** (*Secrets for Profiting in Bull and Bear Markets*, 1988): the four-**stage** model of a stock's life cycle.
- From **Jesse Livermore** and **Richard Wyckoff**: the "line of least resistance" (the direction price moves most easily once buyers or sellers dominate) and reading supply/demand through price and volume.

## SEPA: the four-filter funnel

**SEPA** stands for **Specific Entry Point Analysis** — Minervini's trademarked process. It is a funnel that narrows the entire market down to a handful of candidates. A stock should satisfy **all four** components, in this order:

1. **Trend** — the stock must be in a confirmed Stage 2 uptrend (the Trend Template, below). This is the gate and comes first.
2. **Fundamentals** — accelerating earnings and sales, expanding margins (specific numbers below).
3. **Catalyst** — a concrete reason institutions would want to buy: a new product, drug approval, sector theme, or a blowout earnings report (the "A" in SEPA).
4. **Timing** — the precise, low-risk entry, which is where the VCP comes in.

<Callout type="key">

SEPA's order matters. **Trend is the gate.** A cheap stock with great fundamentals but a broken chart (a Stage 4 downtrend) is disqualified before you even look at its earnings. Minervini buys strength, not value.

</Callout>

### The four stages (from Weinstein)

| Stage | What's happening | Minervini's action |
|-------|------------------|--------------------|
| 1 — Accumulation | Sideways base after a decline | Watch, don't buy |
| 2 — Advancing | Sustained uptrend, rising MAs | **Only stage he buys** |
| 3 — Topping | Choppy, churning at highs | Take profits, tighten stops |
| 4 — Declining | Downtrend, falling MAs | Avoid longs entirely |

## The Trend Template: defining Stage 2 numerically

Weinstein's stages are conceptual; Minervini makes Stage 2 **measurable** with a checklist (commonly stated as eight criteria). A stock should meet **all** of them — this filter alone eliminates the large majority of stocks:

```
Let P = current price
MA50, MA150, MA200 = 50-, 150-, 200-day simple moving averages

1.  P > MA150  AND  P > MA200
2.  MA150 > MA200
3.  MA200 is trending up for at least 1 month (ideally 4-5 months+)
4.  MA50 > MA150 > MA200          (proper "stacked" alignment)
5.  P > MA50
6.  P is at least 25-30% above its 52-week low
7.  P is within 25% of its 52-week high   (the closer, the better)
8.  Relative Strength (RS) ranking is high (at least 70, ideally 80-90+)
```

<Callout type="note">

Criteria 1–5 are really one stacked condition — **P > MA50 > MA150 > MA200, all rising.** Different sources split or merge them, which is why you'll see the "8 criteria" listed in slightly different orders and why the 52-week-low threshold is quoted as **25% in some sources and 30% in others.** The substance is identical; don't get hung up on the exact numbers.

</Callout>

### Then layer the fundamentals

Among the trend-qualified names, Minervini keeps only genuine **leaders**:

- **Quarterly EPS growth above ~20–25% year-over-year, and *accelerating*** (40%+ is excellent).
- **Revenue growth above ~15–20% YoY, accelerating.**
- **Expanding profit margins.**
- **Institutional ownership in a healthy band (roughly 30–70%)** — enough sponsorship to fuel a move, not so saturated that there are no new buyers left.
- A **fundamental catalyst** (the SEPA "A").

<Callout type="warning">

Don't confuse **RS ranking** with **RSI.** RS ranking is an IBD-style cross-sectional percentile of price performance (80 = stronger than 80% of the market). **RSI** is J. Welles Wilder's bounded 0–100 momentum oscillator. They share three letters and nothing else. Minervini means the former — and this is the single most common beginner mix-up with this method.

</Callout>

## The Volatility Contraction Pattern (VCP)

This is Minervini's signature contribution. A VCP is a specific kind of base — a consolidation **inside** a Stage 2 uptrend — that shows a series of progressively **tighter** pullbacks on **declining** volume. The logic: each pullback shakes out weaker holders while institutions quietly accumulate; when sellers are exhausted, the stock is coiled and ready to break out along the "line of least resistance."

Minervini labels each contraction a **"T"** (for "tight"): **T1, T2, T3…** A textbook VCP has **2 to 4 contractions** (3–4 is typical), and each one should be both **shallower** *and* **shorter** than the last. The collection of these contractions is the pattern's **footprint**.

### Illustrative example (numbers invented for teaching)

```
Stock building a VCP base after a Stage 2 advance:

  T1:  -25%  over ~6 weeks   (deep flush; weak holders sell)
  T2:  -12%  over ~3 weeks   (smaller; absorption continuing)
  T3:   -6%  over ~1.5 weeks (very tight; supply nearly gone)

  Each contraction's HIGH steps higher.
  Volume in T3 dries up well below the 50-day average.
  Pivot (buy point) = just above the high of T3, say $105.
```

### The two-dimensional rule

A real contraction shrinks in **both** magnitude (depth %) **and** duration (time) at the same time. Magnitude-only tightening can just be slow chop; duration-only can mean selling isn't truly being absorbed. **This two-dimensional test is the key.**

```
VALID:    T1 -18%/4wk -> T2 -12%/3wk -> T3 -6%/1.5wk   (both shrink)
INVALID:  T1 -12% -> T2 -15% -> T3 -8%                  (T2 deeper than T1)
```

If a later contraction is **deeper** than an earlier one — or depths just bounce around randomly ("high-level chop") — the pattern is **broken**: selling is increasing, not being absorbed. Reject it.

### Volume and the pivot

Volume should **fade** with each successive T — that fade *is* the evidence that supply is drying up. The entry — the **pivot point** — is a breakout above the high of the final, tightest contraction, and it must come on **expanding volume**, commonly cited as **at least 1.4–1.5× the 50-day average.** A breakout that limps out only 10–20% above average lacks institutional fuel and tends to fail.

## A worked trade

```
Setup (illustrative):
  Pivot / buy point    = $105.00
  Breakout volume      = ~3x the 50-day average  (strong confirmation)
  Stop just below final T low, ~7% down = $97.65
  Risk per share       = $105.00 - $97.65 = $7.35

Position sizing with a fixed $600 risk unit (account $48,000, risk 1.25%):
  Shares = $600 / $7.35  ~= 81 shares
  Position size ~= 81 x $105 = $8,505   (about 18% of the account)
  If stopped at $97.65: loss = 81 x $7.35 ~= $600 = 1.25% of account.  Controlled.

The tighter the VCP, the closer the logical stop, the MORE shares
you can hold for the SAME dollar risk -> capital efficiency.
```

The position-sizing rule is always the same: **`Shares = (Account × Risk%) ÷ (Entry − Stop)`.** The dollar you put at risk is fixed; a wider stop automatically forces a *smaller* position. This is exactly why Minervini hunts tight VCPs — a 6% stop lets you take a far larger position for the same risk budget than a 12% stop does.

### Why a low win rate still makes money

Here is the lesson beginners most need to internalize. Suppose you take **5 trades**, each sized to risk **$600 (≈1.25%)**. Four are losers, stopped out at roughly −1R each. One is a winner that clears its pivot and runs +40% before you sell into strength:

| Trade | Outcome | P/L |
|-------|---------|-----|
| 1 | stopped out | −$600 |
| 2 | stopped out | −$600 |
| 3 | stopped out | −$600 |
| 4 | stopped out | −$600 |
| 5 | +40% on an $8,505 position | +$3,402 |
| **Net** | **20% win rate** | **+$1,002** |

You were **wrong 80% of the time and still made money.** That is the entire engine: **small fixed losses + occasional large wins = positive expectancy.** Gains and losses are best tracked in **R**, where **1R = your initial dollar risk** (the $600). A full stop-out is −1R; the winner above is about +5.7R.

<Callout type="key">

Minervini's edge is **asymmetry**, not prediction accuracy. The VCP is just the tool that lets him place a tight, logical stop close to entry; the stop discipline is what makes the asymmetry survivable. A modest win rate is fine — *as long as the winners are allowed to dwarf the losers.*

</Callout>

## Risk control — the part beginners skip

Minervini is more famous among professionals for risk management than for chart reading.

- **A hard stop ~7–8% below entry, treated as non-negotiable.** Because the VCP base is tight, this sits naturally just below the final contraction's low. His own back-of-envelope test: a disciplined 10% stop would have left an account *up 72% instead of down* over a rough stretch.
- **Risk only ~1–2% of total equity per trade** (his classic guidance is about 1.25%). One bad trade should be a scratch, never a wound.
- **The "free roll":** once a position shows a solid gain (Minervini often cites about **+20%**), sell roughly half and move the stop on the rest toward breakeven — so the remaining shares can no longer hurt you.
- **Sell into strength:** take profits aggressively when a stock goes vertical, and watch short MAs (10-day / 21-day) on heavy volume as exit signals. Let the rest run.
- **Market environment first.** He tracks **distribution days** (high-volume down days on the indexes) to judge whether the overall market is healthy. In a correcting market even perfect VCPs fail at high rates, so he **goes mostly to cash.** His 1997 championship win came partly from *being in cash at the right times.*

## Honest criticisms

- **Performance claims are largely self-reported and unaudited.** The ~33,554% / ~220%-per-year figure comes from Minervini himself and has been openly questioned. The 1997 USIC +155% is the one figure most consistently described as audited. A common skeptic point: huge *percentage* returns are far easier on a small account than at scale, so they may not be reproducible with meaningful capital.
- **It performs poorly in choppy and bear markets.** This is a momentum/trend method. In sideways, whipsaw, or declining markets, breakouts fail repeatedly ("fuel-less breakouts"), producing a string of small losses. The system's own answer — *go to cash and stop trading* — means long idle stretches many beginners can't psychologically tolerate. One independent study of ~1,000 breakouts across 2015–2025 found the edge is real but **regime-dependent**, not a guarantee.
- **Long-only.** No shorting; it offers nothing in sustained downturns except sitting out.
- **VCP identification is subjective.** Counting contractions, judging "tightness," and calling volume "dried up" are interpretive. Two skilled traders disagree on the same chart, which makes the method hard to backtest cleanly and easy to rationalize a bad setup. Mechanical scanners capture only a crude shadow of the discretionary read.
- **Survivorship/hindsight bias in teaching.** Examples are usually shown on charts that *did* break out and run; the real failure rate of breakouts is high.
- **Whipsaw cost of tight stops.** The 7–8% stop that limits losses also shakes you out of stocks that later work, forcing re-entries that erode returns in noisy conditions.
- **Commercial conflict of interest.** Much of the most enthusiastic material comes from Minervini's own paid platform and workshops, so independent verification matters.

## Common pitfalls and misconceptions

<Callout type="warning">

- **Chasing extended breakouts.** If the stock gaps more than ~5% above the pivot, the stop distance balloons and the risk/reward is gone. Don't chase.
- **Buying before the pivot.** Entering *during* a contraction (hoping to get in cheaper) ruins the tight risk and the whole asymmetry.
- **Ignoring the market environment.** Trading great setups in a Stage 4 / downtrending market is the #1 way beginners get chopped to pieces. Market direction outranks setup quality.
- **Skipping or widening the stop.** The strategy *only* works if every loss stays small (5–8%). Widening or canceling a stop is the cardinal sin.
- **Forcing a VCP that isn't there.** Calling random chop a "VCP," or accepting a pattern where a later contraction is deeper than an earlier one.
- **Buying breakouts on weak volume.** No volume surge = no institutional demand = high failure rate.
- **Trading laggards / "cheap" stocks** instead of true high-RS leaders. SEPA buys strength, not bargains.
- **Confusing RS ranking with RSI** (flagged above).
- **Selling winners too early.** Cutting a winner at the first wobble caps the big gains needed to offset the losers.
- **Believing the headline returns are typical** and abandoning the system after a normal losing streak.

</Callout>

## How to apply it today

1. **Screen for the Trend Template** on any modern platform (TradingView, TC2000, TrendSpider, and Finviz all support the MA / 52-week / RS filters).
2. **Layer fundamentals:** keep only names with accelerating EPS/sales growth, healthy institutional ownership, and a clear catalyst.
3. **Wait for a VCP base** to form and tighten. Mark each T. Confirm both depth *and* duration shrink and that volume fades into the final contraction.
4. **Buy the pivot on a volume surge** (~1.5×+ the 50-day average), placing your stop just below the final contraction's low — and inside the 7–8% rule.
5. **Size with the formula:** `Shares = (Account × Risk%) ÷ (Entry − Stop)`, risking only ~1–2% of equity.
6. **Manage:** take partial profits and move the stop to breakeven after a strong gain (the free-roll, ~+20%), trail the rest with short MAs, and respect the market's overall stage — go to cash when the indexes show distribution.
7. **Practice in a journal first.** Mark up 50 historical charts of past leaders, label the T's, and note where the real pivot, stop, and exits would have been — before risking a dollar.
