## Why this pair matters to you

Almost every piece of modern investing advice you've ever heard — *"just buy a
low-cost index fund," "you can't time the market," "tilt toward value and small
caps for higher long-run returns"* — traces back to the work of two economists.
Together, **Eugene Fama** and **Kenneth French** answered two questions that sit
at the very center of how to invest:

1. **Can you beat the market by being clever?** (Mostly no.)
2. **If not, how do you still earn higher returns?** (By deliberately bearing
   specific, well-understood risks — and waiting.)

These two answers look like they contradict each other. A huge part of this
lesson is showing you why they fit together perfectly. Get that, and you've
absorbed the intellectual foundation of index investing and "smart beta" alike.

<Callout type="key">
We teach Fama and French as **one unit** on purpose. Their most famous work — the
factor models — is jointly authored, and their reputations are intertwined.
Fama supplied the theory of efficient markets; together they supplied the
empirical machinery that explains where returns actually come from.
</Callout>

## Who they were

**Eugene F. Fama** — often called *"the father of modern finance"* and *"the
father of the efficient-market hypothesis."*

- Born **February 14, 1939, in Boston, Massachusetts** (his grandparents
  emigrated from Italy). A common mix-up: Chicago is where he built his entire
  career, not where he was born.
- Undergrad at Tufts (Romance Languages, *magna cum laude*, 1960); MBA and PhD
  from the **University of Chicago Booth School of Business** (advised by Nobel
  laureate Merton Miller and Harry Roberts). His doctoral thesis showed that
  short-term stock prices are very nearly unpredictable — a **"random walk."**
- Spent his **whole career at the University of Chicago.**
- Won the **Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences in 2013** (shared with
  Robert Shiller and Lars Peter Hansen) for the empirical analysis of asset
  prices.

**Kenneth R. French**

- Born **March 10, 1954, in Franklin, New Hampshire.** (Dartmouth is where he
  *teaches* today — not his birthplace.)
- B.S. in mechanical engineering (Lehigh, 1975); MBA, M.S., and **PhD in finance
  from the University of Rochester (1983).**
- Now the **Roth Family Distinguished Professor of Finance at the Tuck School of
  Business, Dartmouth College** (he previously taught at MIT, Yale, and Chicago
  Booth). Past president of the American Finance Association.
- He maintains the free, public **"Kenneth R. French Data Library"** — the
  canonical place where academics and professionals download the actual factor
  return series. We'll come back to it and *use* it; it's the closest thing
  investing has to a public utility.

Both have long been affiliated with **Dimensional Fund Advisors (DFA)**, the fund
company built explicitly to harvest the factors they discovered.

## The two big ideas, in plain language

### Idea A — The Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH), 1970

The core claim is simple: **a stock's price already reflects all the information
available to investors.** The instant news appears, thousands of traders pounce
and the price adjusts almost immediately. So by the time *you* hear the
"information" and act on it, the price already moved.

The practical punchline for you as a beginner:

> You cannot reliably beat the market by picking stocks or timing trades. On a
> risk-adjusted basis, **after costs**, the average active investor will **not**
> consistently outperform a cheap index fund.

This is the intellectual bedrock of index investing.

### Idea B — The Fama-French factor models, 1992–2015

If markets are efficient, here's a puzzle: why have some *groups* of stocks
reliably earned higher long-run returns than others? Fama and French's answer is
the heart of the factor revolution:

> Higher return is **compensation for bearing certain risks (called factors)** —
> not evidence that someone is "beating" the market.

Beyond the overall market, they found two extra characteristics that historically
predicted returns: being a **small company (the size factor)** and being a
**cheap, "value" stock (the value factor)**. Later they added two more,
**profitability** and **investment.**

<Callout type="note">
**The bridge between A and B.** EMH says *don't try to outsmart the market.*
Factor models say *you can still expect higher returns — but only by deliberately
and patiently taking on more of these specific risks, not by stock-picking
genius.* They are complementary, not contradictory: the factors are the **risks
that earn return inside an efficient market.**
</Callout>

## EMH — the full method

EMH isn't one claim; it comes in **three forms**, each defined by *what
information is already baked into the price.* The more information that's
priced in, the harder it is to win. Read it as a checklist — pick the form, and
it tells you which style of analysis it says can't reliably beat the market:

| Form | What's already in the price | What it says can't reliably win |
|------|------------------------------|--------------------------------|
| **Weak** | All past price & volume data | Technical analysis (chart-reading) |
| **Semi-strong** | All *public* information (news, filings, earnings) | Fundamental analysis on public data |
| **Strong** | Even *private/insider* information | Anyone at all — even insiders |

The strong form is generally **rejected** (insiders clearly *can* profit). The
real debate lives around the semi-strong form.

  chart={`flowchart LR

How Fama and his co-authors actually *built* the case for EMH, step by step — so
you see it's evidence, not just an assertion:

1. **Show prices are nearly a random walk** (Fama's 1965 thesis): tomorrow's move
   is mostly unrelated to today's, so past prices give little edge → supports the
   **weak** form.
2. **Run an "event study"** (Fama, Fisher, Jensen & Roll, 1969 — the first of its
   kind): track exactly how fast prices adjust around a known public event. They
   adjust *fast*, often before you could trade → supports the **semi-strong**
   form.
3. **Test the strong form** by checking whether insiders earn abnormal returns.
   They do → the strong form is **rejected.**

### The joint-hypothesis problem (teach this honestly)

Here is the deepest and most slippery point about EMH, and it matters because
critics use it constantly. **You can never test market efficiency by itself.**

To say a return is *"abnormal"* (proof of inefficiency), you first need a model of
what the *normal*, fair return should have been. So every test of efficiency is
secretly testing **two things at once**: market efficiency AND your pricing
model. When you find an apparent anomaly, you genuinely can't tell whether:

- the market was inefficient, **or**
- your model of "fair return" was simply wrong.

## The factor models — the full method

### The three-factor formula (1993)

This is the model that overturned the old **Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM)**,
which had used only *one* factor (the market). Written for a portfolio or fund:

```
Expected return − Risk-free rate
   =  β · (Market − Risk-free)        ← the market factor
   +  bₛ · SMB                        ← the size factor
   +  bᵥ · HML                        ← the value factor
   ( + α )                            ← leftover "alpha"
```

Decoding the three factors:

- **Market (Rm − Rf):** the excess return of the *whole* stock market over the
  risk-free rate (e.g. Treasury bills). The only factor CAPM ever had.
- **SMB ("Small Minus Big"):** the historical extra return of small-cap stocks
  over large-cap stocks → the **size premium.**
- **HML ("High Minus Low"):** the extra return of high book-to-market
  (cheap "value") stocks over low book-to-market (expensive "growth") stocks →
  the **value premium.**

The coefficients β, bₛ, bᵥ are the **loadings** — how strongly your portfolio is
tilted toward each factor, estimated by regression. They can be positive *or*
negative (a growth fund has a *negative* HML loading). In-sample, the three-factor
model explained roughly **90%+** of the return variation of diversified
portfolios, versus about **70%** for the one-factor CAPM.

<Callout type="warning">
**Memorize the direction of HML.** Value = **HIGH** book-to-market = *cheap*
stocks (lots of accounting book value per dollar of price). Growth = low
book-to-market = *expensive*. Beginners constantly flip this sign and conclude
the opposite of what the data says.
</Callout>

<Callout type="note">
**This "value" is the same value you met in the fundamental school.** When Fama
and French call high book-to-market stocks "value," they mean exactly the
cheap-relative-to-fundamentals companies that **Benjamin Graham** and Warren
Buffett hunt for by hand. The big difference is *method*: a Graham-style
fundamental investor reads each company's statements and buys the bargains one by
one; Fama and French simply *sort every stock by one cheapness ratio and buy the
whole cheap basket.* Same underlying bet — cheap beats expensive over the long
run — captured mechanically instead of by judgment. If you've read the
[Benjamin Graham lesson](/gurus/benjamin-graham), think of HML as Graham's
margin-of-safety instinct turned into a downloadable spreadsheet column.
</Callout>

### The five-factor formula (2015)

Fama and French later added two more factors, both motivated by the idea that a
firm's fundamentals predict returns:

```
Expected return − Rf
   =  β·(Market − Rf) + bₛ·SMB + bᵥ·HML + b_r·RMW + b_c·CMA   ( + α )
```

- **RMW ("Robust Minus Weak"):** firms with strong (robust) operating
  profitability minus firms with weak profitability → the **profitability
  factor.**
- **CMA ("Conservative Minus Aggressive"):** firms that invest conservatively
  (grow assets slowly) minus those that invest aggressively → the **investment
  factor.**

Notably, in US data **1963–2013 the value factor (HML) became largely
redundant** — its return was well explained by the other four, especially CMA
(≈0.7 correlation with HML). And **momentum was deliberately left out** (more on
that in the criticisms).

Here's roughly how the five factors have historically *ranked* as average annual
premiums — small, positive, and won't show up every year.

return.
</Callout>

### How the factors are actually built (the "2×3 sort")

This is the concrete recipe Fama and French use — and it's worth understanding so
you know these aren't mystery numbers:

1. Take all stocks on the **NYSE, AMEX, and NASDAQ.**
2. **Split by size** into "Small" vs. "Big," using the **median NYSE market cap**
   as the dividing line.
3. **Split by value** into Low (bottom 30%), Medium (middle 40%), High (top 30%)
   book-to-market, using NYSE breakpoints. Drop firms with negative book value.
4. Cross those splits into **six value-weighted portfolios** (S/L, S/M, S/H, B/L,
   B/M, B/H).
5. **SMB** = average return of the 3 small portfolios − average return of the 3
   big portfolios. **HML** = average of the 2 high-B/M portfolios − the 2 low-B/M
   portfolios. (Portfolios are rebuilt once a year.)
6. To analyze *your* fund, run a **linear regression** of its excess returns on
   these factor series. The resulting coefficients (the loadings) tell you how
   strongly the fund is *tilted* toward each factor. Whatever return is left over
   is **alpha (α)** — the part NOT explained by factors.

<Callout type="note">
See it now? Building a factor is just **sorting stocks into boxes and subtracting
one box's return from another's.** There's no forecasting, no stock-picking

exactly why the premiums count as *risk compensation* rather than skill.
</Callout>

## A worked example — is this manager skilled, or just tilted?

This is the single most useful thing the model does for an everyday investor:
**it separates genuine skill (alpha) from a mere factor tilt.**

Suppose a small-cap value fund, over one period, faces these conditions:

- Risk-free rate **Rf = 2%**
- Market return **Rm = 8%** → market excess = **6%**
- Size premium that period **SMB = 3%**; value premium **HML = 4%**

A regression of the fund's past returns gives these tilts (loadings):

- Market β = **1.1**
- Size bₛ = **0.7**  (positive → it leans toward small caps)
- Value bᵥ = **0.5**  (positive → it leans toward value)

Plug into the three-factor formula:

```
Expected return = Rf + β·(Rm−Rf) + bₛ·SMB + bᵥ·HML
                = 2% + (1.1 × 6%) + (0.7 × 3%) + (0.5 × 4%)
                = 2%  + 6.6%       + 2.1%       + 2.0%
                = 12.7%
```

Now compare what each model would have *expected* against what the fund actually
delivered (say it returned **13.0%**):

**The interpretation that changes how you judge funds:** a plain CAPM would have
predicted only `2% + 6.6% = 8.6%`. The extra **4.1%** above that comes entirely
from the fund's deliberate small-cap and value tilts — exposures you could have
bought cheaply yourself in an index. Since the fund actually returned 13.0%, its
**alpha is only about +0.3%.** Almost all its "outperformance" was factor
exposure, not manager genius. *(Figures illustrative; the real factor series are
downloadable from the French Data Library — see the next section.)*

<Callout type="key">
This is why factor models matter even if you never run a regression: they let you
ask of *any* fund or "guru," **"Is this skill, or is it just a tilt I could buy
for 0.05% a year?"** Most of the time, it's the tilt.
</Callout>

## Do it yourself — the Kenneth R. French Data Library

Here is the genuinely beginner-followable part: the actual factor returns Fama and
French use are **free to download**, and you can run the analysis above in a
spreadsheet. This is what separates "I read about factors" from "I can check a
fund myself."

1. **Go to the Data Library** (Tuck/Dartmouth) and pick a dataset — e.g.
   *"Fama/French 3 Factors"* or *"Fama/French 5 Factors (2×3)."* Choose your
   frequency (monthly is the standard starting point).
2. **Download and unzip** the file. The columns are **Mkt-RF, SMB, HML** (plus
   **RMW, CMA**, and **Mom** in the larger sets) and a separate **RF** column for
   the risk-free rate.
3. **Compute your portfolio's *excess* return** for each period:
   `your return − RF`. (You subtract RF because the factors are measured as excess
   returns too — Mkt-RF is already net of the risk-free rate.)
4. **Regress** your excess returns on the factor columns. The slopes are your
   loadings (β, bₛ, bᵥ, …); the intercept is your **alpha.** A near-zero alpha
   means everything you earned was explained by known risk exposures.

<Callout type="warning">
**The three Data-Library gotchas that wreck a beginner's regression:**

1. **Values are in *percent per period.*** A `2.0` means **2%**, i.e. 0.02 — not
   2.0 in decimal. Mixing the two destroys your numbers.
2. **The factors are *excess* returns.** Mkt-RF already has the risk-free rate
   removed, so you must compare it to *your* excess return (`return − RF`), not
   your raw return.
3. **Missing data is coded as `-99.99` (or `-999`).** These are placeholders, not
   real returns. Feeding them in raw will badly skew the regression — filter them
   out first.
</Callout>

## The key books and papers

- **1965** — *"The Behavior of Stock Market Prices,"* Journal of Business
  (Fama's dissertation; the random-walk evidence).
- **1969** — *"The Adjustment of Stock Prices to New Information"* (Fama, Fisher,
  Jensen, Roll), International Economic Review — the first major **event study.**
- **1970** — **"Efficient Capital Markets: A Review of Theory and Empirical
  Work,"** Journal of Finance — defines the weak/semi-strong/strong forms. The
  EMH cornerstone.
- **1992** — Fama & French, **"The Cross-Section of Expected Stock Returns,"**
  Journal of Finance — the empirical case that size and value beat CAPM beta.
- **1993** — Fama & French, **"Common Risk Factors in the Returns on Stocks and
  Bonds,"** Journal of Financial Economics — the **three-factor model.**
- **2015** — Fama & French, **"A Five-Factor Asset Pricing Model,"** Journal of
  Financial Economics — adds RMW and CMA.
- Books: *Foundations of Finance* (Fama, 1976); *The Fama Portfolio: Selected
  Papers* (2017).

## Honest criticisms and limitations

No serious lesson on Fama and French hides the pushback — there's a lot of it,
and some of it comes from people who won Nobels of their own.

- **The joint-hypothesis problem makes EMH nearly unfalsifiable.** Because you can
  always blame "the wrong model," critics like Philip Pilkington have called EMH
  *"a tautology masquerading as a theory."*
- **Behavioral finance.** Kahneman, Tversky, and Richard Thaler showed investors
  are systematically biased (overconfidence, overreaction, loss aversion). After
  2008, critics like Jeremy Grantham and Paul Volcker blamed "faith in efficient
  markets" for missing the bubble. Fama maintained EMH *"held up well."*
- **Skeptical great investors.** Warren Buffett pointed to a cluster of
  consistently successful value investors as evidence skill is real, not luck;
  defenders dismiss them as lucky outliers — which critics say turns EMH into an
  un-losable argument.
- **HML became redundant.** In US data (1963–2013), the value factor (HML) turned
  out to be fully *explained by the other four factors* — the investment factor
  (CMA) alone has roughly 0.7 correlation with it. The five-factor model also
  still **fails the formal Gibbons-Ross-Shanken (1989) test**, mainly because of
  small firms that invest heavily despite weak profitability.
- **No momentum factor.** A well-documented momentum premium is deliberately left
  out; Cliff Asness argued it belongs, and the Carhart (1997) four-factor model
  adds it.
- **Data-mining / "factor zoo."** Factors found in US 1963–2013 data may weaken,
  vanish, or reverse in other periods and regions; hundreds of factors have since
  been proposed, most likely spurious. Griffin (2002) found local factors beat
  global ones — the premiums are country-specific.
- **Micro vs. macro efficiency (Samuelson's quip).** Individual stocks may be
  priced fairly while the *whole market* still swings on sentiment.

<Callout type="note">
None of this means the models are useless — they remain the standard benchmark in
academic finance and the backbone of trillions in "smart-beta" assets. It means
you should treat factors as *useful, imperfect descriptions of risk*, not as laws
of physics.
</Callout>

## Common beginner pitfalls

<Callout type="warning">
1. **Thinking "efficient" means "always correctly priced."** EMH says prices
   reflect *available information* and are hard to *beat after costs* — not that
   they're always right in hindsight. Fama himself doubts anyone can reliably spot
   bubbles in real time.
2. **Confusing a size/value tilt with skill.** Factor returns are *risk premiums
   earned by exposure over long periods* — not alpha. A manager who beat the
   market with a value tilt captured a factor; they did not prove skill.
3. **Expecting the premiums every year.** Size and value can underperform for a
   *decade.* They are long-run, risk-based, and demand patience.
4. **Flipping the factor letters.** SMB = size (small−big), HML = value
   (high−low B/M, i.e. cheap), RMW = profitability, CMA = investment.
5. **Mishandling the Data Library files.** Values are *percent*, are *excess*
   returns, and use `-99.99` for missing data — feeding them in raw wrecks a
   regression.
6. **Forgetting the joint-hypothesis problem.** "I found an anomaly, so the market
   is inefficient" ignores that your risk model might simply be wrong.
7. **Chasing the newest factor.** More factors ≠ better; most don't survive
   out-of-sample. And **ignoring costs and taxes**, which erode most apparent
   edges.
8. **Biography slip-ups.** Fama was born in **Boston** (career in Chicago);
   French was born in **New Hampshire** (teaches at **Dartmouth**).
</Callout>

## Putting it to work

You don't need a PhD or a regression package to act on this. Concretely:

1. **Default to low-cost, broad index funds.** EMH's honest conclusion is that
   the average active bet is a loser after fees. Start from the index.
2. **Decide, deliberately, whether you want factor tilts.** If you can stomach
   long stretches of underperformance, you might tilt a *portion* of your
   portfolio toward small-cap and value (and now profitability/investment) funds
   to chase the premiums — knowing they're risk, not free money.
3. **Hold the tilts for a decade-plus.** The premiums are long-run averages.
   Bailing after three bad years is the surest way to capture all the risk and
   none of the reward.
4. **Audit any fund or guru with the factor lens.** Before paying for
   "outperformance," ask whether a cheap value/small-cap index would have
   delivered the same thing. Usually it would.
5. **Download the real data and check it yourself.** The French Data Library gives
   you the actual factor series for free — run the regression from the worked
   example in a spreadsheet, exactly the way professionals do.

<Callout type="key">
The one-line takeaway: **Don't try to be smarter than the market. Be more patient
than it, and be deliberate about which risks you take on.**
</Callout>
