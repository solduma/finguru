## Why start here

Almost every serious value investor alive traces their thinking back to one man.
Warren Buffett called his book *"by far the best book about investing ever
written."* Before Benjamin Graham, buying stocks was mostly tip-chasing and
speculation. Graham turned it into something you could reason about: a stock is
**a fractional ownership share of a real business**, and a business has a
**value** you can estimate — separate from whatever price the market happens to
shout at you today.

Learn Graham and you learn the grammar of value investing: *intrinsic value*,
*Mr. Market*, and the *margin of safety*. Everything else in the fundamental
school is a refinement of these three ideas. By the end of this lesson you
should be able to actually screen a stock the way Graham taught — not just
admire the philosophy.

## Who he was

**Benjamin Graham** (born Benjamin Grossbaum, May 9, 1894, in London; died
September 21, 1976, in Aix-en-Provence, France) emigrated as a child to New York
City, where the family anglicized its name. He entered **Columbia University**
young and graduated as **salutatorian** at about 20, in roughly three and a half
years — reportedly offered teaching posts in three departments (math, English,
and philosophy). He chose Wall Street instead.

- In the 1920s he made his name partly through early **shareholder activism** —
  the so-called **"Northern Pipeline Affair,"** where he pressured a Rockefeller
  pipeline company to return excess cash to shareholders.
- He co-founded the **Graham–Newman Corporation** with Jerome Newman, which ran
  from roughly **1936 to 1956** and closed when Graham retired.
- He taught the value-investing course at **Columbia Business School** (from
  1928, later with David Dodd) and afterward at **UCLA's Anderson School.**

<Callout type="key">
Graham was a teacher first. His two great books — *Security Analysis* (1934) and
*The Intelligent Investor* (1949) — were written to be **followed**, not
admired. He even gave investors a precise definition of what they were doing:
*"An investment operation is one which, upon thorough analysis, promises safety
of principal and an adequate return. Operations not meeting these requirements
are speculative."* Everything in this lesson serves those two words — **safety**
and **adequate return.**
</Callout>

### The track record (and an honest hedge)

Graham–Newman is often cited as earning roughly **20% annualized from 1936 to
1956**, versus about **12% for the broad market.** Treat that figure as
*approximate* — it is widely repeated but hard to pin to audited primary
records. The most famous single position was **GEICO**: the firm bought a 50%
stake in 1948 for **$712,500**; regulators later forced that stake to be
distributed to fund shareholders, and it grew into the partnership's largest
profit by far — ironically, by **breaking** Graham's own diversification rules.
Even the father of the method made his biggest gain on the exception to it.

His students became the **"Superinvestors of Graham-and-Doddsville"** — most
famously **Warren Buffett** (who studied under Graham at Columbia and worked at
Graham–Newman from 1954–56), plus Walter Schloss, William Ruane, Irving Kahn,
and Charles Brandes.

## The core idea in plain language

Graham's whole philosophy rests on separating two things that beginners
constantly blur together: **price** and **value.**

- **Price** is what Mr. Market quotes you today. It swings wildly with mood,
  headlines, and crowds.
- **Value** (intrinsic value) is what the business is actually worth — rooted in
  its assets and its earning power. It moves far more slowly and sanely.

Your one job: **estimate what the business is worth, then buy only when the
price is meaningfully below that estimate.** The gap between price and value is
your **margin of safety** — the cushion that protects you when your estimate is
too rosy or bad luck strikes.

> **Example:** In 1973–74 the U.S. market roughly halved, and household-name
> companies sold for single-digit P/E ratios — well below the value of their
> earnings and assets. Their *businesses* hadn't lost half their worth in two
> years; their *prices* had. That gap between a sane estimate of value and a
> panicked price is exactly the bargain Graham hunted, and it's why his students
> made their largest buys precisely when others were selling.

### Meet Mr. Market

Graham's most beloved teaching device is **Mr. Market** — imagine you co-own a
business with a moody partner. Every single day he knocks on your door and
quotes a price: he'll buy your half or sell you his at that number. Some days
he's euphoric and quotes a silly-high price; some days he's panicked and quotes
a silly-low one.

The key insight: **you are never obligated to trade.** Mr. Market is there to
*serve* you, not instruct you. You exploit his mood swings — buy when he's
depressed and cheap, sell to him (or ignore him) when he's manic — rather than
catch his emotions.

> **Example:** Picture a solid, profitable company you reckon is worth about $50
> a share. One quarter it misses earnings slightly and Mr. Market, in a foul
> mood, offers you shares at $32. Nothing real has changed — the factories,
> brands, and customers are all still there — so you happily buy from his panic.
> A year later, on a wave of hype, he bangs on your door offering $80 for the
> same shares. You sell into his euphoria. You didn't predict his moods; you
> simply let his emotions set prices and acted only when those prices were
> absurd relative to value.

<Callout type="note">
**On the famous "voting machine / weighing machine" line.** You'll often hear:
*"In the short run the market is a voting machine, but in the long run it is a
weighing machine."* The **idea is genuinely Graham's** — short-term prices
reflect popularity, long-term prices reflect real value — but the exact catchy
sentence is **disputed as a verbatim quote** (it was popularized through
Buffett). Teach the idea; don't put the precise wording in Graham's mouth.
</Callout>

## The full method — concrete, beginner-followable steps

Graham gave **two tracks**, depending on how much work you want to do.
**Beginners should use the Defensive track.** The Enterprising "net-net" track
is more powerful but harder to find in modern markets and demands real research.

### Track A — The Defensive Investor: seven stock-selection rules

For people who want safety and minimal ongoing effort. (Graham also suggested
splitting your portfolio between high-grade bonds and stocks, never holding less
than 25% or more than 75% in either.) For *which stocks to own*, the rules come
from *The Intelligent Investor* (Chapter 14). A stock should pass roughly **all
seven** tests. The first four are *quality* filters (is this a sound, durable
business?); the last three are *price* filters (am I paying a sane price?).

1. **Adequate size.** Avoid tiny companies — they're fragile. Graham used a
   minimum sales figure; today this is usually modernized to a **minimum market
   capitalization** (market cap = share price × shares outstanding).
2. **Strong financial condition.** Two checks:
   - **Current ratio ≥ 2** — current assets are at least twice current
     liabilities (the company can easily cover its near-term bills).
   - **Long-term debt ≤ working capital** (working capital = current assets −
     current liabilities). The firm isn't drowning in long-term debt.
3. **Earnings stability.** **Positive earnings in each of the past 10 years** —
   no loss years.
4. **Dividend record.** **Uninterrupted dividends for at least 20 years** — a
   sign of durable, shareholder-friendly cash generation.
5. **Earnings growth.** At least a **one-third (33%) rise in per-share earnings
   over 10 years**, measured using **3-year averages** at the start and end to
   smooth out noise.
6. **Moderate P/E.** Price `≤ 15×` average earnings of the past 3 years. (P/E,
   the price-to-earnings ratio, is price per share ÷ earnings per share.)
7. **Moderate price-to-assets.** Price `≤ 1.5×` book value (book value =
   net assets on the balance sheet). Rules 6 and 7 may be combined into a single
   test: **(P/E) × (P/B) ≤ 22.5.**

<Callout type="note">
Rules 6, 7, and the **22.5** product are verified (they're the basis of the
"Graham number" below). The exact thresholds in rules 1, 3, and 4 are the
standard widely-cited values from Chapter 14 — solid, but adjust the *size*
number for inflation and don't treat the *20-year dividend* bar as sacred for
modern firms (many great companies reinvest instead of paying dividends).
</Callout>

### Track B — The Enterprising "net-net" strategy

For people willing to do serious research for potentially higher returns. The
Enterprising investor hunts bargains the market has thrown away: stocks below
book value, out-of-favor low-P/E names, special situations, and — Graham's
deepest-value tool — **net-nets.** This one looks past earnings entirely and
asks: *what would be left for shareholders if we liquidated the company today?*

- Compute **Net Current Asset Value (NCAV) = current assets − total
  liabilities.** This deliberately ignores *all* fixed assets (factories,
  equipment, goodwill, intangibles) — a brutally conservative, near-liquidation
  figure.
- **NCAV per share = NCAV ÷ shares outstanding.**
- **Buy only when price ≤ two-thirds (≈66.7%) of NCAV per share.** That ~33%
  discount *is* the margin of safety: you pay less than the company's liquid
  assets net of *every* debt and get the operating business thrown in for free.
- Hold a **diversified basket** of such names (Graham owned dozens) and sell as
  they recover toward value. Any single cheap-but-troubled company can fail; the
  edge is statistical, across the group. Buffett later nicknamed these
  **"cigar butts"** — one last free puff. Academic studies of the strategy have
  shown large historical outperformance, though treat those figures as
  illustrative of a past edge, not a promise.
- A stricter cousin, **Net-Net Working Capital (NNWC)**, haircuts receivables
  and inventory further before subtracting liabilities — even more conservative.

### Two valuation shortcuts Graham published

**The Graham number** — a quick maximum "fair" price for a *defensive* stock:

```
Graham number = √( 22.5 × EPS × Book Value Per Share )
```

The 22.5 is just rule 6 × rule 7 (15 × 1.5). It's the geometric mean of the
earnings-justified and asset-justified prices — a single ceiling that bakes in
both price filters at once. (Both EPS and book value must be *positive*, or the
formula is meaningless.)

**The growth formula** — original form `V = EPS × (8.5 + 2g)`, where 8.5 is the
base P/E for a no-growth firm and *g* is the expected 7–10-year growth rate. The
1974 revision adjusts for interest rates: `V = EPS × (8.5 + 2g) × 4.4 / Y`,
where 4.4 was the 1962 average AAA corporate-bond yield and *Y* is the current
AAA yield.

<Callout type="warning">
Graham explicitly called the growth formula **"illustrative only,"** built to
*mimic* the popular growth formulas of his era — and warned the results should
not be assumed to have "any high degree of reliability." It's a way to
*reverse-engineer* what growth rate the market is already pricing in — **not** a
precise intrinsic-value machine. Beginners abuse it constantly: a tiny change in
*g* swings the "value" wildly. Use it to ask *"what growth would justify today's
price?"*, not to compute a magic number to bet on.
</Callout>

### The general process for a beginner

1. **Pick your track** — Defensive for most people.
2. **Screen** for the quantitative rules above (free stock screeners let you
   filter on P/E, P/B, current ratio, and earnings history).
3. **Estimate intrinsic value conservatively** — Graham number, net asset value,
   or earning power. Lowball it on purpose.
4. **Demand a margin of safety** — buy *well below* value, not *at* value.
5. **Diversify** — Graham favored breadth, not concentration.
6. **Hold for years** — let the slow "weighing machine" work; ignore Mr.
   Market's daily noise.
7. **Re-check periodically** — sell when price reaches value, or when the
   fundamentals genuinely break.

## Worked examples

### Margin of safety — the master idea

Before the formulas, the concept they all serve. Say your best estimate of a
business's intrinsic value is **$100 per share**, and you demand a **40% margin
of safety.** Then you only buy at **$60 or below.**

Now suppose your estimate was too optimistic and the *true* value is only
**$80.** You still paid $60 — below true value — so you avoided a loss. The 40%
buffer absorbed your own error. That cushion against being wrong *is* the margin
of safety; every Graham rule below is just a different way of guaranteeing it.

### Net-net (Track B)

A company has **current assets $100M**, **total liabilities $60M**, and **10M
shares outstanding.**

- NCAV = $100M − $60M = **$40M**
- NCAV per share = $40M ÷ 10M = **$4.00**
- Graham's buy threshold = ⅔ × $4.00 = **$2.67**

If the stock trades at **$2.50**, it qualifies. You're paying $2.50 for $4.00 of
*net liquid* assets — a **~37% discount to liquidation-style value** — and
getting the operating business thrown in for free.

### Graham number (Track A)

A stock with **EPS = $3.00** and **book value per share = $20.**

- Graham number = √(22.5 × 3 × 20) = √1,350 ≈ **$36.74**

A defensive investor should pay **no more than ~$36.74.** At **$28** there's a
healthy margin of safety; at **$45** it's simply too expensive on Graham's
rules, no matter how exciting the story.

## Key books and primary sources

- ***Security Analysis*** (**1934**, with **David Dodd**) — the foundational
  textbook of value investing; it coined "margin of safety," drew the line
  between investment and speculation, and introduced the NCAV / net-net method.
  Revised many times (1940, 1951, 1962, 1988, 2008, 2023). Dense and
  professional — the "urtext."
- ***The Intelligent Investor*** (**1949**; major revised 4th edition 1971–73) —
  the one to read first. Buffett wrote a preface; Jason Zweig added modern
  commentary in the 2003 edition (a further revised edition appeared in 2024).
  The Mr. Market parable (Ch. 8), the seven Defensive rules (Ch. 14), and
  "Margin of Safety: The Central Concept of Investment" (Ch. 20) all live here.
- ***Storage and Stability*** (1937) and ***World Commodities and World
  Currency*** (1944) — his commodity-reserve-currency works.
- *The Memoirs of the Dean of Wall Street* (autobiography, published 1996).
- Buffett's 1984 essay **"The Superinvestors of Graham-and-Doddsville"** — the
  best short defense of the whole approach.

## Honest criticisms and limitations

Graham would want you to know where his method strains:

- **True net-nets have nearly vanished.** They were everywhere in the
  beaten-down 1930s–50s markets Graham fished in. In today's picked-over,
  more-efficient markets they're rare — clustering in tiny, illiquid, or
  genuinely distressed names — and the strategy no longer scales to large
  portfolios.
- **Book value is less meaningful now.** Graham's era was asset-heavy
  (factories, inventory). Today's economy is dominated by **asset-light,
  intangible-heavy** firms (software, brands, IP) whose real worth never appears
  on the balance sheet. The Graham number and P/B test systematically misjudge
  these and exclude many of the best businesses.
- **It ignores quality and growth.** Pure Graham buys "cheap" regardless of how
  *good* the business is. **Buffett himself evolved away** from strict Graham —
  under Charlie Munger's influence — toward *"wonderful companies at fair
  prices."* That's an explicit acknowledgment of the limit.
- **Intrinsic value is an estimate, not a fact.** Two careful analysts can
  compute very different values; the method's rigor can create false precision.
- **The growth formula is fragile** (Graham said so) and routinely misused.
- **Value can stay cheap or get cheaper** — the dreaded value trap, and value
  investing can lag growth for years. Margin of safety *reduces* but never
  *eliminates* permanent loss.
- **It prefigured — but doesn't guarantee — the academic "value factor."**
  Graham's low-P/E, low-P/B hunting foreshadowed the Fama–French value factor
  (HML, 1992). But that factor endured long droughts (notably ~2007–2020), so
  "cheap beats expensive" is not an automatic edge.

## Common beginner pitfalls

<Callout type="warning">
1. **Confusing "cheap price" with "cheap value."** A $5 stock is not cheaper
   than a $500 stock — only price *relative to intrinsic value* matters.
2. **Treating the growth formula (or Graham number) as gospel.** They were
   rough screens, not buy buttons. Don't compute one magic "intrinsic value" and
   bet the farm on its precision.
3. **Confusing "cheap" with "good" — the value trap.** A low P/E often signals
   real trouble. The Defensive *quality* rules exist precisely to filter these
   out; don't skip them to reach the price tests faster.
4. **Skipping the margin of safety.** Buying at fair value, or because a stock
   "only" looks 5% undervalued (inside your own error band). The discount *is*
   the strategy.
5. **Buying a single net-net / under-diversifying.** These are statistically
   distressed companies; Graham *always* used a broad basket. One concentrated
   bet can blow up.
6. **Ignoring liabilities and off-balance-sheet items** in net-net math —
   pensions, leases, and share dilution can wreck a clean-looking NCAV.
7. **Obeying Mr. Market instead of exploiting him.** Panic-selling at lows and
   FOMO-buying at highs is the exact opposite of the lesson. The thesis needs
   *years.*
8. **Applying asset-based tests to asset-light companies** (tech, services),
   and applying 1949 thresholds blindly without adjusting for inflation, modern
   accounting, and intangibles.
</Callout>

## Putting it to work

You don't need a Bloomberg terminal to start. Open any free stock screener and
set the Defensive filters: market cap above your floor, current ratio ≥ 2,
positive earnings for 10 straight years, P/E below 15, P/B below 1.5 (or the
combined **P/E × P/B ≤ 22.5**). The list that survives is your candidate pool.

For each survivor, compute the **Graham number** and compare it to today's
price. Only act where price sits *comfortably below* that ceiling — that gap is
your margin of safety. Buy a **basket**, not a single name. Then do the hardest
part: **wait.** Let the slow weighing machine do its work, ignore Mr. Market's
daily theatrics, and re-check the thesis only when the *business* — not the
*price* — changes.

That discipline, more than any single formula, is what Graham actually taught.
