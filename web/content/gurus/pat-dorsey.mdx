## The problem Dorsey set out to solve

Warren Buffett gave us a beautiful image: a wonderful business is like a castle
protected by a wide **"economic moat"** — a structural advantage that keeps
competitors from storming in and stealing its profits. (Buffett has used the
metaphor in Berkshire Hathaway's letters since **1986**.) Inspiring. But for a
beginner it's almost useless as a *tool*, because Buffett never handed you a
checklist. How do you actually *look* at a company and decide whether it has a
moat — or whether you're just admiring a popular product?

That's the gap **Pat Dorsey** fills. He didn't invent the moat idea; his
contribution was **systematizing** it — taking the fuzzy, admire-it-when-you-see-it
notion and turning it into something you can **diagnose, step by step.** By the
end of this lesson you'll be able to pick up a company and ask, concretely, "Does
this have a moat — and if so, which kind, and how wide?"

<Callout type="key">
A great **company** is not the same thing as a great **stock**, and a cheap
stock isn't enough either. Dorsey's framework sits at the intersection: find a
business with a durable competitive advantage (a moat), confirm it can reinvest
its profits, *then* buy it only at a sensible price.
</Callout>

## Who he is

**Patrick "Pat" Dorsey, CFA** is an American equity analyst, author, and
investor. He holds a bachelor's degree in government from **Wesleyan University**
and a master's in political science from **Northwestern University**, and is a
**CFA charterholder**.

From **2000 to 2011** he was **Director of Equity Research at Morningstar**, the
big investment-research firm in Chicago. There he grew the equity-research team
from roughly **20 to 90 analysts** and was the principal **architect of
Morningstar's "Economic Moat" rating** — the system that classifies a company as
having a **wide moat, a narrow moat, or no moat**, plus a **"moat trend"**
(positive / stable / negative: is the advantage widening, holding, or eroding?).
That is his signature achievement: turning Buffett's qualitative metaphor into a
repeatable analytical system applied across hundreds of stocks.

After Morningstar he served as Director of Research for Sanibel Captiva Trust,
then founded **Dorsey Asset Management**, a Chicago registered investment adviser
running a single, concentrated global stock strategy built entirely around moats.
The firm hunts for three things together: **(1) economic moats, (2) long runways
to reinvest capital, and (3) skilled capital allocation** — the belief being that
a competitive advantage is worth the most when a business can pour its profits
back in at high returns for many years.

His two books are the reason a beginner can learn this at all:

- ***The Five Rules for Successful Stock Investing*** (Wiley, 2004) — a full
  "how to analyze a stock" manual, including the valuation toolkit.
- ***The Little Book That Builds Wealth: The Knockout Formula for Finding Great
  Investments*** (Wiley, 2008) — the short, canonical statement of the moat
  framework, and the focus of this lesson. **This is the one to read first.**

## The core idea: a moat is a high return that refuses to fall

Here is the single most important sentence in this lesson, so read it twice:

**The financial fingerprint of a moat is a high return on capital that stays
high for years.**

To unpack that, you need one term: **Return on Invested Capital (ROIC)**. Don't
panic — it just answers "for every dollar the business has tied up in its
operations, how many cents of profit does it spit out each year?" A company with
$1,000 of capital that earns $200 of profit has a 20% ROIC.

Now the key economic law. Earning a **high** return attracts competition. If your
shoe store is wildly profitable, rivals open shoe stores next door, cut prices,
and compete your fat profits away. In economics this is the gravity that pulls
returns *down* toward the **cost of capital** — roughly the minimum return
investors demand (say ~10%). Left alone, almost every company's ROIC drifts back
down to that floor.

A **moat is whatever stops that gravity.** It's the structural reason
competitors *can't* pile in and compete the profits away. So when you see a
company that has earned 20%+ ROIC year after year after year, your job isn't to
say "nice number" — it's to ask **"what is preventing competitors from killing
that number?"** The answer to that question *is* the moat. A moat is what extends
the period over which a company earns excess returns — and that extended period
is precisely what makes the business worth more today.

Dorsey's blunt summary of where moats come from: **"Bet on the horse, not the
jockey."** In his words, *"nine times out of 10, the competitive dynamics of an
industry will have a much greater impact"* than even a brilliant CEO. The moat is
a property of the **business and its industry structure**, not of the people
running it this year.

## The full method, step by step

### Step 1 — Confirm returns on capital are genuinely high

A moat leaves fingerprints in the financials: consistently high returns and
strong free cash flow. Dorsey's rough benchmarks:

| Metric | What it measures | Dorsey's "moaty" bar |
|--------|------------------|----------------------|
| **ROA** (return on assets) | profit per dollar of *assets* | ≥ ~7% |
| **ROE** (return on equity) | profit per dollar of *shareholder equity* | ≥ ~15% |
| **ROIC** (return on invested capital) | profit per dollar of *all* invested capital | ≥ ~15% |

You want these sustained over **several years and across a full business cycle
(5–10 years)** — not just one lucky year.

- **High and steady returns** → there's probably a moat. Go to Step 2.
- **Low or wildly bouncing returns** → probably no moat. Be skeptical of the story.

High returns are the *symptom*. The next step finds the *cause*.

### Step 2 — Identify which moat source explains the high returns

A real moat has to trace to at least one specific structural source. **If you
cannot name which one, the high returns are probably temporary** and will be
competed away. Dorsey's book lays out **four** sources; Morningstar later added a
**fifth** (more below).

**1. Intangible assets** — brands, patents, and government licenses.

- A **brand** is a moat *only if it lets the company charge a higher price* —
  Tiffany can charge a premium for the same diamond because of the blue box. A
  famous-but-cheap brand is *not* a moat.
- **Patents** are valuable but legally challengeable and expire — one patent
  about to lapse is a cliff, not a moat.
- The best **regulatory** moats come from **many small rules** (hard to attack
  one by one), not one big rule that a single court case or repeal can erase.

**2. Switching costs** — the time, money, retraining, or integration risk a
customer must absorb to leave. Think of a bank holding your salary, mortgage, and
bill-pay, or the enterprise software (Oracle, SAP) a company's entire operation
runs on. A useful tell for B2B firms: products embedded in a client's daily
operations and booked as ongoing **OPEX** (hard to cut) have stronger switching
costs than those sold as one-time **CAPEX**.

**3. Network effect** — the product gets **more valuable as more people use it.**
A credit-card network (**Visa, Mastercard**) is useless if no shops accept it and
no one carries it — but once *everyone* does, no newcomer can break in, because
they can't offer the network. Common in businesses that connect or share
information (card networks, exchanges, marketplaces), rare in physical goods.
Dorsey calls this the **most powerful but rarest** moat.

**4. Cost advantages** — the company can sustainably produce or deliver for
*less*, from cheaper processes, scale, a unique resource, or location. **Caution:**
process-based advantages (a clever method) are the **weakest**, because rivals
can copy them; advantages from location, hard-to-replicate assets, or genuine
scale are sturdier. Walmart's logistics, or a gravel quarry next to a city
(rivals would have to truck rock in and eat the freight), are structural.

<Callout type="note">
**A fifth source — efficient scale.** Morningstar added this *after* the 2008
book (the *Little Book* itself presents four): a market just **big enough for one
or a few players** to serve profitably. A new entrant would flood capacity and
push everyone into losses, so rivals rationally stay away — think pipelines,
regional utilities, or certain rational oligopolies. Treat it as the later,
institutional fifth source, not as content from the original book.
</Callout>

### Step 3 — Reject the FALSE moats

Dorsey is emphatic that beginners mistake these for moats. **None is durable on
its own** — each is a *result*, not a cause, and each can be copied or lost:

- **A great product.** Anyone can copy a product. Ask: what *stops* them? Dorsey's
  line: *"The best engineer in the world can't build a 10-story sandcastle."*
- **Strong market share or size.** Share can evaporate without a structural
  barrier — ask Nokia or pre-bankruptcy GM. Size doesn't keep competitors out.
- **Great execution / efficiency.** Admirable, but not proprietary. Rivals can be
  efficient too.
- **Great management ("the jockey").** Managers leave, and even brilliant ones
  can't fix a structurally bad business. Bet on the horse, not the jockey.

<Callout type="warning">
This is the test that separates beginners from analysts. When you feel the urge
to call something a moat, check it against this list first. "But the product is
amazing!" / "But they're the market leader!" / "But the CEO is a genius!" are the
exact sentences that precede most moat mistakes. Be especially skeptical of
**consumer services** — restaurants and retailers have low switching costs and
easily copied concepts, so they rarely have moats.
</Callout>

### Step 4 — Judge the moat's width and trend

- **Width:** How *long* can the excess returns last? A decade-plus = **wide**; a
  few years = **narrow**.
- **Moats are absolute, not relative.** A mediocre firm in a great industry can
  have a *wider* moat than the best operator in a brutal one.
- **Trend (positive / stable / negative):** Is the moat **widening, holding, or
  eroding?** A wide-moat rating is a *forecast*, not a fact — so reassess
  regularly.

**Watch for these erosion warning signs (Step 4b):**

- Weakening pricing power / customers getting more price-sensitive
- The product commoditizing
- Technological change — more dangerous for tech-*enabled* firms than for tech
  *sellers*
- The customer base becoming more concentrated
- A competitor that **isn't trying to make money** (state-backed, or "growth at
  all costs")
- The company itself chasing growth into **moatless** businesses
- **Profit warnings** — about **one-third of large profit warnings are followed
  by another, often larger, within a year.** Erosion is usually *gradual, not
  sudden*, which is exactly why you must keep watching.

### Step 5 — Tie the moat to value via the reinvestment runway

A moat is worth far more if the company can **reinvest its high-return profits
back into the business at the same high returns** for years — a long
**"reinvestment runway."** A wide-moat business that throws off cash but has
*nowhere* to redeploy it (a mature "cash cow") is worth less than one that can
keep compounding capital internally. Same moat, very different value — this is
Dorsey Asset Management's signature emphasis.

### Step 6 — Value the business and demand a margin of safety

A company is worth all the future free cash flow ("owner earnings") it will
generate, driven by four factors: **growth, ROIC, risk, and how long the moat
lasts.** Use **several valuation lenses, not one**:

- **P/E** — but build your *own* "E," averaged across good and bad years.
- **P/S** (price-to-sales) — best when margins are temporarily depressed.
- **P/B** (price-to-book) — best for financial firms (watch goodwill and
  suspiciously low ratios).
- **P/CF** (price-to-cash-flow) — cash flow is harder to manipulate than earnings.
- **Cash return** = (free cash flow + net interest expense) ÷ **enterprise
  value**, where enterprise value = market cap + long-term debt − cash. A quick
  read on how much cash the whole business throws off per dollar you'd pay for it.

Get to an intrinsic value, then **buy only at a discount** — a margin of safety.
(A moat actually *helps* here: durable returns make future cash flows easier to
forecast.)

### Step 7 — Hold; sell only when the *business* changes

Moats pay off over *time*. Hold while the moat is intact and the company keeps
reinvesting. Sell for one of four reasons: you **made an analytical mistake**, the
**moat is deteriorating**, you **found something materially better**, or for
**rebalancing** — **not** because the price moved. As Dorsey puts it, *"Even the
best company will hurt your portfolio if you pay too much for it."*

## Worked examples

### A — Confirming a moat with the cash-return tool

*(Illustrative figures, built straight from the formula.)* Company A reports:

- Market cap = **$8,000M**, long-term debt = **$1,000M**, cash = **$500M**
  - → **Enterprise value = 8,000 + 1,000 − 500 = $8,500M**
- Free cash flow = **$600M**, net interest expense = **$40M**
  - → **Cash return = (600 + 40) ÷ 8,500 = 640 / 8,500 ≈ 7.5%**

A ~7.5% cash yield on a business that *already* clears the ROIC ≥ 15% bar (Step 1)
and is backed by a nameable moat (Step 2) is an attractive starting point: you're
earning a solid cash yield *and* the moat should let that cash stream grow and
persist.

### B — Why a moat = sustained excess profit

Two companies each start with **$1,000 of capital** and a **10% cost of capital**
(so a "normal" return is $100/year). Both earn a fat **25% ROIC = $250** in year
1 — **$150 of excess profit** above the normal $100.

- **No moat:** competitors pile in. By year 5 ROIC has been competed down to ~10%
  ($100). The $150 of excess profit has vanished.
- **Wide moat:** a network effect keeps rivals out. ROIC holds at 25%, so the
  business keeps earning that **$150 of excess profit every single year** for
  15+ years.

That stream of preserved excess profit *is* the dollar value of the moat. Same
starting business, wildly different worth.

### C — The reinvestment runway

Now take a wide-moat firm earning **20% ROIC**:

- **With a long runway** (it can reinvest *all* its profits back at 20%),
  intrinsic value compounds at ~20%/year: $1,000 grows to **1.20¹⁰ ≈ 6.19×**, or
  about **$6,190 in ten years.**
- **With no runway** (it must pay all profits out as dividends), the underlying
  business barely grows — you get the cash, but not the compounding.

Same 20% moat, very different outcome. That's why Dorsey weights the runway so
heavily.

### D — A real false moat: JetBlue vs. Visa/Mastercard

This is Dorsey's own cautionary tale. Early on, **JetBlue** posted enviable
numbers — roughly **17% operating margins and ~20% return on equity** — that
*looked* like a moat and would sail through Step 1. But there was no durable
source behind them (Step 2 fails): as its fleet aged, **costs rose**, and
**Southwest simply copied** its perks (leather seats, free TV). The high returns
reverted to earth.

Contrast **Visa and Mastercard**, whose **two-sided network** moat persisted
through multiple management changes — a genuine, source-backed advantage. **The
lesson:** numbers that pass Step 1 are meaningless if Step 2 (a real, nameable
source) fails.

### E — Valuation and margin of safety

Suppose a moaty firm earns **$2.00 per share** and you judge a **20× P/E** fair
given its durable growth — an intrinsic value of **$40**. Demanding a **25%
margin of safety**, you wait to buy at **$40 × 0.75 = $30.** If the market never
offers $30, you don't buy. The moat tells you *what* to want; the margin of safety
tells you *what to pay*.

## Honest criticisms and limitations

<Callout type="warning">
No framework is magic. Take these as seriously as the method itself.
</Callout>

- **Hindsight / circularity.** It's easy to "identify" a moat *after* a company
  has already earned high returns for years. The framework describes the past
  confidently but is much harder to apply *predictively* to young or changing
  businesses.
- **Subjective "wide vs. narrow."** No precise formula separates them; reasonable
  analysts disagree, and even Morningstar's professional moat ratings have been
  wrong (businesses rated wide-moat that later eroded — and ratings can be slow to
  downgrade).
- **Disruption blind spot.** The "gradual erosion" language understates how *fast*
  technology can destroy a moat: newspapers, Kodak, Blockbuster, and many
  retailers had "moats" the internet erased far faster than expected.
- **Valuation still does the heavy lifting.** A moat tells you *what* to buy, not
  *what to pay*. Dorsey concedes a great company bought too expensively is a bad
  investment — yet his valuation chapters are deliberately introductory, leaving
  beginners under-equipped on the hardest part. (And in 2015–2021, crowding into
  quality names pushed many wide-moat stocks to rich valuations.)
- **"Bet on the horse, not the jockey" can be overstated.** In
  capital-allocation-intensive or founder-led businesses, management quality
  genuinely matters; treating it as near-irrelevant is a simplification.
- **Survivorship in the examples.** The vivid winners are partly chosen with
  hindsight — the same qualitative story could have been told about companies that
  later failed.
- **Concentration risk.** Dorsey Asset Management's concentrated approach
  magnifies the cost of being wrong on any single moat thesis.

## Common beginner pitfalls

1. **Mistaking a hot product, big market share, fast growth, or a star CEO for a
   moat** — Dorsey's four false moats, again. *"The best engineer in the world
   can't build a 10-story sandcastle."*
2. **Assuming "big brand = moat."** Only if it produces **pricing power**.
3. **Skipping Step 1 or Step 2.** High returns with no nameable source = a
   temporary advantage you'll watch evaporate.
4. **Treating a moat as permanent** — failing to monitor the moat *trend* and the
   erosion signals.
5. **Confusing high margins with high returns on capital.** A moat shows up in
   *returns on capital*; a capital-hungry business can have fat margins and
   mediocre ROIC.
6. **Calling a single patent or one big customer a moat** — that's cliff risk.
7. **Forgetting the reinvestment runway** — buying a wide-moat cash cow and
   expecting it to compound.
8. **Ignoring valuation** because the business is "great," or **selling on price
   moves** instead of business changes.
9. **Assuming consumer-facing or fast-growing = moaty.** Restaurants and retailers
   rarely have moats, and growth into moatless lines can *destroy* value.

## Putting it to work

You don't need a Bloomberg terminal to start. Tonight, pick **one company you
already understand** and run Dorsey's checklist by hand:

1. Look up its **ROIC, ROE, and ROA** for the last 5–10 years. Clearing
   ~15% / 15% / 7%, steadily?
2. If yes, **name the source** — intangibles, switching costs, network effect,
   cost advantage, or efficient scale. Write the actual sentence: "Competitors
   can't beat them because ___."
3. **Stress-test it** against the four false moats. Are you secretly just
   describing a nice product or a popular brand?
4. Check the **trend** — any of the erosion warning signs showing up?
5. Ask whether it has somewhere to **reinvest** profits at those high returns.
6. Only then glance at the **price** — estimate intrinsic value (try the
   cash-return tool) and decide what discount you'd demand before buying.

Do that for ten companies and the difference between a *story* and a *moat* stops
being abstract. That instinct — earned, not memorized — is the whole gift of Pat
Dorsey's work.
