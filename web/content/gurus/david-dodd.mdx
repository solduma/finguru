## Why this name is on the spine of every value investor's bible

Open the most famous investing textbook ever written and you'll see two names on
the cover: *Security Analysis*, by **Graham and Dodd.** Almost everyone has heard
of Benjamin Graham — Warren Buffett's teacher, the "father of value investing."
Far fewer can tell you who the *Dodd* was.

That's a shame, because the partnership only worked because of him. Graham was
the practitioner genius — restless, brilliant, scattered. **David Dodd was the
scholar who caught it all, organized it, sourced it, and turned a working
investor's instincts into a method you and I can actually follow, step by step.**
Without Dodd, Graham's ideas might have stayed lecture-hall folklore. With him,
they became a discipline.

This lesson teaches that discipline. By the end you'll be able to take a real
company's financial statements and decide — using nothing but conservative
arithmetic — whether its stock is a genuine bargain, *and* you'll know whether
the approach has actually worked when tested on decades of real returns.

## Who he was

**David LeFevre Dodd** (August 23, 1895 – September 18, 1988) was an American
finance professor who spent his *entire* working life at **Columbia University.**
Born in Berkeley County, West Virginia, he earned a B.S. from the University of
Pennsylvania (1920), an M.S. from Columbia (1921), and a Columbia Ph.D. (1930).

At Columbia he rose through every rank — instructor of economics, then of
finance, assistant professor (1930), associate professor (1938), full professor
(1947), and associate dean of the business school (1948–52) — before retiring as
professor emeritus in 1961. One institution, one career, four decades.

But the moment that made history happened in the wreckage after the **1929 crash**,
which had nearly wiped Graham out. Benjamin Graham, already a working investor,
agreed to teach his investing course at Columbia on one condition: someone had to
take careful notes, so he couldn't later be accused of giving away tips that lost
money. Dodd — then a young instructor about five years Graham's junior —
volunteered. **Those meticulous transcriptions became the structural backbone of
*Security Analysis* (1934).**

<Callout type="key">
Think of the division of labor like this: **Graham was the inventor; Dodd was the
engineer who wrote the manual.** Graham supplied the original investment genius,
the real-world track record, and the metaphors (Mr. Market, the
voting-vs-weighing machine). Dodd supplied the academic rigor — he transcribed,
organized, sourced, and structured loose insight into a comprehensive,
teachable system. The enduring brand "Graham and Dodd" reflects exactly that
partnership.
</Callout>

Columbia honored him with an honorary Doctor of Letters in 1984, on the 50th
anniversary of *Security Analysis*, and his name lives on today in Columbia's
**Heilbrunn Center for Graham & Dodd Investing** (endowed with the help of his
daughter, Barbara Dodd Anderson).

<Callout type="warning">
A common mix-up: the slim classic *The Interpretation of Financial Statements*
(1937) was written by **Benjamin Graham and Spencer B. Meredith — not Dodd.** And
*The Intelligent Investor* (1949) is **Graham's solo work.** Dodd's books are
*Security Analysis* (with Graham) and his solo academic work *Stock Watering*
(1930). Don't credit him with the wrong title.
</Callout>

## The core idea, in plain language

Here is the whole philosophy in one breath:

> A stock is not a flickering number on a screen. It is a **fractional ownership
> share of a real business**, and that business has a calculable underlying worth.
> Most of the time the market price drifts away from that worth, because crowds are
> emotional. Your job is to estimate the worth from hard facts, then buy only when
> the price is well *below* it.

Two terms do all the heavy lifting. Learn them now and the rest follows:

- **Intrinsic value** — what a business is *actually* worth, judged from its assets,
  earnings power, and financial strength, regardless of today's stock price. It's an
  estimate, a range — not a precise figure.
- **Margin of safety** — the gap between intrinsic value and the price you pay. If a
  business is worth roughly $100 per share and you pay $65, your margin of safety is
  the $35 cushion. That cushion is your protection against bad luck, bad analysis,
  and plain bad timing.

Graham captured the crowd's mood swings with his famous **"Mr. Market"** parable: a
manic-depressive business partner who shows up every day offering to buy your shares
or sell you his, at a wildly different price each time depending on his mood. You're
free to ignore him — or to take advantage when he's panicking and quotes you a
ridiculously low price. You profit *from* the market's mood swings instead of being
ruled by them.

You are not predicting the future. You are buying a dollar of demonstrable value for
60 or 70 cents and letting the gap close over time.

## The full method — concrete steps a beginner can follow

### Step 1 — Decide whether you're investing or speculating

Graham's definition (straight from the book) is your filter for everything that
follows:

> *"An investment operation is one which, upon thorough analysis, promises safety of
> principal and an adequate return. Operations not meeting these requirements are
> speculative."*

Notice the order: **analysis first, safety second, return third.** If you're buying
because a stock "feels like it's going up," that's speculation. The Graham-Dodd
investor demands a reason rooted in the numbers.

### Step 2 — Get the primary documents

Pull the company's three core financial statements from its annual report (the
**10-K** for U.S. companies):

- the **balance sheet** (what it owns and owes),
- the **income statement** (what it earned), and
- the **cash-flow statement** (the actual cash moving in and out).

Read **several years**, not a single snapshot — you want trends, not one lucky
quarter. Dodd's entire ethos is grounding your judgment in **audited numbers**,
not stories, press releases, or "the chart looks good."

### Step 3 — Screen for quality and safety first

Before you reach for a calculator, filter out the obviously fragile. Favor
companies with:

- **steady positive earnings** across multiple years (not one-off spikes),
- **relatively low debt**, and
- a **consistent ability to pay dividends.**

Avoid speculative, story-driven names with no earnings record. Cheapness only
matters once a company is solid enough to survive long enough for the value to be
recognized.

### Step 4 — Estimate intrinsic value conservatively

Two beginner-friendly methods. Start with the first — it's the purest expression of
the Graham-Dodd mindset.

**Method A — Net Current Asset Value (NCAV), the "net-net" check.** This is the most
conservative valuation possible: it asks what the company would be worth if it shut
down *today* and liquidated.

```
NCAV           = Total Current Assets − Total Liabilities
NCAV per share = NCAV ÷ Shares Outstanding
```

<Callout type="warning">
The single most common NCAV mistake: subtracting only *current* liabilities. You must
subtract **ALL** liabilities — including long-term debt, pensions, lease obligations,
and preferred stock. The whole point is to be brutally conservative. Skipping
liabilities inflates the value and destroys the safety the method is built on.
</Callout>

Why only *current* assets (cash, receivables, inventory) and not the factories and
equipment? Because in a liquidation, plant and machinery often fetch pennies on the
dollar, while current assets are close to cash. Graham deliberately assigns **zero
value** to fixed assets and goodwill and counts only what's nearly liquid. That's the
conservatism — and that's why anything you do get from the buildings, brand, and
future earnings is essentially free.

**Method B — the earnings-based formula (a quick cross-check).** From Graham's later
book *The Intelligent Investor* comes a rough screen for a company's value based on
earnings:

```
V = EPS × (8.5 + 2g)
```

where `EPS` is earnings per share, `8.5` is the base price-to-earnings (P/E) ratio
Graham assigned to a no-growth company, and `g` is the expected annual growth rate
over the next 7–10 years (as a whole number, so 5% growth means g = 5). For the
underlying earnings, use a **normalized, multi-year average** rather than the latest
year, so one good or bad year doesn't distort the estimate.

<Callout type="note">
Treat Formula B as a rough sniff test, not gospel. Graham himself was cautious about
it: it ignores interest rates entirely and can wildly overvalue a company if you plug
in optimistic growth. And note the provenance — this formula is from *The Intelligent
Investor* (1949), **not** from *Security Analysis*. Beginners constantly miscredit it.
</Callout>

Also worth knowing while you screen: low **P/E**, low **P/B** (price-to-book, ideally
near or below book value), and a decent dividend yield are the classic "is it cheap?"
filters. They flag candidates; they don't replace the valuation work above.

### Step 5 — Apply the margin of safety

Estimating value is only half the job. Now you refuse to pay full price.

- For the **net-net** approach, the classic Graham rule of thumb is to buy only when
  the price is **at or below about two-thirds (≈67%) of NCAV per share** — roughly a
  33% discount to liquidation value.
- More generally, aim to pay around **"90 cents for a dollar"** of value for a strong,
  high-quality company, and demand a discount of up to **50%** for a riskier one.

<Callout type="note">
The exact "two-thirds of NCAV" threshold is universally cited as Graham's rule, and
it's a sound, well-established convention — but treat the precise 67% as a dial, not a
law of nature. The *principle* (pay meaningfully less than liquidation value) is what
matters; the precise cutoff is yours to set.
</Callout>

### Step 6 — Match the strategy to who you are

Graham and Dodd split investors into two honest categories. Pick the one that fits
your temperament — and be honest, because the wrong fit is how people get hurt. The
crucial point is that **how much work you're willing to do should determine which
tools you use**, and net-nets belong firmly in the active camp.

<ConceptDiagram
  caption="The defensive/enterprising split decides which toolkit is yours. Ask one honest question — how much original work will you really do? — and it routes you to a strategy.   B --> D[Broad diversification]
  B --> E[Large, strong, dividend-paying names]
  B --> F[Buy at moderate prices, hold long, no timing]
  C --> G[Original financial-statement analysis]
  C --> H[Net-nets and deep-value screens]
  C --> I[Special situations / workouts]`}
/>

- **The Defensive (passive) investor** wants minimal effort and minimal worry. The
  prescription: broad diversification, long holding periods, no market timing, and a
  focus on large, financially strong, dividend-paying companies bought at moderate
  prices.
- **The Enterprising (active) investor** is willing to do real, original analysis to
  hunt for exceptional bargains and special situations. **This is where net-nets and
  deep-value screens belong** — they take work, and they reward it.

### Step 7 — Diversify and be patient

Net-nets are usually unloved, struggling companies (Graham called them "cigar butts"
— one free puff left in a discarded stub). Any single one might keep declining. The
defense is a **basket**: you spread your money across many of them, knowing that *on
average* the discount-to-value gap closes often enough to profit. Avoid leverage and
margin — forced selling can knock you out before your thesis plays out. Then you wait,
sometimes for years, and sell when price reaches your value estimate or the
fundamentals deteriorate. You buy *from* the market's folly; you don't join it.

## A worked numeric example

*(Illustrative numbers, built straight from the formulas.)*

Suppose **Acme Widgets Inc.** reports:

| Item | Value |
|------|-------|
| Total current assets | $120 million |
| Total liabilities (all of them) | $70 million |
| Shares outstanding | 10 million |
| Current market price | $3.00 / share |

Market capitalization (what the whole company costs at today's price) is therefore
`$3.00 × 10 million = $30 million`. Now run the net-net test:

1. **NCAV** = current assets − all liabilities = `$120M − $70M = $50M`
2. **NCAV per share** = `$50M ÷ 10M shares = $5.00`
3. **Two-thirds buy target** = `$5.00 × 0.667 ≈ $3.33`
4. The market price is **$3.00**, which is *below* the $3.33 target → **it passes the
   screen.** Your margin of safety versus NCAV is `($5.00 − $3.00) / $5.00 = 40%`.
5. **Sanity check:** NCAV ($50M) is greater than market cap ($30M) — confirming the
   stock trades below its conservative net asset value.

So you buy at $3.00. Now comes the part the textbook keeps stressing: **you wait.**
The whole thesis is that the $2.00 gap between price and NCAV eventually closes as
the market reprices the stock toward its real worth. That can take months or years —
and it almost never happens in a straight line.

**The interpretation in plain English:** you are paying $30 million for a business
whose current assets alone — after paying off *every single liability* — are worth
$50 million. You barely need the factory to make money for this to work out. That
asymmetry *is* the Graham-Dodd edge.

<Callout type="note">
**Watch the threshold, not just the discount.** If Acme instead traded at **$3.50**,
it would still be below NCAV ($5.00) — but it would be *above* the $3.33 two-thirds
buy limit, so it would **fail** the strict net-net discipline. Below NCAV is
necessary; below two-thirds of NCAV is what Graham actually required before buying.
</Callout>

## Did it actually work? The empirical record

A method is only worth learning if it survived contact with real markets. The
net-net strategy has been tested repeatedly — and the published evidence is
unusually strong for a rule this simple.

- **Henry Oppenheimer (1986),** *Financial Analysts Journal*, "Ben Graham's Net
  Current Asset Values: A Performance Update." Buying U.S. stocks priced below NCAV
  and rebalancing annually returned roughly **33.7% per year vs. about 12.1%** for
  the market over **1971–1983.** That is a very large, sustained margin.
- **Xiao & Arnold (2008)** tested the London Stock Exchange over **1980–2005** and
  found net-nets returned about **31.1% vs. 20.5%** for the broad market.
- A **1993** study of Japanese stocks (1975–1988) found roughly **19.7% vs. 16.6%**,
  and **Dudzinski & Kunkel (2014),** *The Journal of Investing,* reported about
  **24.7% annualized (2003–2010)** with returns that standard risk models (CAPM and
  Fama-French-Carhart) couldn't fully explain — an excess return the models couldn't
  attribute to ordinary risk factors.

<Callout type="warning">
Read these numbers honestly. They are **gross, historical, rebalanced-portfolio**
results — not what a beginner clicking "buy" will personally earn. The studies
generally **don't** fully net out trading costs, the difficulty of actually filling
orders in tiny illiquid stocks, taxes, or survivorship issues, and the very best
window (Oppenheimer's 1970s) was unusually rich in net-nets. The honest takeaway:
the *approach* has a real, repeatedly documented edge — but it lived in small,
hard-to-trade, often unpleasant companies, and capturing it required the
diversification and patience the next section is about. Past performance is never a
guarantee.
</Callout>

## The key books

- **_Security Analysis_** (Benjamin Graham & David L. Dodd) — first edition **1934**,
  McGraw-Hill (Whittlesey House). This is the foundational text of value investing
  and the longest-running investment book in print. It is where the **"margin of
  safety"** idea was first laid out as a systematic principle — though the phrase
  became most famous later, as the title of the closing chapter of Graham's *The
  Intelligent Investor* (1949), which he called the central concept of all investing.
  Major editions: 1st (1934), 2nd (1940), 3rd (1951, the last with both men on the
  Columbia faculty), 4th (1962, the final Graham–Dodd edition), then later editions
  updated by others — 5th (1988), 6th (2008, with a foreword by Warren Buffett), and
  7th (2023).
- **_Stock Watering: The Judicial Valuation of Property for Stock-Issue Purposes_**
  (David L. Dodd, 1930, Columbia University Press) — his solo academic work, grown
  from his doctoral dissertation.

Related books you'll hear about but that are **not** by Dodd: *The Intelligent
Investor* (Graham, 1949 — source of the "Mr. Market" parable and the
`V = EPS × (8.5 + 2g)` formula) and *The Interpretation of Financial Statements*
(Graham & Meredith, 1937).

## Honest criticisms and limitations

No method is complete without knowing where it breaks. Be honest about these:

- **It's backward- and balance-sheet-looking.** The method leans on reported assets
  and past earnings, so it can systematically *miss* great businesses whose value
  isn't on the balance sheet — software, brands, networks, and other intangibles. A
  modern software firm can be a fantastic investment and never come close to passing
  a net-net screen.
- **Net-nets are nearly extinct.** They were plentiful in the Depression-era markets
  of the 1930s–40s; in today's efficient, heavily-scanned markets, genuine net-nets
  are rare and tend to be tiny, illiquid, distressed micro-caps — precisely the names
  with real business decline. You may screen hundreds of companies and find a handful.
- **The "cigar-butt" trap.** Cheapness alone can be a value trap: a stock cheap
  relative to its assets may simply keep *destroying* those assets. Cheap can get
  cheaper.
- **Quantitative over qualitative.** *Security Analysis* deliberately downplayed soft
  factors like management quality and competitive moat — exactly the things that make
  a great business great.
- **The earnings formula is crude.** `V = EPS × (8.5 + 2g)` ignores interest rates and
  can badly overvalue optimistic growth assumptions.
- **Even Buffett evolved past pure cheapness.** Warren Buffett — taught by both Graham
  and Dodd at Columbia — migrated, under Charlie Munger's influence, toward buying
  wonderful businesses at fair prices, famously concluding: *"It's better to buy a
  great company at a fair price than a fair company at a great price."* That is an
  explicit acknowledgment of the original net-net framework's limits.

<Callout type="note">
The right takeaway is not "this method is obsolete." It's that **the *principles* —
intrinsic value, margin of safety, treating stocks as businesses — are timeless,
while the specific *tool* (the net-net) is now a niche technique.** The mindset
outlived the mechanics, and the mindset is what every later value investor inherited.
</Callout>

## Common beginner pitfalls

<Callout type="warning">
1. **Confusing a low price with value.** A $2 stock, or a stock down 50%, is not
   "cheap." Cheapness is *relative to intrinsic value*, never relative to the dollar
   price tag or to its own past price.
2. **Skipping the margin of safety.** Buying *at* your estimated value, instead of
   *below* it, removes the entire protective cushion — the one thing the method exists
   to give you.
3. **Forgetting to subtract ALL liabilities in NCAV** (long-term debt, pensions,
   preferred stock, and so on), and assuming inventory or receivables will fully
   liquidate at book value. This inflates the apparent value and is the most frequent
   computational error.
4. **Over-concentrating in net-nets.** These are a *basket* strategy by design.
   Betting big on one fragile micro-cap — or using margin — is how you get hurt.
5. **Treating intrinsic value as a precise number.** It's a range and an estimate —
   which is exactly *why* you demand a margin of safety, not a reason to skip one.
6. **Mis-crediting the framework.** The `V = EPS × (8.5 + 2g)` formula is from *The
   Intelligent Investor*, not *Security Analysis*; and *The Interpretation of Financial
   Statements* is Graham & Meredith, not Dodd.
7. **Ignoring business quality entirely.** Pure statistical cheapness, with no glance
   at whether the business is bleeding cash, leads straight into value traps.
</Callout>

## Putting it to work

You don't need a Bloomberg terminal or a finance degree to start practicing
Graham-Dodd — that was the whole point of making it *teachable*. Here's a concrete
first project:

1. Pick a stock screener that lets you filter on **price-to-book** or net current
   assets, and look for companies trading below their NCAV.
2. For each candidate, open the actual **10-K** and compute NCAV per share *by hand* —
   current assets minus *all* liabilities, divided by shares outstanding. Doing it
   yourself once cements the discipline.
3. Apply the two-thirds rule. Anything trading above ~67% of NCAV gets set aside.
4. For the survivors, read enough of the business description to ask: *is this company
   merely unloved, or is it actively dying?* Quality is your tiebreaker.
5. If you act at all, build a **diversified basket** of these names rather than betting
   the farm on one, avoid borrowing, and commit to **patience** while the gaps close.

Even if you never buy a single net-net, internalizing the Graham-Dodd habits —
**value the business, demand a margin of safety, ignore Mr. Market's moods, and
ground every judgment in audited numbers** — will make you a more disciplined investor
in *any* strategy. That discipline is David Dodd's real legacy: he didn't just help
write a book, he turned investing into something a beginner can be taught.
