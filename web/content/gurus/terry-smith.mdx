## The whole strategy in one breath

Terry Smith has spent fifteen years repeating the same three sentences, because
he believes investing really is that simple — and that the difficulty is *doing*
it, not understanding it:

> **"Buy good companies. Don't overpay. Do nothing."**

That's the entire method. Each clause hides a discipline:

- **Buy good companies** — only businesses that earn very high returns on the
  money they put to work, protected by a durable advantage.
- **Don't overpay** — even a wonderful company is a bad investment at a silly
  price.
- **Do nothing** — once you own a great business, the hardest and most
  profitable thing you can do is *leave it alone* and let it compound.

This lesson unpacks all three so a complete beginner can actually run the
strategy — and, unusually, Smith publishes the *exact* portfolio averages he
holds himself, so you get hard numbers to aim for instead of vague advice.

<Callout type="key">
Smith's edge is **behavioral as much as analytical.** Most investors lose money
not because they can't spot a good company, but because they over-trade, chase
fads, and panic-sell. "Do nothing" is the part everyone underestimates.
</Callout>

## Who he is

**Terence "Terry" Smith** was born on 15 May 1953 in East London. He read
history at University College Cardiff (a First, 1974) and later took an MBA at
Henley Management College (1979). He started out at Barclays Bank, then became a
sell-side research analyst — rated the **top-ranked bank analyst in London from
1984 to 1989** — before becoming Head of UK Company Research at UBS Phillips &
Drew.

Two chapters of his career matter for understanding his investing:

**1. *Accounting for Growth* (1992).** After companies like Polly Peck collapsed
despite reporting healthy profits, Smith wrote a book cataloguing **twelve
accounting techniques** firms use to flatter their results. UBS — whose clients
included some of the named companies — demanded he withdraw it. He refused; they
**suspended, fired, and sued him.** The publicity drove the book to **No. 1,
knocking Stephen Hawking's *A Brief History of Time* off the top** (it sold over
100,000 copies). That fight is the origin of his lifelong obsession with one
question: *does the reported profit actually turn into cash?*

**2. Fundsmith (2010).** After building the inter-dealer broker Tullett Prebon
into the world's #2 (he was CEO from 2000), Smith founded the fund manager
**Fundsmith in 2010** and launched the **Fundsmith Equity Fund in November
2010**. By the end of 2023 it managed roughly **£36 billion**, and Smith had
personally invested over **£250 million** of his own money in it — which is why
the press nicknamed him "the English Warren Buffett."

<Callout type="note">
Smith eats his own cooking. A founder with hundreds of millions of his *own*
money in the fund has the same incentives as you — a useful thing to check
before trusting anyone with your savings.
</Callout>

## Why "quality" beats almost everything

The intellectual core is a single number: **Return on Capital Employed (ROCE)**
(closely related to ROIC, return on invested capital).

> **ROCE** = operating profit ÷ capital employed
> (capital employed = the equity *plus* debt the business actually uses).

It answers: *for every £1 of capital this business uses, how many pence of
profit does it generate each year?* A company earning 30% ROCE turns £1 of
capital into 30p of profit annually. A company at 8% turns the same £1 into 8p.

Here is the magic, and why Smith cares about ROCE more than anything: **if a
company can reinvest its profits back into the business at that same high rate,
shareholder value compounds at roughly that rate, year after year.** Smith's own
portfolio averages around **30–32% ROCE** — roughly double the wider market.

The whole strategy is just a hunt for businesses that can keep this engine
running for a very long time — and then the patience to hold them while it does.

## The full method, step by step

### Step 1 — Pass the company through the "quality gate"

Before you look at the price, decide whether the *business* is good enough.
Fundsmith publishes the **portfolio-average metrics** it actually holds, which
give a beginner concrete bars to aim for. The figures below are the look-through
averages Smith reported for 2024 (confirmed at the 2025 shareholder meeting):

| Metric | What it measures | Fundsmith portfolio | The market (index) |
|--------|------------------|---------------------|--------------------|
| **ROCE / ROIC** | profit per £1 of capital used | **~30–32%** | ~15% |
| **Gross margin** | pricing power | **~64%** | ~40% |
| **Operating margin** | profitability after running costs | **~30%** | ~15% |
| **Cash conversion** | free cash flow ÷ net income | **~88–100%** | — |
| **Interest cover** | profit ÷ interest owed (low debt reliance) | **~27×** | ~9× |

Each line is a quality test in disguise:

- **High ROCE** is the single most important number — it shows the business is
  genuinely profitable on the capital it uses, and that reinvested profits will
  compound at a high rate.
- **High gross and operating margins** signal **pricing power** (the ability to
  raise prices without losing customers), not commodity economics.
- **High cash conversion** is the *Accounting for Growth* lens: if the profits
  are real, the cash should show up. Free cash flow should track net income
  (Smith aims near 100%; it was ~88% in 2024, dented by one holding's heavy
  capital spending). *(Free cash flow = the cash left after the business pays to
  run itself and maintain its assets.)*
- **High interest cover** means the business isn't propped up by debt — its
  returns are earned, not borrowed.

He also wants **capital-light growth** (it can grow without constantly borrowing
or issuing new shares), a **wide, durable moat** — advantages "difficult to
replicate and largely impervious to technological change" (brands, patents,
distribution networks, high switching costs, network effects) — and **everyday,
repeat, low-ticket sales** (consumer staples, medical consumables, payments,
recurring software).

<Callout type="note">
These are *portfolio averages* Fundsmith publishes, not official screening
cut-offs — Fundsmith states its criteria qualitatively and then reports what the
resulting portfolio looks like. Use the numbers as a calibration target (aim for
businesses in this league), not as a mechanical pass/fail line.
</Callout>

### Step 2 — Apply the hard exclusions

Smith simply *refuses entire sectors* that structurally fail the quality test.
This is a feature, not laziness — it shrinks the universe to something a small
team can actually know deeply.

Excluded: **banks, insurers, airlines, autos, real estate / property, heavy
industrials, resources and commodities, and most cyclicals.** These tend to be
capital-hungry, cyclical, or priced like commodities. Smith has said that on his
criteria **fewer than about 70 companies in the entire world** qualify.

<Callout type="warning">
This is unusual and you should understand the trade-off: by excluding banks,
energy, and cyclicals, Smith *guarantees* he will badly lag the market in years
when exactly those sectors lead. **In 2022 — his worst year ever — Fundsmith
Equity fell −13.8%, worse than the MSCI World's −7.8%**, as rising rates hammered
highly-rated growth stocks. He accepts that to avoid the businesses he thinks
destroy capital over the long run.
</Callout>

### Step 3 — Don't overpay

Quality is necessary but **not sufficient.** A great business bought at a crazy
price is still a poor investment. Smith's valuation lens is the **free-cash-flow
yield**:

> **FCF yield** = free cash flow per share ÷ share price.

It's the cash-earnings version of flipping a P/E upside down — "how much real
cash am I getting back each year for the price I pay?" He judges that yield
against the company's quality and growth, expecting to pay an *above-average*
multiple for an above-average business — but **not any price.** In 2024 the
portfolio's FCF yield was about **3.1%**, versus roughly **3.7%** for the S&P
500 — meaning Smith was paying a **~20% premium** to the market for what he
argues is far higher quality. (We'll put numbers on this in the worked example.)

Here is the beginner-counterintuitive heart of it. The same exact company hands
you a *smaller* slice of real cash for every pound you spend as the price-to-FCF
multiple climbs — and your return rides on that yield. Pay 26× and you collect
3.8% of your money back in cash each year; pay 65× for the *identical business*
and you collect just 1.5%. The quality didn't change; only the price did, and
the price quietly ate most of your return.

Why does a thin starting yield matter so much? Because your long-run return is
roughly *the cash yield you buy plus the rate the business grows that cash* —
**minus** whatever the rich multiple gives back when it inevitably drifts toward
normal. Overpay, and you start the race a lap behind: the growth has to first
work off the excess price before it earns anything for *you*.

<Callout type="note">
"Don't overpay" does **not** mean "buy only cheap." Smith has held richly-rated
stocks when the quality and growth justify it — see the Novo Nordisk example
below, which he bought on a P/E around 28. The discipline is to demand that the
*future cash you'll receive* justifies the price, not to refuse high multiples on
principle.
</Callout>

### Step 4 — Concentrate

Hold only about **20–30 stocks.** Few enough that you could defend every single
one in depth; enough that one company blowing up won't sink you. Smith favors
large, liquid names you can buy and sell easily.

### Step 5 — Do nothing

Run **very low turnover.** This is the discipline that gives the strategy its
name. In 2024, Fundsmith's portfolio turnover was just **3.2%**, costing the fund
about **0.02% (£447,000)** in trading — a rounding error. Every trade costs money
and invites mistakes. You sell only when the **investment thesis breaks**, never
because the price wobbled:

- the moat is eroding,
- ROCE has structurally fallen,
- or the stock has become wildly overvalued.

A 20% price dip in a company whose business is unchanged is *not* a sell signal —
it may be a buying opportunity. This is the discipline beginners find hardest.
Smith likens it to the Tour de France: the winner isn't the rider who wins every
stage, but the one who picks a sound strategy and **sticks with it** while
compounding quietly.

### Step 6 — Keep costs low and ignore the noise

Minimize fees and trading frictions, and tune out macro forecasts. Smith refuses
to market-time — "we have no clue how to do it… which I think is an advantage."
Nobody reliably predicts interest rates or recessions, so building a strategy on
those guesses is a waste. Stay fully invested in quality through downturns.

## A worked example with numbers

### Part 1 — Why ROCE, not growth, wins

Imagine you're screening two companies. Both employ £1,000m of capital today.

**Company A — "BrandCo" (a consumer staple):**
- Operating profit £300m → **ROCE = 300 / 1,000 = 30%** (Smith's territory)
- Net profit £220m; free cash flow £220m → **cash conversion = 220 / 220 = 100%**
  (the profit shows up as real cash — exactly what Smith demands)
- Grows ~8% a year, funded from its own cash, no new shares issued

**Company B — "GrowthTech":**
- Operating profit £80m → **ROCE = 80 / 1,000 = 8%**
- Net profit £60m; free cash flow £20m → **cash conversion = 20 / 60 = 33%**
  (profits are *not* turning into cash — a red flag)
- Grows 20% a year, but funds it by repeatedly issuing new shares

<BarChart
  caption="BrandCo vs. GrowthTech on the two numbers Smith cares about most. GrowthTech grows faster, but its low ROCE and weak cash conversion mean the growth doesn't create much value per share — and the cash to fund it isn't even there."
  labels={["ROCE %", "Cash conversion %"]}
  series={[
    { label: "BrandCo", data: [30, 100] },
    { label: "GrowthTech", data: [8, 33] }
  ]}
  yLabel="percent"
/>

Smith buys **A, not B** — even though B *grows faster*. If each company reinvests
its earnings at its own ROCE, then over **10 years** BrandCo compounds at 30%
(≈ 1.30¹⁰ ≈ **13.8×**) while GrowthTech compounds at 8% (≈ 1.08¹⁰ ≈ **2.2×**).
The compounding gap dwarfs any difference in growth headlines or purchase price.
GrowthTech's fast growth is funded by dilution and isn't backed by cash; it's the
classic "fast growth ≠ quality" trap.

### Part 2 — Now check the price

Suppose BrandCo trades at a market value of £5,000m.

- FCF yield = £220m ÷ £5,000m = **4.4%** (a price-to-FCF of about **23×**).

For a business compounding intrinsic value at a high rate with rock-solid cash,
Smith would argue that's **acceptable** — you're paying up for genuine quality.
But watch what happens if enthusiasm pushes the price to £14,500m:

- FCF yield = £220m ÷ £14,500m = **1.5%** (a price-to-FCF of about **66×**).

Now you're **overpaying.** Same wonderful company, but at 66× your future return
is crushed regardless of how good the business is.

*That* is the tension between "buy good companies" and "don't overpay" — and step
3 exists precisely to stop you from paying the 1.5%-yield price.

### Part 3 — A real holding Smith has owned

To see "don't overpay ≠ only buy cheap," look at **Novo Nordisk**, which Smith
discussed in 2025: a **gross margin of 85%, operating margin 44%, return on
capital 69%**, growing revenue around **25%** — on a **P/E of about 28**, which
gave a **~27% FCF yield on his original cost** as earnings grew into the price.
Even at a "high" P/E, the sheer quality and growth made it attractive. The lesson:
a top-tier compounder can justify a premium multiple; a mediocre one cannot.

<Callout type="warning">
The single most common beginner error is assuming a great company is *always* a
great stock. It isn't. Price is what protects (or destroys) your return after
you've found quality.
</Callout>

## Where this sits in the bigger picture

Smith's "quality" approach is the hands-on version of something academics have
measured and named the **quality factor** (also called the **profitability
factor**). Two key papers:

- **Robert Novy-Marx (2013), "The Other Side of Value: The Gross Profitability
  Premium"** — showed that highly profitable companies have, historically,
  delivered higher returns.
- **Asness, Frazzini & Pedersen, "Quality Minus Junk" (QMJ)** — showed that
  high-quality stocks (profitable, growing, safe, well-managed) have earned higher
  *risk-adjusted* returns than "junk."

In other words, Smith is doing by hand, with judgment, what these researchers
documented with data: tilting hard toward quality has paid off over the long run.

## Honest criticisms and limitations

No strategy wins every year. Be clear-eyed about where this one hurts.

- **It's not all-weather.** Quality and growth shone from 2010–2020, but **2022
  was Fundsmith Equity's worst year ever (−13.8%, vs the MSCI World's −7.8%)** as
  value, energy, and rate-sensitive cyclicals led and high-multiple compounders
  de-rated.
- **Sell-decision misses.** Smith has publicly called **selling Amazon his
  "worst" move** — the stock rose roughly 74% afterward. He also exited PayPal,
  Intuit, and Meta after losses, drawing scrutiny over his timing.
- **A real failure to learn from.** His **Fundsmith Emerging Equities Trust (FEET)
  went into voluntary liquidation in 2022** after poor performance (~27% total
  return over its life) — evidence the quality formula doesn't transplant cleanly
  into emerging markets.
- **Valuation is the fuzzy part.** Critics argue Fundsmith holds richly-priced
  staples and tech (its FCF yield runs at a premium to the market) and rarely
  sells on valuation, which hurts when multiples compress.
- **"Expensive defensives."** When everyone crowds into the same high-quality
  compounders, future returns can be mediocre even if the businesses stay
  excellent.
- **Fees are a permanent drag.** Critics note a low-cost quality ETF can capture
  much of the same factor far more cheaply than an actively managed fund.

<Callout type="note">
Smith's own defence is that all of this is the *price of the discipline*: you
accept years of underperformance and a narrow style in exchange for owning
genuinely great businesses through a full cycle. Whether that trade-off suits you
depends on your patience and time horizon.
</Callout>

## Common beginner pitfalls

<Callout type="warning">
1. **Buying "quality" at any price.** The cardinal error. A great company isn't
   automatically a great stock — always do the FCF-yield/valuation step.
2. **Mistaking high ROE for high ROCE.** A company drowning in debt can post a
   flashy *return on equity* while its *return on capital employed* is mediocre.
   Use capital **employed** (equity + debt) and check the debt isn't doing the
   work — Smith's portfolio carries ~27× interest cover precisely to avoid this.
3. **Trusting reported profit over cash.** The whole *Accounting for Growth*
   point: confirm free cash flow tracks net income (aim near 100%). Profit can be
   massaged; cash is harder to fake.
4. **Confusing fast growth with quality.** A 30%-growth company burning cash at
   8% ROCE is *not* a Smith company.
5. **Judging on one good year.** Quality means *sustained* high returns across a
   full cycle, not one peak year.
6. **Over-trading / abandoning "do nothing."** The strategy's edge *is*
   inactivity (Fundsmith turns over ~3% a year). Selling a sound compounder on a
   20% dip is the classic mistake.
7. **Buying excluded sectors because they look "cheap."** Banks, airlines, and
   miners are off-limits in this framework for structural reasons, not snobbery.
8. **Over-diversifying into junk.** Padding the portfolio with 50+ mediocre names
   to "feel safe" dilutes away the quality edge. There are only so many great
   businesses.
</Callout>

## Putting it to work

A beginner can actually run a stripped-down version of this:

1. **Build a watchlist** of businesses you understand that sell everyday, repeat
   products. Pull each one's **ROCE** and **free cash flow vs. net profit** for
   the last 5–10 years (these are in the annual report or on most data sites).
2. **Keep only** the ones with consistently high ROCE (aim toward Smith's
   ~25–30%+) *and* cash that tracks profit. Cross off anything in the excluded
   sectors.
3. **Check the price** with the FCF yield. Don't chase a 1.5% yield just because
   you love the company; wait for a fairer entry.
4. **Concentrate** into your best ~20–30 ideas — or, if that's too much work, use
   a low-cost quality ETF to get the factor cheaply.
5. **Then do nothing.** Re-read your reasons for owning each company once a year.
   Sell only if the *business* breaks, not when the *price* dips.

The strategy is genuinely simple to state and genuinely hard to follow — which,
Smith would say, is exactly why it keeps working.
