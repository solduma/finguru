## Why Keynes belongs in an investing course

Most people know **John Maynard Keynes** as the economist who reshaped how
governments fight recessions. Fewer know that he was also a real, money-on-the-line
investor — and one of the best of his generation. He ran an insurance company's
board, managed a Cambridge college's endowment for two decades, traded his own
account, and died wealthy.

What makes him worth a whole lesson is not just that he succeeded. It's *how* he
succeeded: he started with the strategy that feels most natural to a brilliant
mind — *"I understand the economy, so I'll predict the market"* — and it **nearly
bankrupted him twice.** Only after abandoning that approach and switching to
something humbler and slower did he produce the record that endures. Warren
Buffett and George Soros have both pointed to Keynes the investor admiringly.

That arc — from confident, leveraged market-timer to patient business-owner — is
one of the most useful stories a beginner can absorb. It is a warning and a method
in one.

<Callout type="key">
Keynes left investing two ideas everyone should know — the **"beauty contest"**
(prices are a vote on other people's opinions) and **"animal spirits"** (markets
run on emotion). But his most personal lesson is the one his own track record
teaches: **even a genius could not reliably time markets, yet he could profit by
patiently owning a few cheap, sound businesses he understood deeply.**
</Callout>

## Who he was

**John Maynard Keynes** (June 5, 1883 – April 21, 1946), later **1st Baron
Keynes**, was an English economist, almost universally regarded as the most
influential of the 20th century — often called the "father of macroeconomics." He
took a first-class degree in mathematics at King's College, Cambridge (1904),
moved in the artistic **Bloomsbury Group**, and shaped world events directly: he
quit the British delegation to the 1919 Versailles peace conference in protest and
wrote the blistering bestseller *The Economic Consequences of the Peace*, and in
1944 he led the British delegation at the **Bretton Woods** conference, where he
proposed a world reserve currency (the "bancor") and an International Clearing
Union — the rival U.S. plan ultimately prevailed.

But for our purposes, the crucial fact is this: Keynes was a **practicing
investor**, not just a theorist.

- He chaired the board of the **National Mutual** life-assurance company (board
  member from 1919, chairman 1921–1938).
- From the early 1920s until his death in 1946 he ran the discretionary endowment
  of **King's College, Cambridge** — a portfolio known as the **"Chest" fund** —
  as the college's **First Bursar.**
- He traded his own account aggressively, including in the new forward foreign
  exchange (FX) market.

Despite heavy charitable giving and **two near-ruinous setbacks**, he died wealthy:
his net worth at death in 1946 was **just short of £500,000** — a large fortune for
the era.

<Callout type="note">
An **endowment** is a long-term investment pool that funds an institution (here, a
Cambridge college) in perpetuity. As **bursar**, Keynes was the officer in charge
of the college's finances — so he had a real, multi-decade mandate to invest other
people's money, with results that were later carefully reconstructed by academics
(Chambers & Dimson). That long-horizon, patient-capital mandate matters: it is
exactly the setting in which his mature method works best.
</Callout>

## The arc that matters: from timer to owner

Keynes's investing life splits cleanly into two phases, and the break between them
is the whole lesson.

<ConceptDiagram
  caption="Keynes's investing career in one picture: he began as a top-down macro market-timer, nearly went broke twice (the 1920 currency bets and the 1929 crash), and then renounced timing in favour of bottom-up, concentrated value investing — the approach that produced his durable record."

  B --> C[1929 crash:\nfailed to foresee it,\nassets nearly destroyed again]
  C --> D{Hard lesson:\ntiming the cycle\nis too hard, even for me}
  D --> E[Phase 2:\nBottom-up VALUE\nbuy cheap, sound businesses,\nconcentrate, hold]
  E --> F[Durable\nlong-run\noutperformance]`}
/>

**Phase 1 — the macro timer (1920s).** Keynes believed his grasp of the business
cycle and central-bank policy gave him an edge. He traded currencies and
commodities, trying to anticipate the *credit cycle* — the boom-and-bust rhythm of
borrowing and lending — and he traded on **fundamentals**, his read of where
economies were headed, not on momentum. He used **leverage** (borrowed money) to
amplify his bets. It nearly destroyed him **twice**: his leveraged currency
speculation in the spring of **1920** moved violently against him and he had to be
bailed out before the positions later recovered, and he **failed to foresee the
1929 crash**, which "nearly wiped out" his personal fortune. (His father helped him
financially after both setbacks.)

<Callout type="warning">
The *pattern* — two near-wipeouts from leveraged macro timing (1920 and 1929) — is
well documented. A precise pound figure for the 1920 currency loss is **not**
reliably available from accessible sources, so treat any specific number you see
for it as unconfirmed. The lesson lives in the pattern, not the decimal.
</Callout>

**Phase 2 — the value investor (from roughly 1932).** Chastened, Keynes concluded
that predicting the cycle's turns — what he called "credit cycling" — was too hard
to do reliably, *even for one of the great economists of the century.* He flipped
to the opposite approach: stop guessing the market, and instead **buy a few
clearly-undervalued businesses and hold them patiently.** He arrived at value
investing largely independently, around the same time Benjamin Graham was
formalizing it. This is the approach that produced his strong long-run record —
and it is the one a beginner can copy.

## His two big ideas (and why they matter to you)

Before the method, you need the two concepts Keynes gave to finance. They explain
*why* markets misbehave — which is exactly what a value investor exploits. Both
come from the same chapter: **Chapter 12, "The State of Long-Term Expectation," of
*The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money* (1936).**

### The "beauty contest": prices are a vote, not a measurement

Keynes compared the stock market to a newspaper contest popular in his day: readers
pick the six prettiest faces from 100 photographs, and **you win if your picks
match the most popular faces** across all entrants.

The naïve player (1st degree) picks the faces *they* find prettiest. The smarter
player (2nd degree) picks the faces *they think the crowd will pick.* But everyone
reasons that way — so you end up choosing based on **"what average opinion expects
the average opinion to be"** (3rd degree). Keynes noted that some players push to
the fourth, fifth, and higher degrees.

<ConceptDiagram
  caption="The beauty-contest logic, leveled up. A naïve investor asks what THEY think is best. A smart one asks what the CROWD thinks. The truly self-aware investor knows everyone is doing that — so short-run price becomes a hall of mirrors of expectations about expectations. Keynes's point: short-run price is a popularity vote, not a measurement of worth."
  chart={`flowchart TB
  A[1st degree:\nWhat do I think\nis undervalued?] --> B[2nd degree:\nWhat does the\ncrowd think?]
  B --> C[3rd degree:\nWhat does the crowd\nthink the crowd thinks?]
  C --> D[...higher degrees]
  D --> E[Short-run price =\na vote on opinion,\nNOT intrinsic worth]`}
/>

**The takeaway for you:** in the *short run*, a stock's price is a popularity
contest about what other people will think — not a measurement of what the
business is worth. That gap between *price* (the vote) and *value* (the worth) is
the value investor's entire opportunity. Note carefully: the beauty contest is
Keynes's *description of speculation*, not his *recommendation*. His own money went
the other way.

### "Animal spirits": markets run on emotion

Also from Chapter 12: Keynes argued that big decisions about an uncertain future
"can only be taken as a result of **animal spirits** — of a spontaneous urge to
action rather than inaction," and *not* as "the outcome of a weighted average of
quantitative benefits multiplied by quantitative probabilities."

In plain English: when the future is genuinely unknowable, people act on **mood
and confidence**, not on a tidy spreadsheet of probabilities. Confidence drives
booms; when confidence evaporates, markets seize up. This makes markets inherently
unstable — and it is the seed of modern **behavioral economics** (the study of how
psychology, not pure logic, drives financial decisions). Akerlof and Shiller
revived the exact phrase in their 2009 book *Animal Spirits.*

<Callout type="warning">
**Do not attribute "the market can stay irrational longer than you can stay
solvent" to Keynes.** It is one of the most famous "Keynes quotes" on the internet,
and it is **apocryphal** (falsely attributed). Quote investigators trace it to the
financial analyst **A. Gary Shilling** — earliest known around 1986, widely cited
from his *Forbes* column on February 15, 1993. Keynes wasn't credited with it until
1999, over 50 years after his death. (The confusion may come from a genuinely
Keynes-attributed but separate line: *"There is nothing so disastrous as a rational
investment policy in an irrational world."*) A safe, documented Keynes line is far
humbler: *"When my information changes, I alter my conclusions."*
</Callout>

There's a productive tension here worth naming: Keynes's *theory* says markets are
ruled by mass psychology that's hard to beat — yet Keynes the investor *did* beat
them. The resolution is that his beauty-contest insight described the **short run**
(the noisy vote), while his value method bet on the **long run** (worth eventually
reasserting itself).

## The full method — what to actually do

This is Keynes's **mature value approach** (roughly post-1932), reconstructed from
his King's College memoranda and letters. Read it as *"what he did once he stopped
trying to time the market."* Each step is followable by a beginner.

### 1. Decide whether you're speculating or investing — then pick investing

Keynes drew a sharp line between **speculation** (guessing short-term crowd
psychology — the beauty contest) and **enterprise** (judging the long-term earning
power of an asset). He concluded, from painful experience, that he was **bad at the
former and good at the latter.** Step one is to make the same honest choice: pick
the investing game, not the guessing game.

### 2. Buy businesses, not tickers — and demand a margin of safety

Estimate what a company is actually *worth* from its earnings and dividends (its
**intrinsic value**), and only buy when the **price is clearly below that
estimate.** The cushion between the lower price you pay and the higher value you
estimate is the **margin of safety** — your protection against being wrong. (This
is the same value logic Benjamin Graham was developing in the same era; Keynes
arrived alongside Graham rather than from him — credit Graham for the formal
doctrine of "intrinsic value / margin of safety.")

### 3. Favor income and durable earning power

Keynes leaned toward stocks whose **dividend yield and earning power** were
attractive relative to a cheap price — including out-of-favor, smaller and
mid-sized companies the crowd was ignoring, and (unusually for a British investor
of his day) significant holdings **abroad**, including US stocks and preferred
shares. A *dividend* is the cash a company pays shareholders; a high dividend yield
on a beaten-down price means **you get paid to wait.**

<Callout type="note">
The specific *character* of his holdings — abroad-heavy, US stocks, preferred
shares, smaller and out-of-favour companies — comes from the academic literature on
his portfolio (Chambers & Dimson) rather than a line-by-line holdings list. Take it
as the documented *shape* of his book.
</Callout>

### 4. Concentrate in your best ideas

Rather than spreading money thinly across dozens of names, Keynes held a **small
number of large positions** in companies he understood and believed were cheap. He
argued it is a mistake to think you reduce risk by spreading money into things you
know little about; better to put meaningful money into a few "favourites" you can
actually explain.

<Callout type="warning">
This is the single most dangerous step to copy blindly. Concentration worked for
Keynes because it sat on top of deep valuation work — and it *still* didn't save
him from near-ruin in his timing years. For most beginners, broad diversification
is the safer default. See Criticisms below.
</Callout>

### 5. Diversify across *opposed risks*, not just across many names

Keynes refined "diversification" beyond "own lots of stuff." He valued holding
assets "likely to move in **opposite directions** when there are general
fluctuations" — an early, intuitive grasp of what we now call **correlation-based
diversification.** A handful of holdings that zig when others zag protects you far
better than a hundred holdings that all rise and fall together. His heavy use of
overseas assets was partly this idea in action: don't bet everything on one
country's fortunes.

<ConceptDiagram
  caption="Two ways to 'diversify.' Owning many holdings that all move together (left) barely cuts risk — when the market drops, everything drops. Holding a few assets whose returns are negatively correlated (right) — they tend to move in opposite directions — smooths the whole portfolio. Keynes prized the second kind, plus heavy overseas exposure."

### 6. Hold for the long term — accept paper losses "with equanimity"

Once he owned a cheap, sound business, Keynes intended to hold it through
volatility — "through thick and thin" — rather than trade in and out. In a 1938
memorandum he wrote that it is **"the duty of the serious investor to accept the
depreciation of his holdings with equanimity"** and that doing otherwise is
"anti-social, destructive of confidence, and incompatible with the working of the
economic system." This patience is the mechanism that lets the beauty-contest
*noise* wash out so intrinsic *value* can reassert itself.

<Callout type="note">
"Steadfastness" assumes your **original thesis was sound.** Accepting paper losses
"with equanimity" is discipline only when the business is still cheap and healthy.
Holding a *broken* thesis isn't equanimity — it's denial. See pitfall 4.
</Callout>

### 7. Be a contrarian — use the crowd's mood against it

Because prices are a beauty contest driven by animal spirits, the long-term
investor's edge is **buying when others are fearful** and the price has detached
from value — and tolerating looking wrong in the short run. He "bought into
pessimism." Again: the beauty contest *describes* how prices form; it is **not** a
call to chase the crowd.

### 8. Don't try to time the macro cycle — and respect leverage

This step is the lesson Keynes paid for twice. He gave up "credit cycling" because
even his economic forecasts weren't reliable enough to trade on. And the
**leverage** he used in his speculative phase amplified both his gains and his
near-wipeouts — it is *how* he nearly went broke. The honest framing for a
beginner: your macro opinions are probably not an edge, and borrowed money turns a
wrong opinion into a catastrophe.

> **One-line summary:** Don't time the market; buy a few clearly-undervalued,
> dividend-paying businesses you understand, hold them patiently, and be willing to
> look wrong while the crowd catches up — and never borrow to do it.

## Worked example 1 — the power of a durable edge

Managing the King's College Chest fund over **1921–1946**, Keynes earned roughly
**16.0% per year versus about 10.4% for the broad UK equity market** — an
outperformance the *Journal of Economic Perspectives* summarized as **roughly 8
percentage points per year** over the quarter-century.

<Callout type="note">
These figures come from the modern academic study by **Chambers, Dimson & Foo**
(and JEP summaries of it). They are well-corroborated across sources but rest on
that body of work — treat them as authoritative-but-secondary, not a number you
re-derived. Note also the arithmetic: 16.0 − 10.4 = **5.6 percentage points** of
simple annual spread. You'll sometimes see his edge quoted as ~7.7% or "6–8%/yr,"
reflecting a different return basis (e.g. total vs. price returns). The safe way to
state it is *"he beat the market by roughly 6–8 percentage points a year."* Don't
write 16.0 − 10.4 = 7.7.
</Callout>

That "small" annual edge compounds into something enormous over 25 years:

- Keynes at 16.0%/yr → 1.16²⁵ ≈ **41×** your money.
- Market at 10.4%/yr → 1.104²⁵ ≈ **11.9×** your money.

The lesson isn't "aim for 16%." It's that a **modest, durable edge held for a long
time** matters far more than any single hot year.

## Worked example 3 — the beauty contest and margin of safety in numbers

Now the mechanics of a single Keynesian buy. *(Illustrative numbers.)*

Suppose a company earns **£1.00 per share** and pays a **£0.60 dividend.** You
judge a fair multiple of 15× earnings, so your **intrinsic value estimate is £15.**
Then "animal spirits" turn fearful and the quoted price falls to **£9** — a 6.7%
dividend yield (£0.60 ÷ £9).

- **The Keynesian move:** £9 sits well below your £15 estimate — a **40% margin of
  safety** (£15 − £9 = £6 cushion, which is 40% of £15). You buy for the income and
  the eventual re-rating, and you hold.
- **If price later returns to £15,** that's a **+67% capital gain** (£6 ÷ £9), *plus*
  the dividends you collected while waiting.
- **The beauty-contest warning:** the crowd's vote can stay negative for a while —
  price might fall to **£7 first** before recovering. Keynes's answer was *accept
  the depreciation with equanimity, stay concentrated in your best ideas* — not
  panic, and emphatically **not** the apocryphal "stay solvent" line.

## Honest criticisms and limitations

A good lesson on Keynes must be as candid about the holes as he was about his own
mistakes.

- **A sample of one.** One brilliant person beating the market for 25 years over
  one window doesn't prove the method generalizes. It's an inspiring data point, not
  statistical proof. Modern analysis even debates how much of his edge was *skill*
  versus a tilt toward the **value and small-size factors** — well-documented
  sources of historical return a mechanical rule could have captured — and a
  favourable era for contrarian small/mid-cap buying.
- **He failed at his first strategy — badly.** The leveraged macro timing he began
  with nearly ruined him **twice (1920, 1929).** Half of "Keynes the investor" is
  what *didn't* work.
- **Leverage hid the risk.** His use of margin amplified results, and a smooth
  long-run return series can understate how close he came to ruin; his drawdowns
  were severe (notably around 1929–1930). The exact peak-to-trough percentages live
  in the full academic papers, which I could not open directly.
- **A different era, different rules.** The 1920s–40s had far less disclosure and
  competition, and Keynes — as a famous economist and college bursar with a
  long-horizon mandate — had access and patience ordinary investors don't.
- **Concentration is not universally endorsed.** His "few favourites" stance
  directly conflicts with the broad diversification most modern finance recommends
  for non-experts.
- **"Animal spirits" is descriptive, not a strategy.** It explains *why* markets
  misbehave; it does not tell you what to buy.
- **Theory vs. practice.** His own beauty-contest theory says markets are driven by
  hard-to-beat mass psychology — yet he beat it. That tension is real; don't smooth
  it over.

## Common beginner pitfalls

<Callout type="warning">
1. **Misquoting him.** "The market can stay irrational longer than you can stay
   solvent" is **A. Gary Shilling's**, not Keynes's.
2. **Confusing the two Keyneses.** Beginners cite the "beauty contest" (a
   *description* of speculation) as if it were his *strategy.* His actual strategy
   was the opposite: ignore the contest and buy long-term value. The beauty contest
   is not advice to herd — Keynes's own money was **contrarian.**
3. **Copying the concentration without the analysis.** Putting everything into 3–5
   stocks is how amateurs blow up. Keynes did it backed by deep valuation work — and
   still nearly went broke earlier.
4. **Mistaking conviction for stubbornness.** "Accept depreciation with equanimity"
   assumes your original thesis was sound. Holding a *broken* thesis is denial, not
   steadfastness.
5. **Trying to time the macro cycle.** Even Keynes couldn't. Beginners with far less
   information should be especially humble here.
6. **Using leverage to "be like Keynes."** Margin is exactly what nearly wiped him
   out — twice.
7. **Treating 16% as a promise.** It's a historical, single-account, possibly
   factor-driven result from a different era — not a repeatable target.
8. **Confusing him with Graham or Buffett.** He's adjacent to and influenced value
   investing, but Graham formalized "intrinsic value / margin of safety." Credit
   accurately.
</Callout>

## Putting it to work

You don't "trade Keynes" with a button. You internalize a posture:

- **Separate price from value.** Before any buy, write down what you think the
  business is *worth* and why. If you can't, you're voting in a beauty contest, not
  investing.
- **Demand a discount.** Only buy with a real margin of safety — a price clearly
  below your value estimate — so being somewhat wrong still leaves you whole.
- **Expect the crowd to be moody.** Animal spirits mean your good buy can get
  *cheaper* before it works. Plan to be patient and a little lonely, and to accept
  paper losses with equanimity.
- **Diversify the smart way.** Prefer holdings whose risks are *opposed*, and don't
  bet everything on one country — but diversify *more* than Keynes did unless you've
  truly done the work, because concentration is a power tool, not a starter tool.
- **Be honest about timing and leverage.** The parts of Keynes that nearly
  bankrupted him are the parts to leave on the shelf.

The deepest takeaway is the arc itself: a man brilliant enough to redesign global
economics could *not* reliably outguess the market — but he *could* get rich slowly
by buying good businesses cheap and waiting. If that was true for Keynes, it's a
safe bet for the rest of us.

## Further reading

- John Maynard Keynes, *The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money*
  (1936) — read **Chapter 12, "The State of Long-Term Expectation"** for the beauty
  contest and animal spirits.
- Chambers, Dimson & Foo, "Keynes the Stock Market Investor" (2013/2015) — the
  modern quantitative study of his actual record, his pivot to value, and his use

  standard life, covering his investing in depth.
