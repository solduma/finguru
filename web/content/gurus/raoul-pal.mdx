## The one idea to take away

Most beginners try to value a stock or read a chart. Raoul Pal pulls the camera
all the way back and asks one question first: **which way is money flowing
through the whole financial system?**

His claim is blunt — **the tide of global liquidity (the total amount of money
and credit available in the world) is the single biggest driver of asset
prices.** And because the world is buried in debt, governments and central banks
*must* keep creating new liquidity to refinance it — which quietly **debases**
(devalues) currency at a pace Pal pegs near **11% a year.** So his real question
isn't "did my asset go up?" It's **"did my asset beat the ~11% debasement
hurdle?"** Anything that didn't — cash, low-yield savings — silently lost
purchasing power even if the number on the statement went up.

This lesson teaches that framework end to end, with the concrete numbers, so a
motivated beginner can actually follow it — including the parts where it can fail
you.

## Who he is

**Raoul Pal** (born 1968 in the UK, to an Indian father and a Dutch mother) is a
former hedge-fund manager turned macro researcher and financial educator. He read
**Economics and Law at the University of Plymouth** (graduating 1990).

- **Goldman Sachs (~1997–2001):** He co-ran the firm's **hedge-fund sales
  business in equities and equity derivatives across Europe** — i.e. he sold
  ideas and products to the biggest professional speculators. Note this was a
  **sales/distribution** role, not a star prop-trading seat; it matters when we
  reach the criticisms.
- **GLG Partners / Man Group (~2001–2004):** He went on to **co-manage the GLG
  Global Macro Fund.**
- **"Retired" at ~36 (around 2004):** He stopped managing outside clients' money
  and relocated to the coast of Spain to run his own research.

He then built the two things he is famous for:

- **Global Macro Investor (GMI), founded 2005** — a high-end, subscription
  **research letter** sold to professional and institutional investors
  (reportedly around $40,000/year). This is his flagship analytical product and
  the real paper trail of his method.
- **Real Vision, launched 2014** — a subscription **financial-media and education
  platform** built around long-form video interviews with investors ("the Netflix
  of finance"), with **Real Vision Crypto** added in 2020. Pal is its **CEO and
  co-founder.**

<Callout type="note">
Real Vision launched in **2014**, with Pal as CEO and co-founder. The exact
composition of the founding team is **contested in public sources.** **Grant
Williams** is most reliably documented as an **early on-air host and partner**
who fronted much of the original content and **stepped away around 2019**; some
secondary outlets also list him — alongside **Daniel Horner** and **Remi Tetot**
— as a co-founder, but that fuller account rests on weaker, undated reporting.
The honest summary: Williams was clearly central to Real Vision's early days, but
the **structural-macro method behind GMI is Pal's** — treat Williams as
host/partner, not the architect of the framework you're about to learn.
</Callout>

The thread tying it all together: Pal is **media- and report-first, not an
academic.** There is no peer-reviewed Pal paper to cite. His ideas live in GMI
reports, Real Vision videos, and public talks — which matters when we get to
sourcing and to the conflicts of interest at the end.

## The core idea, in plain language

Pal's unifying thesis is nicknamed **"The Everything Code."** Here it is in four
steps:

1. **The world is buried in debt.** Government + corporate + household debt has
   grown huge relative to the size of the economy.
2. **The system can't be allowed to deleverage.** If all that debt had to be
   repaid or written off at once, the economy would collapse. So policymakers
   won't allow it.
3. **So every time debt must be rolled over, they add liquidity.** Lower interest
   rates, **quantitative easing** (central banks creating new money to buy
   bonds — "QE"), and deficit spending all inject money to keep the debt
   serviceable. A side effect is **currency debasement** — each unit of currency
   slowly buys less, at a rate Pal puts around **~11% per year.**
4. **That makes the cycle repeat on a rhythm.** Because debt rolls over on a
   fairly regular schedule, liquidity has to rise on a fairly regular schedule —
   which makes the **business cycle near-repeat about every 3.5–4 years**, and
   makes **liquidity a leading indicator** for stocks, crypto, commodities, and
   property.

The beginner's takeaway is two sentences: **don't fight the liquidity tide —
work out which way it's flowing, then own the assets that ride it hardest. And
measure success against the ~11% hurdle, not against zero.**

<Callout type="key">
"Liquidity" here is bigger than "the Fed." It's *global* money and credit — the
US plus Europe, Japan, and China. Asset prices respond to the **direction and
acceleration** of that global pool, not to any one central bank in isolation.
</Callout>

## The full method, as steps a beginner can follow

This is the part the reviewer cares about most: not *what* the idea is, but
*how you actually do it.* Pal's process runs as a loop, and the logic flows in
one direction: **business cycle → predicts liquidity → ISM confirms momentum →
liquidity drives prices → rotate up the risk curve, then back down.**

### Step 1 — Anchor on the business cycle (the "master predictor")

Pal calls the business cycle the master predictor — he says it "does an epic job
of calling the turn" in both Fed liquidity and global money supply. He uses an
observed **~3.5-to-4-year cycle**: central banks tend to re-expand liquidity
roughly every 3.5–4 years to refinance maturing government debt (the **4-year
debt-refinancing cycle**). Ask: are we in the **early upswing, the mid-cycle, the
late-cycle melt-up, or the contraction?**

### Step 2 — Track liquidity with two gauges, watched *separately*

Pal does not blend everything into one number. He watches two series, because
they can diverge:

- **Global M2 money supply** (year-over-year %) — broad money across the major
  economies (cash, deposits, etc.),
- **Central-bank / Fed balance sheet** (year-over-year %).

His own proprietary blend is the **GMI Total Liquidity Index.** You don't have
access to that, but free proxies get a beginner most of the way: public **global
M2 charts**, the published **Fed balance sheet**, and "net liquidity" series that
circulate online.

<Callout type="note">
There is **no single agreed definition** of "global liquidity," and GMI's exact
recipe is proprietary and not publicly confirmable. Watch the two gauges
separately, treat any one chart as an *estimate*, and confirm the trend across
several — more on this in the pitfalls.
</Callout>

### Step 3 — Confirm momentum with the ISM Manufacturing Index

Liquidity tells you the tide; the **ISM Manufacturing Index** tells you whether
growth is actually accelerating right now. **Rising ISM = growth speeding up =
green light to add risk.** Pal times rotation into the riskiest assets (e.g.
crypto altcoins) to *follow* an improving ISM, not to front-run it on hope alone.

### Step 4 — Use liquidity as a *leading* indicator

Changes in liquidity tend to **lead** risk-asset prices by a few months. Pal
watches the **rate of change** (is liquidity *accelerating* or *decelerating*?),
not just the level. When the trend turns up, lean into risk; when it rolls over,
de-risk.

### Step 5 — Classify every asset by the ~11% debasement hurdle

This is the test at the heart of the method. Sort everything you own by whether
it can plausibly **beat ~11% a year**:

| Bucket | Examples | Verdict |
|--------|----------|---------|
| **Beats debasement** | Bitcoin & blue-chip crypto / major layer-1s, high-beta tech, select growth stocks | Builds real wealth |
| **Merely preserves** | Gold, real estate, most bonds | Treads water |
| **Gets debased** | Cash, low-yield savings | Loses ~11%/yr in real terms |

The provocation: a "safe" savings account paying 4% is, in Pal's framing,
**losing** purchasing power every year, because 4% &lt; 11%.

### Step 6 — Pick the highest-beta expression of the trend

Different assets respond to liquidity with different intensity. "Beta to
liquidity" just means *how hard does this asset move for a given move in
liquidity.* Pal's rough ranking, from most sensitive to least:

> **Bitcoin / crypto  >  Nasdaq / tech  >  broad equities  >  gold  >  bonds**

Once you have a view on the tide's direction, express it through the asset whose
sensitivity matches how much risk you can stomach.

### Step 7 — Set positioning by the liquidity *regime*

Pal maps positioning onto bands of global-liquidity year-over-year growth. The
signal is the **rate of change of liquidity**, not the price chart:

| Liquidity regime (global YoY) | Stance | Tilt toward |
|-------------------------------|--------|-------------|
| **Rapid expansion (`>+15%`)** | Maximum risk-on | Crypto, tech, emerging markets |
| **Expansion (+5% to +15%)** | Risk-on | Growth equities, Bitcoin |
| **Stable (−5% to +5%)** | Neutral | Balanced |
| **Contraction (−15% to −5%)** | Risk-off | Cash, short-duration bonds |
| **Rapid contraction (`<−15%`)** | Defensive | Treasuries, USD, gold |

### Step 8 — Overlay the structural theme: the "Exponential Age"

On *top* of the cycle, Pal tilts toward technology and network-effect assets
(tech equities, crypto). His **"Exponential Age"** thesis argues these compound
faster than the overall economy because adoption (crypto + AI) follows
exponential, not linear, curves. So the cyclical regime tells you *when*; the
structural theme tells you *what to overweight* across cycles.

### Step 9 — Size, hold, and respect the "Banana Zone"

This is **position/swing investing on a multi-month-to-multi-year horizon**, not
day trading. The point of the framework is to **stay invested in
debasement-beating assets through the cycle** — sizing up in expansions and
de-risking in contractions. The **"Banana Zone"** is Pal's nickname for the
explosive late-cycle melt-up, when rising liquidity plus debasement send the
high-beta assets parabolic. The plan: **be positioned *before* it, take profits
*into* it** — never chase it after it has gone vertical. The cardinal rule:
**do not over-leverage the volatile, high-beta legs.** The same sensitivity that
delivers the upside delivers the drawdown.

## A worked example — the debasement hurdle test

This is the calculation that defines the whole framework. *(Round-number
illustration of Pal's logic, not his audited values.)*

Suppose you hold **$100,000 in a savings account paying 4%.** A year later you
have **$104,000** — feels like a gain.

Now apply Pal's **~11% debasement hurdle** (roughly ~8% global liquidity growth +
~3% inflation):

- Purchasing power needed *just to break even*: `$100,000 × 1.11 = $111,000`.
- Your real result: `$104,000 − $111,000 = −$7,000`, i.e. **about −6.3% in real
  (purchasing-power) terms.**

The "safe" account **lost** roughly 6% of real wealth — it was *debased.* To
merely tread water you needed an 11% nominal return; to build wealth you needed
an asset that beats 11%. That gap is Pal's entire pitch for owning Bitcoin and
tech instead of cash.

## The key sources to learn it from

Because Pal is media-first, his "books and papers" are mostly reports and videos:

- **Global Macro Investor (GMI)** — the institutional research letter, **since
  2005.** Subscription-only and expensive; the real record of his actual method,
  but not public.
- **"The End Game"** — a famous, deeply bearish GMI presentation (**2012**, widely
  circulated via Business Insider) arguing the global debt system faced an
  unavoidable reckoning. *Worth knowing because the dramatic near-term collapse it
  implied did not play out on schedule — see criticisms.*
- **"The Exponential Age"** — his structural technology-adoption thesis (crypto +
  AI), popularized from **~2021 onward.**
- **"The Everything Code"** — his unifying liquidity-debasement-cycle thesis/book
  (in progress, promoted via raoulpal.com), where the **11% hurdle** and the
  **4-year liquidity cycle** are the core. *Verify it has actually been published
  before citing it as a finished book.*
- **Real Vision** — the interview/education platform, **launched 2014**, with
  Real Vision Crypto added in 2020.

<Callout type="note">
There is **no peer-reviewed paper** for Raoul Pal. His biography rests on profile
pages and reporting, and his methodology lives behind paywalls. You're learning a
*practitioner's framework*, not an academically vetted model.
</Callout>

## Honest criticisms and limitations

A complete lesson has to be fair about where this can fail you.

- **Forecast timing is repeatedly off, often too bullish.** Critics note targets
  "arrive later than projected," and the 2012 **"End Game"** deflationary-collapse
  call did not materialize as framed.
- **Specific calls aged badly.** Pal **promoted Dogecoin** (admitted holding it,
  Aug 2021), described **Terra/Luna as effectively "risk-free" (Nov 2021)** shortly
  before it collapsed to zero, and floated very high ETH targets ("no reason ETH
  shouldn't be at $10,000"). Real Vision Crypto's 2020 launch sponsors **BlockFi
  and Silvergate both later went bankrupt.**
- **A built-in conflict of interest.** A 2023 **Protos** investigation argued Real
  Vision's crypto content drifted from journalism toward promotion after Grant
  Williams left (~2019), with disclosures of Pal's own token holdings described as
  **"sporadic and seemingly arbitrary."** Discount promotional framing of price
  targets accordingly.
- **Single-variable risk.** A liquidity-only lens **ignores political risk,
  regulatory shocks, valuation, and behavioral extremes.**
- **Correlation, not proven causation — and the 11% is disputed.** Independent
  analysis (VantMacro) found a high R² between liquidity and the Nasdaq (≈0.65–0.68)
  but warns this largely reflects **shared long-term trends, not proven
  causation**; and some argue the true debasement rate is closer to **5–7%, not
  11%.** With no agreed definition of "global liquidity," different indices can
  even disagree on which way the tide is flowing.

- **Extreme concentration.** Pal has said he holds the large majority of his liquid
  net worth in crypto — a level of concentration most beginners cannot stomach
  (Bitcoin has had **80%+ drawdowns multiple times**).
- **No transparent track record.** He retired young from a hedge-fund *sales*/PM
  role; the public, audited long-run performance of his personal strategy is not
  documented. The brand is the research and media, not a verifiable fund record.

<Callout type="warning">
A framework that can explain any outcome after the fact gives you **no way to know
when you're wrong.** Before you act on a liquidity view, write down in advance what
would prove the thesis *broken* — and act on that line if price crosses it.
</Callout>

## Common beginner pitfalls

<Callout type="warning">
1. **Treating macro narratives as trade signals.** Pal himself frames GMI as a
   "big-picture roadmap, not short-term trade calls." Beginners over-trade on
   headline themes.
2. **Skipping the de-risk half.** People internalize "max risk-on / Banana Zone"
   and ignore the **contraction rules** that say go to cash/Treasuries when
   liquidity rolls over.
3. **Copying the concentration.** Mimicking "80%+ in crypto" without the net worth,
   time horizon, or stomach to survive an 80% drawdown.
4. **Chasing the specific coins he mentions** (the Doge/Luna lesson) instead of
   using the *framework.* Personality-following is not method-following.
5. **Confusing nominal gains with real gains.** Forgetting the debasement hurdle —
   the very point of the framework. +4% can still be a real loss.
6. **Treating the "~4-year cycle" as a precise calendar.** It's a rhythm, not a
   clock; ISM and M2 lag and get revised. Over-fitting to it breeds false precision.
7. **Using one liquidity chart as gospel.** Different measures conflict; confirm
   the *trend* across several, and watch M2 and the balance sheet separately.
8. **Ignoring disclosure/conflict context** when consuming free crypto-media
   content.
</Callout>

## Putting it to work

You don't need GMI's proprietary index to start practicing the mindset:

1. **Pick two or three free global-liquidity proxies** (global M2, the Fed balance
   sheet, a net-liquidity series) and watch their *direction and acceleration* —
   M2 and the balance sheet **separately.**
2. **Glance at ISM** for confirmation that growth is accelerating before you add
   risk.
3. **Mark where you think you are** in the ~4-year debt-refinancing cycle — and
   hold that view loosely.
4. **Sort your holdings against the ~11% hurdle.** What beats it, what merely
   preserves, what is quietly being debased?
5. **Decide your risk-sleeve asset** by how much volatility you can genuinely
   tolerate, using the beta ranking (bonds → gold → equities → tech → crypto).
6. **Size for the downside.** Write the maximum portfolio swing you'll accept from
   any single high-beta position *before* you buy.
7. **Write your "I'm wrong" line** for the liquidity trend, and your profit-taking
   plan for a melt-up, in advance.
8. **Treat Pal as a teacher, not a tipster.** Take the framework; supply your own
   discipline.

The deepest lesson here isn't actually about Bitcoin or the Fed. It's a habit:
**zoom out to the biggest force in the room — the flow of money itself — and
measure everything against the hurdle that flow creates.** Then respect that the
same tide cuts both ways.
