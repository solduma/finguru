## Why this matters

Before T. Rowe Price, "growth stock" was barely a phrase. Wall Street in the
1930s was a world of value hunters and dividend collectors — you bought
something cheap, you clipped your coupon, you sold when it ticked up. Price
asked a different and, at the time, almost heretical question: *what if the
biggest fortunes come not from buying things cheaply, but from finding a young,
superbly run company in a growing industry and simply staying with it for
decades while it compounds?*

That idea — that **time and quality, not cheapness, build the largest
fortunes** — is now so mainstream that it's easy to forget someone had to invent
it. Price did. He's widely called **"the father of growth investing,"** and the
investment firm that still bears his name (T. Rowe Price Group) manages money for
millions of people today.

<Callout type="key">

Price's one big idea: a **growth stock** is a company whose earnings per share
reach a **new high at the peak of each successive business cycle**, with every
sign of continuing to do so. Find one of those early, inside a young industry
with a long runway, buy the best-run leader, and **hold it through the growth
phase.** The compounding does the work.

</Callout>

Two terms before we go further, defined plainly:

- **Earnings per share (EPS)** — a company's annual profit divided by the number
  of shares. It's "how much profit each share earns." Rising EPS over years is
  the heartbeat of a growing business.
- **Business cycle** — the economy's regular rhythm of expansion (boom) and
  contraction (recession). Most companies' profits sag during recessions. Price's
  test was demanding precisely because it asked whether a company's *peak* profits
  in each boom keep climbing higher than the last boom's peak.

## Who he was

**Thomas Rowe Price Jr.** (March 16, 1898 – October 20, 1983) was born in
Glyndon, Maryland, and died in Baltimore at 85. He did *not* train as a
financier. He earned a **B.S. in chemistry from Swarthmore College in 1919**, and
many accounts credit that scientific schooling for the rigorous,
criteria-driven way he later analyzed companies — less hunch, more hypothesis and
evidence.

He drifted into finance in the 1920s, working at Baltimore brokerages including
**MacKubin, Legg and Co.** (a forerunner of Legg Mason). There he hit an ethical
wall that shaped everything afterward: brokers were paid by **commission**, which
rewarded them for getting clients to trade constantly — "churning" an account —
whether or not it served the client. Price found that incentive corrupting.

So in **1937**, at 10 Light Street in Baltimore, he founded **T. Rowe Price &
Associates** on three ideas that were genuinely unusual for the era:

1. **Charge a fee based on assets under management**, not on how many trades you
   generate. (If the client's money grows, the firm earns more — interests
   aligned.)
2. Act strictly as a **fiduciary** — legally and ethically bound to put the
   client first.
3. Invest for **growth**, not value.

Forbes later dubbed him **"the Sage of Baltimore."** He stepped back from running
the firm through the 1960s and **fully retired in 1971.**

<Callout type="note">

**A note on dates.** Sources disagree on exactly when Price left: some say he
resigned as president in **1963**, others that he sold his shares around
**1966**, and the cleanest summary is that he **stepped back through the 1960s and
fully retired in 1971.** This kind of fuzziness is common in older financial
biographies — we flag it rather than pretend to a false precision.

</Callout>

## The core idea, in plain language

Most "growth" definitions are mushy — "a company whose earnings go up." Price's
was sharp, and the sharpness is the whole point.

He said a true growth company posts a **new EPS high at the top of each major
business cycle.** Think about how strict that is. Almost any company can have one
great year. Many can string together a few good years inside a single boom.
Price's bar is harder: across *multiple* economic cycles — through recessions
that knock everyone down — the company's *best* years keep setting fresh records,
each cycle's peak above the last.

and down but never really climb; a true growth company dips in recessions too,
but each peak towers over the one before.

The strategic insight that followed: the easiest place to find such a company is
a **young industry early in its life** — before the field is crowded, before
competition crushes profit margins, before the easy growth is gone. Price called
this a **"fertile field."** Buy the **best-managed leader** of a fertile field
early, and you ride the whole industry's expansion *and* the leader's rising
share of it.

## The "fertile field" — six selection criteria

In his foundational **1939 Barron's articles, "Picking 'Growth' Stocks"**, Price
laid out what to look for. Note the order carefully: he picks the **field
(industry) first**, then the **leader within it.** Beginners routinely flip this
and fall in love with a single company while ignoring whether its whole industry
is even growing.

Paraphrasing his criteria:

1. **A fertile field** — an industry with a long growth runway ahead of it, not a
   saturated one.
2. **Superior research** — the company keeps developing better products and
   opening new markets.
3. **A lack of cut-throat competition** — meaning **pricing power**: the company
   can raise prices without instantly losing customers, so margins survive.
4. **Comparative immunity from heavy government regulation and price controls** —
   freedom to run the business on its own terms.
5. **Low total labor cost as a share of sales** — yet with **well-paid,
   productive employees** (efficiency, not exploitation).
6. **A high and sustained return on invested capital (ROIC), plus superior EPS
   growth.** Secondary summaries of his criteria cite a **return-on-capital
   benchmark of roughly 8% or better** — treat that as a guideline, not a hard
   law.

That sixth point needs a definition. **Return on invested capital (ROIC)** is the
profit a company earns for every dollar of capital (debt + equity) put to work in
it. A high, *durable* ROIC is the single best sign that a business has a real
competitive advantage — that it isn't just growing, but growing *profitably* and
fending off imitators.

## The corporate life cycle

Price's most memorable framework borrowed from human life. Every company, he
argued, moves through three stages: **Growth → Maturity → Decadence (Decline).**

- **Growth.** The young, expanding phase. The industry is fresh, the leader is
  taking share, ROIC is high and rising. *This is where Price wanted to own the
  stock* — maximum capital gain for the least risk, because the business itself is
  getting stronger every year.
- **Maturity.** Growth flattens. ROIC stops climbing and levels off. The field
  fills with competitors. The stock starts to behave like an ordinary cyclical
  name — overly sensitive to the general economy rather than driven by its own
  expansion.
- **Decadence (Decline).** The company has lost its edge. Products grow obsolete,
  earnings stagnate or shrink, the competitive moat is gone.

### The buy/sell rule

The rule follows directly from the life cycle: **buy early in the growth phase**
— and be willing to **"pay up"** for genuine quality rather than wait for a
bargain that may never come — then **hold for the long run.** Sell only when it
becomes **obvious that the company has stopped growing**, i.e. when it has clearly
crossed from maturity into decadence.

<Callout type="warning">

A popular paraphrase says Price told investors to "sell before the decline
begins." That reads cleaner than what he actually argued — and it's misleading.
His real stance was to sell **once decadence is obvious**, not to magically time
the top in advance. Price was explicit that **no one can see that far ahead**, so
treating "sell before the decline" as a precise, verbatim rule overstates how
much foresight he claimed. We treat it as a loose paraphrase, not a quote.

</Callout>

## The funds — putting the theory into vehicles

Price didn't just write the theory; he built funds around it, and their launch
dates trace his thinking:

- **Growth Stock Fund (1950)** — the firm's first mutual fund and the purest
  expression of the theory.
- **New Horizons Fund (1960)** — the growth theory applied to *smaller, emerging*
  companies earlier in their life cycle, including the era's technology names.
- **New Era Fund (1969)** — the one that signals his late-career change of mind,
  which we turn to next.

## The honest reversal — the "New Era" inflation pivot

Here is the part of Price's story most retellings skip, and it's the most
instructive. By the **late 1960s**, Price concluded that the comfortable
post-WWII era of stable industrial growth — the very world his growth theory was
built for — was **ending.** He believed the 1970s would be dominated by
**inflation** (rising prices that erode the value of money).

So he changed course. He shifted toward **tangible, "hard" assets** — natural
resources, real estate, gold and silver — things whose value tends to hold up
when paper money is losing purchasing power. The **New Era Fund (1969)** was the
vehicle for this view. His reasoning, broadly attributed: when inflation rages,
people won't want paper dollars; they'll want **real property.** (Treat the exact
wording as attributed, not verbatim.)

He was, broadly, right about the decade. The 1970s *were* defined by stagflation,
and pure growth investing struggled badly. One secondary account reports the New
Era hard-asset strategy rose roughly **130% between 1978 and 1981** — but this
comes from a single source and isn't independently confirmed, so treat it as
**approximate and illustrative**, not a verified track record.

<Callout type="key">

The reversal is the lesson. Price built the canonical theory of growth
investing — and then, when the economic regime changed, he was honest enough to
say his own framework no longer fit the world and to invest *against* it. Two of
his most reliable lines: **"Change is the investor's only certainty"** and **"It
is better to be early than too late in recognizing the passing of one era."** A
method that worked brilliantly for thirty years can stop working — and clinging to
it out of pride is its own failure mode. (He was disarmingly blunt about his own
record too: *"I make more mistakes than anybody else,"* he said.)

</Callout>

## Honest criticisms and limitations

Price's theory is foundational, but it is not a money machine. Be clear-eyed:

- **The hit rate is low.** Rigorous research reportedly identifies a *true*
  long-term grower only a minority of the time — one secondary source frames it as
  roughly **1 in 4** (treat the exact 25%/75% split as **illustrative**). The shape
  is real: most candidates never become great compounders, which is exactly why
  Price made **diversification** his first principle.
- **No formula tells you when growth turns to maturity.** The life cycle is a
  brilliant *concept*, but no clean number flashes red at the turn; it's usually
  only obvious in hindsight — by which point the stock has already de-rated.
- **The modern overpaying trap.** "Buy the leader and hold," taken uncritically,
  can tempt investors to **overpay** for a beloved growth story and **hold far too
  long** as it matures — converting a great business into a mediocre investment.

<Callout type="warning">

The most dangerous misreading is treating "growth stocks always win, so just buy
quality and never sell" as a law of nature. Price's own life is the
counter-evidence: he abandoned that very stance when the 1970s arrived. Price
combined growth *with* hard quality tests (especially durable ROIC and pricing
power) and with **diversification** precisely because he knew most picks would
disappoint and that the world would eventually change underneath him.

</Callout>

## Putting it to work

You don't need a fund to think like Price. Try this on one company you follow:

1. **Field first.** Pull up the company's annual EPS for the last 10–15 years and
   run the **cyclical-peak test**: did its profit peaks keep setting new records
   across recessions inside a *young, growing* industry?
2. **Locate it on the life cycle** — growth, maturity, or decadence — and decide
   in advance what would make you sell, so you neither panic on a dip nor cling to
   a genuine decline.
3. **Diversify, and stay humble.** Price expected most picks to disappoint;
   spreading bets was, for him, the *first* principle.

Price and his contemporary Philip Fisher are the two great architects of growth
investing — Fisher with his scuttlebutt and Fifteen Points, Price with his
cyclical-peak definition and corporate life cycle. Read together, they teach the
same hard truth from two angles: finding a great growing business is only half the
job; the other half is the patience to hold it — and the honesty to recognize when
the era, or the company, has changed.

## Key takeaways

- T. Rowe Price (1898–1983), a Swarthmore-trained chemist, is **"the father of
  growth investing"** and founded **T. Rowe Price & Associates in 1937** on
  fee-for-assets, fiduciary, and pro-growth principles.
- His precise definition of a **growth stock**: one whose **EPS reaches a new high
  at the peak of each successive business cycle**, with signs of continuing.
- Pick the **"fertile field" (young industry) first**, then the **best-managed
  leader** in it, judged on pricing power, superior research, low relative labor
  cost, and **high, durable return on invested capital** (a benchmark around 8%+).
- Companies move through a **life cycle — Growth → Maturity → Decadence.** Buy
  early in growth, be willing to pay up for quality, hold for the long run, and
  sell only once decline is **obvious**.
- His funds embody the theory: **Growth Stock Fund (1950)**, **New Horizons
  (1960)**, and **New Era (1969)**.
- Late in life he **reversed course**: judging that 1970s inflation would break
  pure growth investing, he pivoted to **hard assets** — proof the theory is
  **regime-dependent**, and a model of intellectual honesty.
- Limitations are real: a **low hit rate** on identifying true growers (hence
  diversification), **no formula** for spotting the growth-to-maturity turn, and a
  modern tendency to **overpay and overstay**. *(Some figures cited from single
  secondary sources are labeled illustrative; this is education, not financial
  advice.)*
