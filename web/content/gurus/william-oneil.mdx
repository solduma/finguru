## The one-sentence version

William O'Neil spent decades dissecting the greatest stock winners in history, found that they shared a handful of fingerprints *before* they took off, and packed those fingerprints into a memorable seven-letter acronym — **CAN SLIM** — that fuses fundamental analysis with chart reading and strict risk control.

Most beginners pick one camp: either you're a "fundamentals person" who studies earnings and balance sheets, or you're a "technicals person" who reads charts and price action. O'Neil's enduring contribution is the insistence that the biggest winners satisfy *both* at once — strong, accelerating earnings **and** a chart that shows large institutions quietly accumulating shares. And he did something most gurus avoid: he told you exactly when to sell if you're wrong — a 7–8% loss, no debating it.

This lesson covers who O'Neil was, what each CAN SLIM letter means in plain English, the base patterns and buy points that drive his timing, the market-timing tool called the follow-through day, a fully worked trade with the risk/reward math, and the honest weaknesses of the whole approach.

<Callout type="key">
**The mental model in one line:** buy a *proven* fast grower (fundamentals), at the *exact moment* it breaks to a new high out of a healthy chart pattern (timing), *only while the broad market is rising* (the "M") — and cut it instantly if it falls 7–8% (risk control). Miss any one of those four and you are not running CAN SLIM.
</Callout>

## Who was William O'Neil?

William J. O'Neil (born March 25, 1933, in Oklahoma City; died May 28, 2023, at age 90) was an American stockbroker, researcher, and publisher. He earned a business degree from Southern Methodist University in 1955, served in the U.S. Air Force, and became a stockbroker at Hayden, Stone & Company in 1958. ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_O%27Neil))

A few facts give a sense of his early intensity. At age 30 he bought a seat on the New York Stock Exchange — reportedly the youngest person to do so at that time — and in 1963 founded **William O'Neil + Co.**, which built one of the first computerized daily stock-price databases. That database is the real engine behind everything else: O'Neil could systematically study what the best-performing stocks of each era actually *looked like* in the months before their big runs, going back to the 1950s. CAN SLIM is not an opinion; it is the pattern that fell out of that historical study.

In 1984 he launched a newspaper, *Investor's Daily*, to compete with *The Wall Street Journal*; it was renamed **Investor's Business Daily (IBD)** in 1991 and was later acquired by News Corp. He laid out his system in the bestselling book *How to Make Money in Stocks* (first edition 1988). O'Neil openly credited earlier traders as influences — Gerald Loeb, Jesse Livermore, Bernard Baruch, and Nicolas Darvas. ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_O%27Neil))

<Callout type="note">
CAN SLIM is a registered trademark associated with Investor's Business Daily. It is a *strategy*, not a mechanical formula — the thresholds below are O'Neil's published guidelines distilled from historical winners, not laws of nature. Different editions and IBD articles state them with slight variations.
</Callout>

## CAN SLIM, letter by letter

CAN SLIM is an acronym for seven characteristics O'Neil found in winning stocks just before their largest advances. ([Wikipedia: CAN SLIM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CAN_SLIM))

| Letter | Stands for | O'Neil's guideline |
|--------|-----------|--------------------|
| **C** | Current quarterly earnings | EPS up at least **25%** vs. the year-ago quarter; faster acceleration is better |
| **A** | Annual earnings growth | Annual EPS up **25%+** over each of the last 3 years; return on equity **17%+** |
| **N** | New | A new product, service, management, or **new price high** out of a base |
| **S** | Supply and demand | Look for **rising volume** on price advances; a smaller float can move faster |
| **L** | Leader or laggard | Own the **leader** in a leading industry; high relative-strength rating (RS ≥ 80) |
| **I** | Institutional sponsorship | Owned by funds/banks, ideally a **growing** number of quality holders |
| **M** | Market direction | Buy mainly in a **confirmed market uptrend** — the single most important factor |

Let's unpack the ones beginners most often misread.

**C — Current quarterly earnings.** "EPS" means *earnings per share* — the company's profit divided by the number of shares outstanding. O'Neil wants the most recent quarter's EPS to be at least 25% higher than the *same* quarter one year earlier (comparing the same quarter avoids seasonal distortions, e.g. a retailer's holiday quarter). Better still is **acceleration** — growth speeding up across recent quarters. Strong sales growth (~20–25%+) should ideally accompany the earnings.

**A — Annual earnings.** One great quarter can be a fluke; O'Neil wants a multi-year track record of roughly 25%+ annual EPS growth over each of the last three years, plus a return on equity (profit generated per dollar of shareholder capital) of around 17% or more. ROE filters for companies that grow *efficiently*, not just by raising cash.

**N — New.** Big advances are usually fueled by something genuinely new — a hit product, new management, an industry shift. The *second* meaning of "new" is technical: the stock makes a **new price high** as it breaks out of a consolidation pattern. Beginners fear buying at new highs; O'Neil argued that's precisely where winners begin — "buy high, sell higher." You'll see exactly what this looks like in the cup-with-handle chart below, where the breakout above the pivot *is* the new price high.

**L — Leader or laggard.** Buy the strongest stock in a strong group, not the cheap straggler that "looks due." IBD measures this with a **Relative Strength (RS) Rating** comparing a stock's price performance to the rest of the market over the prior 12 months, on a 1–99 scale. O'Neil targeted stocks with an **RS Rating of 80 or higher**.

<Callout type="warning">
Don't confuse O'Neil's **Relative Strength (RS) Rating** — a 1–99 *ranking* of a stock against all others — with the **Relative Strength Index (RSI)**, J. Welles Wilder's 0–100 momentum *oscillator* on a single security. They share three letters and nothing else. (See the RSI lesson.)
</Callout>

**I — Institutional sponsorship.** Mutual funds, pensions, and banks move enormous money; their sustained buying is what drives a stock for months. You want *some* and ideally an *increasing* number of quality institutional holders — but not so much that everyone already owns it (no buyers left). IBD reads this through its **Accumulation/Distribution rating**, a gauge of whether big money is net buying or selling.

**M — Market direction.** O'Neil estimated that roughly **three of every four stocks follow the general market**, so he wanted to buy only when major indexes (S&P 500, Nasdaq) are in a confirmed uptrend, and to raise cash during corrections. IBD classifies the market into three states: *confirmed uptrend*, *uptrend under pressure*, and *correction*. O'Neil called M the single most important factor — buying great stocks in a falling market is the fastest way to rack up 7–8% losses.

<Callout type="key">
CAN SLIM's core thesis: the best stocks combine **strong fundamentals** (C, A, L, I) with **good timing** (N's breakout, S's volume, M's market trend). One without the other is a weaker bet — a great company breaking out into a bear market still gets dragged down.
</Callout>

## Bases, pivots, and the buy point

A **base** is a period of sideways price consolidation that forms after an advance — the stock "rests" while shares change hands from weak holders to strong ones. O'Neil's signature pattern is the **cup-with-handle**.

- **The cup:** a smooth, rounded, U-shaped decline and recovery (**not** a sharp V). The cup lasts **at least about 7 weeks** and often runs 1 to 6 months, with a depth of roughly **12–33%** from the left rim down to the bottom in a normal market. ([Wikipedia: Cup and handle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cup_and_handle))
- **The handle:** a short, mild downward drift near the top of the right side, forming in the **upper third** of the base on **light volume**, ideally retracing only about 30–50% of the prior advance and lasting roughly 1 to 4+ weeks. The light-volume drift means sellers are exhausting; the breakout then comes on a *surge* of volume.

Other valid bases O'Neil describes include the **flat base** (a tight, shallow sideways range, often after a prior breakout), the **double bottom** (a W shape whose second low undercuts the first to shake out weak holders), the **saucer-with-handle**, and the **high-tight flag** (a rare, powerful pattern after a very large, fast run).

The **pivot** (or **buy point**) is the price that, once cleared, signals the breakout. For a cup-with-handle, IBD's rule of thumb is: take the **highest point of the handle and add about 10 cents**. With no handle, add 10 cents to the peak on the left side of the cup. Two more rules matter as much as the pivot itself:

1. A valid breakout should come on volume roughly **40–50% (or more) above the stock's average daily volume**, confirming real institutional demand. A breakout on weak volume is suspect.
2. Buy within about **5% above the pivot** — the "buy zone." Chasing a stock already extended more than ~5% past the pivot dramatically raises the odds of being shaken out on a normal pullback.

### Worked example (illustrative)

> *Numbers below are illustrative, not a real stock.*

Stock XYZ passes the CAN SLIM checklist — quarterly EPS +40%, three-year annual EPS +30%, ROE 22%, RS Rating 92, rising fund ownership, and the market is in a confirmed uptrend. It runs up, then forms a cup over about ten weeks, bottoming and recovering back near its left-side peak of ~$50. It then drifts into a two-week handle just below that peak, whose high is **$50.20**.

- **Pivot / buy point** = handle high + $0.10 = **$50.30**
- You buy **200 shares at $50.30** (≈ $10,060) as the stock pushes through on volume ~45% above average.
- **Stop-loss at 7%** = $50.30 × 0.93 ≈ **$46.78**. If it closes there, you sell — no exceptions. Max loss ≈ ($50.30 − $46.78) × 200 ≈ **$704 (~7%)**.
- **Profit target at 20–25%** = $50.30 × 1.20 ≈ $60.36 up to × 1.25 ≈ $62.88. Selling near $62.00 ≈ ($62.00 − $50.30) × 200 ≈ **$2,340 (~23%)**.

**The asymmetry is the whole engine.** You risk ~$704 to make ~$2,340 — roughly a 1-to-3 ratio. With that math, you can be *wrong more often than right* and still come out ahead: even if only one breakout in three works, the single 20%+ winner more than pays for the two ~8% losers. But that arithmetic only holds *if you enforce the 8% stop every single time*. Let one loser run to −30% and a whole string of winners is wiped out.

## The follow-through day (the "M" in action)

How do you know a beaten-down market has actually turned? O'Neil's answer is the **follow-through day (FTD)**, his most famous market-timing tool.

1. **Day 1** is an *attempted rally* — the first up day off a market bottom.
2. The first two-to-three days are *not* trusted (bear-market bounces fail constantly).
3. On **Day 4 or later** (commonly Day 4 through 7), watch for a major index (Nasdaq or S&P 500) to close **significantly higher** — O'Neil's classic threshold is a gain of about **1.25% or more** in a single session — on **volume heavier than the prior day**.

That combination of a strong percentage gain *plus* rising volume confirms the rally as a new uptrend. The signal is invalidated if the index later falls below the low of the follow-through day. IBD's claim is that no major bull market has begun *without* a follow-through day — but **not every FTD leads to a sustained rally**; false signals happen, which is exactly why the stop-loss still rules.

<Callout type="warning">
The single most-broken CAN SLIM rule is the **7–8% stop-loss**. O'Neil's response to critics was blunt: people who lost money were usually cherry-picking the buy rules while ignoring the sell discipline. The system is a *package* — the stop-loss is what keeps a wrong call from becoming a catastrophe, and an 8% loss is easily recovered while a 50% loss requires a 100% gain just to break even.
</Callout>

## When to sell (the rules most people skip)

CAN SLIM has two distinct sell rules, and beginners tend to invert both:

- **Defensive sell — cut every loss at 7–8% below your buy point, no exceptions.** This is the cornerstone. It is mechanical and emotionless on purpose.
- **Offensive sell — take most gains in the 20–25% range** (sell into strength), *unless* the stock is a powerful market leader showing exceptional action, in which case you may hold longer and trail it.
- Sell on clear **distribution / breakdown**: heavy-volume price drops, breaking key moving averages (e.g. the 50-day line on big volume), climax tops, or when M turns negative.

The intended asymmetry — many small ~8% losses paid for by fewer 20–25%+ gains — is destroyed if you do the natural human thing: hold losers hoping they come back, and sell winners early to "lock in a profit."

## Legacy and modern implementation

CAN SLIM became one of the most widely followed retail strategies in America, propagated through IBD, the IBD 50 stock list, the *MarketSmith* charting platform, and CAN SLIM-style ETFs. The American Association of Individual Investors (AAII) named CAN SLIM its top-performing screen over the 1998–2009 period. ([Wikipedia: CAN SLIM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CAN_SLIM))

Today you can implement the framework with most screeners: filter for 25%+ quarterly and annual EPS growth, ROE ~17%+, high relative strength, and rising institutional ownership, then chart the candidates for bases and buy on breakouts *during a confirmed uptrend*.

## Honest criticisms and common pitfalls

- **It's a trading system, not buy-and-hold.** Frequent buying/selling means high turnover, transaction costs, short-term taxes, and constant chart monitoring — impractical for many busy or passive investors.
- **The track record is largely hypothetical/backtested.** O'Neil's own two mutual funds reportedly struggled to match the system's strong backtested returns — a real tension between the studied winners and live fund performance. ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CAN_SLIM))
- **Survivorship and hindsight bias.** Studying the stocks that *did* become huge winners tells you their traits, but many stocks with identical traits failed. The pattern is necessary, not sufficient, and the AAII ranking is one data series, not peer-reviewed proof.
- **Whipsaws in choppy markets.** Breakouts fail frequently in sideways or "uptrend under pressure" conditions; momentum strategies can suffer sharp reversals in sudden bear markets. You can take many small 7–8% losses in a row ("death by a thousand cuts").

- **Subjectivity.** "Is that a proper handle?" and "Is this a confirmed uptrend?" require judgment that takes screen-time to develop. Beginners routinely misread V-shaped or too-shallow/too-deep "cups," wide-and-loose patterns, or handles sitting in the lower half of the base.

<Callout type="warning">
**The classic beginner mistakes with CAN SLIM:**

1. **Skipping the 7–8% stop-loss** — the #1 way people blow up the system.
2. **Chasing extended stocks** — buying more than ~5% above the pivot, then getting shaken out on a normal pullback.
3. **Buying in a down market** — ignoring "M" and buying breakouts that fail because the broad market is falling.
4. **Ignoring breakout volume** — acting on a breakout that lacks the 40–50%+ volume surge.
5. **Buying low-RS laggards because they "look cheap"** — the exact opposite of "leaders, not laggards."
6. **Holding losers and selling winners early** — inverting the profit/loss asymmetry the whole system depends on.
7. **Cherry-picking parts of the system** instead of applying all seven factors *plus* the sell rules together.
</Callout>

<Callout type="note">
CAN SLIM is a **growth-stock, trend-following** method. It is philosophically opposite to deep-value investing (buying cheap, beaten-down stocks). Neither is "correct" — they exploit different edges. Know which game you're playing.
</Callout>

## How to apply it today

1. **Screen** for the fundamentals: 25%+ EPS growth (quarter and year), ROE ~17%+, high relative strength (RS ≥ 80), rising fund ownership.
2. **Check M first.** Don't initiate aggressive buys unless the broad market is in a confirmed uptrend (use the follow-through-day logic).
3. **Wait for a base and a clean breakout** above the pivot on +40–50% volume — and buy *within 5%* of the pivot. Don't chase.
4. **Set the stop immediately** at 7–8% below your buy point.
5. **Cut losers fast, let winners run**, and consider trimming after a 20–25% gain.
6. **Keep a journal.** O'Neil's edge came from reviewing past winners; review your own trades the same way to train your eye for bases.
