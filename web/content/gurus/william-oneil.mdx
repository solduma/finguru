## The one-sentence version

William O'Neil spent decades dissecting the greatest stock winners in history, found that they shared a handful of fingerprints before they took off, and packed those fingerprints into a memorable seven-letter acronym — **CAN SLIM** — that fuses fundamental analysis with chart reading and strict risk control.

Most beginners pick one camp: either you're a "fundamentals person" who studies earnings and balance sheets, or you're a "technicals person" who reads charts and price action. O'Neil's enduring contribution is the insistence that the biggest winners satisfy *both* at the same time — strong, accelerating earnings **and** a chart that shows large institutions quietly accumulating shares. He also did something most gurus avoid: he told you exactly when to sell if you're wrong (a 7-8% loss, no debating it).

This lesson covers who O'Neil was, what each CAN SLIM letter means, the base patterns and buy points that drive his timing, the market-timing tool called the follow-through day, and the honest weaknesses of the whole approach.

## Who was William O'Neil?

William J. O'Neil (born March 25, 1933, in Oklahoma City; died May 28, 2023, at age 90) was an American stockbroker, researcher, and publisher. He earned a business degree from Southern Methodist University in 1955, served in the U.S. Air Force, and became a stockbroker at Hayden, Stone & Company in 1958. ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_O%27Neil))

A few facts give a sense of his early intensity. At age 30 he bought a seat on the New York Stock Exchange — reportedly the youngest person to do so at that time — and in 1963 founded **William O'Neil + Co.**, which built one of the first computerized daily stock-price databases. That database is the real engine behind everything else: O'Neil could systematically study what the best-performing stocks of each era actually looked like *before* their big runs, going back to the 1950s.

In 1984 he launched a newspaper, *Investor's Daily*, to compete with *The Wall Street Journal*; it was renamed **Investor's Business Daily (IBD)** in 1991 and was acquired by News Corp in 2021 for a reported $275 million. He laid out his system in the bestselling book *How to Make Money in Stocks* (first edition 1988). O'Neil openly credited earlier traders as influences — Gerald Loeb, Jesse Livermore, Bernard Baruch, and Nicolas Darvas. ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_O%27Neil))

<Callout type="note">

CAN SLIM is a registered trademark associated with Investor's Business Daily. It is a *strategy*, not a mechanical formula — the thresholds below are O'Neil's published guidelines, not laws of nature.

</Callout>

## CAN SLIM, letter by letter

CAN SLIM is an acronym for seven characteristics O'Neil found in winning stocks just before their largest advances. ([Wikipedia: CAN SLIM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CAN_SLIM))

| Letter | Stands for | O'Neil's guideline |
|--------|-----------|--------------------|
| **C** | Current quarterly earnings | EPS up at least **25%** vs. the year-ago quarter; faster acceleration is better |
| **A** | Annual earnings growth | Annual EPS up **25%+** over each of the last 3 years; return on equity **17%+** |
| **N** | New | A new product, service, management, or **new price high** out of a base |
| **S** | Supply and demand | Look for **rising volume** on price advances; smaller float can help |
| **L** | Leader or laggard | Own the **leader** in a leading industry; high relative-strength rating |
| **I** | Institutional sponsorship | Owned by funds/banks, ideally a **growing** number of quality holders |
| **M** | Market direction | Buy mainly in a **confirmed market uptrend** |

Let's unpack the ones beginners most often misread.

**C — Current quarterly earnings.** "EPS" means *earnings per share* — the company's profit divided by the number of shares outstanding. O'Neil wants the most recent quarter's EPS to be at least 25% higher than the same quarter one year earlier (comparing to the same quarter avoids seasonal distortions, e.g. a retailer's holiday quarter). Better still is **acceleration** — growth speeding up across recent quarters.

**A — Annual earnings.** One great quarter can be a fluke; O'Neil wants a multi-year track record of roughly 25%+ annual growth over each of the last three years, plus a return on equity (profit generated per dollar of shareholder capital) of around 17% or more.

**N — New.** Big advances are usually fueled by something genuinely new — a hit product, new management, an industry shift. The *second* meaning of "new" is technical: the stock makes a **new price high** as it breaks out of a consolidation pattern. Beginners fear buying at new highs; O'Neil argued that's precisely where winners begin.

**L — Leader or laggard.** Buy the strongest stock in a strong group, not the cheap straggler. IBD measures this with a **Relative Strength (RS) rating** comparing a stock's price performance to the rest of the market over the prior 12 months.

**I — Institutional sponsorship.** Mutual funds, pensions, and banks move enormous money; their buying is what drives a stock for months. You want *some* and ideally *increasing* institutional ownership — but not so much that everyone already owns it.

**M — Market direction.** O'Neil estimated that most stocks follow the general market, so he wanted to buy when major indexes (S&P 500, Nasdaq) are in a confirmed uptrend, not during a correction. IBD classifies the market into three states: confirmed uptrend, uptrend under pressure, and correction.

<Callout type="key">

CAN SLIM's core thesis: the best stocks combine **strong fundamentals** (C, A, N, L, I) with **good timing** (N's breakout, S's volume, M's market trend). One without the other is a weaker bet.

</Callout>

## Bases, pivots, and the buy point

A **base** is a period of sideways price consolidation that forms after an advance — the stock "rests" while shares change hands. O'Neil's signature pattern is the **cup-with-handle**.

- **The cup:** a rounded, U-shaped decline and recovery (not a sharp V). The cup typically lasts about 1 to 6 months. ([Wikipedia: Cup and handle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cup_and_handle))
- **The handle:** a short, mild pullback near the top of the right side, ideally retracing only about 30-50% of the cup's prior advance, lasting roughly 1 to 4 weeks. Volume should dry up during the handle (sellers exhausting), then surge on the breakout.

Other common bases include the **flat base** (a tight, shallow sideways range, often after a prior breakout) and the **double bottom** (a W shape with two lows, the second ideally undercutting the first to shake out weak holders).

The **pivot** (or **buy point**) is the price that, once cleared, signals the breakout. For a cup-with-handle, IBD's rule is: take the **highest point of the handle and add about 10 cents**. With no handle, add 10 cents to the peak on the left side of the cup. A valid breakout should come on volume roughly **40-50% above the stock's average**, confirming real demand.

### Worked example (illustrative)

> *Numbers below are illustrative, not a real stock.*

A stock runs up, then forms a cup over four months, bottoming and recovering to a left-side peak near $50. It then drifts into a two-week handle whose high is **$52.00**.

- **Pivot / buy point** = handle high + $0.10 = **$52.10**
- You buy as the stock pushes through $52.10 on volume ~45% above average.
- **Stop-loss** = 7-8% below your buy: $52.10 × (1 − 0.07) ≈ **$48.45**. If it closes there, you sell — no exceptions.

If the stock advances 20-25% within a few weeks of the breakout, O'Neil's playbook says consider taking at least partial profits. The asymmetry is the point: you risk ~7-8% to make 20%+.

## The follow-through day (the "M" in action)

How do you know a beaten-down market has turned? O'Neil's answer is the **follow-through day (FTD)**, his most famous market-timing tool.

1. **Day 1** is an *attempted rally* — the first up day off a market bottom.
2. The first two-to-three days are not trusted.
3. On **Day 4 or later** (commonly Day 4 through 7), watch for a major index (Nasdaq or S&P 500) to close **significantly higher** — O'Neil's classic threshold is a gain of about **1.25% or more** in a single session — on **volume heavier than the prior day**.

That combination of a strong percentage gain *plus* rising volume confirms the rally as a new uptrend. The signal is invalidated if the index later falls below the low of the follow-through day. IBD's claim is that every major market bottom has been accompanied by a follow-through day — but **not every FTD leads to a sustained rally**; false signals happen, which is why the stop-loss still rules.

<Callout type="warning">

The single most-broken CAN SLIM rule is the **7-8% stop-loss**. O'Neil's response to critics was blunt: people who lost money were usually cherry-picking the buy rules while ignoring the sell discipline. The system is a package; the stop-loss is what keeps a wrong call from becoming a catastrophe.

</Callout>

## Legacy and modern implementation

CAN SLIM became one of the most widely followed retail strategies in America, propagated through IBD, the IBD 50 stock list, the *MarketSmith* charting platform (rebranded from O'Neil's Daily Graphs service in 2010), and an ETF (the Innovator IBD 50, launched 2015) tracking CAN SLIM-style picks. The American Association of Individual Investors (AAII) named CAN SLIM its top-performing screen over 1998-2009. ([Wikipedia: CAN SLIM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CAN_SLIM))

Today you can implement the framework with most screeners: filter for 25%+ quarterly and annual EPS growth, high relative strength, and rising institutional ownership, then chart candidates for bases and buy on breakouts during a confirmed uptrend.

## Honest criticisms and common pitfalls

- **The track record is mostly hypothetical/backtested.** O'Neil's own two mutual funds (the O'Neil Fund, launched 1965, and the New USA Fund, launched 1992) both ended quickly with lackluster results — a real tension between the studied winners and live fund performance. ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CAN_SLIM))
- **Survivorship and hindsight bias.** Studying the stocks that *did* become huge winners tells you their traits, but many stocks with identical traits fail. The pattern is necessary, not sufficient.
- **Whipsaws in choppy markets.** Breakouts fail frequently in sideways or "uptrend under pressure" conditions; you can suffer many small 7-8% losses.

- **Subjectivity.** "Is that a proper handle?" and "Is this a confirmed uptrend?" require judgment that takes screen-time to develop.
- **High turnover and taxes.** Frequent buying/selling generates costs and short-term taxable gains.

<Callout type="note">

CAN SLIM is a **growth-stock, trend-following** method. It is philosophically opposite to deep-value investing (buying cheap, beaten-down stocks). Neither is "correct" — they exploit different edges. Know which game you're playing.

</Callout>

## How to apply it today

1. **Screen** for the fundamentals: 25%+ EPS growth (quarter and year), ROE ~17%+, strong relative strength, rising fund ownership.
2. **Check M first.** Don't initiate aggressive buys unless the broad market is in a confirmed uptrend (use the follow-through-day logic).
3. **Wait for a base and a clean breakout** above the pivot on big volume — don't chase.
4. **Set the stop immediately** at 7-8% below your buy point.
5. **Cut losers fast, let winners run**, and consider trimming after a 20-25% gain.
6. **Keep a journal.** O'Neil's edge came from reviewing past winners; review your own trades the same way.
