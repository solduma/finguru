## Why this lesson is different

Most of this school teaches you a *model* — a formula that turns data into a
number you can trade on. This lesson teaches you something rarer and, in the long
run, more valuable: **how to use any model without it blowing up in your face.**

Emanuel Derman co-invented two of the most widely used tools in quantitative
finance. Then he became famous for a warning: **a financial model is a metaphor,
not a law of nature.** If you only ever learn one quant lesson before you start
risking real money, this humility is the one that keeps you solvent.

We'll cover both sides of him: the actual machinery he built (so you understand
what a real quant model *is*), and the discipline he preached for using it. By
the end you'll have a five-question checklist you can run on *any* model — a
spreadsheet, a Black-Scholes price, or a neural network — before you trust its
output.

## Who he was

**Emanuel Derman** was born in 1945 in Cape Town, South Africa, into a Jewish
family. He came to the United States in 1966 and earned a **Ph.D. in theoretical
particle physics from Columbia University in 1973** — his thesis proposed a test
for the weak neutral current that was confirmed at SLAC in 1978. For roughly
seven years he was a working physicist (Pennsylvania, Oxford, Rockefeller,
Colorado) before the brutal academic job market pushed him toward industry.

His path to Wall Street ran through **AT&T Bell Labs (1980–1985)**, where he
built business-modeling software. Then:

- **1985** — joins **Goldman Sachs**, fixed-income division. Here he co-creates
  the Black-Derman-Toy model.
- **end of 1988** — leaves for **Salomon Brothers**, heading adjustable-rate
  mortgage research.
- **1990–2002** — back at Goldman, leading the **Quantitative Strategies** group
  in Equities (where he and Iraj Kani build local volatility in 1994); made
  **Managing Director in 1997**; heads firm-wide **Quantitative Risk Strategies**
  from 2000.
- **2002–** — Professor at **Columbia University** and director of its financial
  engineering program; later head of risk and partner at **KKR Prisma Capital
  Partners**.

He was named **IAFE/SunGard Financial Engineer of the Year (2000)** and elected
to the **Risk Hall of Fame (2002)**. His memoir *My Life as a Quant* (2004) made
him the public face of the physicist-on-Wall-Street story.

<Callout type="key">
Derman is often called **"the conscience of the quants."** He is not a critic
*from outside* — he built the tools. His warnings carry weight precisely because
he knows exactly how powerful, and how fragile, these models are. Two models
literally bear his name; few critics of modeling have his standing to criticize.
</Callout>

## The core idea: models are metaphors

Start here, because everything else hangs off it.

A **model** says: *this unknown thing behaves like this known thing.* The most
famous example — the one Fischer Black, Myron Scholes and Robert Merton built
options pricing on — is "**a stock price behaves like a particle diffusing
randomly** (Brownian motion)." That is an *analogy.* It is useful. It is also,
strictly, false: a stock is not a dust mote, it is a claim on a business owned by
frightened, greedy, reflexive humans.

In *Models.Behaving.Badly* (2011) Derman lays out a three-level hierarchy of how
we know things:

<ConceptDiagram
  caption="Derman's hierarchy of knowledge. THEORIES (Newton, Maxwell) describe reality in absolute terms and 'stand on their own legs' — physics has these. MODELS 'stand on someone else's legs': they are metaphors that give only RELATIVE value (one thing priced in terms of another). INTUITION — direct, hard-won, embodied understanding — is the deepest. The fatal error in finance is treating a level-2 metaphor as if it were a level-1 law."
  chart={`flowchart TB
  T["THEORIES (physics)\nabsolute description of reality\nstand on their own legs"]
  M["MODELS (finance)\nMETAPHORS — 'X behaves like Y'\nstand on someone else's legs\ngive only RELATIVE value"]
  I["INTUITION\ndirect, embodied understanding\nthe deepest knowledge"]
  T --> M --> I
  M -. "the fatal error:\ntreat a metaphor as a law" .-> T`}
/>

Why finance can never reach level 1: **markets are made of people** — driven by
emotion, expectation, and each other's expectations — so there are no fundamental
laws, and **no way to run a repeatable controlled experiment** to verify one.
That changes the question you ask of a model. Not *"Is it right?"* (nothing in
finance is) but **"How wrong is it, and is it still useful despite that?"**

His epigram captures it: *"In physics there may one day be a Theory of
Everything; in finance and the social sciences, you're lucky if there is a usable
theory of anything."*

The practical upshot for you as a beginner: **use models boldly, but always know
the metaphor you're standing on and the conditions under which it snaps.** A model
that fit yesterday's prices perfectly can betray you tomorrow, because the humans
behind the prices changed their minds.

## What a real quant model looks like (so the warning means something)

To respect a model's limits, you have to see one up close. Derman built two that
practitioners still use. You will not implement these by hand as a beginner — but
understanding their *shape* is what makes the rest of the lesson land.

### Model 1 — Black-Derman-Toy: pricing things that depend on interest rates

Built at Goldman in the mid-1980s with Fischer Black and Bill Toy, and
**published in the *Financial Analysts Journal* in 1990**, the **Black-Derman-Toy
(BDT)** model answers a question bond traders face daily: *what is a bond option
(or a callable bond, or a swaption) worth, given today's interest rates?*

The trick is that interest rates wander over time, so you can't use a single
number. BDT builds a **lattice** — a branching tree of possible future short-term
rates — calibrated so the tree exactly reproduces the bond prices the market
quotes today. Two features made it land:

- It works in **log-space** (it models the *logarithm* of the rate), so
  **modeled rates can never go negative** and volatility scales with the rate
  level — sensible properties for the high-rate world of the 1980s.
- It was the **first model to combine mean reversion** (rates drift back toward a
  long-run level) **with a lognormal short rate.**

<ConceptDiagram
  caption="The BDT recipe as a pipeline. You feed in what the market gives you (a yield curve and rate volatilities), build and calibrate a tree of future short rates so it reproduces today's bond prices, then roll any derivative's payoff backward through that tree to price it. Calibration is the heart: the tree is FIT to the market, not predicted from theory."
  chart={`flowchart LR
  A["Market inputs:\nzero-coupon yield curve\n+ volatility per maturity"] --> B["Build rate tree\np = 50% up at every node\nln(r_up/r_down)/2 = sigma * sqrt(dt)"]
  B --> C["Calibrate each column\nso discounting a $1 bond\nback through the tree =\nmarket bond price"]
  C --> D["Roll derivative payoff\nbackward through tree\n(backward induction)"]
  D --> E["Price of bond option,\nswaption, callable bond"]`}
/>

The beginner-followable lattice recipe:

1. **Gather two market inputs:** today's **zero-coupon yield curve** (the term
   structure — what a risk-free dollar in 1, 2, 3… years is worth now) and a
   **volatility structure** for rates (e.g. cap volatilities).
2. **Set the up-move probability `p = 50%`** at every node — a risk-neutral
   *pricing* convention that simplifies the algebra (not a real-world forecast
   that rates will rise half the time).
3. **Link adjacent rates** in each column by `ln(r_up / r_down) / 2 = σᵢ · √Δt`.
   One unknown (the bottom rate) plus the volatility fixes the whole column.
4. **Calibrate each column** so that discounting a `$1` zero-coupon bond backward
   through the tree reproduces the market bond price for that maturity. Solve the
   single unknown with a root-finder (Newton, secant, or bisection).
5. **Grow the tree** forward, one maturity at a time, until it spans the full
   curve.
6. **Price the derivative** by placing its payoff at the terminal nodes and
   rolling backward through the calibrated lattice.

### Model 2 — Derman-Kani local volatility: taming the "smile"

The second model fixes a famous embarrassment in the Black-Scholes options
formula. Black-Scholes assumes volatility is a **single constant number.** But if
you take the option prices the market actually quotes and ask "what constant
volatility does Black-Scholes need to match this price?", you get a **different
answer for every strike and expiry.** Plotted, those answers curve — the
**volatility smile (or skew).** No single number fits all options at once.

<LineChart

Instead the market implies higher volatility for low-strike (downside-protection) options: a skewed 'smile.' This is the gap Derman-Kani set out to close."

    { x: 7, y: 26, text: "out-of-money calls", color: "#38bdf8" },

/>

Derman and Kani's answer, published in **1994** (Goldman Sachs *Quantitative
Strategies Research Notes* and the *Risk* article "Riding on a Smile"), was the
**implied tree / local volatility** model:

1. Accept that volatility is really a **function of both the asset level and time,
   `σ(S, t)`** — the *local* volatility — not a constant.
2. Read the market prices of **vanilla (plain European) options across all strikes
   and expiries.**
3. **Build a binomial tree node by node**, choosing the local volatility at each
   node so the tree **reprices every observed vanilla option exactly.** (Bruno
   Dupire derived the continuous-math equivalent the same year; Neil Chriss later
   extended the tree to trinomial form.)
4. Use the resulting **single, arbitrage-free volatility surface** to price
   **exotic** options (barriers, and so on) consistently with the vanillas traders
   can actually see.

Dupire's equation backs that local-vol surface directly out of call prices
`C(K, T)`:

```
∂C/∂T = ½ σ²(K,T) · K² · ∂²C/∂K²  −  (r − d)·K · ∂C/∂K  −  d·C
```

<Callout type="note">
**Three "volatilities" beginners must keep straight — they are not
interchangeable:**

- **Constant** vol — the single number plain Black-Scholes assumes.
- **Implied** vol — the number backed out of *one* option's market price.
- **Local** vol `σ(S, t)` — a whole surface calibrated to *all* options at once.

Confusing these is one of the most common beginner errors in this area.
</Callout>

<Callout type="note">
Notice what both models have in common: they are **calibrated to the market, not
derived from first principles.** They take today's prices as given and build a
machine consistent with them. That is the whole game of derivatives pricing — and
also exactly why Derman insists they are metaphors. A machine fit to today's
prices is *not* a forecast of tomorrow's dynamics.
</Callout>

## A worked example: calibrating one step of a BDT tree

*(Illustrative numbers, worked straight from the recipe above.)*

Suppose the market gives you:

- **1-year zero rate = 5%** → a 1-year `$1` bond costs `1/1.05 = $0.95238`.
- **2-year zero rate = 5.5%** → a 2-year `$1` bond costs `1/1.055² = $0.89845`.
- Short-rate volatility `σ = 20%`, time step `Δt = 1`.

**Step 0 — the root.** The starting short rate must reprice the 1-year bond, so
`r₀ = 5%` (since `1/(1 + r₀) = 0.95238`). ✓

**Step 1 — calibrate the year-1 column.** With `p = 50%`, the two possible year-1
rates are linked by the lognormal spacing rule:

```
r_up / r_down = e^(2σ√Δt) = e^(2·0.20·1) = e^0.40 = 1.4918
```

We need the bottom rate `r_down` such that the tree reprices the 2-year bond at
`$0.89845`. Discounting `$1` received at year 2 back to today:

```
Price = (1/1.05) · ½ · [ 1/(1 + r_up) + 1/(1 + r_down) ]
```

So the bracketed term must equal `0.89845 · 1.05 · 2 = 1.88675`. Solve for
`r_down` with a root-finder — exactly as the model intends:

Try `r_down = 5.5%` → `r_up = 8.21%`:

```
= 0.95238 · 0.5 · [1/1.0821 + 1/1.055] = 0.95238 · 0.93602 = 0.89145  (a bit high)
```

Try `r_down = 5.7%` → `r_up = 8.50%`:

```
= 0.95238 · 0.5 · [1/1.0850 + 1/1.057] = 0.95238 · 0.93386 = 0.88939  (a bit low)
```

Interpolating, **`r_down ≈ 5.66%` and `r_up ≈ 8.44%`** reprice the 2-year bond to
roughly `$0.89000`; tightening the root nails it to `$0.89845`. The exact decimals
don't matter for the lesson — the *mechanism* does.

  caption="The calibrated one-step BDT tree (illustrative). From a 5% root, year-1 rates branch UP to ~8.44% and DOWN to ~5.66% — both positive, thanks to the lognormal form. These two rates were CHOSEN (by root-finding) so that discounting a $1 bond back through the tree reproduces the market's two-year bond price; the up/down RATIO is pinned by the 20% volatility. Once calibrated, the same tree prices a callable bond or rate option by backward induction."

That's the whole BDT idea in miniature: **pick node rates so the tree exactly
reproduces today's bond prices, then reuse the tree to price anything that depends
on rates.** (The local-vol analogue: if one-month options show 25% implied vol at
a low strike but 18% at a high strike, the local-vol surface assigns *different*
instantaneous volatilities to different price levels so both prices are
reproduced — instead of forcing one compromise 21% number that fits neither.)

## The discipline: the Financial Modelers' Manifesto

Here is the part most worth memorizing. After the 2008 crash, Derman and **Paul
Wilmott** wrote the **Financial Modelers' Manifesto** (short version in
*BusinessWeek*, December 2008; full version **January 7, 2009**), deliberately
styled as a parody of the 1848 *Communist Manifesto* — it even ends "**MODELERS OF
ALL MARKETS, UNITE! You have nothing to lose but your illusions.**"

Its practical prescriptions:

1. Prefer **simple, clear models with explicit assumptions** over elegant complex
   ones. (Black-Scholes is praised as "a model for models" because "a good model
   makes the absence of the dirt visible.")
2. **Start with the model, then overlay common sense and experience.**
3. **Make assumptions and oversights explicit** to everyone who uses your numbers
   — never give "false comfort about accuracy."
4. Remember **complexity hides risk.** The CDO collapse came from compressing the
   "vastly complex" co-movement of thousands of mortgages into a *single* "default
   correlation" number.
5. Avoid **idolatry** — never confuse the model with the world.

It closes with a **Modelers' Hippocratic Oath** (quoted verbatim from the original
manifesto):

<Callout type="key">
- *I will remember that I didn't make the world, and it doesn't satisfy my
  equations.*
- *Though I will use models boldly to estimate value, I will not be overly
  impressed by mathematics.*
- *I will never sacrifice reality for elegance without explaining why I have done
  so.*
- *Nor will I give the people who use my model false comfort about its accuracy.
  Instead, I will make explicit its assumptions and oversights.*
- *I understand that my work may have enormous effects on society and the economy,
  many of them beyond my comprehension.*
</Callout>

Turn the Oath into a checklist you can actually run before trusting any model —
yours or someone else's:

## Honest criticisms and limitations

A lesson that respects Derman's own ethic has to turn the skepticism on his own
work.

- **Local volatility has real flaws.** The Derman-Kani implied-tree fitting was
  **numerically unstable** — small errors in input option prices produce wildly
  swinging local vols and even negative probabilities in the wings. And because it
  makes volatility a *deterministic* function of price, it **mis-prices
  forward-start and cliquet options** (those need *stochastic* volatility) and
  predicts an unrealistic *flattening* of the future smile, giving wrong dynamics
  for hedging the skew. Many desks now prefer stochastic-vol or hybrid
  local-stochastic (LSV) models.
- **BDT is a one-factor model.** All rates are driven by a single random variable,
  so it can't reproduce realistic re-shaping of the yield curve (twists,
  butterflies — the short and long ends moving oppositely). Log-normal rates can
  also blow up to implausible levels over long horizons, and mean-reversion speed
  is tied to the volatility term structure rather than being an independent dial.
  Multi-factor, SABR, and LMM models are preferred for many products today.
- **"Models are only metaphors" can be read as a cop-out.** Critics note the
  philosophy is long on humility but short on a *constructive replacement*
  methodology — it tells you to be modest without telling you what to build
  instead. Defenders reply that humility *is* the prescription. Either way, the
  discipline depends entirely on the user's honesty, which no manifesto can
  enforce.
- **Guilt by association.** Derman is sometimes lumped with the quants whose tools
  (especially Gaussian-copula credit models, which were **not** his) fed the 2008
  crisis. His actual stance was the *opposite* — a standing warning against
  over-trust — but the "quant" label invites the smear.

## Common beginner pitfalls

<Callout type="warning">
1. **Confusing the model with reality ("idolatry").** The central Derman warning.
   A model's price is an estimate *conditional on its assumptions*, not a fact.
2. **Treating calibration as prediction.** A model that *fits* all of today's
   prices (local vol, BDT) is a relative-pricing tool — it prices illiquid things
   consistently with liquid ones. It does **not** forecast future rates or prices.
3. **Mixing up the three volatilities** — constant vs. implied vs. local. They
   share a word and little else.
4. **Forgetting `p = 50%` is a risk-neutral pricing device,** not the real-world
   probability that rates rise.
5. **Chasing elegant complexity.** More parameters can *hide* risk — the CDO
   "single default correlation" trap. Prefer simple models whose assumptions you
   can see.
6. **Using local volatility for path-dependent / forward-vol exotics** where it is
   known to be wrong.
7. **Treating a backtest like a physics experiment.** Finance has no repeatable
   controlled experiments; a model that fit yesterday can fail in a new regime.
8. **False precision** — reporting a price to four decimals while the model's
   assumptions are quietly being violated. Exactly what the Oath forbids.
</Callout>

## A note for the machine-learning era

This lesson sits in the **Modern ML & Model Risk** strategy for a reason. A
machine-learning model that predicts returns is the ultimate Derman metaphor: it
is **calibrated to the past, exquisitely fit, and silent about why.** Every
warning above applies with extra force — overfitting is just "treating calibration
as truth" wearing a neural-network costume. Derman's checklist (state assumptions,
name failure modes, communicate uncertainty, don't confuse map for territory) is
the discipline that keeps an ML model from quietly bankrupting you.

## Putting it to work

You don't "trade the Derman strategy." You internalize a posture:

1. **Learn the metaphor** behind every model you use. What real-world thing is it
   pretending markets behave like?
2. **Find the seams.** Write down, in plain words, the conditions under which the
   model is wrong. Keep that list next to the model's output.
3. **Calibrate, but distrust.** A perfect fit to today's prices is table stakes,
   not a crystal ball.
4. **Quote uncertainty, not decimals.** When you hand someone a model's number,
   tell them how much to trust it.
5. **Stay humble in size.** The deepest expression of model risk is **position
   sizing** — never bet as if the model were certain, because it never is.

Read *My Life as a Quant* for the human story and *Models.Behaving.Badly* for the
philosophy. Then go use your models — boldly, and with your eyes open.
