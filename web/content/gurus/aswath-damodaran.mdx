## Why this lesson matters

Most investing advice tells you *what* to buy. This lesson teaches you something
more durable: how to figure out what a business is actually **worth** — so you
can decide for yourself whether today's price is a bargain or a trap.

That skill has a patron saint. **Aswath Damodaran** has spent four decades
teaching valuation at NYU, and — unusually — he gives the entire toolkit away
for free: the spreadsheets, the data, the lectures, the blog. By the end of this
lesson you'll be able to value a company yourself, with numbers, the way he
teaches it — and just as importantly, you'll know when *not* to trust the answer.

## Who he is

**Aswath Damodaran** (born 1957, Chennai, India) is a Professor of Finance at
the **NYU Stern School of Business**, where he has taught since 1986 and holds
the **Kerschner Family Chair in Finance Education**. He earned a B.Com. in
accounting in Chennai, a postgraduate diploma from IIM Bangalore, and an MBA and
a PhD from UCLA Anderson. He has won a stack of teaching awards, including NYU's
Distinguished Teaching Award and the **Herbert Simon Award (2013)**.

In the financial press he's popularly called the **"Dean of Valuation."**

<Callout type="note">
"Dean of Valuation" is a media nickname — widely used by outlets like the
*Financial Times* and CNBC — not a formal title. It captures his reputation well,
but don't mistake it for an official post.
</Callout>

What truly built his reputation is **openness**. He runs the free website
**"Damodaran Online"** and the blog **"Musings on Markets,"** and every year he
publishes openly licensed datasets — equity risk premiums, industry betas, costs
of capital, tax rates — plus the valuation spreadsheets to use them. Analysts,
professors, and students worldwide lean on these numbers. His core message:
rigorous valuation should be **transparent and available to everyone**, not a
black box sold by Wall Street.

## The core idea: price is not value

Start with the single distinction the whole method rests on.

- **Price** is what the market quotes for a share right now. It's set by supply,
  demand, mood, and momentum.
- **Value** is what the business is actually worth, based on the cash it will
  generate over its life.

> Damodaran's foundational claim: **the value of any asset is the present value
> of the cash flows you expect it to generate over its life, discounted back at a
> rate that reflects its risk.**

You buy when **price &lt; value** and (consider) selling when **price &gt; value**.
The market eventually tends to close the gap — but "eventually" can take years,
which is the catch we'll return to.

<Callout type="key">
"Present value" means future money is worth less than money today, so we shrink
("discount") each future dollar back to today's terms. A dollar you'll receive in
ten years, discounted at 8%, is worth only about **46 cents** to you now. Risk
makes you discount harder: the less certain the cash, the higher the discount
rate, the lower the value.
</Callout>

### Two ways to estimate value

Damodaran insists every beginner learn that there are **two distinct families of
valuation**, and they answer slightly different questions:

- **Intrinsic valuation (a DCF):** value a business from *its own* expected
  future cash flows. This is the main event — and the bulk of this lesson.
- **Relative valuation (multiples):** value a business by comparison to how
  *similar* businesses are priced today (P/E, EV/EBITDA, and friends). Fast, but
  it inherits whatever mispricing is baked into the peer group.

A complete answer uses intrinsic value as the anchor and relative value as a
cross-check. We'll build both.

### Narrative *and* Numbers

Damodaran's signature contribution is the idea that a good valuation is a
**bridge between a story and a spreadsheet.**

- A **story** describes the business: what it is, what market it competes in, how
  it will grow, how profitable it can become.
- The **numbers** turn that story into forecasts — revenue, margins,
  reinvestment, cash flow — and finally into a value.

The two must reconcile. Every number has to trace back to the story ("*Why* is
growth 12%?"), and every story has to survive being converted into numbers (a
story that produces absurd numbers is a bad story). He says a story should be
**possible, plausible, and probable** — and you discipline it with three
"spokes": **target revenue** (market size × your share), **target operating
margin** (how profitable you can be at scale), and **reinvestment** (how much
capital that growth eats).

### Where a company sits in its life cycle changes everything

A second Damodaran theme: a firm's **life-cycle stage** decides *where its value
comes from*, so you can't value every company the same way.

| Stage | Where value lives | Valuation reality |
|-------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Young / startup** | Almost entirely in the *story* and far-future cash | Wide ranges; the narrative does the heavy lifting |
| **Growth** | A mix of near-term traction and a big terminal value | The interesting modelling years |
| **Mature** | Current cash flows and what it returns to owners | The "easy" case — most reliable DCF |
| **Declining** | Cash being wound down; asset/liquidation value | Watch for shrinking, not growing, cash |

The worked example below is deliberately a **mature** firm — the most reliable
case for a beginner to start with.

## The full method, step by step

What follows is an **intrinsic valuation** — a discounted-cash-flow (DCF) model
of a company. It looks like a lot of steps, but each one is small. We'll value
the whole firm first, then bridge down to a per-share number you can compare to
the stock price.

A quick vocabulary primer you'll need:

- **FCFF — Free Cash Flow to the Firm:** the cash a business throws off each year
  after paying its operating costs, taxes, and the investment needed to keep
  growing — but *before* paying lenders or shareholders. It's the pot everyone
  who funded the business shares. Discount it at the **WACC**.
- **FCFE — Free Cash Flow to Equity:** the cash left for *shareholders only*,
  after lenders are paid. Discount it at the **cost of equity**.
- **WACC — Weighted Average Cost of Capital:** the blended return that the
  company's investors (both lenders and shareholders) demand.

<Callout type="key">
**Pick FCFF *or* FCFE, then match the discount rate.** FCFF (cash to everyone) is
discounted at WACC and gives you the *whole firm*; FCFE (cash to shareholders
only) is discounted at the cost of equity and gives you *equity directly*. For
**banks and financial firms**, debt is raw material rather than financing, so
Damodaran values them with FCFE or dividends — never FCFF. The fatal beginner
error is mixing the two (e.g. discounting equity cash flows at WACC). This lesson
uses the FCFF route.
</Callout>

### Step 1 — Tell the story

Write a few honest sentences: What does the business do? How big is its market?
How strong is its competitive position? How will it evolve over the next decade?
This anchors every number that follows. Skip it and you'll just be making up
growth rates.

### Step 2 — Forecast the cash flows (FCFF)

For each year of an explicit forecast window (commonly **5–10 years**), compute:

```
FCFF = EBIT × (1 − tax rate)        ← after-tax operating profit
     + Depreciation & amortization  ← a non-cash expense, add it back
     − Capital expenditures         ← cash spent on plant, equipment, etc.
     − Change in working capital     ← cash tied up in inventory/receivables
```

The drivers — revenue growth, operating margin, reinvestment — come straight
from your Step 1 story.

### Step 3 — Find the cost of equity with CAPM

Shareholders take more risk than lenders, so they demand a higher return. The
**Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM)** estimates that required return:

```
Cost of equity = Risk-free rate + Beta × Equity Risk Premium
```

- **Risk-free rate** — the yield on a long-term government bond (e.g. the 10-year
  Treasury) in your valuation's currency. The "safe" baseline return.
- **Beta** — how much the stock moves relative to the overall market. Beta of 1.0
  moves with the market; 1.5 is 50% more volatile; 0.7 is calmer. Damodaran
  recommends a stable **bottom-up (industry) beta** rather than a noisy beta from
  regressing one stock's own history.
- **Equity Risk Premium (ERP)** — the extra return investors demand for holding
  stocks instead of the risk-free bond (covered in detail just below).

For companies exposed to riskier economies, Damodaran adds a **country risk
premium** on top of the mature-market ERP.

### Step 3.5 — The equity risk premium, the Damodaran way

The ERP is the single input he is most associated with, so it's worth pausing on.
There are two ways to estimate it:

- **Historical:** average the realized return of stocks *minus* the risk-free
  rate over many decades. Simple, but it's a rear-view mirror and lands anywhere
  in a wide band (often ~5–7%).
- **Implied (forward-looking) — his preferred method:** take *today's* index
  level (say the S&P 500) as the present value of *expected future cash flows*
  (dividends + buybacks), then solve for the single discount rate that makes those
  cash flows equal today's price. That solved rate is the market's expected
  return; subtract the current risk-free rate and you have the **implied ERP** —
  typically around **4–5% for the U.S.** He recomputes and publishes it monthly,
  and annually in his *"Equity Risk Premium"* paper (every year since 2008).

The beauty of the implied approach is that it's **internally consistent**: it
reflects today's prices and today's rates, not the average of a vanished era.

### Step 4 — Blend in debt to get the WACC

Debt is usually cheaper than equity (and interest is tax-deductible), so we blend
the two by their market-value weights:

```
WACC = (E/V) × cost of equity
     + (D/V) × cost of debt × (1 − tax rate)
```

where `E` is the market value of equity, `D` the market value of debt, and
`V = E + D`. This WACC is the discount rate for FCFF.

### Step 5 — Discount the explicit-period cash flows

Bring each forecast year's FCFF back to today by dividing by `(1 + WACC)` raised
to the number of years away. Year-3 cash discounted at 9% is worth
`FCFF₃ / 1.09³` today.

### Step 6 — Estimate the terminal value

You can't forecast forever, so at the end of the explicit window you assume the
firm settles into **stable, perpetual growth** at rate `g`:

```
Terminal value = FCFF(next year) / (WACC − g)
```

<Callout type="warning">
**`g` must not exceed the long-run growth rate of the economy** (roughly the
risk-free rate). If you let a company grow faster than the economy *forever*, you
are implicitly claiming it eventually becomes larger than the entire economy —
which is impossible. This is the single most abused number in DCF.
</Callout>

This terminal value is a number measured *at the end of the explicit window*, so
you must then discount it back to today, just like in Step 5. (A private-equity
or VC valuation often substitutes an **exit multiple** — final-year EBITDA × an
assumed sale multiple — for the perpetuity formula, but the discipline is the
same: be modest.)

### Step 7 — Sum to firm (enterprise) value

```
Firm value = PV of explicit cash flows + PV of terminal value
```

### Step 8 — Bridge down to value per share

```
Equity value = Firm value − Net debt (debt − cash) − other claims
              (minority interests, employee options, etc.)

Value per share = Equity value / shares outstanding
```

Now compare that per-share value to the market price. Cheaper than your value =
a potential buy; pricier = a potential sell.

This bridge is the step beginners most often botch — they value the whole firm,
then forget that lenders get paid before shareholders. Walk it explicitly,
using the numbers from the worked example below:

### Step 9 — Cross-check with relative valuation

Now run the *other* family of valuation as a sanity check (we go deeper on it in
its own section below). Compute a market multiple — **P/E**, **EV/EBITDA**,
**EV/Sales** — for a set of comparable companies, take the **median**, and apply
it to your target. If your DCF says $15 and the peer multiples imply $14–16,
that's reassuring; if they're miles apart, find out *why* before trusting either.
This is a sanity check on your intrinsic number, **not** a replacement for it.

### Step 10 — Stress-test the story

Re-run the model with more pessimistic and optimistic assumptions to get a
**range** of values, and revisit it whenever the facts change. Valuation is a
living estimate, not a one-time verdict.

## A worked example, with numbers

Let's value a simple business end to end. *(Round numbers, instructor-built — the
method is Damodaran's; the company is made up.)*

**The story:** A stable, mature consumer-goods firm. Its products are boring and
dependable; it isn't going to surprise anyone. We'll model it as generating
**$100M of FCFF next year, growing 3% a year forever** — slow, steady, economy-
like growth. Because it carries modest debt, we'll keep this first pass simple
and treat the cost of equity as the discount rate, then bridge for debt at the
end.

**Discount rate (CAPM):**

```
Risk-free rate = 4.0%
Beta           = 0.90   (a bit calmer than the market)
ERP            = 5.0%

Cost of equity = 4.0% + 0.90 × 5.0% = 8.5%
```

**Step A — the explicit 5-year forecast.** We follow the real DCF recipe: lay out
an explicit window (here 5 years), then bolt a terminal value onto the end. FCFF
starts at $100M and grows 3% a year. Discount each year back at 8.5%:

| Year | FCFF ($M) | Discount factor (1.085ⁿ) | PV ($M) |
|------|-----------|--------------------------|---------|
| 1 | 100.00 | 1.085 | 92.2 |
| 2 | 103.00 | 1.177 | 87.5 |
| 3 | 106.09 | 1.277 | 83.1 |
| 4 | 109.27 | 1.386 | 78.8 |
| 5 | 112.55 | 1.504 | 74.9 |
| | | **PV of explicit 5 yrs** | **≈ 416** |

**Step B — the terminal value at the end of year 5.** Year-6 FCFF is
`112.55 × 1.03 = $115.93M`. Capitalize it as a growing perpetuity, then discount
that lump sum back five years:

```
Terminal value (end of yr 5) = 115.93 / (0.085 − 0.03) = $2,108M
PV of terminal value         = 2,108 / 1.085⁵ ≈ $1,402M
```

**Step C — sum to firm value:**

```
Firm value = PV of explicit cash flows + PV of terminal value
           = 416 + 1,402
           = $1,818M
```

<Callout type="note">
**A neat shortcut, and why it works.** Because this firm grows at a *constant* 3%
from year 1, the explicit-window-plus-terminal-value calculation collapses into a
single growing-perpetuity formula valued from today:

```
Firm value = FCFF(next year) / (r − g)
           = 100 / (0.085 − 0.03)
           = 100 / 0.055
           = $1,818M  ← identical
```

The two routes give the **same $1,818M**. We built out the full 5-year schedule
anyway because real firms *don't* grow at one steady rate — the explicit window is
where you model the interesting, changing years before things settle down. The
shortcut only works when growth is constant from the start.
</Callout>

**Bridge to per share.** Subtract net debt of $300M → equity value = $1,518M.
With 100M shares outstanding:

```
Value per share = 1,518 / 100 = $15.18
```

So if the stock trades at **$12**, it looks undervalued on this story; at **$20**,
overvalued.

### The most important intuition: the denominator is everything

Look at what happens if our story gets just slightly more optimistic about
long-run growth — `g` rises from 3% to 4%, while `r` stays 8.5%:

```
Firm value = 100 / (0.085 − 0.04) = 100 / 0.045 = $2,222M
```

A one-percentage-point change in `g` lifted firm value by **22%**. The reason is
that `(r − g)` is a small number in the denominator, so small changes in it swing
the answer hugely.

## Relative valuation: the fast cross-check

Intrinsic value is the anchor; **relative valuation** is the quick reality check,
and it's how most of Wall Street actually prices things. The idea is simple:
similar assets should sell for similar prices, so value a company by comparing its
**multiple** to a peer group's.

The recipe:

1. **Pick the right multiple.** **P/E** (price ÷ earnings) for profitable firms;
   **EV/EBITDA** when capital structures differ; **P/B** (price-to-book) for banks
   and asset-heavy firms; **EV/Sales** or **P/S** for fast-growing or
   loss-making companies with no earnings to divide by yet.
2. **Choose genuine comparables** — same industry, similar growth, risk, and
   profitability. Cherry-picking flattering peers is the cardinal sin here.
3. **Compute the peer multiple, using the median** (the middle value) rather than
   the mean — one outlier shouldn't drag the whole comparison.
4. **Apply it** to your target's metric (peer median P/E × your firm's EPS = an
   implied price).
5. **Compare to the current price** — and to your DCF.

### A quick worked multiple

Suppose our consumer-goods firm earns **$1.30 of EPS**, and three close peers
trade at P/E ratios of **11×, 13×, and 18×**.

```
Peer median P/E = 13×   (the middle of 11, 13, 18 — note 18 is an outlier)
Implied price   = 13 × $1.30 = $16.90
```

That $16.90 sits comfortably near our DCF value of $15.18 — the two methods
roughly agree, which raises our confidence. Had we naively used the *mean* P/E of
14× (pulled up by the 18× outlier), we'd have gotten $18.20 and overstated value.

<Callout type="warning">
**Every multiple hides a DCF inside it.** A high P/E isn't automatically
"expensive" — it can be justified by higher growth, lower risk, or better cash
conversion. So always ask: *"what story about growth and risk makes this multiple
reasonable?"* Relative valuation is fast precisely because it borrows the market's
assumptions — which is also its weakness: it assumes the peers are correctly
priced. In a bubble, every comp is overpriced together.
</Callout>

## Honest criticisms and limitations

Damodaran is unusually candid about the weaknesses of his own toolkit. Take these
seriously.

- **Garbage in, garbage out.** A DCF is only as good as its inputs. The crisp math
  creates a *false sense of precision* around forecasts that are inherently
  uncertain.
- **Terminal value dominates.** Often **60–80%+** of a DCF value sits in the
  terminal value, which hinges on that fragile `(r − g)` denominator. Much of your
  "valuation" is really a bet on the distant, unknowable future.

- **CAPM and beta are contested.** Decades of research (Fama-French and others)
  find weak empirical support for beta as *the* measure of risk — yet the
  framework leans on it.
- **Valuation as advocacy.** Damodaran's own warning: analysts often
  reverse-engineer their inputs to land on a price they already wanted, turning
  valuation into a sales tool — "post-rationalized pricing" dressed up as
  analysis. A compelling story can be stretched to justify nearly any number.
- **Being right about value ≠ being right about timing.** A correct intrinsic
  value can stay disconnected from the market price for years. Value tells you
  *what* something is worth, not *when* (or whether) the market will agree.
- **Hard cases stay hard.** Young loss-making companies, cyclicals, banks, and
  intangible-heavy firms strain DCF. He wrote *The Dark Side of Valuation* for
  exactly these, but the honest output is still a wide range, not a point.
- **His own record is mixed** — as everyone's is. His public valuations of names
  like Tesla and Uber have at times diverged sharply from where the market went,
  and he documents these openly rather than hiding them.

## Common beginner pitfalls

<Callout type="warning">
1. **Confusing price with value** — chasing what the market is doing instead of
   estimating what the business is worth.
2. **Setting terminal growth `g` too high** — it must stay ≤ the risk-free
   rate / long-run economy growth.
3. **Mismatching cash flows and discount rates** — discount FCFF with WACC, and
   equity cash flows (FCFE) with the cost of equity. Never discount equity cash
   flows at WACC.
4. **Botching the equity bridge** — forgetting to subtract net debt, or ignoring
   options, minority interests, and cross-holdings.
5. **Over-precise outputs** — quoting "$47.32" instead of a range. Valuation has
   error bars.
6. **Numbers with no story** (random growth rates) or **a story with no numbers**
   (pure hype). The two must reconcile.
7. **Inconsistent assumptions** — high growth with no reinvestment, or margins
   that expand forever. Growth must be *paid for* with reinvestment, and
   competitive edges fade.
8. **Confusing a great company with a great investment** — a wonderful business
   at too high a price is a bad buy; a mediocre one cheap enough can be a good one.
9. **Cherry-picked comps / stale inputs** — flattering peer groups in relative
   valuation, or a blindly historical ERP and a risk-free rate that doesn't match
   the currency of your cash flows.
</Callout>

## Putting it to work

You don't need a Bloomberg terminal to start — Damodaran built his whole career
proving that.

1. **Read *The Little Book of Valuation* (2011).** It's the gentle on-ramp to
   everything above — both the DCF and the multiples.
2. **Download one of his free spreadsheets** from Damodaran Online and pick a
   simple, stable, profitable company you understand (a consumer-goods or utility
   name is far easier than a startup — a *mature* life-cycle firm).
3. **Pull his free datasets** for the ERP, the industry beta, and the cost of
   capital for that sector — you don't have to estimate them from scratch.
4. **Write the story first**, in plain English, *before* you touch a single
   number.
5. **Build the DCF**, then immediately run a low / base / high case to get a
   *range*.
6. **Cross-check with a multiple** versus a median of genuine peers.
7. **Compare your range to the market price.** If they're miles apart, your first
   job is to interrogate your *own* assumptions, not to assume the market is wrong.
8. **Keep a journal** of your valuations and revisit them as facts change — that
   feedback loop is how the skill actually compounds.

The goal isn't a magic price target. It's the *habit of thinking like an owner*:
demanding that a story and its numbers agree before you risk a dollar.
