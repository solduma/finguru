## The one-sentence version

In 1983 a famous commodities trader bet his partner that he could take ordinary
people off the street, teach them a written set of rules in two weeks, hand them
millions of dollars of his own money, and turn them into great traders. He won
the bet. The rules he taught are the cleanest beginner-level example of
**systematic trend following** — and the decades since are an honest lesson in
how an edge erodes once everyone knows it.

<Callout type="key">
The Turtle method in one breath: **trade a basket of unrelated futures markets,
buy a breakout to a new high (or sell a new low), size every position so the
same dollar amount is at risk regardless of how wild the market is, cut losers
fast, and let the rare big winner run.** Most trades lose small; a few trends
pay for everything. The skill being taught was never prediction — it was
*discipline and risk-sizing.*
</Callout>

## Who they were

In the early 1980s, **Richard Dennis** was a Chicago legend who had famously
turned a tiny stake into a fortune commonly put at **$200 million or more** — and
he credited a *repeatable method*, not a gift.

His business partner, **William Eckhardt**, disagreed. Eckhardt thought great
trading was innate — you either had the instinct or you didn't. To settle the
argument, the two ran a real experiment with real money.

- They placed a recruiting ad in *Barron's* and other papers and sifted
  thousands of applicants.
- They trained **two cohorts** — one in **December 1983**, one in **December
  1984**. You will see "13 Turtles" and "23 Turtles" both quoted: the smaller
  number is the first 1983 class, the larger is the total across both years. The
  safe phrasing is **"about 21–23 across both classes"** (commonly 21 men and 2
  women).
- Dennis named them the **Turtles**. He had recently visited a turtle-breeding
  farm in Singapore and reportedly said, *"We are going to grow traders just
  like they grow turtles in Singapore."*
- After roughly **two weeks** of instruction, each Turtle received a real trading
  account funded with Dennis's own capital — figures from **$250,000 up to
  about $2 million** per trader, scaled up over time for those who did well.

The whole arrangement was secret. The Turtles signed a **10-year
non-disclosure agreement** that expired in **late 1993**. After expiry the rules
began to leak and were even sold by others, which is why one of the original
Turtles, **Curtis Faith**, eventually published the complete rules for free as
the *Original Turtle Trading Rules* (around 2003) — the primary source this
lesson leans on.

## What "trend following" even means

A **trend follower** does not try to predict turning points or find cheap assets.
The entire bet is that **markets that have started moving in one direction tend
to keep moving that way long enough to profit** — and the trader simply climbs
aboard an established move and rides it until it ends.

The mechanism that defines a trend is the **breakout**: price pushing past its
own recent extreme. If a market trades higher than it has at any point in the
last 20 days, *something* has changed, and the trend follower treats that as the
signal to get long.

The catch every beginner underestimates: **most breakouts fail.** The trend
follower is wrong most of the time — winning maybe 30–40% of trades — and stays
profitable only because the losses are tiny and the occasional monster trend is
enormous. That asymmetry is the engine, and tolerating a long string of small
losses while waiting for it is the hard part.

## The Turtle rules, step by step

The Turtles were given a genuinely complete, mechanical system. Here is the
beginner's tour of each piece.

### The markets

They traded only **liquid U.S. futures** — currencies, bonds and interest rates,
metals (gold, silver, copper), energies (crude oil, heating oil, gas), and the
"softs" (coffee, cocoa, sugar, cotton). A **futures contract** is a standardized
agreement to buy or sell something at a set price on a future date; liquid ones
trade in huge volume, so you can get in and out cleanly.

They deliberately **excluded grains and meats** — grains because Dennis already
held the maximum position the exchange allowed, and meats because of a contemporary
floor-trading corruption scandal. The point of using *many unrelated markets* is
diversification: when one market chops sideways and bleeds small losses, another
may be in the big trend that pays for the year.

### "N" — the volatility ruler

Everything in the system is measured in units of **N**, the Turtles' name for
how much a market typically moves in a day. N is essentially the **Average True
Range (ATR)** — the average daily trading range, including overnight gaps —
computed as a **20-day exponential moving average of the True Range:**

> `N = (19 × prior N + today's True Range) ÷ 20`

<Callout type="warning">
A very common mis-statement: N is **not** the standard 14-day Wilder ATR you see
in most charting packages. It is a **20-day exponential moving average of True
Range.** The number 20 and the smoothing both matter — if you "test the Turtle
system" with a 14-day ATR, you are not testing the Turtle system.
</Callout>

N is the ruler that makes every other rule work. A volatile market has a big N
(it moves a lot each day); a calm market has a small N. By expressing position
size, stops, and pyramiding *in multiples of N*, the system automatically treats
a wild market and a sleepy one the same way.

### Position sizing — the genuinely clever part

This is the idea worth stealing even if you never trade a future. The Turtles
defined one **Unit** as the position size where **a 1 N move in price equals 1%
of the account's equity.**

> `Unit size = (1% of account) ÷ (N × dollars per point of the contract)`

Because N is bigger in volatile markets, the formula automatically hands you a
**smaller position** there, and a larger one in calm markets. Every market ends
up contributing roughly the **same dollar risk** — a form of
**volatility-normalized risk parity** (sizing positions so each carries an equal
share of the portfolio's risk).

### Entries — two breakout systems

The Turtles ran two systems side by side:

- **System 1 (faster).** Enter on a **20-day breakout** — one tick beyond the
  highest high (go long) or lowest low (go short) of the prior 20 days. It has a
  **filter:** *skip* the breakout if the **previous** breakout in that market
  would have been a winning trade. Concretely: if the last 20-day breakout in gold
  already paid off, you sit out the *next* one — and only re-engage on the
  following 55-day breakout. The logic is subtle but important: it keeps you flat
  through choppy false starts, then makes sure you are *on board* for the big
  trend that so often follows a whipsaw.
- **System 2 (slower).** Enter on a **55-day breakout**, with **no filter** —
  every 55-day breakout is taken. The longer lookback catches only bigger, more
  established moves, so it needs no skip rule.

### Pyramiding — adding to a winner

A trend that works deserves more money. The Turtles **added one Unit for every
½ N the market moved in their favor**, up to a **maximum of 4 Units** in a single
market. This "pyramiding" loads the most capital onto the trades that are already
proving themselves — the opposite of the amateur instinct to add to losers.

### Stops — where the survival lives

Every Unit carried a **2 N stop** — an automatic exit if price moved 2 N against
it. Since one Unit is sized so 1 N equals 1% of the account, a 2 N stop caps the
loss at about **2% of the account per trade.** As new Units were pyramided on,
the stops on the earlier Units were **raised** so the *total* risk across all 4
Units stayed at roughly 2%. (A "Whipsaw" variant used a tighter ½ N stop for
traders who could stomach more frequent small losses.)

### Exits — the hardest rule to obey

You ride a trend until it breaks the *other* way:

- **System 1** exits on a **10-day breakout against** the position — for a long,
  a new 10-day low.
- **System 2** exits on a **20-day breakout against** the position — a new 20-day
  low for a long.

The rules PDF calls letting profits run *"the single most difficult part."*
Sitting through a winner giving back a chunk of open profit before the 10- or
20-day exit triggers is psychologically brutal — and bailing early is exactly
how trend followers ruin the math.

### Portfolio limits

To stop the book becoming one giant correlated bet, the Turtles capped exposure:
**4 Units** in any single market, **6** across closely correlated markets, **10**
across loosely correlated ones, and **12 Units maximum** in one direction (all
long or all short).

<Callout type="note">
Notice what is **absent** from every rule above: any opinion about *value*, *news*,
*the economy*, or *where the market is going*. The Turtle system is pure price
mechanics. That is a feature — it removes the trader's ego and forecasting from
the loop, which is precisely where most people lose money.
</Callout>

## The numbers — real, but handle with care

By the Turtle-affiliated accounts (the *Original Rules* PDF and Curtis Faith's
*Way of the Turtle*), the group earned an **average compound annual return of
roughly 80%** over the experiment, and Dennis reportedly cleared on the order of
**$100–175 million** from the program.

<Callout type="warning">
The "~80% a year" figure is the **program number from sources tied to the
Turtles themselves** — never independently audited, so attribute it rather than
repeating it as fact. Returns varied enormously *between* Turtles (some were cut
for losing), so a program average is not "what a Turtle made." And the legend
skips Dennis's hard ending: he lost heavily in the **1987–88** turmoil and **wound
down his outside-money operations in spring 1988.** Even the system's designer
took a brutal hit when markets turned violent.
</Callout>

Among the Turtles, two names matter most:

- **Curtis Faith** claimed the strongest record — reportedly about **$31 million**
  in profits — and wrote the best-known Turtle book. **Treat his self-reported
  figures cautiously.** Fellow Turtle **Jerry Parker** and the journalist
  **Michael Covel** (author of *The Complete TurtleTrader*) have publicly disputed
  Faith's account of who performed best and even of how the systems were used.
  Covel's reporting is the standard journalistic counterweight to Faith's memoir.
- **Jerry Parker** became the most *commercially* successful Turtle. He founded
  **Chesapeake Capital** in 1988 and built the Turtle approach into an
  institutional business, helping turn trend following into the modern
  **managed-futures / CTA** industry (a **CTA**, or Commodity Trading Advisor, is
  a regulated manager who trades futures for clients).

## The honest failure modes

This is where the lesson earns its keep. The Turtle story is usually told as a
triumph; the more useful half is what happened *after*.

### A public edge decays

Once the rules were public — the free PDF, then the 2007 Faith and Covel books —
the simple breakout edge eroded as thousands of traders crowded into the same
signals. Back-tests of the *original* system show returns dropping sharply after
the mid-1980s and running roughly **flat from about 1996 to 2009.** An edge that
everyone can see and copy is, by definition, getting harder to harvest.

### Trend following is painfully cyclical

The industry's standard yardstick, the **SG Trend Index**, makes the cycles plain.
Trend-following CTAs broadly underperformed for so long in **2009–2018** that the
period earned the nickname the **"lost decade."** Then trend roared back in
**2022** — its best year in over two decades, as the strategy thrived on rising
rates and commodity moves — only to suffer a record drawdown of about **−18.7%**
over the 12 months to **April 2025**, the worst stretch in the index's 25-year
history.

### Even with the rules, most people can't follow them

The deepest lesson is behavioral. The win rate is low (most breakouts fail), the
losing streaks are long, and the drawdowns are deep. Handed the exact rules,
most people still abandon them mid-drawdown — exiting winners early, skipping the
scary breakout that becomes the year's biggest trend, or quietly oversizing after
a good run. The bet Dennis won was really narrower than "trading can be taught":
what can be taught is **discipline and risk-sizing.** The edge is worthless to
someone who can't sit still.

<Callout type="key">
The Turtle experiment's enduring takeaway is not the specific 20- and 55-day
breakouts — those have largely been competed away. It is the **architecture**:
a fully written, mechanical plan; volatility-based position sizing so every bet
risks the same amount; hard stops that cap each loss near a fixed small percent;
and the patience to let a small number of big winners carry a long string of
small losses. That architecture outlives any one signal.
</Callout>

## Key books and sources

- **Curtis Faith, _Original Turtle Trading Rules_ (~2003, free PDF)** — the
  primary, rule-by-rule source for N, sizing, entries, stops, and exits.
- **Curtis Faith, _Way of the Turtle_ (2007)** — an insider memoir; vivid, but
  treat its self-reported performance claims cautiously.
- **Michael Covel, _The Complete TurtleTrader_ (2007)** — the journalistic
  account and the standard counterweight to Faith's version of events.

## Key takeaways

- The Turtles came from a **1983 bet** between Richard Dennis and William
  Eckhardt over whether great trading can be taught. Dennis trained **about
  21–23 novices** across **1983 and 1984** cohorts, funded them with his own
  money, and won the bet.
- The method is textbook **systematic trend following**: trade many unrelated
  liquid futures, **buy 20- or 55-day breakouts**, cut losers fast, and let rare
  big trends run. No forecasting is involved.
- **"N"** is the volatility ruler — a **20-day EMA of True Range**, essentially
  ATR — and it powers everything: positions are sized so **1 N = 1% of equity**,
  giving smaller bets in wild markets and larger ones in calm markets.
- Risk control is mechanical: a **2 N stop** caps each trade near **2%** of the
  account; pyramid up to **4 Units** every **½ N** of favorable move; exit on a
  **10-day (System 1)** or **20-day (System 2)** breakout against the position.
- The headline **"~80% a year"** return is a **program figure from Turtle-linked
  sources, not independently audited** — and individual results varied hugely.
  Curtis Faith's personal claims are disputed by Jerry Parker and Michael Covel.
- The edge **decayed once the rules went public**, and trend following is
  **deeply cyclical** — a "lost decade" (2009–2018), a 2022 revival, then a
  record drawdown to 2025. The strategy is real but demands patience through long
  bad stretches.
- The transferable lesson is **discipline and volatility-based position sizing**,
  not the specific breakouts. What Dennis proved teachable was risk management,
  not a crystal ball.

<Callout type="warning">
This is educational material, not financial advice. The original Turtle signals
have been widely published and largely arbitraged away; trend following carries
large drawdowns and long losing periods, and futures involve leverage that can
amplify losses well beyond your initial stake. Nothing here is a recommendation
to trade any market or system.
</Callout>
