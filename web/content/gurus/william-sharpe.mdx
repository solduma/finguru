## Why start here

Most beginners chase the investment with the biggest return. Sharpe's life work
says that's the wrong question. The right question is: **how much risk did you
take to get that return — and was that risk even the kind that pays?**

William Sharpe gave us a precise way to answer both halves of that. From him we
inherit one of the most quoted ideas in all of finance (you only get paid for
risk you can't diversify away) and two tools every serious investor still uses:
**CAPM**, which tells you the fair return an asset *should* deliver, and the
**Sharpe ratio**, which scores how much return a portfolio actually delivered
*per unit of risk.* Learn these and you'll never again confuse "made the most
money" with "invested well."

## Who he was

**William Forsyth Sharpe** (born June 16, 1934, in Boston, raised in Riverside,
California) is an American economist. He took all three degrees — B.A. (1955),
M.A. (1956), and Ph.D. (1961) — at **UCLA**, after first enrolling at Berkeley
intending to study medicine, then switching to business and finally economics.

His doctoral work built a simplified, single-factor model of how security prices
relate to one another. It was formally advised by **Armen Alchian**, but his most
important intellectual link was **Harry Markowitz**, the father of modern
portfolio theory, who mentored him informally in a role much like a dissertation
advisor. Markowitz had shown how to build an efficient *portfolio*; Sharpe asked
the natural next question — **what return should a single asset earn inside such a
world?** — and that question became CAPM.

- **Career:** the RAND Corporation (from 1956, where he overlapped with
  Markowitz), then faculty posts at the University of Washington (1961–68), UC
  Irvine (1968–70), and from **1970 Stanford University**, where he taught until
  retiring from teaching in 1989 (he remains Professor Emeritus of Finance). He
  served as President of the American Finance Association.
- **Nobel:** he shared the **1990 Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences**
  with **Harry Markowitz** and **Merton Miller**, principally for developing
  CAPM — the model explaining how asset prices reflect risk.
- **In industry:** he co-founded **Sharpe-Russell Research** (1986) with the
  Frank Russell Company, then **Financial Engines** (1996, with Joseph Grundfest
  and Craig Johnson), a firm that pioneered algorithmic, online retirement advice
  (acquired for roughly $3 billion in 2018).
- **Other lasting tools:** the **single-index model** (1963), a **binomial method
  for valuing options**, a gradient method for asset-allocation optimization, and
  **returns-based style analysis** (inferring a fund's true asset mix from the
  pattern of its returns).

<Callout type="key">
Sharpe didn't invent CAPM alone. **Jack Treynor** (~1962, unpublished),
**John Lintner** (1965), and **Jan Mossin** (1966) arrived at essentially the
same model independently and near-simultaneously. Sharpe's 1964 paper is the most
cited, and he was the one awarded the Nobel — but credit the others too, a small
habit that marks you as someone who actually read the history.
</Callout>

## The core idea: two kinds of risk

Everything Sharpe built rests on splitting risk into two boxes.

- **Unsystematic (idiosyncratic) risk** — danger specific to *one* company: a
  CEO scandal, a product recall, a factory fire. The crucial fact is that you
  can almost *erase* this risk by holding many different stocks. One blows up,
  another surprises to the upside, and across a broad basket they largely cancel
  out. Because any investor can diversify it away essentially for free, the
  market refuses to pay you for bearing it.
- **Systematic (market) risk** — danger that moves the *whole* market at once: a
  recession, an interest-rate shock, a war. No amount of diversification escapes
  it — when the tide goes out, almost every boat drops. Because it *can't* be
  avoided, investors demand extra return to hold it. **This is the only risk
  that earns a premium.**

The single most important takeaway for a beginner: **diversification is not
optional polish — it is how you delete the risk you'd never be paid for anyway.**

How much diversification is "enough"? In a developed market, holding on the
order of **30–40 well-chosen stocks** removes the great majority of
diversifiable risk. (A low-cost index fund does this for you in one purchase.)

<Callout type="note">
The number isn't magic. The first 20–30 names do most of the work; after that
each new holding trims a little less. The lesson is directional, not a precise
target: a handful of stocks leaves you badly exposed to company-specific risk; a
broad basket does not.
</Callout>

## Tool 1 — CAPM: the fair return for an asset

The **Capital Asset Pricing Model** answers: *given how much market risk an asset
carries, what return should it deliver?*

**E(Rᵢ) = R_f + βᵢ × [E(R_m) − R_f]**

In words: the fair expected return equals the **risk-free rate** plus a reward
that scales with how much the asset **amplifies** overall market swings.

### Beta: the one number CAPM runs on

**Beta (β)** measures how much an asset moves *relative to the market.*
Formally it's `Cov(asset, market) / Var(market)` — but you rarely compute it by
hand. In practice you regress the stock's *excess* returns on the market's
*excess* returns, and the slope is beta; finance data sites also publish it. Read
it like this:

- **β = 1** — moves in lockstep with the market.
- **β > 1** — amplifies the market (a β of 1.5 tends to rise ~15% when the
  market rises 10%, and fall harder too). More market risk, higher fair return.
- **β < 1** — dampens the market (defensive). Less market risk, lower fair
  return.
- **β = 0** — uncorrelated with the market (like cash); CAPM says it should earn
  only the risk-free rate.

### The recipe — step by step

1. **Get the risk-free rate (R_f).** Use the yield on a safe government bond
   (e.g., a 3-month T-bill or 10-year U.S. Treasury). Say **4%**.
2. **Estimate the expected market return E(R_m).** Use a broad index (the S&P
   500) and a long-run estimate. Say **10%**.
3. **Compute the market risk premium** = E(R_m) − R_f = 10% − 4% = **6%**. This
   is the reward for bearing one unit of market risk (β = 1).
4. **Find the asset's beta.** Look it up or regress its returns on the market.
5. **Plug in:** E(Rᵢ) = R_f + β × premium. That's the return the asset *should*
   deliver for the market risk it carries.
6. **Compare to your own view.** If you expect *more* than CAPM's number, the
   asset plots **above** the line and looks **undervalued**; *less*, and it plots
   **below** the line and looks **overvalued.**

### The Security Market Line

Draw that equation as a graph — beta on the x-axis, expected return on the
y-axis — and you get the **Security Market Line (SML)**. Its **y-intercept is the
risk-free rate** and its **slope is the market premium.** In theory, every
fairly-priced asset sits *on* the line; CAPM is literally the claim that "every
asset lies on the SML."

### Worked example

A stock with **β = 1.5**, R_f = 4%, expected market return = 10%:

E(R) = 4% + 1.5 × (10% − 4%) = 4% + 1.5 × 6% = 4% + 9% = **13%**.

So 13% is the *fair* return for its risk. If your own homework says it'll return
**15%**, it offers 2% more than its risk warrants → it plots *above* the SML →
looks **undervalued.** If you only expect **11%**, it's *below* the line →
**overvalued**, and you're being underpaid for the market risk you'd take on.

## Tool 2 — the Sharpe ratio: return per unit of risk

CAPM looks *forward* (what return *should* an asset earn?). The **Sharpe ratio**
looks at the *record* (how much return did a portfolio actually earn for the risk
it took?). Sharpe introduced it in 1966, calling it the **reward-to-variability
ratio.**

**S = (R_p − R_f) / σ**

That is: **excess return divided by volatility.**

- **R_p − R_f** is the **excess return** (Sharpe calls it the *differential
  return*) — what you earned *above* parking the money in cash. Beating the
  risk-free rate is the only reward that justifies taking risk at all.
- **σ (sigma)** is the **standard deviation** of that excess return — a measure
  of how much it bounces around. Bigger σ = a wilder, riskier ride.

A higher Sharpe ratio means you were paid more for each unit of stomach-churning
you endured.

<Callout type="note">
In Sharpe's 1994 generalization the figure you subtract can be **any relevant
benchmark**, not only the risk-free rate. The Sharpe ratio is then the average
*differential* return (your return minus the benchmark's) divided by the standard
deviation of that difference.
</Callout>

### The recipe

1. **Collect periodic returns** for the portfolio (e.g., 36–60 monthly returns).
2. **Subtract the risk-free rate (or benchmark)** each period to get the
   "differential return."
3. **Average** those excess returns → the numerator.
4. **Take the standard deviation** of the excess returns → the denominator. In a
   spreadsheet with the excess returns in cells C1:C60, Sharpe's own recipe is
   literally `=AVERAGE(C1:C60)/STDEV(C1:C60)`.
5. **Annualize** if your data is monthly: multiply by √12. (The mean scales with
   time but the standard deviation scales with √time, so the *ratio* scales by
   √T.) A monthly ratio of 0.115 becomes ~0.40 a year.
6. **Compare two investments fairly:** the higher Sharpe ratio gave more reward
   for the risk borne — *even if its headline return was lower.*

### Worked example — why the lower return can win

This is the example Sharpe himself uses to show why you *must* subtract the
risk-free rate. Fund A *looks* like the winner: higher return, but notice it's
also far more volatile.

Now divide each fund's excess return by its volatility:

- **Fund A:** (12% − 5%) / 10% = 0.07 / 0.10 = **0.70**
- **Fund B:** (9% − 5%) / 4% = 0.04 / 0.04 = **1.00**

The ranking **flips.** Plotted on their own axis, the reversal is impossible to
miss — the lower-return fund wins:

Fund B is the **better risk-adjusted performer** despite the lower return.

<Callout type="note">
**The proof this isn't a trick.** Take Sharpe's own example: Fund X returns 5% at
10% σ; Fund Y returns 8% at 20% σ; risk-free = 3%. Raw return ÷ risk makes X
(0.50) look better than Y (0.40). But the *excess-return* Sharpe ratios are
X = (5−3)/10 = **0.20** and Y = (8−3)/20 = **0.25** — Y wins. Why does that
matter? Put **half your money in Y and half in cash**: you get a 5.5% return at
10% σ — beating X's 5.0% return at the *same* 10% σ. The higher Sharpe ratio
genuinely lets you build a better portfolio.
</Callout>

### What's a "good" Sharpe ratio?

You'll hear rules of thumb — above 1 is good, above 2 very good, above 3
excellent. Treat them loosely. For perspective on what's actually achievable
over the long run:

<Callout type="warning">
If someone shows you a strategy with a long-run Sharpe ratio of 3, your first
reaction should be suspicion, not envy. Over long horizons, diversified
portfolios simply don't sustain that. A sky-high ratio usually means a short
sample, cherry-picked dates, hidden leverage, or a tail risk waiting to detonate
(see the criticisms below).
</Callout>

## How the two tools fit together

The two tools answer different questions with **different risk inputs** — and
confusing those inputs is the classic beginner error.

<ConceptDiagram
  caption="CAPM and the Sharpe ratio answer different questions with different risk inputs. CAPM looks forward and uses BETA (systematic risk only) to set a fair expected return. The Sharpe ratio looks back and uses STANDARD DEVIATION (total risk = systematic + idiosyncratic) to score realized performance. Don't mix up the inputs."
  chart={`flowchart LR
  A[CAPM] -->|input: beta\n systematic risk only| B[Fair EXPECTED return\n forward-looking]
  C[Sharpe ratio] -->|input: std deviation\n total risk| D[REALIZED return per unit risk\n backward-looking scorecard]`}
/>

<Callout type="key">
There's a third tool that bridges them: the **Treynor ratio** is the
beta-based cousin of the Sharpe ratio — excess return divided by **beta**
(systematic risk) instead of standard deviation (total risk). Use Treynor when
the portfolio is one slice of an already-diversified whole (idiosyncratic risk is
gone, so only beta matters); use Sharpe when it's your *entire* portfolio.
</Callout>

## Key papers and books

**Papers**

- *A Simplified Model for Portfolio Analysis*, **Management Science** (1963) —
  the single-index model, the precursor to CAPM.
- *Capital Asset Prices: A Theory of Market Equilibrium under Conditions of
  Risk*, **Journal of Finance** (1964) — the CAPM paper (the work cited for his
  Nobel; famously submitted in 1962 and initially *rejected*).
- *Mutual Fund Performance*, **Journal of Business** (1966) — introduces the
  reward-to-variability (Sharpe) ratio.
- *Asset Allocation: Management Style and Performance Measurement*, **Journal of
  Portfolio Management** (1992) — returns-based style analysis.
- *The Sharpe Ratio*, **Journal of Portfolio Management** (1994) — formally
  renames and generalizes the measure (ex-ante vs. ex-post; benchmark-relative
  differential return).

**Books**

- *Portfolio Theory and Capital Markets* (1970; reissued 2000).
- *Asset Allocation Tools* (1987).
- *Investments* (with Gordon Alexander & Jeffery Bailey, 1999).
- *William F. Sharpe: Selected Works* (2012).

<Callout type="note">
A common myth dates the Sharpe ratio to 1994. It doesn't — Sharpe **introduced
it in 1966** as the "reward-to-variability ratio." The 1994 paper is a refinement
of an idea nearly three decades old.
</Callout>

## Honest criticisms and limitations

These models are foundational, not gospel. Knowing where they break is part of
using them well.

**Of CAPM**

- **It doesn't fit the data well.** The real risk-return line is *flatter* than
  CAPM predicts — the **low-volatility anomaly** (low-beta stocks beat their
  prediction). The **size effect** (small caps outperforming) and the **value
  effect** (cheap stocks outperforming) also contradict it. Fama and French
  (2004) concluded its empirical problems make "most applications of the model
  invalid," which spurred their **three-factor model.** The **Black (1972)
  zero-beta CAPM** patches the flatter-line problem.
- **Roll's critique (1977):** the true "market portfolio" would include
  *everything* — every stock, bond, house, and even human capital. That's
  unobservable, so CAPM is arguably untestable; we only ever test a proxy.
- **Beta isn't stable** — it's treated as fixed but drifts over time, and is a
  noisy estimate that changes with the look-back window.
- **The assumptions are idealized:** no taxes or trading costs, everyone can
  borrow and lend unlimited amounts at the risk-free rate, all investors share
  the same expectations and information and act rationally, and risk is measured
  only by variance (ignoring downside and skew). None is strictly true.

**Of the Sharpe ratio**

- **It assumes returns are roughly normal.** Real markets have **fat tails and
  skew** — rare, violent moves that σ undercounts and understates as risk.
- **It punishes upside and downside equally.** A big *gain* raises σ and *lowers*
  your Sharpe ratio, which feels backwards. The **Sortino ratio** fixes this by
  counting only downside volatility.
- **It can be gamed.** Strategies that sell deep out-of-the-money options, or
  hold illiquid assets whose prices update slowly (stale, smoothed marks),
  artificially lower apparent volatility and post a beautiful Sharpe ratio right
  up until they blow up. Goetzmann et al. (2002) showed that *maximizing* the
  Sharpe ratio perversely rewards taking hidden tail risk; Ponzi-like strategies
  look superb — until disaster.
- **It misleads over short samples.** The t-statistic of a Sharpe estimate is
  **Sharpe × √T**, so few data points = weak statistical significance and an
  overstated ratio. This is why researchers built the **deflated Sharpe ratio**
  (Bailey & López de Prado, 2012).
- **It ignores correlation.** As Sharpe himself stresses, "the Sharpe Ratio does
  not take correlations into account" — so it can't tell you how an asset fits
  *inside* an existing portfolio (that's where the Treynor ratio or full
  portfolio math comes in).

## Common beginner pitfalls

<Callout type="warning">
1. **Thinking higher raw return = better.** Without dividing by risk you can't
   compare. A wild 20% can be worse, risk-adjusted, than a calm 10%.
2. **Forgetting to subtract the risk-free rate (or benchmark).** Using raw
   return ÷ volatility gives wrong rankings — see the X-vs-Y proof above. It must
   be a *differential* return.
3. **Mixing time frequencies / not annualizing consistently.** Never compare a
   monthly Sharpe to an annual one; multiply monthly figures by √12 first.
4. **Confusing the two tools.** CAPM *predicts* a fair expected return from beta;
   the Sharpe ratio *scores realized* return per unit of total risk. Different
   inputs, different jobs.
5. **Mixing up the risk measures.** CAPM/beta reward only **systematic** risk;
   the Sharpe ratio's denominator is **total** risk (systematic + idiosyncratic).
   They are not interchangeable — Treynor is the beta-based cousin.
6. **Believing idiosyncratic risk pays.** It doesn't — that's *why* you
   diversify. Betting big on one "great" stock loads you with risk you aren't
   compensated for.
7. **Treating a high Sharpe ratio as "safe."** A high ratio can hide catastrophic
   tail risk (option-selling, illiquid marks). Volatility ≠ all risk.
8. **Trusting beta as fixed and precise.** It's a noisy estimate that drifts.
9. **Treating Sharpe rules of thumb as law.** A "3" over a few months is most
   likely luck, manipulation, or hidden tail risk — not skill.
10. **Using CAPM as a precise price oracle.** It's a teaching and benchmark
    framework with documented empirical failures, not a guaranteed valuation tool.
11. **Crediting Sharpe alone for CAPM, or dating the Sharpe ratio to 1994.** See
    above.
</Callout>

## Putting it to work

You don't need a quant desk to use Sharpe's ideas tomorrow:

- **Diversify by default.** Own a broad, low-cost index fund (or 30+ varied
  stocks) so the only risk you carry is the kind that actually pays. This is
  Sharpe's deepest practical lesson.
- **Judge everything risk-adjusted.** Before admiring a fund's return, ask for
  its volatility and compute a rough Sharpe ratio (`=AVERAGE/STDEV` of the excess
  returns in a spreadsheet). The flashy fund is often the worse buy.
- **Sanity-check what an asset "should" earn.** Look up a stock's beta and run
  the CAPM line in your head. If the only way the story works is by expecting far
  more than its fair return, be skeptical.
- **Distrust the spectacular.** A Sharpe ratio that looks too good usually is —
  it's hiding a short track record or a buried tail risk.

Sharpe didn't promise to beat the market. He gave you something more durable: a
clear-eyed way to see how much risk you're really taking, and whether you're
being paid for it.
