## The one-sentence version

A company's accountants can dress up its profits, but they cannot fake a cash
dividend — the money is either paid or it isn't. Geraldine Weiss built her whole
method on that single honest signal: **"dividends don't lie."** And from it she
extracted a strikingly simple way to value a blue-chip stock — by comparing
*today's* dividend yield to the stock's *own history*.

> **Dividend yield** = annual dividend per share ÷ share price.
> If a stock pays $2 a year and trades at $50, its yield is 2 ÷ 50 = **4%**.

Here's the key mechanical insight. The dividend a mature blue chip pays barely
moves from quarter to quarter — companies hate cutting it. So if the *yield*
swings around, it's almost entirely because the *price* moved. That makes yield a
quiet, low-noise gauge of cheapness:

- Yield near its **historical high** → the price is unusually **low** →
  **undervalued** (a buying zone).
- Yield near its **historical low** → the price is unusually **high** →
  **overvalued** (a selling zone).

<Callout type="key">
The whole theory rests on one assumption you must always check: that the dividend
is **secure** and the **business is stable.** When that holds, yield mean-reverts
around a reliable band and the method works. When it breaks — a coming dividend
cut, a changed business model — the band lies, and so does the signal.
</Callout>

## Who she was

**Geraldine Weiss** (born Geraldine Schmulowitz, **16 March 1926, San Francisco**;
died **25 April 2022, San Diego**, aged 96) earned a degree in business and
finance from **UC Berkeley in 1945.** She spent years reading every investing book
she could find — she leaned heavily on the value tradition of Benjamin Graham and
David Dodd — but in the male-dominated finance world of the 1950s and 60s, no firm
would hire a woman as an analyst.

So she went around the gatekeepers. In **1966**, at age 40, she co-founded the
investment newsletter ***Investment Quality Trends* (IQT)** (initially with a
partner, Fred Whitmore, whom she later bought out). She is widely credited as the
**first woman to found a U.S. investment-advisory newsletter** — a genuine
pioneer.

The signature human detail is this: she signed the newsletter **"G. Weiss,"**
deliberately hiding her *first name and gender*, because prospective subscribers
in that era simply doubted a woman could pick stocks. (Note that she was not
*anonymous* — readers knew "G. Weiss"; they just didn't know she was a woman.) She
kept the disguise for over a decade, until a **1977 appearance on the television
program *Wall Street Week with Louis Rukeyser*** finally revealed her publicly. The
press dubbed her **"the Grande Dame of Dividends."**

She ran IQT until **2002 — thirty-six years** — then handed the editing to
**Kelley Wright** while staying involved on strategy. The newsletter still
publishes today.

<Callout type="note">
Weiss laid out her method in two books. ***Dividends Don't Lie: Finding Value in
Blue-Chip Stocks*** (with co-author Janet Lowe), published in the **late 1980s
(sources give 1988 or 1989)**, is the canonical statement. ***The Dividend
Connection*** (1995) was co-written with her son, Gregory Weiss. A later update,
*Dividends Still Don't Lie* (2010), was written by **Kelley Wright, not by Weiss**
— so don't attribute it to her.
</Callout>

## Why dividends, and not earnings?

To understand why Weiss anchored everything to the dividend, you have to see what
she distrusted: **reported earnings.**

A company's reported profit is an *accounting* number. It depends on choices —
how fast to depreciate assets, when to recognize revenue, how to treat one-off
charges. None of that is necessarily dishonest, but it's all judgment, and
judgment can be nudged to flatter the result. Earnings can be "managed."

A **dividend** is different. It is cash that leaves the company's bank account and
lands in yours. A board cannot pay an imaginary dividend, and it is deeply
reluctant to cut a real one, because a cut screams "we're in trouble" and
hammers the share price. So the dividend is the one number management both *can't
fake* and *won't lightly change.* That stability is exactly what makes it useful.

This is the difference from a classic value tool like the P/E ratio (price ÷
earnings per share). The P/E's denominator is the noisy earnings number. Weiss's
yield uses the *dividend* as its anchor instead — a far steadier reference point.

## The core tool: the dividend-yield band

Here is the method in one picture. Take a blue chip with a long dividend history —
Weiss wanted **at least 5 years**, ideally more — and plot its dividend yield over
time. You'll typically find the yield oscillating between two fairly stable
extremes: a **historical high-yield line** it keeps bouncing up off, and a
**historical low-yield line** it keeps topping out at.

Those two lines define the stock's **valuation band.** Because the dividend itself
is roughly constant, *high yield = low price = cheap*, and *low yield = high price
= expensive.*

### Turning the band into a price target

The band also gives you a **fair-value estimate.** The standard expression of
Weiss's method (this is the textbook formulation used by later writers, not a
verbatim quote of hers) is:

> **Fair value ≈ expected annual dividend ÷ historical average dividend yield.**

Work through it. Suppose a stock is expected to pay **$3.00** next year, and over
the last decade its dividend yield has averaged **4%.** Then:

> Fair value = 3.00 ÷ 0.04 = **$75.**

Now compare that to the current price:

- If the stock trades at **$50**, its yield is 3 ÷ 50 = **6%** — well above the 4%
  average, so it looks **cheap** (price below the $75 fair value).
- If it trades at **$100**, its yield is 3 ÷ 100 = **3%** — below average, so it
  looks **expensive** (price above fair value).

<Callout type="note">
Notice the elegant symmetry: you can express the same judgment as a *price* (is it
below or above $75?) or as a *yield* (is it above or below the 4% average?). Weiss
preferred the yield framing because the dividend, her honest anchor, sits right in
it.
</Callout>

## The quality gate comes first

This is the part beginners miss. Weiss did **not** apply yield theory to just any
stock. The mean-reversion logic only holds for a financially sound, established
company whose dividend is safe. So before the yield band, every candidate had to
clear a **blue-chip quality screen.**

The commonly cited version has **seven criteria** (sources vary slightly on the
exact thresholds; these are the most frequently quoted):

1. The dividend yield is **undervalued relative to its own history** (the band
   test above).
2. The dividend has been **raised about 10%+ compounded annually over roughly 12
   years.**
3. The stock trades at **no more than 2× book value** (book value = the company's
   net assets per share).
4. The **P/E ratio is about 20 or lower** (`P/E ≤ ~20`).
5. The **payout ratio is 50% or less** — the payout ratio is the share of profit
   paid out as dividends; keeping it under half leaves room to keep paying and
   raising the dividend even in a bad year.
6. **Debt is no more than 50% of the company's market value.**
7. It passes a stricter **"blue chip" sub-screen** of fundamental tests.

That final sub-screen is itself a checklist — typically six tests: the dividend
**raised at least 5 times in 12 years**, an **S&P quality rating of A or better**,
**at least 5 million shares outstanding**, **at least 80 institutional holders**,
**at least 25 years of uninterrupted dividends**, and **earnings up at least 7-fold
over 12 years.**

<Callout type="note">
You'll sometimes see Weiss's method described as "six criteria" and sometimes as
"seven." IQT's own materials describe **six** tests that narrow roughly **4,000
stocks down to about 300 blue chips.** The "six vs. seven" difference is just
nesting — whether you count the blue-chip sub-screen as one criterion or break out
its parts. Don't be thrown by the discrepancy.
</Callout>

The quality gate isn't a side note — it's *what makes the yield band meaningful.*
A junk company can have a high yield because it's about to collapse, not because
it's a bargain. The screen is designed to keep those out.

## The track record

How well did it work? Be careful here, because the figures get misquoted and most
trace back to IQT's own marketing and to columns by the newsletter-tracker Mark
Hulbert.

The best-supported number: over IQT's run under Weiss, roughly **1966–2002**, the
strategy returned about **11% annualized versus about 10% for the S&P 500** — call
it **roughly a point a year over the market.** A separate Hulbert claim credits IQT
with beating the broad **Wilshire 5000 by about a point a year** over a measured
window. The **Hulbert Financial Digest** ranked the newsletter highly, especially
on a **risk-adjusted** basis (IQT cites a Hulbert measurement window of
**30 Sept 2002 – 30 Sept 2022**).

<Callout type="warning">
Treat these numbers as "roughly a point a year over the market," not as a precise,
audited alpha. The exact periods and benchmarks differ by source, and the figures
lean heavily on IQT's own promotional materials and Hulbert's columns. The honest
takeaway is a *modest, durable edge with relatively low risk* — which is exactly
what you'd expect from a conservative, blue-chip, dividend-anchored method, not a
home-run strategy.
</Callout>

## When the theory breaks — the honest failure modes

Every method has terrain where it fails. Weiss's depends on two fragile
assumptions — that the dividend is **secure** and the business is **stable** — and
when either cracks, the yield band lies to you. There are three classic ways this
happens.

### 1. The yield trap (a coming dividend cut)

A yield that spikes *above* its historical high band can mean the market knows
something you don't: the dividend is about to be **cut.** The price falls (pushing
yield up) precisely because investors are pricing in the cut. Buying then isn't
catching a bargain — it's catching a falling knife.

The canonical example is **General Electric.** Its dividend yield climbed well
above its normal band, which looked like a screaming "undervalued" signal. But the
business was deteriorating; GE then **slashed its dividend**, and afterward the
yield fell *below* its old average. The band had become worthless because the
anchor — the dividend — was no longer stable.

This is why the quality gate insists on a low payout ratio, low debt, and decades
of uninterrupted dividends — all of which make a cut far less likely. But no
screen catches every GE.

### 2. The low-payout growth stock (the tool simply doesn't apply)

Some excellent companies pay a tiny dividend or none at all, choosing instead to
**reinvest** their cash to grow. Their returns come from rising earnings, not
income. A stock like **Roper Technologies**, with a yield under 1%, has almost no
meaningful yield band to mean-revert around — the dividend is too small to anchor
anything. For these, **the P/E ratio (or a cash-flow measure) is the right tool**,
and Weiss's method is simply the wrong instrument. There's no shame in that; it's
like trying to weigh something with a ruler.

### 3. A permanent change in the business

Mean reversion assumes the *old* yield range still describes the company. But if
the business changes structurally — a different growth rate, a new industry
position, a permanently lower margin — then the **old band is obsolete.** The
example often cited is **General Mills**: when its long-run fundamentals shifted,
the "new" yield became the **new normal**, and waiting for it to revert to the old
band was waiting for something that would never come.

<Callout type="warning">
The deepest trap is treating the yield band as a law of nature. It isn't — it's a
*pattern* that holds only while the dividend stays secure and the business stays
recognizably the same. The method gives you **no signal** for non-dividend payers,
cyclicals with erratic dividends, or any company whose past simply no longer
predicts its future. Knowing when the tool doesn't apply is half of using it well.
</Callout>

## Where this sits in the bigger picture

Weiss's Dividend Yield Theory is a specific, disciplined member of a broader family
of ideas:

- It's a **value** approach in the Graham–Dodd tradition (buy when cheap, sell when
  dear), but it swaps Graham's earnings-and-assets focus for a single cleaner
  anchor: the dividend.
- It's a **mean-reversion** strategy — it bets that a stable quantity (the yield)
  returns to its long-run range, the same logic behind many statistical valuation
  tools.
- And it's a **quality / income** strategy: by demanding decades of dividend
  growth, low debt, and a modest payout ratio, it naturally selects for the kind of
  durable, cash-generative blue chips that other quality investors also prize.

Its great virtue is *humility and clarity*: one honest number, a visible band, and
explicit rules for when not to trust it.

## Key takeaways

- **Dividends don't lie.** Earnings can be massaged by accounting; a cash dividend
  is real money paid out, so it's the honest signal Weiss anchored everything to.
- **The yield band is the core tool.** For a stable blue chip, dividend yield
  mean-reverts: yield near its **historical high** means the price is low
  (undervalued); yield near its **historical low** means the price is high
  (overvalued).
- **Fair value ≈ expected dividend ÷ historical average yield** (the standard
  textbook form of her method). It's just the yield rule expressed as a price
  target.
- **Quality first.** The yield logic only holds for vetted blue chips — long
  dividend-growth records, payout ratio `≤ 50%`, low debt, A-grade ratings, 25+
  years of uninterrupted dividends. Weiss screened ~4,000 stocks down to ~300.
- **The edge was modest and durable:** roughly **a point a year over the market**
  (≈11% vs ≈10%, 1966–2002), best understood as a low-risk conservative result, and
  drawn largely from IQT's own and Hulbert's figures — not a precise audited alpha.
- **Know the failure modes:** the **yield trap** (a spike before a dividend cut, as
  with GE), **low-payout growth stocks** where P/E is the right tool (Roper), and
  **permanent business change** that makes the old band obsolete (General Mills).
- **A pioneer.** As "G. Weiss," she founded the first U.S. investment-advisory
  newsletter run by a woman (1966) and concealed her gender until 1977 — the method
  earned its credibility before its author could.
- **Not advice.** This is educational material. The dividend is only an honest
  signal while it's *secure*; always verify the business can keep paying before you
  trust its yield.
