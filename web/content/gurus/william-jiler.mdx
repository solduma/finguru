## The one-sentence version

In 1962, a markets professional named William L. Jiler wrote a slim book that took the secret-handshake craft of "reading the tape" (interpreting the live stream of price-and-volume quotes that used to print on a paper ticker-tape machine) and turned it into something an ordinary investor could actually learn — and the chart patterns he popularized are still among the first things most traders study today.

If you have ever heard someone say "that stock is forming a head and shoulders" or "it bounced off support," you are speaking a dialect that Jiler helped standardize for the public. He did not invent these patterns, but he packaged them with rare clarity — and, just as importantly, with rare honesty about their limits. This lesson covers who he was, the core patterns he taught, exactly how to act on them (with worked numbers), and where the approach is weak and how beginners get burned.

<Callout type="key">

A **chart pattern** is a recognizable shape that price traces out over time, which technical analysts believe tends to precede a particular kind of move. Jiler's gift was explaining these shapes plainly enough that a beginner could spot them — and insisting they shift the odds in your favor rather than predict the future.

</Callout>

## Jiler's philosophy: an edge, not a crystal ball

Before any pattern, absorb the mindset, because it governs how you use everything below. Jiler's two most-quoted lines say it best:

> "When a stock is found to be following a given trend line, it is more likely to continue along that line than not to. Not certain, just likely."

> "It's not necessary to be right all of the time to make money in the stock market. It's only necessary to be right more often than you are wrong."

That is the whole game. Charts give you a **probabilistic edge**, confirmed by **volume** and managed with a **risk plan** for the times you are wrong. A beginner who reads patterns as predictions will blow up; a beginner who reads them as "likely, not certain" can actually use them. Keep these two sentences in mind every time you see a shape you like.

## Who was William L. Jiler?

William L. Jiler was an American markets professional best known as the author of *How Charts Can Help You in the Stock Market*, first published in 1962 by Commodity Research Publications Corp. (later editions carried the Trendline and McGraw-Hill imprints). The book ran roughly 200 pages — cataloged at 202 pages by Open Library and 218 by the Internet Archive's scanned copy — and became a long-running staple of beginner technical-analysis reading lists. It is often described as the first book to explain technical analysis in plain English — the "how and why" rather than a dense reference — making it the chartist's counterpart to Graham and Dodd's fundamental classic.

Jiler's career straddled the **stock** and **commodities** worlds (commodities being raw goods like wheat, oil, and metals, traded as standardized contracts). His family is tied to the **Commodity Research Bureau (CRB)**, a commodity-research firm founded in 1934 by his brother Milton Jiler; William later led it and expanded its chart services, and is credited as an originator of the **CRB Futures Chart Service** and the **Trendline Chart Service**. Trendline's printed chart books were, for decades, how many investors got their charts before personal computers, and the stock charts in his 1962 book came straight from them. Book-jacket bios of the era describe Trendline as a division of Standard & Poor's and Jiler as its president.

<Callout type="note">

Some obituary-style listings give Jiler's dates as roughly 1925–2019 (born in Bridgeport, CT; later Boca Raton, FL) and his schooling as Bates College and USC, but these appear only in directory and aggregator entries that could not be confirmed against a primary biography — treat the personal dates and the "division of Standard & Poor's" tie as reported-but-unverified. The professional core — the 1962 book, his Trendline and CRB chart-service work, and the family's CRB lineage — is well attested.

</Callout>

Jiler worked in an era — the 1950s and 60s — when charting was viewed by much of Wall Street as fringe. His contribution was less theoretical innovation than *democratization*: clear definitions, simple rules, and the honest framing that charts shift the odds in your favor rather than predicting the future.

## The core idea: supply, demand, and crowd behavior

Jiler's whole method rests on one premise: **price already reflects everything** — every hope, fear, fact, and rumor of every buyer and seller. So instead of studying a company's earnings, you study the *picture* of supply and demand drawn on the chart. Recognizable shapes recur because **crowd psychology repeats**, and those shapes give probabilistic clues about what comes next. Everything below is just specific shapes.

### Trendlines, support, and resistance

These are the foundation — the first things you draw.

- **Trendline**: a straight line drawn along a series of higher lows (an **uptrend** — connect the bottoms) or lower highs (a **downtrend** — connect the tops). As long as price respects the line, the trend is presumed intact.
- **Support**: a price area where buying (demand) tends to overwhelm selling — a "floor" where declines stop.
- **Resistance**: a price area where selling (supply) overwhelms buying — a "ceiling" where advances stop.

Jiler's practical rules for drawing and trusting them:

- **Use at least 3 touch points.** A line drawn off only two points is a guess; the more times price touches and respects a line, the more reliable it is. Longer-timeframe lines (weekly, monthly) beat short daily ones.
- **Use closing prices**, not intraday spikes, to define support and resistance — a close beyond a level means far more than a brief wick through it.
- **The 3% break rule.** A trend is likely reversing only when price breaks the established trendline by about **3%, on increased volume** — a small filter that screens out meaningless pokes through the line.
- **Watch for the pull-back.** After a break, price often returns to *retest* the broken line before continuing. A failure to reclaim the line confirms the break (and gives you a logical stop placement).
- **The 50% rule.** After a move, price often retraces about **half** of it before support or resistance reasserts itself.

### Volume — the confirming witness

Volume applies to *every* pattern below, so learn it once here. Jiler's rule: **"the greater the volume, the greater the significance of a price movement."**

- A healthy **uptrend** shows volume **rising on rallies and shrinking on pullbacks.**
- A valid **upside breakout** must come with **expanding volume.** A downside breakdown can *begin* on light volume but should then be confirmed by rising volume as price falls.
- Volume often **shifts before price** — a quiet tell of a coming reversal.

A breakout without a volume surge is suspect, and often a false move. Treat volume as the lie-detector for every signal that follows.

### Reversal patterns (the trend is about to flip)

**Head and shoulders top.** The most famous reversal. Forms after an uptrend. Three peaks: a **left shoulder**, a higher **head**, and a lower **right shoulder** roughly level with the first. A **neckline** connects the two reaction lows between the peaks (a *reaction low* is the dip price makes between two peaks). The neckline can slope up, down, or sit flat — a downward slope is considered more bearish. Volume typically **declines with each successive peak** (weakening demand), then surges on the break. The pattern is *not complete* until price **closes below the neckline.** If price drifts sideways and never breaks the neckline, the pattern aborts — do not act early. The **inverse (or bottom) head and shoulders** is the mirror image at the end of a downtrend.

**Double top.** Two peaks at roughly the same level — an "M" shape. A double top signals the market tried twice to push higher, failed, and is rolling over. Confirmation comes only when price breaks **below the trough** between the two peaks. True double tops are *rare*, so Jiler offered a reality-check before you trust one:

- Did price fall a meaningful amount (Jiler's rule of thumb: roughly **5% or more**, and modern references such as StockCharts often want 10–20%) between the two peaks?
- Was there unusual volume at the peaks?
- Is this happening at a major turning point, not mid-trend?
- Is the broad market neutral-to-down (for a top)?

If it does not pass the checklist, you are probably forcing a pattern onto noise.

**Double bottom.** The mirror image — two troughs at roughly the same level, tracing a "W" at the end of a downtrend. The market tested the same floor twice, found buyers both times, and the middle bounce sets a ceiling. The pattern is *not* a buy signal until price closes **above that intervening peak** (the middle of the "W"); the same size guideline filters out noise. The upside target is the height of the pattern projected up from the breakout — the reverse of the double-top measurement. (A **triple top/bottom** adds a third peak or trough before the break; rarer still, and rarer yet on weekly or monthly charts.)

**Rounding bottom (the "saucer").** A slow, bowl-shaped base where a downtrend gradually flattens and curves up into an uptrend. Volume mirrors the bowl: heavy as the decline begins, lightest at the base, and rising into the advance and breakout. StockCharts describes it as essentially a head-and-shoulders bottom without distinct shoulders — and it is a favorite Jiler example of patient sentiment change.

### Continuation patterns (the trend pauses, then resumes)

**Triangles (Jiler's "coil").** Converging trendlines as price coils into a narrowing range. A **symmetrical triangle** (lower highs and higher lows) is neutral — direction is undecided until the breakout; volume **dries up** as it narrows, the quiet before the storm. An **ascending triangle** (flat top, rising lows) means buyers are strengthening → leans **bullish**. A **descending triangle** (flat bottom, falling highs) means sellers are strengthening → leans **bearish**. **Measuring rule:** take the triangle's **widest vertical height** and project it from the breakout point — added above for ascending/upside breaks, subtracted below for descending/downside breaks.

**Rectangle (trading range).** Price bounces sideways between a flat ceiling and a flat floor, repeatedly testing the same support and resistance — a horizontal box. As a *continuation* pattern it usually resolves in the direction of the trend that entered it, and the measuring rule is familiar: project the height of the box from the breakout point. (The same box at the *end* of a long trend can instead be a reversal — context decides, so wait for the break.)

**Flags and pennants.** Short, sharp pauses after a steep move (the **flagpole**). A flag is a small rectangle tilted against the trend; a pennant is a tiny symmetrical triangle (converging highs and lows). Volume is heavy on the flagpole, **contracts sharply** during the pause, then **expands** on the breakout that resumes the trend. They "fly at half-mast" — tending to form near the **midpoint** of the whole move. **Measuring rule (measured move):** measure the flagpole (from the move's start to the flag), then project that same length from the breakout point — up for bull flags, down for bear flags.

<Callout type="note">

**Gaps.** A *gap* is an empty space on the chart where price jumps from one bar's range to the next with no trading in between — often on news. Jiler-era charting treats them as clues: a **breakaway gap** launches a new move out of a pattern, a **runaway (measuring) gap** appears mid-trend and roughly marks the halfway point, and an **exhaustion gap** near the end often signals the move is running out of steam. Most ordinary gaps eventually get "filled" as price trades back over them, but breakaway gaps on heavy volume frequently do not.

</Callout>

## The beginner workflow (do these in order)

Jiler's method is a checklist, not a hunch. Run these six steps every time:

1. **Identify the trend** and draw the trendline — connect the lows (uptrend) or highs (downtrend), using **3+ touches** and closing prices.
2. **Mark support and resistance** zones, again using closing prices.
3. **Spot the pattern** forming, and classify it: reversal or continuation?
4. **Wait for confirmation** — the neckline/boundary break on a **closing** basis, **with a volume surge** (and, for a trendline, the ~3% filter). Do *not* anticipate.
5. **Compute the measuring objective** to set a price target (see the worked example below).
6. **Manage risk** — accept you will be wrong sometimes; place a logical stop just beyond the broken line or neckline, and aim to be right *more often* than wrong. That, not prediction, is Jiler's edge.

## A worked example (illustrative)

Let's measure a head-and-shoulders top. The classic target rule: measure the height from the head down to the neckline, then project that distance below the neckline at the breakout point. This height-projection trick is commonly called a *measured move*.

```
ILLUSTRATIVE — numbers chosen to show the method, not a real stock

Left shoulder  = $50
Head           = $60   (highest peak)
Right shoulder = $51
Neckline       = $45   (drawn under the two reaction lows)

Step 1 — CONFIRM: price closes below $45 on rising volume.
         (Before this close, do nothing.)

Step 2 — MEASURE the height:  Head - Neckline = $60 - $45 = $15

Step 3 — PROJECT the objective: Neckline - height = $45 - $15 = $30
         → minimum downside target ≈ $30

Step 4 — PULL-BACK: price may bounce back to retest ~$45 (old
         support, now resistance). Failure to reclaim $45 confirms
         the breakdown; a stop just above $45 caps the risk.
```

So once price closes below $45, the measured objective is about $30 — a *minimum* estimate, not a promise. The same projection logic runs in reverse for bottoms (project up), for rectangles (project the box height from the break), for triangles (project the widest height from the break), and for flags and pennants (project the flagpole length from the breakout).

**Continuation cross-check (bull flag).** If a stock ran from $20 to $32 — a **$12 flagpole** — then paused in a small downward-sloping flag near $30 and broke out at **$31** on rising volume, the target is $31 + $12 = **$43**.

## How to read patterns the way Jiler intended

| Element | What to check | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Prior trend | Is there a real trend to reverse or continue? | A "reversal" needs something to reverse |
| The break | Did price *close* beyond the neckline/boundary? | The pattern is incomplete until then |
| Volume | Does it confirm (surge on breakout)? | Volume is the lie-detector for price |
| Filter | A ~3% break, or it holding 2–3 days | Reduces false breakouts |
| Measured target | Height projected from the break | A *minimum* objective, not exact |
| Risk plan | Stop just beyond the broken line | You *will* be wrong sometimes |

## Modern implementation and legacy

Jiler's patterns are baked into nearly every charting platform — TradingView, StockCharts, thinkorswim — and into the standard curriculum of the **CMT (Chartered Market Technician)** program. Robert Edwards and John Magee's *Technical Analysis of Stock Trends* (1948) is the heavyweight reference, and Richard Schabacker's *Technical Analysis and Stock Market Profits* (1932) the earlier source of much pattern theory, but Jiler's book remains the gentler on-ramp, and his Trendline chart services trained a generation of investors. The vocabulary — neckline, breakout, support/resistance, the coil — is now universal.

## Honest criticisms

Intellectual honesty requires saying this plainly: classical chart patterns are controversial.

- **Subjectivity.** Where exactly is the neckline? Is that a shoulder or just noise? Two analysts can see two patterns on the same chart. This makes patterns hard to test rigorously.
- **Hindsight bias.** Patterns are obvious *after* they complete. The textbook examples are survivors; the failures rarely get printed. In real time, a "head and shoulders" can still morph into a continuation.
- **Weak empirical support.** Academic finance (and the efficient-market hypothesis) has long argued that past prices cannot predict future prices. Many studies find chart patterns offer little to no edge once trading costs and data-snooping are accounted for; the evidence is mixed at best.
- **Self-fulfilling, then self-defeating.** Patterns may "work" partly because enough traders watch them — but widely known edges tend to erode as they get arbitraged away, and false breakouts are common.
- **Targets are guidelines, not guarantees.** Every measuring rule is a *minimum projection*; Jiler himself framed everything as "likely, not certain."
- **No fundamentals.** The method ignores valuation, earnings, and macro entirely — a stock can be technically perfect and fundamentally rotten.

<Callout type="warning">

Beginners routinely mistake pattern recognition for a crystal ball. A pattern is a *probabilistic edge at best*, not a prediction. Always pair it with risk management (a stop-loss — a pre-set exit price that caps your loss).

</Callout>

## Common pitfalls and misconceptions

1. **Trading before confirmation.** No *closing* break of the neckline/boundary, on volume, no trade.
2. **Ignoring volume.** A breakout on weak volume is suspect and often false.
3. **Forcing patterns.** Seeing an "M" or a "head and shoulders" in random noise. True double tops/bottoms are *rare* — run Jiler's checklist (≥5% move, unusual volume, major turning point, market context).
4. **Too few trendline touches.** Drawing a "trend" off only two points.
5. **Treating the target as a guarantee** rather than a minimum probabilistic objective.
6. **No stop / no risk plan** — forgetting that you *will* be wrong; survival depends on small losses and being right *more often* than wrong.
7. **Using intraday spikes instead of closing prices** for support/resistance.
8. **Confusing reversal vs. continuation** — e.g., trading a triangle as a reversal when it is usually a continuation.

## How to apply it today

- Start on **daily or weekly charts**; intraday charts are noisier and pattern-failure rates are higher.
- **Wait for the close beyond the boundary plus a volume surge** before acting.
- Use a **filter** (the ~3% break, or the break holding for 2–3 days) to dodge false signals.
- Always set a **stop-loss** just beyond the pattern's invalidation point (e.g., back above a broken neckline, where the pull-back would prove you wrong).
- **Backtest by eye**: pull up 30 historical examples, mark each pattern *before* the outcome, and tally how often it actually worked for you. This builds judgment and humility at once.
