## The one-sentence version

In 1962, a markets professional named William L. Jiler wrote a slim book that took the secret-handshake craft of "reading the tape" and turned it into something an ordinary investor could actually learn — and the chart patterns he popularized are still among the first things most traders study today.

If you have ever heard someone say "that stock is forming a head and shoulders" or "it bounced off support," you are speaking a dialect that Jiler helped standardize for the public. He did not invent these patterns, but he packaged them with rare clarity. This lesson covers who he was, the core patterns he taught, a worked example, and — just as importantly — where this approach is weak and how beginners get burned.

<Callout type="key">

A **chart pattern** is a recognizable shape that price traces out over time, which technical analysts believe tends to precede a particular kind of move. Jiler's gift was explaining these shapes plainly enough that a beginner could spot them.

</Callout>

## Who was William L. Jiler?

William L. Jiler was an American markets professional best known as the author of *How Charts Can Help You in the Stock Market*, first published in 1962 by Commodity Research Publications Corp. (later editions carried the Trendline and McGraw-Hill imprints). The book ran roughly 200 pages — cataloged at 202 pages by Open Library and 218 by the Internet Archive's scanned copy — and became a long-running staple of beginner technical-analysis reading lists.

Jiler's career straddled the **stock** and **commodities** worlds (commodities being raw goods like wheat, oil, and metals, traded as standardized contracts). In 1956 he **founded Trendline**, a financial chart service whose printed chart books were, for decades, how many investors got their charts before personal computers. He later sold Trendline to Standard & Poor's and became president of S&P's Trendline Division. He was also a former president of the **Commodity Research Bureau (CRB)**, a research firm, and is credited as the founder of the **CRB Futures Price Index**, a long-running benchmark for overall commodity-price movement. (The CRB index was first calculated in 1957 and debuted in the 1958 *CRB Commodity Year Book*.)

<Callout type="note">

Some obituary-style listings give Jiler's dates as roughly 1925–2019 (Boca Raton, FL), but these appear only in directory entries and could not be confirmed against a primary biography, so treat the exact dates as unverified. The professional facts above — the 1962 book, his founding of Trendline in 1956, his CRB presidency, and the CRB Futures Price Index — are well attested.

</Callout>

Jiler worked in an era — the 1950s and 60s — when charting was viewed by much of Wall Street as fringe. His contribution was less theoretical innovation than *democratization*: clear definitions, simple rules, and the honest framing that charts shift the odds in your favor rather than predicting the future.

## The core idea: supply, demand, and crowd behavior

Jiler's whole method rests on one premise: a price chart is a picture of the tug-of-war between buyers (demand) and sellers (supply). When that battle produces a recognizable shape, the shape hints at who is winning. Everything below is just specific shapes.

### Trendlines, support, and resistance

These are the foundation.

- **Trendline**: a straight line drawn along a series of higher lows (an uptrend) or lower highs (a downtrend). As long as price respects the line, the trend is presumed intact.
- **Support**: a price level where buying has previously been strong enough to stop a decline — a "floor."
- **Resistance**: a price level where selling has previously stopped an advance — a "ceiling."

<Callout type="key">

A core principle Jiler emphasized: once **broken, support becomes resistance and resistance becomes support**. A ceiling that finally gives way often becomes the new floor.

</Callout>

### Reversal patterns (the trend is about to flip)

**Head and shoulders top.** Forms after an uptrend. Three peaks: a **left shoulder**, a higher **head**, and a lower **right shoulder** roughly level with the first. A **neckline** connects the two reaction lows between the peaks (it can slope up, down, or sit flat — a downward slope is considered more bearish). The pattern is *not complete* until price breaks below the neckline — ideally on rising volume. Volume tends to be heaviest on the left shoulder and lighter on the head's advance, then surges on the neckline break. The **inverse (or bottom) head and shoulders** is the mirror image at the end of a downtrend.

**Double top / double bottom.** Two peaks (or troughs) at roughly the same level — an "M" or "W" shape. A double top signals the market tried twice to push higher, failed, and is rolling over. Confirmation comes only when price breaks the trough between the two peaks. StockCharts recommends requiring at least a roughly 10% decline (commonly 10–20%) between the peaks to avoid noise.

**Rounding bottom (the "saucer").** A slow, bowl-shaped base where a downtrend gradually flattens and curves up into an uptrend. Volume mirrors the bowl: heavy as the decline begins, lightest at the base, and rising into the advance and breakout. StockCharts itself describes it as essentially a head-and-shoulders bottom without distinct shoulders — and it is a favorite Jiler example of patient sentiment change.

### Continuation patterns (the trend pauses, then resumes)

**Triangles.** Converging trendlines as price coils into a narrowing range. A **symmetrical triangle** (lower highs and higher lows) is neutral; an **ascending triangle** (flat top, rising bottoms) leans bullish; a **descending triangle** (flat bottom, falling tops) leans bearish. The breakout direction is what matters.

**Flags and pennants.** Short pauses after a sharp move (the **flagpole**). A flag is a small rectangle tilted against the trend; a pennant is a tiny symmetrical triangle. Volume is heavy on the flagpole, contracts during the pause, and expands on the breakout. They usually mark the *midpoint* of a move.

## A worked example (illustrative)

Let's measure a head-and-shoulders top. The classic target rule: measure the height from the head down to the neckline, then project that distance below the neckline at the breakout point.

```
ILLUSTRATIVE — numbers chosen to show the method, not a real stock

Head (highest peak)          = $50
Neckline level at breakout   = $44
Pattern height               = $50 - $44 = $6

Downside price target        = Neckline - height
                             = $44 - $6
                             = $38
```

So after price breaks below $44, the measured objective is about $38. Note the target is an *estimate*, not a promise. The same projection logic runs in reverse for bottoms, and for flags (project the flagpole length from the breakout). For a double top, subtract the distance from the broken support up to the peaks.

<Callout type="warning">

The neckline break is the trigger — not the formation of the right shoulder. Acting before the break means you are guessing. Many promising head-and-shoulders patterns never break and simply resume the uptrend.

</Callout>

## How to read patterns the way Jiler intended

| Element | What to check | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| Prior trend | Is there a real trend to reverse or continue? | A "reversal" needs something to reverse |
| The break | Did price actually breach the neckline/support? | The pattern is incomplete until then |
| Volume | Does it confirm (surge on breakout)? | Volume is the lie-detector for price |
| Measured target | Height projected from the break | A rough, not exact, objective |
| Filters | A ~3% move or a few days holding | Reduces false breakouts |

## Modern implementation and legacy

Jiler's patterns are baked into nearly every charting platform — TradingView, StockCharts, thinkorswim — and into the standard curriculum of the **CMT (Chartered Market Technician)** program. Robert Edwards and John Magee's *Technical Analysis of Stock Trends* is the heavyweight reference, but Jiler's book remains a gentler on-ramp, and his Trendline chart services trained a generation of investors. The vocabulary — neckline, breakout, support/resistance — is now universal.

## Honest criticisms

Intellectual honesty requires saying this plainly: classical chart patterns are controversial.

- **Subjectivity.** Where exactly is the neckline? Is that a shoulder or just noise? Two analysts can see two patterns on the same chart. This makes patterns hard to test rigorously.
- **Hindsight bias.** Patterns are obvious *after* they complete. The textbook examples are survivors; the failures rarely get printed.
- **Efficient Market Hypothesis.** Academic finance has long argued that past prices cannot predict future prices, which would make patterns useless. The evidence is genuinely mixed — some studies find modest predictive value, many find none after costs.
- **Self-fulfilling, then self-defeating.** Patterns may "work" partly because enough traders watch them — but widely known edges tend to erode as they get arbitraged away.

<Callout type="warning">

Beginners routinely mistake pattern recognition for a crystal ball. A pattern is a *probabilistic edge at best*, not a prediction. Always pair it with risk management (a stop-loss — a pre-set exit price that caps your loss).

</Callout>

## Common pitfalls and misconceptions

1. **Trading before confirmation.** No neckline/support break, no trade.
2. **Ignoring volume.** A breakout on weak volume is suspect.
3. **Forcing patterns.** If you have to squint, it isn't there.
4. **Treating the target as a guarantee.** It's a guide; price may overshoot or fall well short.
5. **Skipping the prior trend.** A "reversal" pattern in a sideways market is meaningless.

## How to apply it today

- Start on **daily or weekly charts**; intraday charts are noisier and pattern-failure rates are higher.
- **Wait for the break and a confirming volume surge** before acting.
- Use a **filter** (e.g., a 3% break, or the break holding for 2–3 days) to dodge false signals.
- Always set a **stop-loss** just beyond the pattern's invalidation point (e.g., back above a broken neckline).
- **Backtest by eye**: pull up 30 historical examples, mark each pattern *before* the outcome, and tally how often it actually worked for you. This builds judgment and humility at once.
