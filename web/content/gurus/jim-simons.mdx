## Why he matters

Almost everyone you meet in investing tells a **story**. "I bought this stock
because the company is great." "I sold because a recession is coming." Jim
Simons did the opposite. He decided that stories were a trap, that human
judgment was a bug, and that the only thing worth trusting was **data run
through mathematics.**

The result was the **Medallion Fund** — by most accounts the best-performing
investment fund ever measured, returning roughly **66% a year before fees for
three decades**. That is not a typo, and it is not a one-off lucky streak: it
ran from 1988 to 2018 *without a single losing year.*

This lesson does **not** claim to hand you Medallion's secret formula — that
formula is one of the most closely guarded secrets in finance, and anyone
selling it to you is lying. Instead it teaches the **principles** Simons
pioneered, the ones a careful beginner can actually understand and, in a
simplified form, follow.

<Callout type="warning">
**Read this before anything else.** Medallion is essentially impossible for an
ordinary investor to copy (we will be very honest about why). Treat Simons as a
*case study in disciplined, data-driven thinking* — not a get-rich blueprint.
The most likely honest takeaway for most people is closer to "buy a low-cost
index fund," which even Simons's opposite number, Warren Buffett, recommends.
</Callout>

## Who he was

**James Harris Simons** (April 25, 1938 – May 10, 2024) was a mathematician
*first* and an investor second. The order matters: it explains everything about
how he ran money.

- **A genuine, world-class mathematician.** He earned a BS in math from **MIT**
  in three years (1958) and a **PhD from UC Berkeley at 23** (1961). With
  Shiing-Shen Chern he co-discovered the **Chern–Simons form** — deep geometry
  that later became foundational in theoretical physics and string theory. He
  won the **Oswald Veblen Prize** (1976), geometry's highest honor.
- **A code-breaker.** From 1964–1968 he broke codes for the NSA at the
  **Institute for Defense Analyses**, learning to **find signal hidden inside
  noise** — the exact skill he would later monetize. He was pushed out for
  publicly opposing the Vietnam War.
- **An academic leader.** He chaired the **Stony Brook University** math
  department (1968–1978) before switching careers entirely. He was elected to the
  **U.S. National Academy of Sciences** in 2014.
- **The founder.** In **1978** he started the firm (first "Monemetrics,"
  renamed **Renaissance Technologies** in 1982) and launched the **Medallion
  Fund in 1988**, named for math medals he and colleague James Ax had won.
- **A great philanthropist.** He gave **over $4 billion** away — the Simons
  Foundation, Math for America, the Flatiron Institute, and a record **$500
  million** gift to Stony Brook in 2023. An asteroid, **6618 Jimsimons**, is
  named for him. His net worth at death was roughly **$31 billion.**

<Callout type="key">
Simons did not learn investing on Wall Street and then add math. He was a
**mathematician who treated the market as a giant data set to be decoded** — the
same way he had decoded enemy communications. That reversal is the whole idea.
</Callout>

### The team — scientists, not stockbrokers

Renaissance's other defining choice was *who* it hired. Wall Street experience
was treated as a **negative.** Instead Simons recruited mathematicians,
physicists, astronomers, and statisticians — about **half of the ~150
researchers held PhDs.** The two men who eventually succeeded him as co-CEOs,
**Robert Mercer** and **Peter Brown**, were **speech-recognition experts from
IBM Research.** They brought a machine-learning engine built for *language* —
**Hidden Markov Models** — into the trading models. Other key figures: **Leonard
Baum** (co-inventor of the Baum–Welch algorithm behind those models), **James
Ax**, **Elwyn Berlekamp**, and **Henry Laufer.**

## The core idea, in plain language

Simons's belief can be stated in one paragraph:

> Markets are a noisy stream of data. Hidden inside that noise are tiny,
> fleeting, non-random **patterns**. If you collect enough clean data and let
> statistical and machine-learning models hunt for those patterns — then trade
> them automatically, thousands upon thousands of times — many tiny edges
> **compound** into enormous returns.

Four pillars hold it up:

1. **Data over opinion.** Decisions come from models trained on mountains of
   historical data — never from a hunch, a news headline, or a CEO's promises.
2. **A tiny edge, repeated enormously.** Renaissance was reportedly right on only
   about **50.75% of its trades** — barely better than a coin flip. But repeated
   across *millions* of trades, that sliver of an edge becomes a money machine,
   exactly like a casino's small house edge.
3. **Scientists, not financiers.** Pattern-finding is a science problem, so you
   hire scientists.
4. **Automate everything.** The system finds the patterns *and* places the
   trades, with little human intervention. Holding periods were short — intraday
   to a few days.

## What "a tiny edge" really means

Here is the single most important intuition in the whole lesson. Most beginners
assume a great trader must be *right most of the time.* Simons proved you can be
right barely more than half the time and still build the greatest fortune in
trading history — **if you bet small, bet often, and let the math do its work.**

A **casino** does not win because it wins every spin of the roulette wheel. It
wins because it has a small, fixed edge on *every* spin and the wheel turns
thousands of times a day. The losses and the wins both happen, but over enough
spins the edge is mathematically guaranteed to surface. That guarantee is called
the **law of large numbers**: the more times you repeat a bet with a positive
edge, the more certainly your actual result converges on the expected result.

Medallion *is* that casino — and Renaissance was the house.

## The full method, as concrete steps

These are the **generalizable principles** of systematic quant investing that
Simons pioneered. (Again: the actual Medallion signals are secret. This is the
framework, not the recipe.)

### Step 1 — Collect and obsessively clean data

Renaissance gathered decades of price data plus anything else it could find —
volumes, weather, news feeds. The unglamorous 90% of the work is **cleaning** it:
fixing errors, gaps, and bad ticks. *Garbage in, garbage out* is not a cliché
here; it is the whole ballgame.

> **Beginner version:** download several years of clean historical price data
> for one liquid asset (a major index ETF, say). Inspect it for obvious errors
> before doing anything else.

### Step 2 — Treat the market as a statistical process

Stop asking "is this a good company?" Start asking **"what does the data say
tends to happen *next* after a given pattern?"** You are studying behavior, not
business quality.

### Step 3 — Form a hypothesis (a "signal")

A **signal** is any measurable pattern that gives a slight edge in predicting
the next move. Two classic, beginner-friendly examples:

- **Mean reversion** — "after a sharp drop, this asset tends to bounce back
  toward its recent average." (It overshoots, then snaps back.)
- **Trend following / momentum** — "what has been rising tends to keep rising for
  a while."

### Step 4 — Backtest rigorously, after costs

**Backtesting** means running the signal against historical data to see whether
it *would have* made money. Two rules make a backtest honest:

- **Subtract trading costs and slippage first.** (More on this below.)
- **Test on data the model has never seen** — called **out-of-sample** testing.
  If you tune a rule on 2010–2015 data, you must test it on 2016–2020 data it has
  never touched.

### Step 5 — Fear overfitting above all

This is the **cardinal sin** of quant work. **Overfitting** (or "curve-fitting")
is when a model memorizes the *random noise* in past data instead of learning a
real, repeatable pattern. It looks brilliant on history and dies instantly in
live trading.

### Step 6 — Combine many weak signals

No single signal is reliable. The power comes from **blending dozens of
slightly-better-than-random signals** into one portfolio — the way a crowd of
mediocre forecasters, averaged together, can be surprisingly accurate. One signal
being wrong barely dents the whole.

### Step 7 — Size by risk, diversify, leverage carefully

Spread your bets across many **uncorrelated** positions (ones that don't all rise
and fall together) so no single one can sink you. Renaissance used **leverage**
(borrowed money) to amplify its small per-trade edge — but leverage magnifies
losses just as much as gains, so it must be controlled obsessively.

### Step 8 — Automate execution and crush costs

Because the edge per trade is tiny, **transaction costs and slippage** — the gap
between the price you expected and the price you actually got — can swallow all
your profit. Cost control is not an afterthought; it is *part of the strategy.*
And trading by rule, not emotion, removes the human panic that wrecks discretionary
traders.

### Step 9 — Let strategies decay gracefully

Every edge erodes as markets change and competitors copy it. You must
**continuously research new signals to replace dying ones.** A quant fund is a
treadmill, not a finish line.

### Step 10 — Respect capacity limits

The strategy only works at limited size. As more money chases the same tiny
inefficiencies, the edge shrinks. Renaissance famously **capped Medallion** and
returned profits to keep it nimble — a profound admission that more money would
have *destroyed* the returns.

## A worked example: how a coin-flip edge becomes billions

Let's make Pillar #2 concrete with numbers.

**Setup.** Suppose each trade risks \$1 to win \$1, you win **50.75%** of the
time, and lose 49.25%.

- Expected profit per trade = (0.5075 × \$1) − (0.4925 × \$1) = **\$0.015** —
  one and a half cents. Almost nothing.
- **Now scale it.** Over **1,000,000 trades**, expected profit ≈ 1,000,000 ×
  \$0.015 = **\$15,000**. And by the law of large numbers, the *actual* result
  clusters tightly around that expectation — the randomness averages out. That
  is the Medallion machine in one line.

Why does the randomness "average out"? Because the **edge grows faster than the
uncertainty.** Over `n` independent trades, your expected profit grows in
proportion to `n`, but the swing around it (the standard deviation) grows only
like the square root of `n`. So the edge pulls ahead and the noise falls behind —
the more you trade, the more certain the win.

worthless to someone who places ten trades and priceless to a machine that places
millions.
</Callout>

- Medallion's average **gross** return was about **66% per year** from 1988–2018,

## The key books and sources

- **Gregory Zuckerman, *The Man Who Solved the Market* (2019).** The definitive,
  accessible account, by a *Wall Street Journal* reporter with insider access.
  **Start here.**
- **Scott Patterson, *The Quants* (2010).** The broader history of quantitative
  trading and its culture.
- **The Baum–Welch algorithm papers (Leonard Baum et al., 1966–1970).** The

- **Eugene Fama's Efficient-Market Hypothesis work (1960s–70s)** — the academic
  theory Simons's results appear to *defy* (see below).

## The big theoretical fight: did Simons break the rules?

Mainstream finance has a famous claim called the **Efficient-Market Hypothesis
(EMH)**: prices already reflect all available information, so you *cannot*
consistently beat the market — any apparent winners are just lucky, like the
people who happen to flip ten heads in a row.

Medallion is the hardest case against that view. A 31-year record with a **Sharpe
ratio above 2.0** (the Sharpe ratio measures return earned per unit of risk; most
celebrated funds are *below 1*) and **no down year** is almost impossible to
explain as luck.

But notice the careful conclusion: Medallion is best understood as **the
exception almost no one can imitate**, not as proof that *you* can beat the
market. It took proprietary data, decades of research, elite PhD talent, custom
infrastructure, and brutal capacity discipline to produce. For nearly everyone
else, EMH's practical advice — *don't try, just hold a low-cost index* — still
holds.

## Honest criticisms and limitations

- **It is essentially not replicable.** Renaissance's own funds open to outsiders
  — **RIEF** and **RIDA** — use *different* strategies and have **badly trailed**
  Medallion. RIEF's long-biased fund fell roughly **20% in 2020**, and clients
  yanked about **\$5 billion** out between December 2020 and February 2021. The
  genius simply does **not** transfer to vehicles you can actually buy. The magic
  is in the closed fund.
- **It is capacity-constrained.** Beating the market this way is a *small-money*
  game; Medallion is deliberately kept to the **low tens of billions** because its
  high-turnover edge would vanish at, say, \$100 billion. Renaissance forces
  profits *out* every year to keep the fund small enough to work.
- **The methods are secret.** Anyone "teaching Medallion's strategy" in detail is
  guessing. Learn the *principles.*
- **Reputational and ethical baggage.** A 2014 U.S. Senate investigation found
  Renaissance used "basket-options" trades through banks to recharacterize
  short-term gains as lower-taxed long-term gains; in **September 2021** Simons,
  Mercer, and others agreed to pay **up to \$7 billion** to settle with the IRS.
  Separately, co-CEO Robert Mercer was a major political donor — controversial,
  and unrelated to the investing method.
- **Survivorship bias in the whole genre.** We study Simons *because he won.*
  Thousands of quant funds with similar ideas failed and are forgotten. Don't
  mistake the one survivor for the typical outcome.

## Common beginner pitfalls

<Callout type="warning">
1. **Thinking you can copy Medallion.** You can't. Use it as inspiration for
   *discipline and data*, not as a blueprint.
2. **Overfitting / curve-fitting.** Tuning a strategy until it looks perfect on
   past data. It *will* fail live. Always test out-of-sample.
3. **Ignoring transaction costs and slippage.** A backtest that ignores costs is
   fiction. Tiny edges are eaten alive by fees and bad fills.
4. **Confusing gross with net returns.** Medallion's headline 66% is *gross*;
   outsiders got ~39% net, and now zero access. Always ask "after fees?"
5. **Mistaking luck for skill.** A short winning streak proves nothing. An edge
   must be statistically significant across a *large* sample.
6. **Over-leveraging.** Leverage magnifies a small edge — and magnifies ruin.
   Renaissance controlled risk obsessively; beginners chase returns and blow up.
7. **Believing one strong signal is enough.** The power came from combining many
   weak ones and constantly replacing the dead.
8. **Story-picking dressed up as "quant."** Systematic means following rules
   consistently, not rationalizing a hunch with a couple of indicators.
</Callout>

## Putting it to work

You will not run Medallion. But you *can* absorb the Simons mindset, and even a
beginner can practice it honestly:

1. **Pick one liquid asset** and gather several years of clean price data.
2. **Write down one simple, testable rule** before you look at recent prices
   (e.g., "buy when price closes 5% below its 20-day average; sell when it
   returns to the average").
3. **Backtest it across the full history, subtracting realistic costs**, and then
   re-test it on a later slice of data the rule never saw.
4. **Be ruthless with yourself about overfitting.** If you had to add five
   special exceptions to make it look good, it's probably noise.
5. **If it survives, size it small and treat it as one of many** — never your
   whole portfolio.

Most importantly, internalize the deepest lesson Simons taught: **emotion and
storytelling are the enemy; process, evidence, and humility are the edge.** For
the vast majority of people, the most rational application of that lesson is not
to build a quant fund at all — it's to follow a simple, low-cost, rules-based
plan and stick to it without flinching.

<Callout type="key">
Simons's true legacy for a beginner is not a strategy you can run — it's a
*standard of evidence.* Before you trust any investing idea, ask the Simons
questions: Is this backed by data or a story? Has it been tested on data it has
never seen? Does it survive after costs? Could this just be luck?
</Callout>
