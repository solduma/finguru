## The one-sentence version

Steve Nison is the analyst who took a centuries-old Japanese charting method that almost no one in the West understood and, through a 1991 book, turned it into the visual language nearly every trading screen on the planet now speaks.

If you have ever looked at a stock chart and seen little red and green boxes with thin lines poking out the top and bottom, you have used candlesticks. That picture feels obvious today. It was not obvious in the 1980s. Back then the standard Western chart was a thin bar, and the rich, psychology-revealing world of Japanese candles was locked away in Japanese-language texts and trading-floor folklore. One person did more than anyone to unlock it for English-speaking traders. This lesson is about him, the history he uncovered, the patterns he taught the West to read, and — just as important — the two rules he insisted you never skip.

<Callout type="key">

A candlestick does not show you any new data. It shows you the *same four numbers* as a bar chart — open, high, low, close — but arranged so your eye instantly grasps the tug-of-war between buyers and sellers. Candlesticks are a **visualization of market psychology**, not a new data source.

</Callout>

## Who Steve Nison is

Steve Nison is an American technical analyst (technical analysis = the study of price charts to forecast future price moves, as opposed to studying a company's finances). He is widely and consistently credited as the person who introduced Japanese candlestick charting to the Western world, and he founded and runs **Candlecharts.com**.

He did this primarily through two books:

- **_Japanese Candlestick Charting Techniques_** — first published in **1991** (a second edition, from Prentice Hall / New York Institute of Finance, followed in **2001**). This is the book that put candlesticks on Western trading desks.
- **_Beyond Candlesticks: New Japanese Charting Techniques Revealed_** — published by **John Wiley & Sons in 1994**, which went deeper and introduced additional Japanese methods.

<Callout type="note">

**A note on biography honesty.** Nison is well documented as a working analyst, author, and lecturer who studied the Japanese material directly (including learning from Japanese traders and translating their concepts). You will see claims online that he worked at specific firms such as Merrill Lynch or Daiwa, or that he holds the CMT (Chartered Market Technician) credential. Those specific employment and certification details are *not* confirmed by the primary references used for this lesson, so treat them as unverified. What is solidly established is his authorship and his role as the popularizer of candlesticks in the West.

</Callout>

What makes Nison historically important is not that he *invented* anything. It is that he was a careful researcher and translator. He went back to the original sources, took them seriously, and even corrected popular myths about candlestick history — which is exactly the kind of intellectual honesty this guide values.

## The history Nison uncovered: rice, Homma, and the Meiji myth

The story usually told is that candlesticks were invented in the 1700s by **Munehisa Homma** (1724–1803), a legendary rice merchant from the town of Sakata who traded at the **Dojima Rice Exchange** in Osaka during Japan's Tokugawa period. Homma is real and remarkable. He is said to have built a network of signalers — men stationed roughly every 6 km along the roughly 600 km route between Sakata and Osaka — to relay prices faster than competitors, and to have written one of the first books on market *psychology*, _San-en Kinsen Hiroku_ (三猿金泉秘録, "The Fountain of Gold — The Three Monkey Record of Money"), around 1755, in which he argued that traders' emotions, not just supply and demand, move prices.

Here is the honest part, and it comes from Nison himself. In _Beyond Candlesticks_, Nison pushed back on the neat legend: he concluded it is **unlikely Homma actually used candle charts**, and that the candlestick chart as we know it more likely emerged later — probably after about 1850, in the early part of the Meiji period (Meiji began in 1868), so the late 1800s. So Homma is better understood as the intellectual grandfather of the *idea* — price reflects crowd emotion — while the candle *drawing* came decades after his death.

<Callout type="warning">

Beware the tidy origin story. "A samurai-era rice trader invented candlesticks" is repeated everywhere, but the person who did the most to popularize candlesticks (Nison) is also the one who said the timeline is probably wrong. Repeating myths confidently is a habit good analysts avoid.

</Callout>

## Candle anatomy: reading one candle

Every candlestick summarizes one time period (a day, an hour, five minutes — whatever your chart is set to) using four prices:

```
                 │   ← upper shadow (a.k.a. wick): the HIGH of the period
               ┌───┐
               │   │  ← real body: the range between OPEN and CLOSE
               └───┘
                 │   ← lower shadow (wick): the LOW of the period
```

- **Real body** — the thick block between the **open** and **close** prices.
  - If price closed **higher** than it opened, the body is **hollow/green** (bullish — buyers won the period).
  - If price closed **lower** than it opened, the body is **filled/red** (bearish — sellers won).
- **Upper shadow / wick** — the thin line above the body, reaching up to the period's **high**.
- **Lower shadow / wick** — the thin line below the body, reaching down to the period's **low**.

<Callout type="note">

**Color vs. hollow — don't let your charting platform confuse you.** The charts in this lesson use solid **green** for up-candles and solid **red** for down-candles, because that is the modern default almost everywhere. But the *original* convention (and the one some platforms still use) is **hollow** for up and **filled/solid** for down, often in a single color such as black or white. So on a real screen a "bullish" candle may show up as a hollow outline rather than green. It is the same information either way — close above open. Whenever you sit down at a new platform, glance at one obvious up-day and one obvious down-day to learn which convention it uses.

</Callout>

The *shape* tells the story. A long body = strong, one-sided conviction. A tiny body = indecision. Long shadows = a price level was tested and rejected.

Two named shapes worth knowing on sight, because the patterns below are built from them:

- **Marubozu** — a body with **no shadows at all**: the open and close *are* the high and low. A white (green) marubozu means buyers controlled the period from the first tick to the last; a black (red) one means sellers did. It is the purest picture of one-sided conviction.
- **Spinning top** — a **small body with long shadows on both sides**. Price ranged far in both directions but closed near where it opened: a snapshot of indecision and a balanced fight.

## The two rules that come before any pattern

Beginners want to jump straight to "what does this pattern mean?" Nison's whole method says: not yet. Two rules govern *everything* that follows.

<Callout type="key">

**Rule 1 — Trend first.** A candlestick pattern has no meaning until you know the trend it appears in. **Bullish reversal patterns only matter inside a downtrend; bearish reversal patterns only matter inside an uptrend.** The same shape in the wrong context is just noise (or, as you'll see with the hammer, means the *opposite* thing).

**Rule 2 — Confirmation before action.** Almost every candlestick pattern is a *potential* reversal, not a guarantee. Wait for follow-through — a strong candle in the new direction, a gap, or a volume surge in the new direction, ideally **within the next 1–3 days** — before you risk money.

</Callout>

## Single-candle signals

All examples below are **illustrative**.

**Doji** — open and close are virtually equal, so the body is a thin horizontal line, like a cross or plus sign. It means **indecision**: bulls and bears fought to a draw. A doji alone is neutral; its meaning comes from where it appears, and it is only significant when it **interrupts a run of long-bodied candles** (a doji among many small bodies says nothing). Common variants: *long-legged* (long shadows both ways), *dragonfly* (a "T", long lower shadow), and *gravestone* (an inverted "T", long upper shadow).

> *Illustrative:* a stock rallies for two weeks, then prints a doji at the top. The relentless buying has stalled — a warning, not yet a sell signal.

**Hammer** — a small body sitting near the top of the range, with a **long lower shadow** (a useful rule of thumb: at least twice the body's height) and little or no upper shadow, appearing **after a decline**. Sellers pushed price way down during the period, but buyers slammed it back up to close near the high. Bullish reversal signal.

**Hanging Man** — the *exact same shape* as a hammer (small body, long lower shadow) but appearing **after an advance**. Here the long lower shadow warns that selling pressure is creeping in. Bearish.

<Callout type="key">

The hammer and hanging man are physically identical candles. What flips their meaning is **context** — the trend that came before. This is the single most important lesson in candlesticks: the same shape says different things in different places. (This is Rule 1 made concrete.)

</Callout>

## Two-candle signals

**Bullish Engulfing** — in a downtrend, a small red body is followed by a large green body whose body completely **engulfs** it (the green open is below the prior red close, and the green close is above the prior red open). Buyers have overwhelmed sellers. A major bullish reversal signal at a bottom.

**Bearish Engulfing** — the mirror image: in an uptrend, a small green body is swallowed by a large red body. A major bearish reversal signal at a top.

**Piercing Line** (bullish, after a decline) — a long red candle, then a green candle that **opens below the prior candle's low (or close) and closes back above the *midpoint* of the prior red body**. Buyers didn't fully engulf the prior candle, but they clawed back more than half of it — a strong bottoming hint.

**Dark Cloud Cover** (bearish, after an advance) — the mirror of the piercing line: a long green candle, then a red candle that **opens above the prior close and closes below the *midpoint* of the prior green body**. Sellers gave back more than half the prior gain — a topping hint.

**Harami** — the *opposite* geometry of an engulfing. A large body is followed by a **small body that is contained inside** the prior large body. The word *harami* (はらみ) is old Japanese for **"pregnant"**: picture the big first candle as the mother and the small second candle as the baby tucked inside it. Where engulfing is a forceful takeover, harami is gentler — it says the prior trend has suddenly **lost its momentum** and may stall or reverse. The smaller the inside body, the stronger the signal; when it shrinks all the way to a doji, it is a *harami cross*, an even stronger warning. As always, **context decides direction**: a bullish harami appears after a downtrend (large red, small body inside), a bearish harami after an uptrend (large green, small body inside).

<Callout type="key">

Engulfing and harami are mirror-image *shapes* with mirror-image *strength*. Engulfing = the **second** candle is bigger and overwhelms the first (a strong reversal). Harami = the **first** candle is bigger and contains the second (a softer "trend is tiring" warning). The piercing line and dark cloud cover sit in between — a *partial* takeover that recovers more than half the prior body. All of them still demand confirmation before you act.

</Callout>

## Three-candle signals

**Morning Star** (bullish, 3 candles) — (1) a large red candle in a downtrend, (2) a small-bodied candle that gaps lower (indecision — sometimes a doji, making a "morning doji star"), (3) a strong green candle that closes well **up into** the first candle's body. Like dawn after night: a bottom reversal.

**Evening Star** (bearish, 3 candles) — the mirror: (1) a large green candle in an uptrend, (2) a small gapping body above it, (3) a strong red candle closing **down into** the first body. A top reversal.

<Callout type="note">

**The stars are special.** Most patterns need a *separate* confirming candle afterward. The three-candle stars (and the rarer "abandoned baby") are often treated as **self-confirming**, because the strong third candle *is* the confirmation — the reversal is already complete inside the pattern. They are among the more reliable candlestick signals for this reason.

</Callout>

## A worked example: bullish engulfing, with the numbers checked

*(Illustrative numbers, built to show the mechanics — not historical prints.)*

A stock has been falling for two weeks, so the **downtrend context (Rule 1) is satisfied.** Two candles print:

```
Day 1 (red)    Open 50.00   High 50.20   Low 48.30   Close 48.50
Day 2 (green)  Open 48.20   High 50.60   Low 48.10   Close 50.40
```

**Check the engulfing rule.** Day 1's body runs from its open 50.00 down to its close 48.50. For a valid bullish engulfing, Day 2's green body must completely cover that:

- Day 2 **opens at 48.20**, which is *below* Day 1's close of 48.50. ✓
- Day 2 **closes at 50.40**, which is *above* Day 1's open of 50.00. ✓

Day 2's body (48.20 → 50.40) fully engulfs Day 1's body (48.50 → 50.00). This is a valid bullish engulfing in a downtrend.

**Now apply Rule 2 — confirmation.** A beginner does **not** buy on Day 2's close. They wait. Suppose **Day 3 gaps up and closes at 51.20 on above-average volume** — that is follow-through in the new direction, within the 1–3-day window. Confirmation met. Entry around 51.20, with a **protective stop placed just below the pattern's low of 48.10** — the structurally meaningful point, because if price falls back through there the bullish story is broken. (If instead Day 3 had closed back *inside* Day 1's body, you would treat the signal as failed and stand aside. You lost nothing by waiting.)

Nison repeatedly stressed **confirmation**. A single bullish candle does not erase the fact that plenty of sellers may remain; a pattern at the bottom is a reason to pay attention, not an automatic buy. And **always know where you are wrong** *before* you enter — for a bullish reversal that is the pattern's low; for a bearish one, its high.

</Callout>

## Honest criticisms and controversies

Candlesticks are beautifully intuitive, but a careful beginner should know the case *against* treating them as a reliable money-making system.

- **The rigorous evidence is mixed at best.** The most-cited academic test, **Marshall, Young & Rose (2006)** in the *Journal of Banking & Finance*, used bootstrap methods on Dow Jones (DJIA) stocks and found candlestick strategies had **no value for investors** — consistent with market efficiency. Earlier, **Caginalp & Laurent (1998)** found *positive* results for three-day reversal patterns on S&P 500 stocks, and **Lo, Mamaysky & Wang (2000)** in the *Journal of Finance* found some technical patterns carry incremental information. The honest summary: the literature genuinely **disagrees**, and many positive findings fade once transaction costs are included.
- **Efficient Market Hypothesis tension.** The EMH (Fama, 1970) holds that past prices can't predict future prices; Burton Malkiel famously likened technical analysis to "astrology" and noted any real regularity tends to become **self-defeating** once it is widely known and traded.
- **Subjectivity.** "Virtually equal" open and close, "small" body, "long enough" shadow — these are judgment calls. Two traders can look at the same candle and label it differently. The supporting literature is also dogged by **data-snooping and survivorship bias** (Irwin & Park, 2007).
- **Short horizon, designed for daily rice data.** Candlestick signals are inherently short-term (roughly 1–3 days) and developed for daily charts in a slower, gap-prone market. On 24-hour markets (forex, crypto) the "gaps" that define stars often barely exist, weakening some patterns.
- **Hindsight bias.** Patterns are dazzlingly obvious *after* the move. The hard part is acting on them in real time, when you don't know what the next candle holds.

## Common pitfalls and misconceptions

<Callout type="warning">

1. **Ignoring trend context.** A hammer in an *uptrend* isn't a buy signal — it's a hanging man (bearish). The trend defines the meaning. (Rule 1.)
2. **Skipping confirmation.** The most common beginner mistake — acting on the signal candle itself instead of waiting 1–3 days for follow-through. (Rule 2.)
3. **Pattern-spotting in noise.** A lone doji or spinning top among small bodies says nothing; these matter only among long bodies or at key levels.
4. **Using candles in isolation.** Nison himself taught candles as *one* input — to be read alongside support/resistance, volume, and trend, never alone.
5. **Over-trading.** Every chart has dozens of "patterns." Most are meaningless. Wait for ones at structurally important levels.
6. **No risk management.** Not placing the stop at the structurally logical point (the pattern's low/high) and sizing the position to that risk.
7. **Treating patterns as certainties.** They are probabilistic, trend-dependent *hints* with contested academic support — not predictions.

</Callout>

## How to apply candlesticks today — a beginner checklist

<Callout type="key">

1. **Set the trend/bias with a separate tool first** (moving averages, support/resistance, Dow-Theory higher-highs-and-lows). Candles are for *timing*, not direction.
2. **Only look for bullish reversals inside a downtrend, and bearish reversals inside an uptrend.** (Rule 1.)
3. **Look for confluence.** A bullish engulfing is far more compelling when it forms *at* a known support level or alongside an oversold RSI reading. Multiple independent reasons pointing the same way is the goal.
4. **Wait for confirmation** — a strong candle in the new direction, a gap, or a volume surge, within 1–3 days — before risking money. (Exception: three-candle stars are largely self-confirming.)
5. **Place the stop at the pattern's structural point** (its low for a bullish signal, its high for a bearish one) and size the trade to that risk.
6. **Respect the timeframe.** A bullish engulfing on a weekly chart is a far stronger statement than one on a 1-minute chart.

</Callout>
