## The one-sentence version

Steve Nison is the analyst who took a centuries-old Japanese charting method that almost no one in the West understood and, through a 1991 book, turned it into the visual language nearly every trading screen on the planet now speaks.

If you have ever looked at a stock chart and seen little red and green boxes with thin lines poking out the top and bottom, you have used candlesticks. That picture feels obvious today. It was not obvious in the 1980s. Back then the standard Western chart was a thin bar, and the rich, psychology-revealing world of Japanese candles was locked away in Japanese-language texts and trading-floor folklore. One person did more than anyone to unlock it for English-speaking traders. This lesson is about him, the history he uncovered, and the patterns he taught the West to read.

<Callout type="key">

A candlestick does not show you any new data. It shows you the *same four numbers* as a bar chart — open, high, low, close — but arranged so your eye instantly grasps the tug-of-war between buyers and sellers. Candlesticks are a visualization of market psychology, not a new data source.

</Callout>

## Who Steve Nison is

Steve Nison is an American technical analyst (technical analysis = the study of price charts to forecast future price moves, as opposed to studying a company's finances). He is widely and consistently credited as the person who introduced Japanese candlestick charting to the Western world.

He did this primarily through two books:

- **_Japanese Candlestick Charting Techniques_** — first published in **1991** (a second edition, from Prentice Hall Press, followed in 2001). This is the book that put candlesticks on Western trading desks.
- **_Beyond Candlesticks: New Japanese Charting Techniques Revealed_** — published by **John Wiley & Sons in 1994**, which went deeper and introduced additional Japanese methods.

<Callout type="note">

A note on biography honesty: Nison is well documented as a working analyst, author, and lecturer who studied the Japanese material directly (including learning from Japanese traders and translating their concepts). You will see claims online that he worked at specific firms such as Merrill Lynch, or that he holds the CMT (Chartered Market Technician) credential. Those specific employment and certification details are not confirmed by the primary references used for this lesson, so treat them as unverified. What is solidly established is his authorship and his role as the popularizer of candlesticks in the West.

</Callout>

What makes Nison historically important is not that he *invented* anything. It is that he was a careful researcher and translator. He went back to the original sources, took them seriously, and even corrected popular myths about candlestick history — which is exactly the kind of intellectual honesty this guide values.

## The history Nison uncovered: rice, Homma, and the Meiji myth

The story usually told is that candlesticks were invented in the 1700s by **Munehisa Homma** (1724–1803), a legendary rice merchant from the town of Sakata who traded at the **Dojima Rice Exchange** in Osaka during Japan's Tokugawa period. Homma is real and remarkable. He is said to have built a network of signalers — men stationed roughly every 6 km along the roughly 600 km route between Sakata and Osaka — to relay prices faster than competitors, and to have written one of the first books on market *psychology*: _San-en Kinsen Hiroku_ (三猿金泉秘録, "The Fountain of Gold — The Three Monkey Record of Money") around 1755, in which he argued that traders' emotions, not just supply and demand, move prices.

Here is the honest part, and it comes from Nison himself. In _Beyond Candlesticks_, Nison pushed back on the neat legend: he concluded it is **unlikely Homma actually used candle charts**, and that the candlestick chart as we know it more likely emerged later — probably after about 1850, in the early part of the Meiji period (Meiji began in 1868), so the late 1800s. So Homma is better understood as the intellectual grandfather of the *idea* — price reflects crowd emotion — while the candle *drawing* came decades after his death.

<Callout type="warning">

Beware the tidy origin story. "A samurai-era rice trader invented candlesticks" is repeated everywhere, but the person who did the most to popularize candlesticks (Nison) is also the one who said the timeline is probably wrong. Repeating myths confidently is a habit good analysts avoid.

</Callout>

## Candle anatomy: reading one candle

Every candlestick summarizes one time period (a day, an hour, five minutes — whatever your chart is set to) using four prices:

```
                 │   ← upper shadow (a.k.a. wick): the HIGH of the period
               ┌───┐
               │   │  ← real body: the range between OPEN and CLOSE
               └───┘
                 │   ← lower shadow (wick): the LOW of the period
```

- **Real body** — the thick block between the **open** and **close** prices.
  - If price closed **higher** than it opened, the body is **hollow/green** (bullish — buyers won the period).
  - If price closed **lower** than it opened, the body is **filled/red** (bearish — sellers won).
- **Upper shadow / wick** — the thin line above the body, reaching up to the period's **high**.
- **Lower shadow / wick** — the thin line below the body, reaching down to the period's **low**.

The *shape* tells the story. A long body = strong, one-sided conviction. A tiny body = indecision. Long shadows = a price level was tested and rejected.

## Key patterns

Patterns come as single candles or multi-candle sequences. Below are the ones Nison made famous. All examples are **illustrative**.

### Single-candle signals

**Doji** — open and close are virtually equal, so the body is a thin horizontal line, like a cross or plus sign. It means **indecision**: bulls and bears fought to a draw. A doji alone is neutral; its meaning comes from where it appears.

> *Illustrative:* a stock rallies for two weeks, then prints a doji at the top. The relentless buying has stalled — a warning, not yet a sell signal.

**Hammer** — a small body sitting near the top of the range, with a **long lower shadow** and little or no upper shadow, appearing **after a decline**. Sellers pushed price way down during the period, but buyers slammed it back up to close near the high. Bullish reversal signal.

**Hanging Man** — the *exact same shape* as a hammer (small body, long lower shadow) but appearing **after an advance**. Here the long lower shadow warns that selling pressure is creeping in. Bearish.

<Callout type="key">

The hammer and hanging man are physically identical candles. What flips their meaning is **context** — the trend that came before. This is the single most important lesson in candlesticks: the same shape says different things in different places.

</Callout>

### Multi-candle signals

**Bullish Engulfing** — in a downtrend, a small red body is followed by a large green body that completely **engulfs** it (the green body's range covers the prior red body). Buyers have overwhelmed sellers. A major bullish reversal signal at a bottom.

**Bearish Engulfing** — the mirror image: in an uptrend, a small green body is swallowed by a large red body. A major bearish reversal signal at a top.

**Morning Star** (bullish, 3 candles) — (1) a large red candle in a downtrend, (2) a small-bodied candle that gaps lower (indecision — sometimes a doji, making a "morning doji star"), (3) a strong green candle that closes well **up into** the first candle's body. Like dawn after night: a bottom reversal.

**Evening Star** (bearish, 3 candles) — the mirror: (1) a large green candle in an uptrend, (2) a small gapping body above it, (3) a strong red candle closing **down into** the first body. A top reversal.

## A small worked example (illustrative)

Suppose stock XYZ has fallen from $52 to $40 over three weeks. On the next day:

```
Open  = 40.20
High  = 40.40
Low   = 38.00
Close = 40.10
```

The body is tiny (40.20 → 40.10) and sits at the top; the lower shadow runs all the way down to 38.00. That is a **hammer**: sellers drove price to 38 but were overpowered, and price closed back near 40. On its own, it is a *hint*. The disciplined read: wait for **confirmation** — say, the next day opening higher and closing above 41 on strong volume. If instead the next day plunges through 38, the hammer failed. You did not lose by waiting.

<Callout type="warning">

Nison repeatedly stressed **confirmation**. A single bullish candle does not erase the fact that plenty of sellers may remain; a hammer at the bottom is a reason to pay attention, not an automatic buy. Confirmation = a follow-through move (a strong candle in the expected direction, a gap, or a volume surge) that supports the signal before you act.

</Callout>

## Honest criticisms and controversies

- **Subjectivity.** "Virtually equal" open and close, "small" body, "long" shadow — these are judgment calls. Two traders can look at the same candle and label it differently.
- **Reliability is debated.** Academic and practitioner backtests of candlestick patterns are mixed; many patterns show little statistically reliable edge once trading costs are included, especially in liquid modern markets. Candlesticks are best treated as a lens on psychology and timing, not a stand-alone "buy/sell" oracle.
- **Designed for daily rice data.** The patterns were developed for daily charts in a slower, gap-prone market. On 24-hour markets (forex, crypto) the "gaps" that define stars often barely exist, weakening some patterns.
- **Hindsight bias.** Patterns are dazzlingly obvious *after* the move. The hard part is acting on them in real time, when you don't know what the next candle holds.

## Common pitfalls and misconceptions

- **Trading patterns without context.** A hammer means nothing without a prior downtrend to reverse.
- **Ignoring the timeframe.** A bullish engulfing on a 1-minute chart is a far weaker statement than one on a weekly chart.
- **Skipping confirmation.** The most common beginner mistake — acting on the signal candle itself.
- **Memorizing dozens of patterns.** You do not need them all. Doji, hammer/hanging man, engulfings, and morning/evening stars cover most of the useful ground.

## How to apply candlesticks today

1. **Use them for timing, not direction.** Decide trend/bias from a separate tool (moving averages, support/resistance), then use candles to time your entry.
2. **Combine with confluence.** A bullish engulfing is far more compelling when it forms *at* a known support level or alongside an oversold RSI reading. Multiple independent reasons pointing the same way is the goal.
3. **Always require confirmation** before risking money.
4. **Respect the timeframe** — higher timeframes give stronger signals.
