## The one idea that matters most

Most beginners think great traders are great because they're **right** most of
the time. Paul Tudor Jones is famous for the opposite belief:

> "Don't focus on making money; focus on protecting what you have."

He does not try to win often. He tries to **lose small when he's wrong and win
big when he's right.** Get that asymmetry right and you can be wrong on *most* of
your trades and still get rich. Get it wrong — by letting losers run — and you
will blow up no matter how clever your market calls are.

That is the whole lesson. Everything below is the machinery that makes it work.

<Callout type="key">
Jones's edge is **not** prediction. It's the *structure* of his bets: small,
pre-defined risk on every trade, and a payoff several times larger when he's
right. Survival first; profit second. As he put it: *"Five to one means I'm
risking one dollar to make five. I can be wrong 80% of the time and still not
lose."*
</Callout>

## Who he was

**Paul Tudor Jones II** was born September 28, 1954, in Memphis, Tennessee. He
earned a BA in economics from the University of Virginia in 1976 — where he was
also a welterweight boxer, a detail that fits the disciplined, combative
temperament he brought to trading. He was accepted to Harvard Business School
but chose not to attend, heading to the trading floor instead.

He learned the craft on the floor. His mentor was **Eli Tullis**, a legendary
cotton-futures broker who traded at the New York Cotton Exchange. Tullis drilled
into him **emotional control** — the ability to sit through brutal pressure
without panicking. Jones then worked as a commodities broker at **E.F. Hutton &
Co.** in his mid-twenties.

In **1980** he founded **Tudor Investment Corporation** (today based in Stamford,
Connecticut), starting with roughly $30,000 to trade. It grew into one of the
world's best-known **global macro** hedge funds, reported at around $12 billion
in assets in the early 2020s, charging fees well above the standard "2 and 20."
Forbes has placed his personal net worth in the multi-billion-dollar range
(roughly $5–8 billion across recent years).

Two years built the legend:

- **1987:** the fund returned roughly **125.9% after fees** — Jones had
  positioned short ahead of the October crash, a year that reportedly earned him
  around $100 million personally.
- **1990:** roughly **87.4%**, in part by shorting the bursting Japanese stock
  bubble.

<Callout type="note">
Jones **never wrote a book.** His method survives through two primary sources:
the 1987 PBS documentary *Trader*, which filmed him during the Black Monday
period (he later had it pulled from circulation, which only added to its
legend), and his chapter in Jack Schwager's *Market Wizards* (1989). Tony
Robbins's *MONEY: Master the Game* (2014) also contains an interview where Jones
lays out his 200-day rule and the 5:1 framework in plain language. Be skeptical
of anything claiming to be "Paul Tudor Jones's book."
</Callout>

He is also a major philanthropist: he co-founded the **Robin Hood Foundation**
(1988) to fight poverty in New York City, founded the Excellence Charter School,
co-founded JUST Capital, and signed the Giving Pledge in 2019.

## What "global macro" means

A quick definition, because it frames everything. A **global macro** trader bets
on the big picture — interest rates, currencies, commodities, and stock indices
— rather than on individual companies. The view is **top-down**: "rates are
going to fall," "the yen is overvalued," "this stock market is a bubble."

Jones is a **discretionary** macro trader (he uses judgment, not a fixed
computer formula). But here is the twist that makes him useful to a beginner. He
splits every trade into three questions:

- **What** to trade — his macro thesis: rates, inflation, central-bank policy,
  intermarket links. The story tells him *which market is stretched.*
- **When** to act — price action, momentum, support/resistance, and the trend
  filter. *"I am a slave to the tape and proud of it."*
- **When he's wrong** — the price level that kills the thesis. This is decided
  *before* he enters.

The macro story chooses the battlefield; **price** controls the actual trade and
gets the final word. He never lets a beautiful narrative override a stop.

## The core principle: asymmetric risk and reward

"Reward-to-risk" is the ratio of how much you stand to gain if you're right to
how much you'll lose if you're wrong. Jones is famous for targeting roughly
**5:1** — risk one dollar to make five.

Why does that ratio change everything? Because it **decouples profit from being
right.** Watch what a 5:1 payoff does to your win-rate requirement:

<BarChart

  yLabel="Break-even win rate (%)"

  series={[{ label: "Win rate needed just to break even", data: [50, 33, 25, 17] }]}
/>

At 1:1 you must be right more than half the time merely to stay even. At 5:1 you
break even winning roughly **1 trade in 6** (about 17%) — so even being right
just **20% of the time turns a profit.** That is exactly what Jones means by
"wrong 80% of the time and still not losing." Being right is almost *optional*;
cutting losers fast is *mandatory.*

<Callout type="key">
The asymmetry only works if the **loss side stays small and fixed.** The whole
edifice collapses the moment you let one loser grow past its planned size. That
is why the risk rules below are not optional decoration — they *are* the strategy.
</Callout>

## The full method, step by step

These principles come mainly from the *Market Wizards* interview, the Tony
Robbins interview, and the *Trader* documentary. A beginner can follow them
literally.

### 1. Identify the dominant trend — and don't fight it

Jones's first teaching rule: *"Always want to be with whatever the predominant
trend is."* His primary trend filter is the **200-day moving average (200-DMA)**.
A **moving average** is just the average closing price over the last N days,
recalculated each day so it glides along the chart.

- Price **above** the 200-DMA → bullish bias; only hunt for **longs.**
- Price **below** the 200-DMA → bearish bias; only hunt for **shorts** (or step
  aside).

The goal isn't a precise buy/sell trigger — it's to **never be holding a large
position while fighting the primary trend.** When an asset breaks below its
200-DMA, you *"play defense, and you get out."* This is the very signal that
flagged 1987 for him.

### 2. Define your risk BEFORE you enter

For every trade, decide in advance the exact price at which you'll admit you're
wrong — your **stop** — and translate that into a dollar amount. Jones is
constantly asking, *"Where will I be wrong?"* before the trade, not during it. If
you can't answer that before clicking buy, you have no trade. Place the stop at a
**structural level** (just below a recent swing low, or below the 200-DMA), not
at an arbitrary round number.

### 3. Demand an asymmetric payoff (aim ~5:1)

Only take trades where the realistic profit target is a large multiple of the
amount risked — ideally about five times. If the upside isn't several times the
downside, **skip it.** Most ideas won't qualify — that's the point. The ratio is
a filter that forces patience and rejects mediocre setups.

### 4. Size positions small — by the loss, not the hope

Risk only a small, fixed fraction of capital per trade and size by **the dollar
you can lose**, never by the profit you're dreaming of. *"I always size my
positions so that I can sleep at night."* Be aggressive only when you're already
right and winning; stay small when uncertain.

### 5. Cut losses fast — "losers average losers"

Jones's most quoted rule, the note famously taped above his desk. **Never add to
a losing position** to lower your average price. "Averaging down" *feels* smart
(you're buying cheaper) but it quietly turns a small, planned loss into an
account-killer. The traders who blow up almost always blew up adding to losers.

### 6. De-risk during drawdowns — size down when losing, up when winning

When your equity is falling, **shrink** your position sizes and trade small until
you're back in sync with the market. Press harder only when things are going your
way. This is the exact opposite of the beginner instinct to "make it back fast"
by sizing up after a loss. Trade biggest when winning, smallest when losing.

### 7. Re-evaluate every position daily, as if you were flat

Each morning ask: *"Knowing only what I know right now, would I put this trade on
today?"* If the answer is no, get out. Your **entry price is irrelevant** — the
market doesn't know or care what you paid. This kills *anchoring*, the beginner
habit of holding a loser just because of where you bought.

### 8. Have no ego — protect capital above all

The market is right; your opinion is not. Jones will flip from bullish to bearish
the moment price proves him wrong — but only after a *confirmed* failure, not on
a hunch. Survival outranks profit. You cannot compound if you blow up. Every rule
above serves this one.

## A worked example: the 5:1 math in action

*(Illustrative numbers, built straight from the rules. The 1% risk figure is a
standard conservative convention — what Jones actually fixes is the 5:1 ratio and
"sleep at night" sizing.)*

Start with a **$50,000 account** and risk a fixed **1% ($500) per trade**,
targeting **5:1**.

**The trade.** Stock XYZ trades at **$100** and is **above** its 200-day MA (the
trend filter passes — Step 1). You set your stop at **$95**, just below a recent
swing low — below that, your thesis is wrong. Your risk is **$5 per share.** To
risk exactly $500 you buy **100 shares** ($500 ÷ $5 = a $10,000 position).

- **Target:** 5 × the $5 risk = +$25, so you exit near **$125.**
- **If it works:** 100 × $25 = **+$2,500** (a "5R" win — five times the risk;
  +5% of the account).
- **If you're stopped out:** 100 × $5 = **–$500** (a "1R" loss; –1% of the
  account).

Now run **10 such trades** where you are *wrong 80% of the time* — right on only
2:

- 8 losers × (–$500) = **–$4,000**
- 2 winners × (+$2,500) = **+$5,000**
- **Net = +$1,000**

A **20% win rate** still produced a profit. That is the entire Jones edge in one
table — and it is why he can shrug off being wrong four times out of five.

<Callout type="warning">
Now break one rule — **average down** on a losing trade instead of stopping out
at $95. Buy 100 more shares at $90 to "improve your average," and the stock keeps
falling to $85. Your single loss balloons toward **–$2,500+**, erasing an entire
5R winner. One broken rule wipes out the math. This is exactly the behavior
"losers average losers" forbids.
</Callout>

## Black Monday, 1987: the strategy's most famous day

On **Monday, October 19, 1987**, the Dow Jones Industrial Average fell **508
points — 22.6% in a single day**, still the largest one-day percentage drop in
its history (the S&P 500 fell about 20.5%). A feedback loop called **portfolio
insurance** — automated programs that sold futures as prices fell, which pushed
prices lower, which triggered more selling — amplified the collapse.

Jones and his research head **Peter Borish** had been bearish for months. Borish
famously overlaid the mid-1980s market onto the run-up to the 1929 crash and saw
the same shape. Tudor was **positioned short**, and the crash delivered the
fund's roughly **125.9% year** — Jones personally is reported to have made around
$100 million.

The lesson for a beginner is **not** "try to short the next crash." It's that

Jones bets it hard. Most days he is playing tight defense, waiting for that rare
pitch.

**Treat the exact percentages as approximate.** "Roughly tripled," "+125.9%

sources. The *direction* and *scale* of the win are well-established; the precise
figure is source-dependent.
</Callout>

## Honest criticisms and limitations

<Callout type="warning">
No method is a money machine. Be clear-eyed about these.
</Callout>

- **It's discretionary — hard to replicate.** Jones's real edge is judgment,
  feel for crowd psychology, and decades on the floor. The 200-DMA and 5:1 rules
  are real, but *which* macro theme and *which* technical signal to act on is not
  a formula a beginner can copy from a book.
- **Survivorship bias / one-trade fame.** The 1987 home run is legendary *because*
  it's rare. Many who tried to "short the crash" were wrong (and bleeding) for
  months first. One spectacular year doesn't validate the approach for everyone,
  and Tudor's later returns have been far more pedestrian.
- **The 200-day MA whipsaws.** In choppy, range-bound markets the trend filter
  flips you in and out repeatedly — you sell low, buy back higher. It's a *coarse,
  lagging* filter that shines in strong trends, not sideways ones.
- **5:1 is aspirational, not a guarantee.** Many good real trades offer only 2:1
  or 3:1. Forcing 5:1 can leave you with very few qualifying trades — or tempt you
  to set the stop *too tight* just to fake the ratio (and get whipsawed out). The
  number is a *discipline*, not a promise markets will hand you 5R moves.
- **Cost and access asymmetry.** Tudor charged premium fees and traded leveraged
  futures and FX with institutional information flow, leverage, and liquidity a
  retail beginner doesn't — and shouldn't — access the same way.
- **Macro forecasting is humbling.** Even Jones has had flat, mediocre stretches,
  and global macro as a category has endured long lean periods. His record is
  uneven *outside* the famous peaks.

## Common beginner pitfalls

<Callout type="warning">
1. **Averaging down on losers** to "fix" a bad trade — the single biggest account
   killer, and the exact thing Jones forbids.
2. **Moving or removing the stop** to avoid being stopped out. Jones's stops are
   respected and never widened.
3. **Sizing for the win instead of the loss** — size by the *dollar you can lose*,
   not the profit you hope for.
4. **Chasing the trend at exhaustion** — buying after a parabolic move with no
   stop, mistaking momentum for safety.
5. **Faking 5:1 with a too-tight stop** — setting an arbitrarily close stop just
   to make the ratio look good, then getting whipsawed out.
6. **Adding risk during a losing streak** ("I'll win it back") — the opposite of
   Jones, who *cuts* size in drawdowns.
7. **Ignoring the trend filter** — fighting the 200-DMA on a personal opinion.
   Jones trades *with* the dominant trend; contrarian entries come only after a
   confirmed failure, never on a hunch.
8. **Treating it as pure technicals** — skipping the macro "why" and trading lines
   on a chart with no context.
</Callout>

## Putting it to work

You don't need a hedge fund to trade like Jones. You need a checklist and the
discipline to obey it:

1. **Check the regime first.** Is price above or below its 200-day MA? Trade
   *with* the primary trend; don't fight it.
2. **Write down your stop and your dollar risk before every entry.** Put the stop
   at a structural level and risk a small, fixed fraction of your account (1% is a
   sensible start).
3. **Score the reward-to-risk.** If it's not at least ~3:1 (aim 5:1), pass — and
   don't tighten the stop just to fake the ratio.
4. **Size by the loss.** Shares = dollar risk ÷ per-share risk. Size so you can
   sleep at night.
5. **Never add to a loser.** If you're stopped, you're out — no exceptions.
6. **Each morning, re-decide every position as if you held cash.** Would you put
   it on today? If not, exit.
7. **Size down when you're cold; size up only when you're hot.**
8. **Keep a journal** of win rate *and* average reward-to-risk. The second number
   matters far more than the first — that realization is the entire point of this
   lesson.

The mindset, in one line: **be wrong small, be right big, and never let a single
trade threaten your survival.**
