## Why he matters

Ask most people what a stock is "worth" and they'll say: *whatever someone will
pay for it.* That answer treats the market like a casino — value is just the next
person's guess, and you win only by finding a "greater fool" to sell to.

In **1938**, a Harvard graduate student named **John Burr Williams** drew a hard
line through that idea. A stock, he argued, is not worth what the crowd will pay.
It is worth **the cash it will actually hand you over its lifetime** — its
dividends and, eventually, its sale price — with future cash counted as worth
less than cash today. He called this the **"rule of present worth,"** and he gave
investors an actual formula to compute it.

That formula is the seed of nearly every valuation method in use today: the
**dividend discount model (DDM)**, the **Gordon Growth Model**, and the broader
**discounted cash flow (DCF)** analysis analysts run every day. More than half a
century later, **Warren Buffett** opened his **1992 Berkshire Hathaway letter** by
crediting Williams with setting "forth the equation for value": the worth of any
stock, bond, or business is the cash that flows in and out over its remaining
life, discounted at an appropriate rate. When Buffett says a business is worth
"the cash it will generate from now until judgment day, discounted back to the
present," he is restating Williams almost word for word.

<Callout type="key">
The whole lesson in one line: **price is what the market quotes; value is the
discounted cash a thing will pay you.** Williams turned that distinction from a
slogan into arithmetic you can do on a napkin.
</Callout>

## Who he was

**John Burr Williams** (November 27, 1900 – September 15, 1989) was not an
ivory-tower theorist. He was a **working security analyst** who got frustrated
that nobody could tell him what a stock was really worth — and went back to school
to solve the problem.

- He studied **mathematics and chemistry** at Harvard, then entered **Harvard
  Business School in 1923.**
- From **1927** he worked as a security analyst and managed private investment
  portfolios — living through the **1929 crash** and the Depression that followed.
  Watching prices detach completely from any sane notion of worth is what drove
  his life's question.
- In **1932** he returned to Harvard for a **PhD in economics.** His thesis-adviser
  circle included the famous economist **Joseph Schumpeter**, who suggested the
  topic: **the intrinsic value of a common stock.**
- The thesis became *The Theory of Investment Value*, published by **Harvard
  University Press in 1938.** He earned the doctorate itself in **1940.**
- He also served as a **visiting professor** at the **University of
  Wisconsin–Madison**, and ran the family investment firm, Burr & Company.

<Callout type="note">
A telling detail: publishers initially balked at the book **because it was full of
algebraic symbols** — too mathematical for the investing audience of the day.
Williams agreed to help fund the printing himself. Benjamin Graham reviewed it in
1939, and the finance theorist Mark Rubinstein later called it an "insufficiently
appreciated classic." The founding text of intrinsic-value math was almost never
published *because* it had equations in it.
</Callout>

## The core idea in plain language

Williams's insight rests on one fact about money: **a dollar today is worth more
than a dollar next year.** Why? Because the dollar you have today can be invested
to earn a return. So a dollar promised in the future has to be *discounted* —
shrunk down — to state its worth in today's money.

Stack that idea up over a company's whole life and you get the rule of present
worth:

> **The value of a stock = the sum of all the cash it will ever pay you, with
> each future payment discounted back to today.**

For a stock, that cash is mainly **dividends** (and the price you eventually sell
it for). For a bond, it's the **coupons plus the face value at maturity.** Same
machine, different cash flows. As Buffett put it in 1992, "the formula is the same
for stocks as for bonds" — the only difference is that a bond's coupons are
printed on it, whereas for a stock **you** must estimate the future "coupons"
(the dividends) yourself. That estimation, not the arithmetic, is the hard part.

<Callout type="note">
A folksy line is widely **attributed** to Williams — roughly *"A cow for her milk,
a hen for her eggs, and a stock, by heck, for her dividends."* It captures his
thesis perfectly (own a thing for the cash it produces, not for resale), but the
exact wording is repeated everywhere without a verifiable source, so treat it as
an attributed paraphrase. The verified, quotable framing is his own opening: real
worth and market price are "separate and distinct things not to be confused."
</Callout>

## The full method — steps a beginner can follow

### Step 1 — Forecast the cash flows to the owner

List what the investment will actually pay you. For a dividend-paying stock,
that's the expected **dividend each year** (`D₁, D₂, D₃, …`). If you plan to sell
one day, add the expected **sale price** in the year you sell.

### Step 2 — Choose a discount rate `r`

This is the annual return you **demand** for taking the risk — also called the
*required return* or *cost of equity.* A safe, boring utility might justify
`r = 7%`; a risky startup might need `r = 15%` or more. **Higher risk → higher
`r`.** Beginners often estimate it with **CAPM** (risk-free rate + beta × equity
risk premium) or simply pick an honest required return like 8–10%. As a sanity
check, `r` should sit well above what a risk-free government bond pays — you're
being paid extra for taking on risk.

### Step 3 — Discount each cash flow to today

A payment `CFₜ` arriving in year *t* is worth this much in today's money:

```
present value = CFₜ / (1 + r)ᵗ
```

The further out the payment, the more those compounding `(1+r)` divisions shrink
it. A dollar 30 years away is worth pennies today.

### Step 4 — Add them all up

The intrinsic value `V` is just the sum of every discounted cash flow:

```
V = D₁/(1+r)¹ + D₂/(1+r)² + D₃/(1+r)³ + … + (sale price)/(1+r)ⁿ
```

That's the whole engine. Everything below is shortcuts for doing this sum without
forecasting infinitely many years by hand.

### Step 5 — Use the shortcut when dividends grow steadily

Forecasting every year out to infinity is impossible. So Williams — and later,
more famously, **Myron Gordon** and **Eli Shapiro in 1956**, 18 years after him —
showed that *if* dividends grow at a **constant rate `g` forever**, the infinite
sum collapses to a tiny formula, the **Gordon Growth Model**:

```
V = D₁ / (r − g)
```

where `D₁` is **next year's** dividend = `D₀ × (1 + g)`. This only works when
`g < r` (more on why in the criticisms). A useful rearrangement shows what your
return actually is: `r = D₁/V + g` — that is, **total return = dividend yield +
growth.** The zero-growth special case is just `V = D₁ / r`.

<Callout type="note">
Williams is the root; Gordon gave the famous one-line shortcut. Williams put forth
the dividend discount model **18 years before** Gordon and Shapiro published their
constant-growth formula in 1956.
</Callout>

### Step 6 — Handle fast-growing or no-dividend companies

Many companies grow fast for a while, then settle down — and some pay no dividend
at all yet. For those, use a **two-stage (multi-stage) model:**

1. **Forecast the high-growth years explicitly** and discount each one (Step 3).
2. Add a **"terminal value"** for the steady phase afterward — usually a
   Gordon-style `D/(r−g)` — and discount *that* back to today too.

For companies that pay no dividend, analysts swap in **earnings or free cash
flow** in place of dividends. The logic is identical; only the cash-flow
definition changes. (This is exactly why modern **DCF on free cash flow** largely
superseded the strict dividend-only model.)

### Step 7 — Compare to the market price, then stress-test

- If `V` is **comfortably above** the price → possibly undervalued.
- If `V` is **below** the price → possibly overvalued.

Then immediately **re-run your inputs** with pessimistic and optimistic `g` and
`r`. If the verdict flips with a tiny nudge, you don't have an edge — you have a
guess (see pitfalls).

## Worked example A — the Gordon shortcut

*(The formula `V = D₁/(r−g)` is the verified Gordon form; the dollar figures below
are illustrative numbers plugged into it to show the mechanics.)*

A company pays a dividend of **D₀ = $2.00** this year. You expect it to grow at
**g = 4%** per year forever, and you require a **9%** return (`r = 0.09`).

- Next year's dividend: `D₁ = 2.00 × 1.04 =` **$2.08**
- Intrinsic value: `V = 2.08 / (0.09 − 0.04) = 2.08 / 0.05 =` **$41.60**

Now read off the verdict against different market prices:

### The sensitivity lesson (do not skip this)

Change a single assumption and watch the value lurch. Keep everything the same but
raise growth from 4% to 5%:

- `D₁ = 2.00 × 1.05 =` **$2.10**
- `V = 2.10 / (0.09 − 0.05) = 2.10 / 0.04 =` **$52.50**

A **one-percentage-point** bump in `g` swung the value from **$41.60 to $52.50** —
about **+26%.** Nudge `g` and `r` together and the answer can double or halve.

<Callout type="warning">
This fragility is the single most important practical fact about Williams's model.
The formula is exact, but the **inputs are forecasts.** "Garbage in, garbage out"
isn't a knock on the method — it's a warning to handle the inputs with humility and
always think in **ranges**, never a single confident number.
</Callout>

## Worked example B — the full sum, with a sale

The Gordon shortcut hides the engine. Here's the engine itself (Step 4), for an
investor who plans to **hold three years and then sell.** You expect:

- Year 1 dividend **$2.50**, Year 2 **$3.00**, Year 3 **$3.25**
- An estimated **sale price of $125** at the end of Year 3
- A required return of **r = 5%** (`0.05`)

Discount each cash flow back to today, then add:

```
V = 2.50/1.05¹ + 3.00/1.05² + (3.25 + 125)/1.05³
  = 2.38      + 2.72      + 110.79
  ≈ $115.89
```

If the stock trades at **$110**, your intrinsic value ($115.89) sits *above* the
price → it looks **undervalued.** Notice two things: the **sale price dominates**
(it's most of the $115.89), and a pure dividends-only model would have ignored it
entirely — which is why, if you include a sale, you must *not* also extend
dividends to infinity for that same window (that would double-count).

## Why the `r − g` denominator is dangerous

Look again at `V = D₁ / (r − g)`. As growth `g` creeps toward the required return
`r`, the denominator `(r − g)` shrinks toward **zero** — and dividing by a number
near zero sends value toward **infinity.** If you ever assume `g ≥ r`, the formula
returns a negative or infinite "value" and becomes meaningless.

The real-world meaning is humbling: **no company can outgrow its cost of capital
forever**, and over the very long run no company can outgrow the whole economy.
A perpetual `g` of 4–5% is already an aggressive, mature-company assumption — keep
it below long-run GDP growth (roughly 2–4%) and always safely below `r`.

## Williams's deeper contributions

Beyond the formula, *The Theory of Investment Value* introduced ideas that
economists later made famous:

- The **"Law of the Conservation of Investment Value"** — the notion that how you
  *slice* a company's financing (more debt vs. more equity) doesn't change the
  total value of the underlying business; it just divides the same pie
  differently. Decades later **Franco Modigliani and Merton Miller** (1958) noted
  that Williams had **anticipated** their celebrated capital-structure theorem —
  though Williams never proved the arbitrage mechanism that makes it hold. M&M
  supplied that proof.
- A clean separation of **investment** (buying for the cash a thing produces) from
  **speculation** (buying to resell to someone else at a higher price) — the same
  distinction Benjamin Graham would soon hammer home.

<Callout type="key">
Williams sits at the headwaters of fundamental analysis. **Graham** built the
*margin of safety* and *Mr. Market* on top of him; **Gordon and Shapiro** gave us
the closed-form shortcut; **Buffett** turned discounted owner-cash into a life's
work and credited Williams by name. Learn Williams and the rest of this school
reads as variations on his theme.
</Callout>

## Criticisms and limitations (be honest)

- **Extreme input sensitivity.** As shown above, small changes in `g` or `r` move
  the answer enormously. The model amplifies your forecasting errors, and its
  precise-looking output is partly an illusion.
- **The `r − g` trap.** The formula breaks down — or returns nonsense — whenever
  `g ≥ r`. Yet real growth firms genuinely *do* outgrow their discount rate for
  years, so the steady-state assumption misfits exactly the exciting companies.
- **It assumes you can forecast cash flows far into the future.** Williams modeled
  dividends "under certainty"; in reality the future is deeply uncertain.
  Predicting dividends decades out is heroic for all but the steadiest blue chips.
- **It doesn't fit non-dividend payers** out of the box. Fast-growing tech firms,
  or a company like Berkshire Hathaway that has never paid a dividend, require
  substituting earnings or free cash flow and invoking "dividend irrelevance."
- **The pure dividend version ignores the sale price** unless you use the
  multi-period form (Example B).
- **The conservation-of-value law was incomplete** — Williams stated the
  intuition but left the rigorous arbitrage proof to Modigliani and Miller.

## Common beginner pitfalls

<Callout type="warning">
1. **Treating the output as a precise truth.** It's a *range*, not a price tag.
   Always run a low/base/high scenario.
2. **Picking `g` too close to `r`** to "justify" a price you already like — that's
   mathematically explosive and intellectually dishonest.
3. **Confusing the growth rate `g` with the discount rate `r`.** They pull in
   opposite directions — `g` raises value, `r` lowers it.
4. **Applying the pure dividend model to a non-dividend stock** — switch to
   earnings or free cash flow instead.
5. **Forecasting a single growth rate forever** instead of using a two-stage model
   that lets fast growth *fade* to a sustainable rate.
6. **Mistaking "the formula" for "the answer."** Buffett's caution: the equation
   is the easy part; estimating the future "coupons" is the actual job.
7. **Assuming a high computed value guarantees a profit.** The market can stay
   "wrong" far longer than you can wait. Intrinsic value is a thesis, not a fact.
8. **Confusing price with value** — the exact error Williams wrote the book to
   destroy. And don't double-count: a sale price *or* perpetual dividends, never
   both for the same period.
</Callout>

## Putting it to work

You don't need a Bloomberg terminal to use Williams. Open a spreadsheet and do
this on a stock you know:

1. Pull its current annual dividend (`D₀`) and a few years of dividend history to
   estimate a **conservative** growth rate `g`.
2. Pick a required return `r` that honestly reflects the risk — and keep it safely
   above `g`.
3. Compute the Gordon value `V = D₀(1+g)/(r−g)` for a steady payer, or build a
   simple two-stage model (or the explicit sum in Example B) for a grower.
4. **Run it three times** — pessimistic, base, optimistic. Write down the range,
   not one number.
5. Compare the *low end* of your range to the market price. Only when price sits
   comfortably below even your cautious estimate do you have Graham's **margin of
   safety.**

If the value only looks attractive under your rosiest assumptions, you haven't
found a bargain — you've found a story. Williams's gift was the discipline to tell
the difference.
