## The trade that made him famous

In the depths of 2008 — the year the financial world fell apart and the average
hedge fund lost roughly 19% — a small fund run from London by a Glaswegian named
Hugh Hendry returned **+31.2%.**

He didn't do it by shorting banks, which is what everyone now claims they would
have done. He did it by noticing something subtler: that the entire world had
fallen in love with one story — *"China's growth is unstoppable"* — and that you
could bet against that story cheaply, in a corner of the market nobody was
watching, in a way that cost almost nothing if you were wrong and paid a fortune
if you were right.

That single idea — **find the consensus, find why it's fragile, then buy cheap
insurance against it** — is the heart of Hugh Hendry. This lesson teaches the
whole playbook clearly enough that you could run it yourself. It also teaches the
part Hendry himself learned the hard way: that a brilliant diagnosis with bad
timing will still ruin you.

## Who he was

**Hugh Hendry** was born on **16 March 1969** in Glasgow, Scotland, and raised in
**Castlemilk**, a working-class housing estate. That outsider, never-belonged-to-
the-establishment background is part of his whole persona. He took a degree in
Economics and Finance from the **University of Strathclyde** in 1990 and went
straight into fund management:

- **1991** — joined **Baillie Gifford**, the venerable long-term Edinburgh asset
  manager.
- A brief stint at **Credit Suisse Asset Management**.
- **1999** — joined **Odey Asset Management** in London after meeting founder
  **Crispin Odey**. He ran Odey's top-performing **Continental Europe Fund** (over
  $1bn of long-only mandates) and the **Eclectica Fund**. Odey later put it:
  *"Odey in the 1990s was a one-man band; Odey in the 2000s was a two-man band."*
  Hendry's own tribute is more telling: Odey *"taught me how to manage money...
  More than that, he taught me how to misbehave."*
- **2005** — left Odey and **co-founded Eclectica Asset Management**, buying the
  Eclectica Fund's management contract. He was founding partner and CIO.
- **Late 2017** — **Eclectica closed** after assets shrank and performance lagged
  through the post-2009 central-bank-driven bull market.

Today he runs **"The Acid Capitalist"** — a podcast and YouTube channel of, in
his own spirit, mind-expanding, anti-consensus "gonzo" macro commentary,
broadcast from the Caribbean island of St. Barts. The "acid" branding is the
whole self-image: a thinker who refuses the crowd's reality.

<Callout type="note">
Hendry is as famous for *talking* as for trading. On BBC **Newsnight** in April
2010 he cut off Nobel laureate Joseph Stiglitz over Greece with *"Hello? Can I
tell you about the real world?"* A month later, asked by Jeremy Paxman what
investors should do about the European debt crisis, he answered in one word:
*"panic."* He is also the anonymous **"Plasticine Man"** profiled in Steven
Drobny's book *Invisible Hands.* That theatrical streak is not a side-show — as
you'll see, **the public defense of the thesis is part of his method.**
</Callout>

## The core idea in plain language

Hendry is a **discretionary global macro** investor. Let's unpack that.

- **Macro** means he doesn't analyze individual companies the way a value
  investor reads a balance sheet. Instead he forms a view about *the whole world*
  — interest rates, currencies, government debt, commodities, credit, China,
  central-bank policy — and then expresses that view.
- **Global** means he hunts everywhere: a view about China might be best
  expressed through *Japanese* corporate credit, a view about Europe through
  German bonds.
- **Discretionary** means a human makes the call based on judgment and narrative,
  as opposed to a **systematic** (rules-and-algorithms) quant fund.

His distinctive twist is **contrarian narrative**. He looks for a thesis that
sits *outside the prevailing belief system* — in his own words, a great manager
establishes "a contentious premise outside the existing belief system." The crowd
is already priced in; the money is in what the crowd *refuses* to believe.

But — and beginners always miss this — he insists this is **not** mere permabear
stubbornness. His ideas, he told the *Financial Times* in 2012, must be "harshly
disciplined by market trends." He even calls himself a **"time investor"**: a
contrarian thesis is worthless until *price and momentum start to confirm it.* A
good idea held at the wrong time still loses money.

<Callout type="key">
The Hendry edge has two halves, and beginners only ever copy the first one:
**(1)** a contrarian thesis about a fragile consensus, expressed through **(2)** an
instrument chosen so that *being early is affordable and being right is enormous.*
Wrapped around both is **timing discipline** — wait for the trend to confirm.
Half one without half two, or without the patience, is how you go broke being
correct.
</Callout>

## What "convexity" means — the heart of the method

Before the steps, you need one concept, because everything hinges on it:
**convexity.**

A trade is **convex** when the payoff is lopsided in your favor: a small,
*known, capped* cost on the downside, and a large, *open-ended* gain on the
upside. Buying insurance is the everyday example — you pay a small premium each
year; if your house burns down, the payout dwarfs every premium you ever paid.

The opposite — picking up nickels in front of a steamroller, where you earn a
little most of the time but can lose everything occasionally — is **concave**,
and it's how most blow-ups happen.

Hendry's genius in 2008 was to find a convex instrument that paid off on his
contrarian thesis. He didn't short China directly — China can keep rising for
years. He bought cheap insurance that would only matter if his thesis came true.

## The full method — concrete, beginner-followable steps

### Step 1 — Name the consensus

Write down, in one sentence, what *everyone* currently believes. "China's growth
is unstoppable." "Greece will be bailed out cleanly." "Deflation is impossible."

You cannot be a contrarian until you've named the crowd. If you can't state the
consensus crisply, you don't understand it well enough to bet against it.

### Step 2 — Find the structural imbalance

Look for an economic distortion the consensus is *ignoring*: too much debt, an
overvalued currency, a credit bubble, a market propped up only by central-bank
intervention. Hendry's career was built on spotting these in China, the
Eurozone, and global credit.

### Step 3 — Build the narrative thesis

Turn the imbalance into a clear, *falsifiable* causal chain: *"Because X is
over-leveraged, when Y happens, Z will collapse."* Make it concrete enough to
explain to a skeptic in two minutes — and specific enough that you'd know if it
were wrong. Vague pessimism is not a thesis.

### Step 4 — Choose the cheapest, highest-convexity instrument

This is where Hendry separates from amateurs. Don't just short the obvious thing
(crowded, expensive, painful if early). Ask: *what is mispriced because the market
hasn't connected it to my narrative yet — what pays the most if I'm right while
losing the least if I'm wrong or early?*

His widely-reported answer around 2007–10 was to buy **credit default swaps
(CDS)** on Japanese industrial companies — steel, machinery — whose order books
quietly depended on Chinese over-investment. A CDS is simply an insurance
contract on a bond: you pay an annual premium, and if the borrower defaults you
collect a large payout. That protection was cheap precisely because the market
saw those sleepy Japanese firms as *safe* and never connected them to a Chinese
slowdown. That is the **second-order** instrument — the cheap side door into the
trade.

### Step 5 — Size for survival, not conviction

Because contrarian trades are often *early*, you risk only the small, fixed
premium you can afford to lose entirely — never a position big enough to wipe you
out before you're proven right. Asymmetry is what lets you be wrong many times
cheaply and still win when one bet pays many multiples.

### Step 6 — Diversify the macro book

A crucial reality check on the lone-genius image: Hendry's actual fund ran *many*
simultaneous themes, not one heroic bet. His 2014 fund commentary, for example,
shows long developed-market growth and tech/biotech equities, short emerging-
market currencies, Japanese equity option spreads, VIX positions, and Treasury
trades — all at once. No single idea is the whole portfolio.

### Step 7 — Respect the trend and pre-define your exit

Combine the thesis with a momentum check (the "time investor" discipline). Decide
*in advance* what price action or news would prove you wrong, or simply too early
— and be willing to capitulate. Hendry did exactly this around 2013, declaring he
could "no longer fight central banks" and turning bullish (more on that painful
lesson below). And his fund letters repeatedly show the harvest side: *booking
profits after a rally, closing call spreads.* You don't marry the position.

### Step 8 — Communicate and pressure-test the thesis publicly

Articulate it, debate it, withstand ridicule. For Hendry, forcing yourself to
state the contentious premise clearly — and defend it against a disbelieving
crowd — both *sharpens* the thesis and is the discipline that stops you from
quietly drifting back into consensus. The Newsnight bravado is, in this light, a
feature of the process. The same discipline cuts both ways: you must also be
willing to *abandon* the narrative when the trend never confirms it.

## A worked numeric example

*(This mirrors Hendry's China-via-Japan-CDS logic, with illustrative round
numbers — not actual fund figures.)*

**The thesis:** A borrower the consensus considers safe is in fact structurally
fragile because of a distortion elsewhere in the world.

**The instrument:** Buy 5-year CDS protection — insurance against default — at
**50 basis points per year** on **$1,000,000 of notional** (the face value being
insured).

Now the math, which is the entire point:

| Item | Calculation | Result |
|------|-------------|--------|
| Annual premium | 0.50% × $1,000,000 | **$5,000 / year** |
| Max cost if nothing happens (5 yrs) | $5,000 × 5 | **$25,000** (capped, known) |
| Payoff if it defaults (≈40¢ recovery) | (1 − 0.40) × $1,000,000 | **$600,000** |

**The asymmetry:** You risk **$5,000 a year** to potentially make **$600,000** —
roughly a **120-to-1** payoff on a single year's premium. And crucially, you can
be *early and still survive*: you only have to be right **once** across the years
you hold the protection. (You don't even need a default — in a credit panic the
*price* of that protection can multiply many times over, and you simply sell it.)

This is the essence of the craft: **don't bet your capital directly on a
contrarian call — buy cheap convexity so that being early is affordable and being
right is enormous.** Run it across a whole book of such bets, and one correct call
can dwarf years of small bleeding premiums — which is how a macro fund posts a
number like **+31% in a year the market collapsed.**

<Callout type="warning">
CDS, options, and futures are **leveraged, professional instruments** carrying
counterparty risk (the seller might not pay), liquidity risk, and "roll/decay"
costs — the premium bleeds continuously, so "cheap" protection still costs you
*every day* you're early. The payoff math above is the *idea*; do not trade these
until you genuinely understand the mechanics. A beginner can express the same
convex spirit far more safely with, for example, **long-dated put options** on an
index, where the most you can lose is the premium you paid.
</Callout>

## The harder lesson: right once, then early or wrong

Here is where most write-ups of Hendry stop — and where the real education
begins. The 2008 call was a direct hit. **Almost everything after it was early or
wrong.**

- He stayed **structurally bearish** while the S&P 500 climbed from its ~666
  bottom in March 2009 past 2,100 — one of the great bull markets in history.
- He fought rallies that "Abenomics" and other central-bank programs powered.
- He **predicted a Eurozone break-up** that European Central Bank intervention
  ("whatever it takes") simply prevented.

Around 2013 he **capitulated**, saying in effect that he could no longer fight
central banks, and turned bullish. By mid-2014 his Eclectica Absolute Macro Fund
was **down roughly 9% for the year**; the firm wound down and **closed in 2017.**
That long fade *is* the lesson:

<Callout type="key">
A correct structural diagnosis is **worthless without correct timing.** As the
old trading proverb (often attributed to Keynes) goes, *"the market can stay
irrational longer than you can stay solvent."* Quantitative easing and policy
backstops can keep an imbalance inflated far longer than any fund can survive
betting against it.
</Callout>

## Honest criticisms and limitations

- **The post-2008 record was poor.** His towering fame rests on essentially *one*
  spectacular year. From roughly 2009 onward, fighting central-bank liquidity
  ("don't fight the Fed") repeatedly hurt him; Eclectica closed in 2017. Being a
  brilliant contrarian in a crisis does not guarantee long-run returns in a
  QE-driven bull market.
- **A bearish/contrarian reputation can become a trap.** A name for doom-mongering
  ("panic") attracts attention but can make a manager structurally negative and
  chronically early — and being early is indistinguishable from being wrong if it
  bleeds capital.
- **The label is slippery.** He's called a contrarian but trades momentum; a
  growth skeptic who ran long high-growth tech and biotech in 2014. The discipline
  is real, but beginners shouldn't take "contrarian" too literally.
- **Hard to replicate.** Asymmetric CDS/options trades, prime-broker access, and
  the ability to size a billion-dollar diversified macro book are not available to
  a retail beginner. The *thinking* is transferable; the *toolkit* largely isn't.
- **Persona over process risk.** The flamboyant TV-and-podcast persona — now the
  "Acid Capitalist" brand — makes him endlessly quotable, but it can blur the line
  between entertainment and investable advice.

<Callout type="note">
Why include all this in a lesson on *how to invest like him*? Because the failures
teach the method better than the one triumph does. The 2008 win shows you the
*shape* of a great trade; the post-2009 misses show you the *risk discipline* that
shape demands. Skip the second half and you've learned only how to lose money
confidently.
</Callout>

## Common beginner pitfalls when imitating Hendry

<Callout type="warning">
1. **Confusing "contrarian" with "permabear."** "The crowd is wrong" is not a
   thesis, it's a bias. Hendry insisted ideas be "disciplined by trends." Always
   pair a specific, falsifiable thesis with a momentum check.
2. **Ignoring the "disciplined by trends" half.** Beginners copy the bold call but
   skip the patience. Being right too early is the most common way to go broke —
   wait for the trend to confirm before committing.
3. **Shorting the obvious target directly.** Directly shorting a bubble is
   expensive and dangerous (unlimited loss, margin calls). Hendry's edge was the
   *cheap second-order* instrument — don't skip the asymmetry hunt.
4. **Over-sizing the "conviction" bet.** The whole point of asymmetry is that each
   bet is small enough to be wrong repeatedly. Betting big on one narrative is the
   opposite of the method.
5. **Mistaking a single great year for a strategy.** +31% in 2008 is famous
   precisely because it's *rare*; the same manager later struggled for years.
   Don't extrapolate one crisis win into a guaranteed edge.
6. **Treating a narrative as a prophecy.** A macro story is a hypothesis to be
   priced and tested, not a certainty. Hold it loosely; cut it when the trend
   never confirms.
7. **Trading instruments you don't understand.** Master the payoff math (the
   worked example above) — and the premium decay and counterparty risk — before
   touching CDS, options, or futures.
</Callout>

## Putting it to work

You don't need a CDS desk to think like Hugh Hendry. Run the playbook on paper:

1. **This week, name one consensus** you keep hearing — a story repeated so often
   nobody questions it.
2. **Ask what fragility it ignores.** Where's the hidden leverage, the propped-up
   price, the imbalance?
3. **Find the cheapest convex way to bet against it** — for a beginner, that's
   usually a small, long-dated put option or simply *not owning* the crowded
   asset, never a leveraged short you can't survive.
4. **Write down, in advance, what would prove you wrong** — and the date by which
   you'd expect the trend to confirm you right.
5. **Defend the idea out loud** to a skeptical friend. If you can't, you don't
   understand it yet.
6. **Size it so that being early for two years doesn't hurt you.** Survival first;
   being right is only valuable if you're still in the game when it happens.

The deepest takeaway is a paradox: Hendry's single greatest trade and his long
fade both came from the *same* contrarian instinct. The difference between them
was entirely **convexity and timing discipline.** Get those two right, and the
contrarian's curse — being right too soon — becomes survivable.

## Further reading

- **Steven Drobny, *Invisible Hands: Hedge Funds Off the Record* (2010)** — Hendry
  is the anonymous "Plasticine Man" chapter, the closest thing to a written
  articulation of his philosophy and risk approach. (His style also appears in
  Drobny's earlier *Inside the House of Money*, 2006.)
- **The Eclectica investor letters (c. 2002–2017)** — his monthly letters are the
  canonical record of his reasoning, showing both the convex bets and the
  multi-theme book.
- **"The Acid Capitalist" podcast and YouTube channel** — his current voice on the
  Eurodollar system, the yen carry trade, China, and AI/data-center capex.

Note: Hendry has **never written a conventional investing book.** His "texts" are
fund letters, interviews, the Drobny profiles, and the podcast — fitting for a
trader whose method is half analysis, half argument.
