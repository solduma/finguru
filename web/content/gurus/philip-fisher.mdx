## Why he matters

Most of the great names in value investing learned to count: cheap price,
low ratios, a margin of safety. Philip Fisher learned to **look**. He argued
that the largest fortunes don't come from buying mediocre companies cheaply and
selling them when they tick up — they come from finding a handful of genuinely
**outstanding, well-managed growth companies** and *holding them for years, even
decades*, while their earnings compound.

Warren Buffett famously described himself as **"85% Benjamin Graham and 15%
Philip Fisher"** (a characterization attributed to author John Train). That 15%
is bigger than it sounds: it's the half of Buffett's brain that asks *"is this a
wonderful business run by honest, capable people?"* — and that half came from
Fisher.

<Callout type="key">
Fisher's one big idea: **the quality and durability of a business — and the
honesty and ability of its management — matter far more than the price you pay.**
A truly superior company grows in value so much over the years that your entry
price becomes a rounding error.
</Callout>

## Who he was

**Philip Arthur Fisher** (September 8, 1907 – March 11, 2004) was born in San
Francisco and lived nearly a century — 96 years. He briefly attended the
brand-new **Stanford Graduate School of Business**, left in **1928** to start
working as a securities analyst at a San Francisco bank, and later returned to
*teach* the investment course at Stanford (reportedly one of only three people
ever invited to do so).

In **1931** — at the very bottom of the Great Depression — he founded his own
firm, **Fisher & Co.**, and ran it for nearly seven decades, retiring in **1999
at age 91.** He was famously private: he took very few clients, gave almost no
interviews, and let his record speak quietly.

His son, **Kenneth (Ken) Fisher**, went on to found Fisher Investments and write
a long-running Forbes column — so the family name is still very much alive on
Wall Street.

<Callout type="note">
**The holding that defined him:** Fisher bought **Motorola** in **1955**, when it
was a maker of radios, and held it until his death in **2004** — roughly a
**49-year hold.** That single position is his entire philosophy in one line:
find a great innovator, then get out of its way.
</Callout>

## The core idea, in plain language

Picture the investors of Fisher's era. Most of them, following Benjamin Graham,
hunted for **statistically cheap** stocks — low price-to-earnings (P/E, the price
you pay per dollar of annual profit), low price-to-book (price versus the
company's net assets). Buy a dollar of value for 60 cents, sell at a dollar,
repeat. It's a beautiful, disciplined approach — and Fisher respected it — but it
has a ceiling: a mediocre business is still mediocre.

Fisher flipped the question. Instead of *"how cheap is it?"* he asked *"how good
is it, and how long can it stay good?"* His reasoning is almost arithmetic: if a
company can grow its earnings at, say, 15% a year for twenty years, the business
itself becomes worth many times what it was — and that compounding swamps any
sensible argument about whether you overpaid by 10% or 20% on day one.

So the *hard work* shifts. It's no longer about valuation arithmetic — anyone can
run a screen. It's about **judging things a screen can't see**: the quality of
the products, the depth of the research pipeline, the honesty of the people in
charge. That judgment is the whole game.

We'll put concrete numbers on this claim in the worked examples below — but the
intuition is simple: a business that compounds at 15% a year doubles roughly
every five years, so over a couple of decades it becomes a different animal
entirely, while the "cheap" plodder barely moves.

### Step A — Generate candidates from real-world strength, not screeners

Fisher was emphatically **anti-screening.** You cannot find a great business with
a stock filter, because the things that make it great — innovation, management
character, a product customers can't stop buying — don't show up as a number.
Ideas come from the world: a product everyone in an industry is talking about,
a company that keeps out-inventing its rivals, a firm with years of strong sales
growth still ahead of it. Fisher distrusted **one-hit companies**; he wanted
businesses whose management was already working on the *next* product before
today's lines matured.

### Step B — Do "scuttlebutt" (the heart of the method)

This is Fisher's signature contribution to investing, and his most famous word.
**Scuttlebutt** means building a 360° picture of a company by talking to the
people *around* it — not just reading its own reports (which are, after all,
written by the company about itself).

Fisher's sources to interview:

- **Customers** — do they love the product? Would they switch to a rival
  tomorrow, or are they locked in by genuine preference?
- **Competitors** — Fisher's signature trick was to visit about **five firms in
  an industry** and ask each one intelligent questions about *the strengths and
  weaknesses of the other four.* A remarkably accurate composite picture emerges,
  because rivals are brutally honest about who's actually winning.
- **Suppliers and distributors** — is the company a fair, growing partner, or a
  difficult one living on borrowed time?
- **Former employees** — what is management *really* like behind closed doors?
- **Trade-association people and industry scientists/researchers** — who track
  the whole field and can place this company within it.

The goal is to **triangulate the truth that doesn't appear in filings.** No
single source is gospel; you cross-check until a consistent picture emerges.

Scuttlebutt is not a historical relic. At Berkshire Hathaway's 2018 annual
meeting, Warren Buffett confirmed that his investment lieutenants Todd Combs and
Ted Weschler still use scuttlebutt to research companies. The tool is over 60
years old and still working.
</Callout>

### Step C — Grade the company against the "Fifteen Points"

In his 1958 book *Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits*, Fisher gave investors a
checklist — fifteen questions to ask about any company. Think of it as a report
card. A truly outstanding company scores well on most of them; a few honest
"no"s are a reason to walk away.

Here they are, **paraphrased from the book** (confirm the exact wording against
the text before quoting verbatim):

1. Do the company's products or services have enough **market potential for
   years of sizable sales growth**?
2. Is management **determined to keep developing new products** once today's
   product lines mature?
3. How **effective is the company's research and development (R&D)** for its
   size?
4. Does the company have an **above-average sales and marketing organization**?
5. Does it earn a **worthwhile profit margin**?
6. What is it doing to **maintain or improve** those margins?
7. Does it have **outstanding labor and personnel relations**?
8. Does it have **outstanding executive (management) relations**?
9. Does management have **depth** — more than one or two key people?
10. How good are its **cost analysis and accounting controls**?
11. Are there **industry-specific factors** (patents, real estate, insurance
    reserves, etc.) that reveal how it stacks up against rivals?
12. Does the company take a **short-range or long-range view of profits**?
    (Long-range is better — it won't squeeze customers or suppliers for a quick
    quarter.)
13. Will growth require **so much new stock issuance that share dilution** wipes
    out the benefit to existing shareholders?
14. Does management **talk freely in good times but "clam up" when trouble
    hits**? (Clamming up is a red flag.)
15. Does the company have **management of unquestionable integrity**?

<Callout type="key">
**Point 15 is non-negotiable.** Fisher believed that no amount of growth or
brilliance compensates for dishonest management — sooner or later they will find
a way to take what should be the shareholders'. Fail this one and you **stop,
regardless of how the other fourteen score.** Beginners chase the exciting points
(growth, R&D) and skip the boring-sounding governance ones. Don't.
</Callout>

A practical tip for a beginner: turn each point into a one-line answer, or score
it **1–5**. The soft points — 7, 8, 9, 14, 15 — are exactly the ones you can't
get from a 10-K, which is why **Step B (scuttlebutt) feeds Step C.**

### Step D — Buy at a *reasonable* price, and weigh *time* as well as price

Fisher cared far less about a bargain P/E than about quality — but he did not
ignore price entirely. He weighed price *and* timing. A favorite tactic: buy
around the launch of a **new product or new plant**, when near-term earnings are
temporarily depressed by start-up costs. The market under-prices the quality
because the profits haven't shown up yet — so you get an outstanding business at
a reasonable entry. This is what he meant by considering **time as well as
price**: *when* in a company's development cycle you buy can matter as much as the
multiple.

### Step E — Concentrate; don't over-diversify

Fisher thought broad diversification was, in his words, close to *"a confession
that you don't really know your companies."* If you've done the scuttlebutt and
the business is genuinely outstanding, why dilute it with dozens of also-rans you
understand only superficially? He favored owning **relatively few stocks** —
commonly cited as roughly **ten to twenty** — that you know **deeply**, with your
very best ideas getting the largest weights.

### Step F — Sell almost never

Asked when to sell, Fisher's answer was startling: **"almost never."** He allowed
only **three legitimate reasons** to sell:

1. **You made a mistake.** Your original analysis was wrong — the company isn't
   what you thought it was.
2. **The company deteriorated.** It no longer passes the Fifteen Points as well
   as it once did — the business or its management has decayed.
3. **You found something clearly better** — and even this should be rare and
   done with great care, because you're swapping a *known* excellent business for
   an *estimate*, and you'll pay taxes on the gain to do it.

Notice what's **not** on the list: "the stock went up a lot," "I'm scared of a
market crash," or "it looks expensive now." Those are exactly the reasons Fisher
told investors to ignore.

### Step G — Avoid the "Ten Don'ts"

Fisher closed the book with two chapters of investor mistakes: **"Five Don'ts for
Investors"** and **"Five More Don'ts for Investors."** Together they're his Ten
Don'ts, paraphrased here:

**The first five — mistakes of judgment:**

1. **Don't** buy into promotional **start-ups with no track record.**
2. **Don't** ignore a good stock just because it trades **over-the-counter**
   (off the main exchange).
3. **Don't** buy a stock just because you **like the "tone" of its annual
   report.**
4. **Don't** assume a **high P/E automatically means future growth is already
   priced in** — sometimes a great grower is still cheap relative to what's coming.
5. **Don't quibble over eighths and quarters** — tiny price differences — and
   lose a great company over a trivial entry difference.

**The next five — mistakes of temperament:**

6. **Don't** overstress diversification (it forces you into businesses you don't
   understand).
7. **Don't** be afraid to buy on a **war scare** (or any macro panic — a scary
   headline rarely permanently impairs a great business).
8. **Don't forget your "Gilbert and Sullivan"** — Fisher's quirky shorthand for
   *don't be swayed by things that don't matter*: a stock's past price range,
   round numbers, and other irrelevant trivia.
9. **Don't** fail to consider **time as well as price** when buying a true growth
   stock.
10. **Don't follow the crowd.**

## A worked example — why quality beats cheapness

This is the arithmetic behind Fisher's whole philosophy. *(Illustrative numbers,
not a real company.)*

You're choosing between two stocks and plan to hold for **20 years**:

| | Company A — the Fisher grower | Company B — cheap & mediocre |
|---|---|---|
| Earnings growth | **15% per year** | **4% per year** |
| Starting EPS | $1.00 | $1.00 |
| P/E you pay | **30** ("expensive") | **10** ("cheap") |
| Purchase price | $30 | $10 |

Now run the clock forward 20 years. Earnings per share grow by
`EPS = $1 × (1 + g)^20`:

- **Company A:** EPS = `1 × 1.15^20` = **$16.37.** And let's be *harsh* — assume
  its growth has matured, so the market re-rates it down from a P/E of 30 to a
  normal **18.** Price = `16.37 × 18` = **$294.60.** From your $30 cost, that's
  about **9.8x**, or roughly **12.1% per year.**
- **Company B:** EPS = `1 × 1.04^20` = **$2.19.** Keep its P/E at 10. Price =
  **$21.90.** From your $10 cost, that's about **2.2x**, or roughly **4.0% per
  year.**

Watch the two share prices pull apart year by year. At the start, Company A
looks like the loser: you pay $30 for it versus just $10 for Company B. For the
first several years the gap barely widens — which is exactly why impatient
investors give up on great businesses too early. But compounding is not linear;
it bends upward. By the end of the horizon the "expensive" grower is worth more
than **thirteen times** the "cheap" stock, and the curves are racing apart
faster every year.

## A second example — "the big money is in the waiting"

Fisher liked to say *"the big money is not in the buying or selling, but in the
waiting."* Here's why he set the bar to sell so high. *(Illustrative numbers,
to teach the mechanics — not a forecast.)*

Suppose you own a quality business compounding at **12% a year** and you simply
**hold**. Now suppose a second investor owns the *same* business but keeps
**trading in and out** — every round trip realizes a long-term capital-gains tax
of ~15% on the gain, and they sit in cash a few months a year waiting for a
better re-entry, missing growth. Those frictions quietly drag their *effective*
compounding from 12% down to about **9% a year.** A modest three-point drag — and
look what three decades do to it:

This is the whole reason Fisher's sell list has only three items: every time you
sell a winner, you pay a tax and risk missing the compounding that follows.

## The books

Fisher wrote sparingly, but every book counts:

- ***Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits*** (1958) — his landmark. It contains the
  Fifteen Points, scuttlebutt, and the Ten Don'ts. Buffett called it a *"very, very
  good book"* and ranks it among the most important investing books ever written.
- ***Paths to Wealth Through Common Stocks*** (1960).
- ***Conservative Investors Sleep Well*** (1975) — introduced his "four dimensions"
  of a conservative investment.
- ***Developing an Investment Philosophy*** (1980) — a short, reflective monograph
  for the Financial Analysts Research Foundation.

Today the last three are usually reprinted together with the 1958 book under the
title ***Common Stocks and Uncommon Profits and Other Writings*** — the single
volume to buy if you read only one. (Fisher was a practitioner, not an academic:
he produced influential books, not peer-reviewed papers.)

## Honest criticisms and limitations

Fisher's method is powerful but far from foolproof. Be clear-eyed about its weak
spots:

- **It's highly subjective and hard to scale.** Judging "management quality" and
  weighing scuttlebutt is personal and fallible. There's no formula and no clean
  back-test the way there is for Graham-style value screens; two careful analysts
  can reach opposite conclusions about the same company.
- **Information access is unequal.** Real scuttlebutt means reaching executives,
  competitors, and ex-employees — far easier for a well-networked professional
  than for a retail beginner. Online proxies (reviews, Glassdoor) are noisier and
  easier to game. And digging too aggressively today can collide with
  **insider-trading rules and Regulation FD** (which restrict the sharing of
  material non-public information) — legal lines that simply didn't exist in 1958.
- **"Hold forever" + concentration can curdle into complacency.** Holding for
  decades works only if the company *stays* outstanding. Plenty of celebrated
  growth companies eventually decline — and a no-sell discipline turns one bad
  judgment in a concentrated book into a very expensive mistake.
- **De-emphasizing price has real limits.** A wonderful company bought at an
  *absurd* multiple can still produce poor returns. This is exactly why Buffett
  blends Fisher's quality lens with Graham's price discipline rather than going
  pure-Fisher.
- **Survivorship bias.** Motorola (1955–2004) is celebrated; the failures get less
  airtime. Picking the *next* multi-decade compounder ahead of time is
  extraordinarily hard.

<Callout type="warning">
The biggest misreadings of Fisher are both born from taking him too literally.
**"Price doesn't matter"** was never license to overpay for *any* hyped story
stock — it applied only to *genuinely outstanding* businesses (Don'ts #4, #5, and
#9 are about *trivial* price differences, not *any* price). And **"hold forever"**
was never "never sell" — Fisher gave three explicit reasons to sell. Strip out the
qualifiers and the philosophy becomes dangerous.
</Callout>

## Common beginner pitfalls

- **Treating the Fifteen Points as a checkbox quiz** instead of doing the deep
  investigation behind each — especially the soft ones (management quality,
  integrity, openness in bad times).
- **Treating "hold forever" as "never sell, ever."** When the thesis breaks
  (mistake or deterioration), Fisher *sells.* Don't hold a rotting business out of
  loyalty or stubbornness.
- **Using "price doesn't matter" to justify overpaying** for any popular growth
  stock. Reserve that attitude for businesses that have genuinely passed the
  Fifteen Points.
- **Fake scuttlebutt.** Reading a few Reddit threads and the company's own
  marketing is *not* research. Real scuttlebutt seeks **independent, even
  adversarial** sources — competitors and ex-employees, not cheerleaders.
- **Confusing a great product with a great stock.** A beloved product run by weak
  or dishonest management fails Points 7–9 and 15. The product is necessary, not
  sufficient.
- **Over-diversifying out of fear** — which defeats the concentration logic — or
  the opposite error, concentrating into businesses you don't actually understand.
- **Selling winners early to "lock in gains"** — the exact behavior Fisher argued
  destroys the most wealth.

## Putting it to work

You don't need a Rolodex of CEOs to start thinking like Fisher. Try this on one
company you already follow:

1. **Pick a business you can understand** — ideally one whose product you or
   people you know actually use.
2. **Run your own mini-scuttlebutt.** Read what *customers* say (not the
   company's ads). Read what *competitors* claim. Search for *ex-employee*
   reviews. Look for what's true across all of them.
3. **Grade it against the Fifteen Points.** Write a one-line honest answer to
   each. Be especially ruthless on **Point 15 (integrity)** — fail it and stop.
4. **Decide on quality first, price second.** Only after you believe the business
   is outstanding do you ask whether the price is *reasonable* — not whether it's
   the lowest possible — and consider *when* in its cycle you're buying.
5. **Write down your thesis** — *why* it passes — so that later you can tell the
   difference between "the stock fell" (ignore) and "the thesis broke" (sell).
6. **Then mostly do nothing.** Let a great business compound. Fisher's edge was
   not activity; it was *patience built on conviction.*

Fisher and Graham are the two poles of fundamental investing — quality versus
price — and the most durable investors, Buffett above all, learned to hold both
ideas at once. Graham keeps you from overpaying; Fisher keeps you from selling
the future too soon.
