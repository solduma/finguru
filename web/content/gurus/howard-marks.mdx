## Why Howard Marks is different

Most of the investors in this guide are stock-pickers — they teach you how to
find a wonderful business and value it. Howard Marks teaches something stranger
and, for a beginner, more useful as a first lesson: **how to think about other
investors.**

His edge was never "I found a great company." It was, "I understand that
everyone else is terrified right now, prices have fallen far below what these
assets are actually worth, and the people selling are *forced* to sell — so I'll
buy." His arena is **credit** — high-yield and distressed debt, not common
stocks — but the *mindset* he built applies to any market you'll ever touch.

That bond-manager DNA matters. A bondholder's best case is simply "I get paid
back"; the worst case is a total loss. There's no glamorous upside to dream
about, only a downside to survive. Living in that world for fifty years is why
Marks is, above all, **obsessed with risk** — not with home runs.

<Callout type="key">
Marks's whole philosophy reduces to one move repeated forever: **figure out
where the crowd is wrong and how much that mistake has distorted price, then act
against the crowd — but only when the math protects you if you're wrong.** His
own four-word motto: *"You can't predict. You can prepare."*
</Callout>

## Who he was

**Howard Marks** (born 1946 in New York City, raised in Queens) is the
co-founder of **Oaktree Capital Management**, one of the largest distressed-debt
investors in the world.

- **Education:** B.S. in Economics (finance) from the **Wharton School**,
  University of Pennsylvania, then an MBA in accounting and marketing from the
  **University of Chicago Booth** (1969, at age 23). He earned the **CFA
  charter** in 1975.
- **Career arc:**
  - **Citicorp (1969–1985):** equity research analyst → Director of Research →
    senior portfolio manager. Crucially, he was handed the convertibles and
    **high-yield ("junk") bond** portfolios — the unglamorous corners of the
    market that became his life's work. He moved to Los Angeles in 1980 to
    manage a high-yield fund.
  - **TCW Group (1985–1995):** led high-yield and convertible investing; in 1988
    he and partner **Bruce Karsh** organized one of the first institutional
    **distressed-debt** funds.
  - **Oaktree Capital Management (co-founded April 1995, Los Angeles):** founded
    with four TCW colleagues. Oaktree went public on the NYSE in **April 2012**
    (ticker OAK); in **March 2019**, **Brookfield Asset Management** acquired
    about **62%**, with Marks and team keeping roughly 38% and operational
    control.

<Callout type="note">
A common mix-up: the "b. 1946" tag is **Marks's birth year**, not Oaktree's
founding. Oaktree was founded in **1995** — when Marks was already nearly 50.
His most famous work came in the *second half* of his career.
</Callout>

**Track record.** Oaktree's distressed-debt funds reportedly compounded around
**19% a year after fees** over the long run, largely **without leverage** — an
exceptional record. During the 2008 financial crisis Oaktree raised a then-record
**$10.9 billion** distressed fund and deployed it as markets collapsed. By the
mid-2020s Oaktree managed well over **$200 billion**, and Marks's net worth was
estimated around **$2.2 billion** (2022).

His public reputation, though, rests less on the returns than on his writing.
Since 1990 he has published free essays called **"memos."**
[Warren Buffett's](/gurus/warren-buffett) endorsement is the one everyone
quotes: *"When I see memos from Howard Marks in my mail, they're the first thing
I open and read."*

## The core idea in plain language

Markets are driven by **people**, and people swing between greed and fear far
more than the underlying facts justify. You can't reliably predict the future —
but you *can* read where the crowd's emotions sit right now, and lean against the
extremes. Everything Marks believes stands on three pillars. Learn these and you
have the skeleton of his entire philosophy.

### Pillar 1 — Second-level thinking

To beat the average investor you must be **more right than the consensus AND
think differently from it.** Marks splits thinking into two levels:

- **First-level thinking** is the obvious reaction: *"It's a good company —
  let's buy the stock."*
- **Second-level thinking** is deeper and asks what's *already priced in:* *"It's
  a good company — but everyone already knows that, so it's overrated and the
  price reflects it. Let's sell."* And in reverse: *"It's a troubled company, but
  the gloom is overdone and the price is far below value — let's buy."*

The key insight: **if your conclusion is identical to everyone else's, you have
no edge** — that view is already baked into the price. You only earn
above-average returns when you hold a *correct, non-consensus* view.

<Callout type="key">
Marks's test for second-level thinking is **three dares:** *Do you dare to be
different? Do you dare to be wrong? Do you dare to look wrong?* Being different is
*necessary* for outperformance but not sufficient — you also have to be **right**
— and a contrarian who is ultimately right can **look** painfully wrong for a
long time first. Different-and-wrong just loses money faster.
</Callout>

### Pillar 2 — Risk is the probability of permanent loss

Academic finance defines risk as **volatility** (how much the price bounces
around). Marks rejects this. To him, risk is **the chance you permanently lose
your capital** — that the money never comes back. A price that wobbles and then
recovers cost you nothing; a price that goes to zero is the only thing that
matters.

And here is his most counterintuitive teaching: **risk is highest exactly when
it feels lowest.** When prices are high, everyone is confident, lending is easy,
and people believe "there's no price too high," that is precisely when you're
most likely to be overpaying — and overpaying is the real source of risk. *"When
something is on the pedestal of popularity, the risk of a decline is high."*
His foundational lesson, learned from the early-1970s "Nifty Fifty" crash:
**there is no asset so good that it can't become overpriced and dangerous** — and
almost none so bad that it isn't a bargain at a low enough price.

### Pillar 3 — Cycles and the pendulum

Markets do not sit politely at "fair value." Economies, profits, credit, and
psychology all move in **cycles**, never in a straight line. Investor mood swings
like a pendulum between **euphoria and despair** — and, as Marks puts it, it
*"spends very little time at the happy medium."* Most of the time the market is
on the way *to* an extreme or on the way back.

You cannot predict exactly when the pendulum will turn. But you *can* know **where
on the arc you currently are** — and that's enough: *"We may never know where
we're going, but we'd better have a good idea where we are."* Marks maps each
swing in **three stages**:

| Stage | Bull market (greed building) | Bear market (fear building) |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | A *few* far-sighted people see improvement coming | A few see that things have gotten too good |
| **2** | *Most* accept that improvement is actually happening | Most accept that deterioration is happening |
| **3** | *Everyone* concludes it can **only** get better forever | Everyone concludes it can **only** get worse |

Stage 3 of a bull is the **danger zone** (maximum greed, maximum risk); stage 3
of a bear is the **opportunity zone** (maximum fear, maximum bargains).

<Callout type="note">
Marks's pendulum is the *psychology* of the cycle. Underneath it sits the
**credit cycle** — the mechanical ebb and flow of how freely money is lent. When
times are good, lenders compete, credit gets cheap and careless, and that very
ease plants the seeds of the next bust. Two other lessons in this guide map the
machinery the pendulum rides on: [Ray Dalio's](/gurus/ray-dalio) **long-term
debt cycle** (how decades of borrowing build up and unwind at the macro level)
and [Hyman Minsky's](/gurus/hyman-minsky) **financial-instability hypothesis**
("stability breeds instability"). Marks reads the *mood*; Dalio and Minsky
explain the *mechanism* that produces it.
</Callout>

## The full method — step by step

Here is Marks's approach broken into concrete steps a beginner can actually
follow. Notice that none of them is "predict the future."

### Step 1 — Force yourself into second-level thinking

For any investment, write down four things on paper:

1. **The consensus view** — what does everyone believe about this asset?
2. **The price that view has produced** — what is it trading at right now?
3. **Where you think the consensus is wrong** — and *why* you'd know something
   they don't.
4. **The payoff if you're right** — how much do you make, and what happens if
   you're wrong?

If your answer to #3 is "I agree with everyone," stop. You have no edge.

### Step 2 — Separate price from value

A wonderful asset at too high a price is a *bad* investment. A mediocre asset at
a deep enough discount can be a *good* one. *"It's not what you buy, it's what you
pay that counts."* Marks insists that **buying below intrinsic value — not buying
quality — is the most dependable way to make money.**

So always estimate **what the thing is actually worth** first (for a bond, its
likely *recovery value* in a worst case; for a business, its intrinsic value),
*then* compare that to the price. Never the other way around.

### Step 3 — Define risk correctly and demand a margin of safety

Ask the blunt question: **"What can permanently destroy my capital here, and how
likely is it?"** Then buy at a discount big enough to protect you *even if your
analysis is somewhat wrong.* That cushion is the **margin of safety** (a concept
Marks inherited from [Benjamin Graham](/gurus/benjamin-graham) — see the
prerequisite lesson).

### Step 4 — Take the market's temperature

You can't measure the pendulum precisely, but you can read the mood. Marks
offers a checklist of opposites — figure out which column the market is in today:

| When the market is **hot (defend)** | When the market is **cold (attack)** |
|---|---|
| Optimistic, euphoric | Depressed, fearful |
| Eager to buy | Eager to sell |
| Prices high, valuations stretched | Prices low, valuations cheap |
| Credit easy, lenders careless | Credit frozen, no one will lend |
| Yield spreads tight | Yield spreads wide |
| IPOs / SPACs / new structures booming | New issuance dead |
| *"There's no price too high"* | *"Nothing is worth buying"* |

<Callout type="warning">
Marks's single strongest bubble warning is hearing the phrase **"this time is
different"** or **"there's no price too high"** in the air. When valuation no
longer matters to people, you are almost certainly in stage 3 of a bull — the
danger zone.
</Callout>

<Callout type="note">
A **yield spread** is the *extra* interest a risky bond pays above a safe
government bond. **Tight** spreads mean investors are demanding almost no extra
reward for risk (complacency — dangerous). **Wide** spreads mean they're
demanding a lot (fear — opportunity). Spreads are one of Marks's favorite
thermometers. The "easy credit, careless lenders, tight spreads" column is
exactly the late-expansion phase that [Hyman Minsky](/gurus/hyman-minsky) warned
about — the boom that manufactures its own bust — and the top of
[Ray Dalio's](/gurus/ray-dalio) short-term credit cycle.
</Callout>

### Step 5 — Calibrate your aggressiveness to the cycle

This is the actionable payoff of his book *Mastering the Market Cycle.* You
**don't time the turns** — you adjust your *posture*:

- Pendulum at **euphoria** (cheap credit, tight spreads, careless lending) →
  **dial down risk, raise defenses, build cash.**
- Pendulum at **panic** (forced selling, wide spreads, blood in the streets) →
  **dial up aggression, deploy your cash.**

### Step 6 — Be contrarian, and be patient ("patient opportunism")

*"Buy when others are despondently selling and sell when others are euphorically
buying."* But contrarianism alone isn't enough — you must combine it with a real
**price-vs-value gap** (Step 2). Most of the time, the right action is to **wait**
— sometimes for years — until the pendulum hands you genuinely mispriced assets.
To do that you must hold **"dry powder"** (cash ready to deploy) so you can act
when others are forced to sell and bargains finally appear.

### Step 7 — Prioritize not losing

Marks aims for a high "batting average" and the **avoidance of losers** rather
than spectacular home runs: *"If we avoid the losers, the winners will take care
of themselves."* He happily accepts roughly *average* returns in booming markets
in exchange for **much smaller losses** in busts. Over a full cycle, losing less
is what compounds (the worked example below shows the brutal math behind this).

### Step 8 — Read the credit / rate regime you're living in

Credit is the **accelerant** of every cycle. Marks's *Sea Change* thesis (2022)
argues that roughly **40 years of falling interest rates** — from about **20% in
the early 1980s to near zero by 2021** — acted like a giant "moving walkway,"
lifting nearly all asset prices and rewarding aggressive, leveraged, borrow-cheap
strategies. The post-2022 reversal to *higher* rates means **the strategies that
won in the easy-money era may not be the winners ahead**, and lending (credit)
can again offer equity-like returns without equity-like risk. The lesson for a
beginner: know which regime you're standing in before assuming the last decade's
winners keep winning.

## Worked example 1 — why losing less wins (the asymmetry of risk)

This is the math behind "prioritize not losing." Two investors each start with
**$100** over a two-year cycle — one booming year, one bust.

- **Investor A (aggressive, first-level):** +40% in the boom, then −40% in the bust.
  - $100 × 1.40 = $140; then $140 × 0.60 = **$84.** Net over two years: **−16%.**
- **Investor B (defensive, Marks-style):** a modest +15% in the boom, but only
  −5% in the bust because he was positioned cautiously.
  - $100 × 1.15 = $115; then $115 × 0.95 = **$109.25.** Net: **+9.25%.**

Investor B *earned less in the good year* and still finished far ahead. Why? A
−40% loss needs a **+67%** gain just to break even; a −5% loss needs only
**+5.3%.** Losses hurt more than equal-sized gains help — so **avoiding the big
drawdown is what compounds.**

## Worked example 2 — distressed debt, Marks's home turf

Now a trade of the kind Oaktree actually does. *(Illustrative numbers, but they
mirror real distressed cases.)*

A company is in trouble. Its **senior bonds** have a **$1,000 face value** (what
the company promised to repay) but are trading at just **$400** — "40 cents on
the dollar." Why so cheap? Because many current holders are **forced sellers**:
funds whose rules forbid holding defaulted or below-investment-grade debt. They
*must* dump the bonds at any price — non-economic sellers, exactly the
inefficiency Marks hunts.

**Your second-level analysis:** you study the bankruptcy and estimate that in a
restructuring, senior creditors will **recover about $700 per bond.** That $700
is your **recovery value** — the credit-world version of intrinsic value.

- **Margin of safety:** you pay $400 for something you reasonably think is worth
  ~$700 — a discount of about **43%.**
- **If you're right:** $700 / $400 − 1 = **+75%.**

But Marks would never stop at the rosy case. He frames risk as *permanent loss*,
so you map out the real probabilities:

| Outcome | Recovery | Probability |
|---|---|---|
| Restructuring goes well | $700 | 60% |
| Partial recovery | $500 | 25% |
| Total wipeout | $0 | 15% |

**Expected value** = (0.60 × $700) + (0.25 × $500) + (0.15 × $0)
= $420 + $125 + $0 = **$545.**

So you're paying **$400 for an expected $545** — an expected return of about
**+36%**, *and* the deep discount cushions you in the bad scenarios. The edge
came from two things working together: a **well-researched, non-consensus
recovery estimate** (second-level thinking) and a **price set by forced sellers**
rather than by reasoned analysis.

<Callout type="note">
**The same logic, in stocks.** Take a company expected to grow earnings. The
first-level investor says *"growth is good — buy at 30× earnings."* The
second-level investor asks what's *priced in:* at 30×, the market already assumes
years of flawless growth, so even good results can disappoint and the downside is
large. The *same* company at 10× — after a fear-driven sell-off — prices in
*failure*, so merely-okay results produce gains and the downside is cushioned.
Same company, opposite decisions, driven entirely by **price vs. expectations.**
That is "where are we in the cycle" applied to a single name.
</Callout>

<Callout type="note">
This mirrors real distressed history: Argentina restructured its defaulted debt
at roughly 30 cents on the dollar, and Elliott Management famously bought
defaulted Peruvian loans at around 55% of face value. The pattern is always the
same — a price driven by forced, non-economic selling, met by a buyer with a
patient, well-researched view of recovery.
</Callout>

## Key memos and books

Marks's memos are the raw material of everything; the books distill them. The
first decade of memos drew almost no reaction — until the **"bubble.com"** memo
flagged the dot-com mania and people finally started reading.

- **"The Route to Performance"** (Oct 1990) — the earliest memo in the public archive.
- **"bubble.com"** (Jan 2, 2000) — called the dot-com mania near its peak.
- **"The Most Important Thing"** (Jul 1, 2003) — the seed of the later book.
- **"The Race to the Bottom"** (Feb 2007) — warned on reckless, undisciplined credit before the 2008 crisis.
- **"Nobody Knows"** (Sep 19, 2008) — written mid-crisis, on how to act under deep uncertainty.
- ***The Most Important Thing: Uncommon Sense for the Thoughtful Investor*** (2011)
  — his core philosophy, distilled from the memos. The single best starting point.
- ***The Most Important Thing Illuminated*** (2012) — an annotated edition with
  margin commentary from other great investors including
  [Seth Klarman](/gurus/seth-klarman) and [Joel Greenblatt](/gurus/joel-greenblatt).
- ***Mastering the Market Cycle: Getting the Odds on Your Side*** (2018) — how to
  read where you are in the cycle and position accordingly.
- **"Sea Change"** (Dec 13, 2022) — the rate-regime / credit-cycle shift thesis.
- **"On Bubble Watch"** (Jan 7, 2025) — bubble psychology and a reading of the current cycle.

## Honest criticisms and limitations

<Callout type="warning">
Marks's wisdom is genuinely hard to *operationalize.* Be honest with yourself
about these limits before you try to "invest like Howard Marks."
</Callout>

- **It resists being a mechanical rule.** "Take the market's temperature" is
  qualitative and judgment-heavy — two skilled people can read the same signals
  differently. There's no formula and no backtest. Marks himself dodges hard
  calls; in *On Bubble Watch* he openly writes, *"I can't speak authoritatively
  about whether we're in a bubble."*
- **It's hard to falsify.** "Be cautious when others are greedy" explains every
  crisis beautifully *in hindsight* but gives little precision in real time.
- **Markets can stay irrational for years.** A correct contrarian "may look wrong
  in the short run" — sometimes a very long run. Acting early can mean large
  drawdowns or years of missed gains before vindication.
- **His record came from tools you don't have.** Oaktree's ~19% record came from
  *institutional* distressed-debt funds with deal sourcing, legal teams, and the
  power to influence restructurings in bankruptcy court. A retail beginner with a
  brokerage account cannot replicate that directly — and the survivorship and
  authority bias from Buffett's praise makes the philosophy *feel* more certain
  than it is.
- **It's domain-specific.** His framework is about **credit and cycles**, not
  stock-picking. Treating it as a general method for selecting individual stocks
  misreads him.
- **Defensiveness has a real cost.** Holding cash and underperforming through a
  long bull (the *Sea Change* "moving walkway" decades) can mean years of lagging
  a simple index — a genuine opportunity cost for most savers.

## Common beginner pitfalls

<Callout type="warning">
1. **Confusing risk with volatility.** Beginners flee assets that have *dropped*
   (now possibly cheaper and safer) and chase assets that have *risen* (now
   riskier). Remember: risk is highest when it feels lowest.
2. **First-level thinking in disguise.** Buying "great companies" or "hot
   sectors" because everyone agrees — the agreement is already in the price.
3. **Mistaking contrarianism for "do the opposite of everyone."** The crowd is
   usually roughly right; contrarianism only pays at *extremes* and only when
   paired with a real value/recovery gap and a margin of safety.
4. **Catching falling knives.** "It's cheap" is not a thesis — permanent
   impairment is real. Without a value estimate and patience, "buying the dip"
   becomes buying a value trap.
5. **Trying to time the exact top or bottom.** Marks says he can't, and neither
   can you. The goal is to *adjust your posture*, not call the turn.
6. **No dry powder.** Being fully invested at euphoria leaves you nothing to
   deploy when the bargains finally appear.
7. **Performance-chasing / FOMO.** Piling into whatever just went up ("there's no
   price too high") is exactly the stage-3 bull behavior Marks calls the biggest
   red flag.
8. **Treating his books as stock tips.** They teach a *temperament and process*,
   not specific buys. Expecting picks misses the entire point.
</Callout>

## Putting it to work

You won't run a distressed-debt fund tomorrow. But you can absorb Marks's
mindset starting today, even with a small account:

1. **Before every buy, write down the consensus and where you disagree.** If you
   can't articulate a non-consensus reason, you're just paying the crowd's price.
2. **Estimate value first, then look at price.** Refuse to buy quality at any
   price.
3. **Keep a "temperature" journal.** Are spreads tight or wide? Are IPOs
   booming? Is the news euphoric or terrified? You don't need to predict — just
   know where you are. To deepen this, read the cycle machinery beneath the mood:
   [Ray Dalio](/gurus/ray-dalio) on the debt cycle and
   [Hyman Minsky](/gurus/hyman-minsky) on why "easy credit" is itself the warning
   sign.
4. **Hold some dry powder.** Being 100% invested at a euphoric top is how
   beginners get hurt. Cash is optionality — the price of being ready.
5. **Pair Marks with a value lesson.** He teaches you *when to be aggressive and
   how to think about risk* — but for the mechanics of *estimating intrinsic
   value*, lean on the value-investing canon
   ([Graham](/gurus/benjamin-graham), [Buffett](/gurus/warren-buffett)). Marks
   tells you *how confident to be*; they tell you *what a thing is worth.*

<Callout type="key">
The single sentence to carry away: **price is what the crowd's emotions have made
of value — your job is to estimate the value coldly, read the crowd's
temperature, and act against it when (and only when) the discount protects you.**
You can't predict. You *can* prepare.
</Callout>
