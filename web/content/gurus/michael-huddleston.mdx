## Why this lesson is different

Every other guru in this guide left a documented, decades-old footprint: published
books, audited records, or a verifiable institutional career. **Michael
Huddleston is different, and you should know that up front.** He is a modern
*internet* educator whose ideas reach millions through YouTube — and whose
trading track record is, as of this writing, **unverified, with documented public
failures.** We include him because "Smart Money Concepts" (SMC) is probably the
most-discussed framework among new traders today, and a serious student needs to
understand it *and* see through it. This lesson teaches the concepts clearly —
concretely enough to chart yourself — and weighs the skepticism honestly.

<Callout type="warning">
Throughout, claims are attributed ("ICT teaches…") rather than asserted as fact.
There is no audited, third-party-verified performance record for Huddleston, and
much of his biography is self-reported. Read this as "here is what the framework
says and what's actually known," not an endorsement. **Do not risk real money on
this believing it's a proven institutional edge — it isn't.**
</Callout>

## Who he is

**Michael J. Huddleston** created the brand **"The Inner Circle Trader" (ICT)**
and originated the vocabulary now spread under the **Smart Money Concepts (SMC)**
umbrella — order blocks, fair value gaps, liquidity pools, the "Power of Three,"
and more. He self-reports a Michigan upbringing, an uncle who introduced him to
futures and options, study of well-known traders **Larry Williams** and **Richard
Dennis**, and "30+ years" in markets.

**What's actually verifiable** is narrow:

- He runs one of the largest retail-trading channels on YouTube
  (`@innercircletrader`), with roughly **1 million-plus subscribers** and millions
  of views a month. This is the most solid fact about him.
- He is credited as the **originator of the ICT terminology** that other
  educators later popularized — his **2022 ICT Mentorship** YouTube series is
  widely cited as the curriculum that defined the modern SMC vocabulary.
- He **moved from paid mentorships to free content**, which genuinely weakens a
  simple "he just sells courses" narrative.

**What is self-reported or unverified** is almost everything else: his entire
biography and résumé, any claim of an *institutional* background, any net-worth
figure, and — most importantly — **any statement about how profitably he trades.**

Bottom line on the man: verifiable = a ~1M-subscriber channel, the originator of

## The lineage: where these ideas really come from

Before the concepts, the honest framing: **SMC is largely a rebranding of three
older bodies of work you've already met in this guide.** ICT's real contribution
is unified terminology and very granular intraday execution rules — not new
market mechanics.

- **Wyckoff → "smart money."** Richard Wyckoff taught reading the market as if a
  single **Composite Operator** accumulated quietly, engineered false moves to
  trap the public, then drove price. That *is* ICT's "smart money." Wyckoff's
  **Spring** (a false break below support to grab stops) ≈ ICT's **manipulation /
  liquidity grab**; the last candle before markup (the trending-up advance) ≈ the
  **order block**; his **Sign of Strength** ≈ a **break of structure**.
- **Dow Theory → market structure.** Dow defined trends by swing sequence
  (higher highs/lows vs. lower highs/lows). ICT's "break of structure" is a Dow
  continuation; "change of character" is Dow's first failure to extend.
- **Supply/demand trading → order blocks.** A classic supply/demand *zone* is an
  area price left sharply; an ICT order block is the *last opposing candle* before
  that move — a narrower, candle-specific refinement of the same premise.

<Callout type="note">
Be fair to both sides: a minority argue order blocks aren't *identical* to
supply/demand zones (they're narrower and "gated" by a following structure
break), so it's a refinement, not a pure copy. But the core premise — institutions
leave footprints you can trade with — is straight Wyckoff.
</Callout>

## The core idea in one breath

ICT's premise: **retail price movement is "engineered" by large institutions
("smart money") who hunt clusters of stop-loss orders ("liquidity") to fill their
big positions, then drive price toward the next pool of liquidity.** Indicators
(RSI, MACD, moving averages) are dismissed as lagging retail noise. Instead you
read raw price action, market structure, and time-of-day to anticipate where
institutions push price next. In one line: *price travels from one liquidity pool
to the next, and you try to ride alongside the "smart money" instead of getting
hunted with the crowd.*

## The core ICT concepts

All session times use New York (ET), ICT's reference timezone.

### Market structure: BOS and CHoCH

- **Break of Structure (BOS):** price breaks a prior swing point *in the trend's
  direction* → the trend **continues**.
- **Change of Character (CHoCH):** price breaks a swing point *against* the trend
  (e.g. breaks the last higher low downward) → first hint of a **reversal**.

Simple rule: **BOS = continue; CHoCH = possible flip.** Confusing the two is one
of the most common beginner errors — a "reversal" entry that's really a
continuation.

### Liquidity — the engine of the whole model

**Liquidity** is clusters of resting orders — mostly stop-losses — that big
players want to fill against.

- **Buy-side liquidity (BSL):** rests *above* price (buy-stops and shorts'
  stop-losses), pooled above swing highs. Price is drawn *up* to grab it.
- **Sell-side liquidity (SSL):** rests *below* price (sell-stops and longs'
  stop-losses), pooled below swing lows. Price is drawn *down* to grab it.
- **Equal highs/lows:** two swings at about the same level — an obvious spot where
  stops stack (the most reliable kind of pool).
- **Liquidity grab / sweep / stop hunt:** price spikes (up into buy-side, or down
  into sell-side) into a pool, triggers those orders, then quickly reverses — the
  signature ICT event. Rule of thumb: *price tends to move toward the nearest
  obvious pool.*

### Order blocks and fair value gaps

- **Order block (OB):** the *last opposing candle before a strong move*. A
  **bullish OB** is the last down-candle before a rally (a demand zone); a
  **bearish OB** the last up-candle before a drop. Treated as *valid* only if the
  move out of it broke structure (caused a BOS or CHoCH).
- **Fair value gap (FVG) / imbalance:** a **three-candle** pattern where a fast
  middle candle leaves a gap between candle 1's and candle 3's wicks — a "skipped"
  price range. ICT teaches that price tends to come back to **fill** ("rebalance")
  it, making FVGs retracement entry zones. The **midpoint of the gap — called
  Consequent Encroachment** — is treated as the most reactive level; the edge of
  the gap is the earliest entry.

### Premium vs. discount

Take a swing high and low (the **dealing range**); the 50% midpoint is
**equilibrium**. The upper half is **premium** ("expensive" — prefer to sell);
the lower half is **discount** ("cheap" — prefer to buy). Core rule: **buy in
discount, sell in premium.** Buying in premium (or selling in discount) is exactly
backwards and a frequent beginner mistake.

### Optimal Trade Entry (OTE) — the Fibonacci entry filter

After a structure break, draw a Fibonacci retracement across the most recent
impulse leg (swing low → high for a long). The **OTE zone is the 62%–79%
retracement band**, with **0.705 (~70.5%) as the "sweet spot."** ICT teaches
entering only when an order block or FVG sits *inside* that band — maximum
confluence is **OB + FVG + OTE all stacked** in the discount half of the range.

### Killzones — the time filter

ICT teaches trading **only** inside specific high-activity session windows, not
all day. Times are New York (ET):

| Killzone | Window (ET) | What ICT expects |
|----------|-------------|------------------|
| **Asian** | ~8:00 PM – 12:00 AM | Sets the overnight range |
| **London open** | ~2:00 AM – 5:00 AM | First big sweep of the day |
| **New York AM** | ~7:00 AM – 10:00 AM | Strongest directional move |
| **NY PM / London close** | ~10:00 AM – 12:00 PM, 1:30 – 4:00 PM | Reversals / continuation |

The idea is to hunt setups *during* these windows and ignore the quiet hours
between them — taking setups outside a killzone is, by ICT's own logic, where the
edge is weakest.

### Power of Three (AMD)

**Accumulation** (a range builds) → **Manipulation** (a false **"Judas swing"** —
a deceptive early move that sweeps stops and traps one side) → **Distribution**
(the real move in the opposite direction).

## The beginner workflow (how it all fits together)

This is the top-down sequence ICT teaches. Follow it in order:

<ConceptDiagram
  caption="The ICT long workflow as a decision chain: bias → mark zones → wait for the killzone → wait for a sweep → confirm with CHoCH → enter at the stacked OB/FVG/OTE → manage."
  chart={`flowchart LR
  A[1. HTF bias\nDaily/4H trend] --> B[2. Mark zones\nOB + FVG in discount]
  B --> C[3. Wait for\nkillzone London/NY]
  C --> D[4. Wait for\nliquidity sweep]
  D --> E[5. Drop to 5M/1M\nwait for CHoCH]
  E --> F[6. Enter at\nOB+FVG+OTE 0.705]
  F --> G[7. Stop beyond sweep\ntarget opposite pool]`}
/>

1. **HTF bias (Daily/4H):** is structure bullish or bearish? Mark the major
   buy-side and sell-side liquidity pools.
2. **Mark zones (1H/15M):** find order blocks and FVGs sitting in *discount* (for
   longs) or *premium* (for shorts).
3. **Wait for a killzone** (London or New York).
4. **Wait for a liquidity sweep** of an obvious high or low.
5. **Drop to 5M/1M and wait for a CHoCH** confirming the reversal. The sweep alone
   is *not* a signal.
6. **Enter** at the OB / FVG that overlaps the OTE band (62–79%, ideally 0.705) —
   maximum confluence is all three stacked.
7. **Stop loss** just beyond the OB / sweep wick; **target the opposite liquidity
   pool.** Typical advertised reward-to-risk is **1:2 to 1:3+.**

## A worked example (stylized, illustrative numbers)

*(Built from ICT's own rules. Public ICT material describes the method and the
1:2–1:3 reward-to-risk but publishes **no** verified numeric trades — so treat
this as a textbook walk-through, not a real result.)*

**EUR/USD, London killzone, bullish daily bias:**

1. **Impulse leg:** a recent advance ran from swing **low 1.0800 → swing high
   1.0900** (a 100-pip leg).
2. **Liquidity grab:** price retraces and dips to **1.0795**, just below the
   1.0800 low — sweeping the sell-side stops resting there — then snaps back up.
3. **Confluence at the entry zone:** a **bullish order block** (the last down
   candle before the original rally) sits at **1.0830–1.0835**. It overlaps the
   **OTE 0.705 level**: `1.0900 − (0.705 × 100 pips) = 1.08295 ≈ 1.0830`. So the
   sweep + OB + OTE 0.705 all line up — and 1.0830 is in the *discount* half.
4. **CHoCH up:** on the 5M chart price breaks a minor lower-high, confirming the
   reversal.
5. **Entry:** **1.0830.** **Stop:** below the sweep wick at **1.0790** (a 40-pip
   risk — 1R). **Target:** the buy-side liquidity above the old high at **1.0910**
   (an 80-pip reward).
6. **Result:** reward-to-risk = **1:2.** Risking 1% of a $10,000 account = $100; if
   it hits target, +$200.

## Honest assessment

**The substantive criticisms:**

- **No verified track record.** The most serious point. After years of teaching,
  there is no audited result and no public Myfxbook/account history from ICT or his
  students; the confirmable income is from content and brand, not verified trading
  profit. For a method claiming an *institutional* edge, that absence is the
  central red flag.
- **A documented public failure.** ICT entered the **2024 Robbins World Cup
  Trading Championship and did not place** — reporting (forum threads and a YouTube
  clip in which he discusses the account) indicates the account was blown, and he
  is absent from the leaderboard. An earlier public "$10K → $1M" attempt is also
  reported to have failed.
- **Largely rebranded concepts** — proprietary jargon layered over Wyckoff and
  supply/demand (see the lineage above).
- **Jargon volume and unfalsifiability.** So many overlapping tools (OB, FVG, BOS,
  CHoCH, OTE, killzones, premium/discount) that a chart can almost always be
  explained *after the fact*, and losses get rationalized as institutions "hunting
  stops" — a self-sealing defense that's hard to disprove or backtest.
- **Subjectivity.** Order blocks and FVGs are drawn discretionarily — two traders
  mark different ones on the same chart — so the method resists automation, and
  setups that look obvious in hindsight are far harder in real time.

**The fair counterpoints:**

- His core **content is now free**, which undercuts the pure-scam framing.
- The **structure/liquidity lens is genuinely useful** for organizing what you see
  on a chart, even if it isn't a guaranteed edge.
- There's a **real kernel of microstructure truth** (microstructure = the
  fine-grained mechanics of how individual orders and trades actually move price):
  peer-reviewed work by **Carol Osler** documents that stop-loss orders cluster
  (especially around round numbers and just beyond obvious support/resistance) and
  can trigger price cascades. The crucial nuance: the evidence supports clustering
  at *obvious* levels — extending it to "smart money hunts your stops *everywhere*"
  goes beyond what research shows.

<Callout type="key">
Verdict: ICT is **not a provable scam, but it is unproven and contested.** No
audited edge, heavy subjective discretion, self-sealing failure explanations, and
at least one documented public blow-up are real weaknesses — yet the content is
free, the framework is a useful chart-reading lens, and there's a narrow but real
microstructure basis under the liquidity idea. Use it as a vocabulary for market
structure, not a belief system.
</Callout>

## Common pitfalls for beginners drawn to ICT

- **Overfitting in hindsight** — drawing the OB/FVG/OTE that "worked" *after* the
  move instead of calling it in advance.
- **Indicator soup of concepts** — stacking so many tools you can justify a trade
  in any direction (analysis paralysis plus confirmation bias).
- **Ignoring the time filter** — taking setups outside killzones, where ICT's own
  logic says the edge is weakest.
- **Wrong premium/discount side** — buying in premium or selling in discount.
- **Stops too tight inside the sweep** — parking the stop *at* the liquidity level
  that's about to be hunted, getting stopped before the intended move.
- **Mistaking BOS for CHoCH** (or vice-versa) — taking a "reversal" that's really
  trend continuation.
- **Treating ICT as a holy grail** — risking real money on an "institutional edge"
  with no verified results. **Demo and journal extensively first.**
