## The idea that broke the textbook

For most of the 20th century, finance had a tidy belief called the **Efficient
Market Hypothesis (EMH)**: prices already reflect everything knowable, so a
stock's *past* price moves tell you nothing about its *future* ones. Looking at
last year's chart to guess next year's was supposed to be like reading tea
leaves.

Then in 1993, two professors — **Narasimhan Jegadeesh** and **Sheridan
Titman** — published a result that should have been impossible. They showed
that if you simply **buy the stocks that went up the most over the last several
months and short-sell the ones that fell the most**, you earn about **1% per
month** — and you keep earning it for *decades*, across thousands of stocks.

That effect is **momentum**. It is one of the most stubborn, best-documented
anomalies in all of finance, and it is the subject of this lesson. The economist
Robert Shiller reportedly called the finding **"a bombshell"** — because a
strategy built purely on past prices was not supposed to work at all.

<Callout type="key">
**Momentum** is the empirical tendency for assets that have recently performed
well to keep performing well — and recent losers to keep losing — over the next
**3 to 12 months**. It directly contradicts the EMH claim that past returns
can't predict future returns.
</Callout>

## The three people behind it

These three are taught as one unit because their work forms a chain: two of them
**discovered** the effect, and the third **packaged it** into a standard tool
the whole industry now uses.

### Narasimhan Jegadeesh
A finance professor who holds the **Dean's Distinguished Chair of Finance at
Emory University's Goizueta Business School**, with a **PhD in Finance from
Columbia University**. He taught at UCLA and the University of Illinois at
Urbana-Champaign along the way. His career-defining research asks whether stock
markets are predictable — and momentum is the answer he and Titman found.

### Sheridan Titman
A professor at the **University of Texas at Austin (McCombs School of
Business)**, where he holds the McAllister Centennial Chair in Financial
Services, with a **PhD from Carnegie Mellon (1981)**. His career spans UCLA and
Boston College, a founding role at HKUST's business school (1992–94), and a stint
as a special assistant at the U.S. Treasury (1988–89). He was **President of the
American Finance Association in 2012** and won the **Smith Breeden Prize** for the
best *Journal of Finance* paper (1997). He co-authored the 1993 momentum paper
with Jegadeesh.

### Mark M. Carhart
A practitioner-academic who earned his **PhD at the University of Chicago**,
advised by Eugene Fama — the architect of the efficient-market view. His 1997
paper grew straight out of that dissertation. He then built a quant career on
factor research: he co-headed **Goldman Sachs' Quantitative Investment
Strategies** group and later co-founded the hedge fund **Kepos Capital**. He is
the one who turned momentum from an *observation* into a *model*.

## Why this is intellectually explosive

Under a strict reading of the EMH, a stock that has already risen "should not
warrant further increase" — the good news is supposedly already in the price.
Momentum says otherwise. The leading explanation is **behavioral
underreaction**: when good news arrives, investors are slow to fully believe it,
so the price drifts upward over months as the truth sinks in, rather than
jumping all at once. The mirror happens for bad news.

There is also a minority "rational" camp that argues momentum can arise even
among fully rational traders, and a separate argument that the returns are
**compensation for a rare crash risk** — more on that later. Notably, even
Eugene Fama and Kenneth French — the architects of the efficient-market view —
have acknowledged momentum as the anomaly their models most stubbornly fail to
explain.

## The full method, Part A — the original 1993 "J/K" strategy

Jegadeesh & Titman didn't test one strategy; they tested a **grid**. They crossed
a **formation period (J)** — how far back you look — with a **holding period
(K)** — how long you hold — trying J and K of 3, 6, 9, and 12 months each. Here
is the recipe a beginner can actually follow.

1. **Pick a universe.** They used all NYSE/AMEX stocks. You might use, say, the
   S&P 500.
2. **Measure past performance (formation).** At the end of each month, compute
   every stock's **total return over the past J months** (e.g. the last 6).
3. **Rank and sort into 10 deciles** by that past return — best 10% ("Winners")
   down to worst 10% ("Losers").
4. **Build a zero-cost long-short book.** Go **long the top decile** and
   **short the bottom decile**, equal-weighted. "Zero-cost" (or "self-financing")
   means the cash you raise shorting the losers funds the longs — so it's
   roughly market-neutral.
5. **Skip a little.** The original paper left a **one-week gap** between ranking
   and buying, to dodge short-term noise (the bid-ask bounce). The modern
   convention — which you should learn — is to **skip the most recent full
   month**; we'll explain why in the pitfalls.
6. **Hold for K months, then rebalance.** They used *overlapping* portfolios: each
   month you refresh **1/K** of the book, so the strategy always holds a
   staggered set of cohorts rather than turning over all at once.
7. **Measure the spread.** Return of Winners minus return of Losers = your
   momentum return. It was profitable across nearly the whole grid; the headline
   6-month-formation / 6-month-holding ("6/6") portfolio earned roughly **1% per
   month** over 1965–1989.

The single most important picture to hold in your head is the **decile sort** in
steps 3–4: line every stock up by its past return, chop the line into ten equal
buckets, then **buy the top bucket and short the bottom bucket** while ignoring
everything in between.

## The full method, Part B — Carhart's standardized "UMD" factor

Carhart's 1997 contribution was to turn the messy, choose-your-own-parameters
momentum strategy into **one standard, reusable number** anyone can compute
identically. He called it **UMD** — "**Up Minus Down**" — also written **MOM**
(or PR1YR). This is the exact version published on **Kenneth French's data
library** and used by quants today.

1. **Look-back window:** the prior **2-to-12-month** return — that is, from month
   **t-12 to t-2**, deliberately **excluding the most recent month (t-1)**.
2. **Form 6 value-weighted portfolios** = 2 size groups × 3 prior-return groups:
   - **Size split** at the **median NYSE market cap** (Small vs. Big).
   - **Prior-return splits** at the **30th and 70th NYSE percentiles** (Low /
     Neutral / High).
   - This yields Small-High, Small-Low, Big-High, Big-Low (the two Neutral
     buckets are unused).
3. **The factor formula:**

```
Mom (UMD) = ½ (Small-High + Big-High) − ½ (Small-Low + Big-Low)
```

In words: average the two *winner* portfolios, average the two *loser*
portfolios, and take the difference. Reconstitute **monthly**. Splitting by size
first keeps the factor from being secretly a bet on small caps.

## The full method, Part C — what UMD is *for*: the four-factor model

Carhart added UMD to the famous **Fama-French three-factor model** (Market, Size,
Value), producing the **Carhart four-factor model**:

```
EXR = αc + βmkt·EXMKT + βSMB·SMB + βHML·HML + βUMD·UMD + ε
```

- **EXR** — a fund's return *above* the risk-free (T-bill) rate.
- **EXMKT** — the market's excess return.
- **SMB** ("small minus big") — the size factor.
- **HML** ("high minus low") — the value factor (cheap vs. expensive stocks).
- **UMD** — momentum (winners minus losers).
- **αc** ("alpha") — the intercept: the return left over *after* accounting for
  all four exposures.

You estimate this with a **regression** of the fund's monthly excess returns on
the four factors. The βs are the fund's "factor recipe" (a high βUMD means it's
effectively running momentum); the intercept α is what's unexplained.

This model became the **workhorse of mutual-fund performance attribution.** The
question it answers: *did this manager actually have skill, or were they just
unknowingly riding the market, small caps, cheap stocks, and momentum?* A
**positive, statistically significant alpha** (you must check its t-statistic)
means genuine skill *beyond* those four well-known bets. Carhart's own headline
finding was deflating: most fund "outperformance" and short-run performance
persistence (the "hot hands" of funds that keep beating peers) **vanished** once
you controlled for the momentum factor plus differences in expenses and trading
costs. The only robust persistence he found was that the *worst* funds reliably
stayed bad — dragged down by high costs.

## A worked example, step by step

### Part 1 — a simple momentum long-short

Imagine a 10-stock universe. Rank by past-12-month return (skipping the latest
month). The top 2 are Winners (go long), the bottom 2 are Losers (go short).

| Rank | Stock | Past return | Position |
|------|-------|-------------|----------|
| 1 | AAA | +60% | LONG (winner) |
| 2 | BBB | +45% | LONG (winner) |
| … | … | … | (no position) |
| 9 | III | −30% | SHORT (loser) |
| 10 | JJJ | −50% | SHORT (loser) |

Put **\$100 long** the winners (\$50 each) and **\$100 short** the losers (\$50
each) — a zero-net-cash book. Over the next month, say winners return **+3%** and
losers return **+1%**:

- Long leg: \$100 × 3% = **+\$3.00**
- Short leg: −(\$100 × 1%) = **−\$1.00**
- **Net = +\$2.00**

That's **+2% on \$100 of gross exposure** — and notice it had nothing to do with
the market going up or down. The edge is the **spread**: winners beat losers by
2 percentage points. Even though the losers *rose*, you still profited because
you were short the weaker group.

<Callout type="key">
The famous "~1% per month" is a **long-winners-minus-short-losers spread**, not
the return of a long-only basket of winners. Forgetting this is the single most
common misreading of the research.
</Callout>

### Part 2 — why a "small" edge is a bombshell

One percent a month doesn't sound dramatic, but it compounds:

```
(1.01)^12 − 1 ≈ 12.7% per year
```

A reliable 12–13% annual spread, market-neutral, sustained for decades, is
exactly why the 1993 paper landed like a bombshell.

### Part 3 — reading a four-factor alpha

A fund returns 14%/year; T-bills pay 4%, so its **excess** return is 10%. A
regression gives these loadings (β), and we multiply each by its factor's premium.

**First, what *is* the regression?** Strip away the jargon and one factor — say
momentum — is just a scatter plot. Each dot is one month: the momentum factor's
return that month on the horizontal axis, the fund's excess return on the
vertical. Fit the best straight line through the cloud. The **slope of that line
is βUMD** (how hard the fund moves when momentum moves), and the **point where the
line crosses x = 0 is the alpha** for that single-factor view — the fund's return
in a month when momentum did nothing. The full four-factor model just does this
in four dimensions at once.

- Market: β 1.0 × 6% = **6.0%**
- Size: β 0.2 × 2% = **0.4%**
- Value: β 0.0 × premium = **0.0%**
- Momentum: β 0.5 × 8% = **4.0%**
- Factors explain **10.4%**, but the fund only earned **10%** excess →
  **α ≈ −0.4%**.

The lesson: the manager's "skill" was actually a **momentum bet** (the 4.0% from
UMD), and after accounting for all four factors they slightly *underperformed*.
That is precisely the kind of verdict the four-factor model was built to deliver:
control for momentum *before* crediting a manager.

## Honest criticisms and limitations

<Callout type="warning">
Momentum is real and robust, but it is **not** free money. Respect these.
</Callout>

- **No agreed theory.** Behavioral underreaction is the leading story, but it
  isn't settled. Some economists worry that an unexplained effect could be
  data-mining — though momentum has survived out-of-sample and across the globe
  (see AQR's work below), which is the best defense against that charge.
- **Momentum crashes.** The strategy suffers rare but **brutal** drawdowns. In
  2009 the momentum factor fell roughly **−73%** over a few months. These crashes
  hit in violent market rebounds: the beaten-down "loser" stocks you're short
  snap back hardest. Returns are **negatively skewed** — long calm stretches
  punctuated by violent reversals — which has led some to argue momentum's
  premium is partly **payment for bearing crash risk.**
- **High turnover, real trading costs.** Constant rebalancing means lots of
  trading, and critics argue costs erode the paper profit — especially in small,
  illiquid stocks. Much of the loser-side profit also requires **shorting**, which
  is expensive and sometimes impossible. Defenders (AQR) show it survives
  realistic costs at reasonable scale, but cost sensitivity is genuine.
- **Long-horizon reversal.** Returns fade after ~12 months and the same stocks
  tend to **reverse** over a 3–5 year horizon (the DeBondt-Thaler overreaction
  effect). Momentum is a *medium-horizon* phenomenon only. Crowding since
  publication may also have shrunk the premium.
- **The factor is descriptive, not causal.** A high four-factor alpha doesn't
  *prove* skill — it just means returns aren't explained by *these four* factors.
  Newer models (Fama-French five-factor, q-factor) capture things a
  momentum-augmented model misses.

## Common beginner pitfalls

<Callout type="warning">
1. **Confusing momentum with "chasing hot tips."** Academic momentum is a
   disciplined, rules-based **relative (cross-sectional) ranking** with fixed
   look-back and holding periods — not gut-feel performance chasing, and not
   "buy whatever has a high price."
2. **Forgetting to skip the most recent month.** Last month's return is dominated
   by short-term **reversal** (microstructure noise, bid-ask bounce). The standard
   factor uses **t-12 to t-2** for a reason; including t-1 hurts results.
3. **Misreading the "1%/month."** It's a long-short *spread*, not a long-only
   return (see the worked example).
4. **Underestimating drawdowns.** People see "1%/month" and forget the −70%-style
   crashes. Momentum needs risk management.
5. **Ignoring trading costs and shorting frictions** in a backtest — frictionless
   assumptions inflate paper returns wildly, and many real "momentum" products are
   long-only because shorting losers is so costly.
6. **Sign/direction errors in UMD.** UMD = winners *minus* losers (long winners,
   short losers). A **negative βUMD** means the fund is *contrarian*, not bad.
7. **Mixing up the two momentums.** *Cross-sectional* momentum (Jegadeesh-Titman:
   rank stocks against *each other*) differs from *time-series* momentum (an asset
   against its *own* past, i.e. trend-following). Carhart's UMD is cross-sectional.
8. **Mixing up the models.** Three-factor = Fama-French (Market / SMB / HML).
   Four-factor = Carhart (adds UMD). Don't credit Carhart with size/value, or
   Fama-French with momentum.
9. **Confusing momentum with RSI.** RSI (see the prerequisite lesson) is a
   short-horizon *oscillator* on one stock; the momentum factor is a months-long
   *cross-sectional* ranking. They share the word "momentum" and little else.
10. **Treating positive returns as alpha.** Alpha is what's left *after* removing
    market, size, value, and momentum — *and* it must be statistically significant
    (check the t-stat). Many "star" funds have zero true alpha once UMD is added.
</Callout>

## Putting it to work

You don't need to short anything to use this. The practical, beginner-friendly
takeaways:

- **Tilt toward strength.** When choosing among comparable holdings, the
  evidence says recent relative winners tend to keep leading over the next
  3–12 months. A simple rule: rank candidates by trailing 12-month return
  (skipping the last month), and lean toward the top.
- **Rebalance on a schedule** (e.g. monthly or quarterly) rather than reacting to
  every wiggle — momentum is a months-long effect, not a day-trading signal.
  Daily chasing produces ruinous turnover.
- **Size for the crash.** Because momentum can fall ~70% in a rebound, never bet
  the farm; momentum pairs well with other factors (especially **value**, which
  tends to do well exactly when momentum suffers).
- **Use the four-factor lens on funds.** Before paying for a "skilled" manager,
  ask whether their returns are just a momentum (or size/value) tilt you could
  get cheaply through a factor ETF.
- **The honest path for most people:** buy a low-cost **momentum factor ETF** (or
  a multi-factor fund) rather than running the long-short book yourself.

<Callout type="key">
The deepest lesson here isn't a trading recipe — it's intellectual. Jegadeesh,
Titman & Carhart showed that markets are **not perfectly efficient**, that
investor psychology leaves measurable footprints in prices, and that those
footprints can be captured systematically. That insight underpins the entire
modern field of **factor investing.**
</Callout>

## Further reading

- **Jegadeesh & Titman (1993),** "Returns to Buying Winners and Selling Losers,"
  *Journal of Finance* 48(1):65–91 — the foundational momentum paper (~1%/month).
- **Jegadeesh & Titman (2001),** "Profitability of Momentum Strategies,"
  *Journal of Finance* 56(2):699–720 — the major follow-up: out-of-sample
  confirmation in the 1990s, plus evidence that momentum profits partly *reverse*
  later (consistent with the overreaction story). (Note: the canonical J&T
  momentum follow-up is the **2001** paper, not a 1997 one that is sometimes
  mis-cited; Carhart's 1997 paper is the correctly-dated 1997 item.)
- **Carhart (1997),** "On Persistence in Mutual Fund Performance,"
  *Journal of Finance* 52(1):57–82 — introduces the four-factor model.
- **Fama & French (1993),** "Common Risk Factors in the Returns on Stocks and
  Bonds," *Journal of Financial Economics* 33(1):3–56 — the three-factor base
  Carhart extends.
- **Asness, Moskowitz & Pedersen (2013),** "Value and Momentum Everywhere,"
  *Journal of Finance* — momentum across countries and asset classes.
- **AQR,** "Fact, Fiction, and Momentum Investing" — myth-busting with
  long-horizon, global evidence.
