## Why this lesson matters

Before 1973, nobody could say what an option was *worth*. People traded them,
but pricing them was guesswork — because the obvious approach ran straight into
a wall: an option's payoff depends on where the stock ends up, so surely its
price must depend on how fast you expect the stock to rise? And nobody can know
that.

The breakthrough of **Fischer Black, Myron Scholes, and Robert Merton** was to
sidestep the wall entirely. Their answer: **you don't need to forecast the stock
at all.** That single idea launched the modern derivatives industry, won a Nobel
Prize, and gave traders a number — *implied volatility* — that markets still
quote every second of every trading day.

An **option**, if the word is new to you, is a contract giving you the *right but
not the obligation* to buy (a **call**) or sell (a **put**) a stock at a fixed
**strike price** on or before an expiry date. You pay a **premium** up front for
that right. The whole question this lesson answers is: *what is the fair
premium?*

<Callout type="key">
The central insight: a call option can be exactly **copied** by a constantly
adjusted mix of (a) some shares of the stock and (b) some borrowing or lending at
the risk-free rate. If you can build a portfolio that behaves identically to the
option, then the option must cost exactly what that portfolio costs — otherwise
there is free money on the table. This is **replication**, and it makes the
stock's expected return *cancel out.*
</Callout>

## Who they were

This is one lesson about three people, because the result is genuinely theirs
jointly — the formula carries two names and was named by the third.

**Fischer Black (1938–1995).** A Harvard physics graduate who took his PhD in
*applied mathematics*, not economics — he came to finance sideways. He worked at
consulting firm Arthur D. Little (where he met Jack Treynor and caught the
finance bug), then taught at the University of Chicago and MIT, and finally
joined Goldman Sachs in 1984, leading its Quantitative Strategies Group until his
death from cancer at 57. Beyond this formula he co-built the **Black–Derman–Toy**
interest-rate model and the **Black–Litterman** portfolio model — a remarkable
career for someone who never took an economics degree.

<Callout type="note">
Black **did not share the 1997 Nobel** — he had died in 1995, and the prize is
not awarded posthumously. The Nobel committee went out of its way to credit his
central role.
</Callout>

**Myron Scholes (b. 1941).** Canadian-born, University of Chicago PhD (advised by
Eugene Fama and Merton Miller), taught at MIT, Chicago, and Stanford. He
co-authored the foundational 1973 paper with Black and shared the 1997 Nobel. In
1994 he co-founded the hedge fund **Long-Term Capital Management** — a story that
becomes a cautionary tale below.

**Robert C. Merton (b. 1944).** MIT PhD under Paul Samuelson, the pioneer of
**continuous-time finance** — the calculus-of-constant-change mathematics that
made the model rigorous. He published his own 1973 paper generalizing the result
(adding dividends and the formal dynamic-hedging argument), and it was Merton who
coined the name "Black–Scholes." He shared the 1997 Nobel and was also a
principal of LTCM.

The **1997 Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences** went to Scholes and Merton
"for a new method to determine the value of derivatives."

## The core idea, slowly

Let's build the intuition before any formula appears.

Imagine you have *sold* one call option to a customer. You're now on the hook: if
the stock rises, you owe the buyer money. You'd like to **hedge** — to hold
something that gains exactly when you lose, so the two cancel.

What cancels your risk? Owning some of the stock. If the call you sold gains $0.64
for every $1 the stock rises, then holding **0.64 shares** offsets it perfectly —
for small moves. That number, 0.64, is the option's **delta**.

But here's the catch: delta isn't constant. As the stock climbs, the call starts
behaving more and more like the stock itself (delta drifts toward 1.0); as the
stock falls, the call goes limp (delta drifts toward 0). So to stay hedged you
must **continuously rebalance** — buy a few more shares as the stock rises, sell
a few as it falls.

The astonishing result Black, Scholes, and Merton proved: if you run this hedging
loop perfectly, the **total cost of doing so is a fixed, knowable number** — and
it does *not* depend on whether you think the stock will go up or down. The
stock's expected growth rate (the "drift") drops out of the math entirely.

What's left? Only five things matter, and four of them are observable:

<Callout type="key">
The price of a European option depends only on:
**S** the stock price, **K** the strike, **r** the risk-free interest rate, **T**
the time to expiry — and **σ (sigma), the stock's volatility**. The expected
return of the stock is *nowhere in the list.* This is the famous result that
"only volatility matters."
</Callout>

### The "risk-neutral" shortcut

There's a second way to see the same answer, and it's the one quants use daily.
Because the drift cancels, you can pretend you live in a make-believe world where
*everyone is indifferent to risk* — a **risk-neutral** world. In that world every
asset is expected to grow at the risk-free rate, so you can price the option by:

1. computing its expected payoff under a special "risk-neutral" probability, then
2. discounting that back to today at the risk-free rate.

This is a calculation trick, not a claim about real investors. But it gives
exactly the Black–Scholes number, and it generalizes to options the formula
can't handle in closed form.

## The formula

For a **European call** on a stock that pays no dividends:

```
C = S·N(d1) − K·e^(−rT)·N(d2)

      ln(S/K) + (r + σ²/2)·T
d1 = ─────────────────────────
              σ·√T

d2 = d1 − σ·√T
```

And the matching **European put** (which you can also get from put-call parity,
below):

```
P = K·e^(−rT)·N(−d2) − S·N(−d1)
```

The pieces:

| Symbol | Meaning | Where you get it |
|--------|---------|------------------|
| **S** | current stock price | the market |
| **K** | strike price | the contract |
| **r** | risk-free rate, *continuously compounded*, annual | T-bill yields |
| **T** | time to expiry, **in years** | the contract |
| **σ** | volatility (annualized std. dev. of returns) | **you must estimate this** |
| **N(x)** | standard normal CDF — the probability a standard normal draw is `≤ x` | a table or `=NORM.S.DIST(x,TRUE)` |

<Callout type="note">
"**European**" here means the option can only be exercised *at expiry*, not
before. It has nothing to do with geography. American options (exercisable any
time) usually need numerical methods instead of this closed-form formula.
</Callout>

Two terms you must keep straight, because beginners swap them constantly:

- **N(d1) is the delta** — the hedge ratio, the number of shares to hold per call.
- **N(d2) is the risk-neutral probability the option finishes in-the-money** (gets
  exercised). It is *not* the delta.

## The five inputs, and which one is the catch

Four inputs — S, K, r, T — you can read off a screen with no judgment required.
The fifth, **volatility σ, is the entire game.** You cannot observe future
volatility; you have to estimate it. Two ways:

- **Historical (realized) volatility** — measure how much the stock has actually
  wobbled in the *past* (the standard deviation of its daily returns, scaled up to
  a year). It looks backward.
- **Implied volatility** — turn the formula around: take the *market price* of the
  option as given and solve for the σ that produces it. It looks *forward*, and is
  what traders actually quote. We give it its own section below.

<Callout type="warning">
Because four inputs are objective and only σ is a judgment call, **option trading
is, in practice, trading volatility.** Two traders looking at the same option
agree on S, K, r, and T — they're really disagreeing about σ. Get σ wrong and the
beautiful formula hands you a confident, precise, *wrong* price.
</Callout>

## A worked example, step by step

Let's price a real, concrete option by hand. Inputs (a clean one-year,
at-the-money case so the arithmetic stays readable):

- **S = 100** (stock trades at $100)
- **K = 100** (at-the-money — strike equals price)
- **r = 5%** = 0.05
- **T = 1** year
- **σ = 20%** = 0.20

**Step 1 — the building block σ√T:**
```
σ√T = 0.20 × √1 = 0.20
```

**Step 2 — compute d1.** Since S = K, ln(S/K) = ln(1) = 0:
```
(r + σ²/2)·T = (0.05 + 0.04/2) × 1 = 0.05 + 0.02 = 0.07
d1 = (0 + 0.07) / 0.20 = 0.3500
```

**Step 3 — compute d2:**
```
d2 = 0.3500 − 0.20 = 0.1500
```

**Step 4 — look up the normal CDF values:**
```
N(d1) = N(0.3500) ≈ 0.6368
N(d2) = N(0.1500) ≈ 0.5596
```

**Step 5 — the discount factor:**
```
e^(−rT) = e^(−0.05) ≈ 0.9512
```

**Step 6 — assemble the call:**
```
C = 100 × 0.6368 − 100 × 0.9512 × 0.5596
  = 63.68 − 53.23
  ≈ 10.45
```

**The call is worth about $10.45.** And we learn two things for free:

- Its **delta is N(d1) ≈ 0.64**, so to hedge one call you sold, start by holding
  about 0.64 shares.
- The **risk-neutral probability it ends in-the-money is N(d2) ≈ 0.56.**

**Step 7 — the put, via put-call parity** (the iron law linking a call and put of
the same strike: `C − P = S − K·e^(−rT)`):
```
P = C − S + K·e^(−rT) = 10.45 − 100 + 95.12 ≈ 5.57
```
So the matching put is worth about **$5.57.** Check it: C − P = 10.45 − 5.57 =
**4.88**, and S − K·e^(−rT) = 100 − 95.12 = **4.88.** Parity holds exactly. ✓

<Callout type="note">
*Numbers computed here and verified (put-call parity holds to the cent).* You'll
see tiny variations across textbooks purely from rounding N(·). Rebuild this in a
spreadsheet — it's the single best way to make the formula stop being a black box.
</Callout>

## The Greeks — the dashboard of sensitivities

Once you have the formula you get its derivatives almost for free, and traders
live by them. Each "Greek" answers *"if one input wiggles, how much does my option
price move?"* Here are the values **for our worked example**, so they're concrete
rather than abstract.

| Greek | Measures sensitivity to… | In plain words | Our example |
|-------|--------------------------|----------------|-------------|
| **Delta** (Δ) | stock price | shares to hold per call; = N(d1) for a call | **0.64** |
| **Gamma** (Γ) | *change in delta* | how fast your hedge goes stale → how often to rebalance | **0.019** |
| **Vega** | volatility σ | gain per +1% of implied vol; always positive for plain options | **+$0.38 per 1% vol** |
| **Theta** (Θ) | passage of time | time decay — what one day costs you | **−$0.018 / day** |
| **Rho** (ρ) | interest rate r | gain per +1% in rates; smallest effect for short options | **+$0.53 per 1% rate** |

Reading them as a hedger: holding **delta = 0.64** shares offsets the call for a
small move. **Gamma = 0.019** says that if the stock rises $1, delta climbs to
about 0.66 — so you'd buy ~2 more shares to stay hedged. **Vega = 0.38** says if
implied vol ticks from 20% to 21%, the call gains about 38 cents. **Theta** quietly
takes about 1.8 cents off the call every day the stock sits still.

The one Greek every option *buyer* feels in their gut is **theta** — time decay.
An option is a wasting asset: hold it and, all else equal, it loses value every
day as expiry approaches and its "time value" bleeds away. The chart shows why
theta is dangerous: the decay is **not linear** — it *accelerates* as expiry
nears.

table above are already scaled the conventional way.
</Callout>

## Implied volatility — running the model backwards

This is the model's most enduring gift. Four inputs (S, K, r, T) are observable.
So take the option's *market price* as given and ask the reverse question: **what
σ makes the formula output that exact price?** That σ is the **implied volatility
(IV)** — the market's own forward-looking forecast of how much the stock will move.

There's no algebra to isolate σ, but there's a clean trick: the option price
*always rises* as σ rises (vega is positive), so there's exactly one answer. You
find it numerically — guess a σ, see if the price is too high or too low, and
close in (Newton's method using vega, or a bisection search).

<Callout type="key">
**IV turns a dollar price into one comparable number.** A $4 option and a $40
option on different stocks aren't comparable as dollars — but their implied
volatilities are. IV is the apples-to-apples gauge of how "expensive" an option
is, and it's what gets quoted on a trading desk.
</Callout>

> **Worked illustration.** Our call above was worth $10.45 at σ = 20%. If the
> *market* quoted that same one-year call at exactly **$10.45**, solving backwards
> returns σ = 20% — that 20% *is* the implied volatility. If instead the market
> quoted it at **$12.00**, the σ needed to reach $12.00 is higher: solving gives
> IV ≈ **24%**. The richer price is the market saying "we expect more movement than
> 20%." (Verified numerically.)

<Callout type="warning">
Don't confuse the two volatilities. **Historical/realized** vol looks backward at
what the stock *did*; **implied** vol is solved from current market prices and is
forward-looking. They are different numbers, and the gap between them is itself a
trade.
</Callout>

### Volatility becomes a tradable thing

Here's the deepest practical consequence. Because an option's price is really a
function of volatility, traders stop thinking in dollars and start thinking in
**vol**. If you believe the stock will *move more* than today's IV implies, you
buy options and **delta-hedge** them — stripping out the up/down direction so that
you profit purely from movement itself, regardless of which way it goes. If you
think it'll move *less*, you sell.

This created entire instruments for trading movement directly:

- **Straddles** — buy a call and a put at the same strike; you win if the stock
  moves far in *either* direction.
- **Variance and volatility swaps** — contracts that pay off on how much the stock
  actually moves versus a strike level of vol.
- **The VIX index** — a weighted average of S&P 500 option implied volatilities,
  the market's famous "fear gauge," with its own futures and options.

## The primary sources

- **Fischer Black & Myron Scholes, "The Pricing of Options and Corporate
  Liabilities," _Journal of Political Economy_ 81(3), 1973** — the founding paper
  with the formula.
- **Robert C. Merton, "Theory of Rational Option Pricing," _Bell Journal of
  Economics and Management Science_ 4(1): 141–183, 1973** — the rigorous
  continuous-time generalization (dividends, dynamic hedging); Merton coined
  "Black–Scholes" here.
- A worthy ancestor: **Louis Bachelier, _Théorie de la spéculation_ (1900)** —
  the first to apply Brownian motion (random-walk math) to option pricing, decades
  ahead of his time. (His arithmetic-Brownian model resurfaced in 2020 — see
  below.)
- **John Hull, _Options, Futures, and Other Derivatives_** — the standard
  practitioner textbook that teaches the model from the ground up.

## Honest criticisms and limitations

The model is a masterpiece *and* a simplification. Take its assumptions
seriously, because the market does not obey them.

- **The volatility smile/skew.** The model assumes one constant σ for every strike.
  Reality flatly disagrees: if you back out implied volatility from real option
  prices, it varies by strike and maturity, forming a "smile" or "skew." This is
  the single most important practical deviation — the market is telling you it
  doesn't believe the model's own assumption.

Pure 'smile' (dashed): both deep out-of-the-money puts (low strikes) and calls (high strikes) cost extra IV — the market pays up for tail risk on both sides. <Callout type="note">
How to read the x-axis: each point is a different *strike* on the **same stock at
the same expiry**. Far left = low strikes (deep out-of-the-money puts / crash
protection); far right = high strikes (out-of-the-money calls); the dip in the
middle is at-the-money. A flat line would mean the model fits perfectly. It never
does — for stock-index options the skew has been pronounced ever since the 1987
crash taught the market to fear downside gaps.
</Callout>

- **Fat tails.** Returns are assumed lognormal, which *underestimates the odds of
  extreme moves* — crashes happen far more often than a bell curve predicts. The
  model systematically underprices deep out-of-the-money options (the
  crash-protection puts).
- **European-only.** The closed form prices options exercisable at expiry only.
  American options (exercisable any time) need numerical methods — binomial trees,
  finite differences.
- **No dividends in the original.** Merton's extension handles them; the standard
  fix replaces S with `S·e^(−qT)` for a dividend yield q.
- **Frictionless markets.** It assumes no transaction costs, continuous costless
  trading, and unlimited borrowing/shorting at the risk-free rate. Real
  rebalancing costs money, and "continuous" hedging is impossible — discrete
  hedging always leaves residual risk.
- **Constant, known r and σ.** Both move in reality.
- **Breaks on negative prices.** When oil futures went *negative* in April 2020,
  the lognormal model (which forbids negative prices) had to be swapped for the
  older **Bachelier** model — a 120-year-old idea suddenly back in production.

<Callout type="warning">
**The cautionary tale: Long-Term Capital Management.** Scholes and Merton
co-founded LTCM, a heavily leveraged hedge fund. After early annual returns near
40%, it lost roughly **$4.6 billion in under four months** during the 1998 Russian
debt crisis and required a bailout coordinated by the Federal Reserve Bank of New
York. Two Nobel laureates in option pricing did not save it. (To be fair, the
collapse was driven by extreme leverage and correlated bond-arbitrage bets, not
the option formula itself.) The lesson is permanent: **elegant models do not
eliminate real-world risk — especially leverage and liquidity risk**, the two
things the frictionless assumptions quietly assume away.
</Callout>

Warren Buffett (2008) warned the formula "can produce absurd results" over long
horizons. Practitioners have a self-aware joke about it: it's *"the wrong number
in the wrong formula to get the right price"* — meaning traders knowingly feed it
a fudged volatility precisely because everyone agrees to speak its language.

## Common beginner pitfalls

<Callout type="warning">
1. **Thinking you must predict the stock's direction.** You don't — the drift
   cancels. Volatility is the input that matters.
2. **Treating the output as the "true" price.** It's a model estimate, only as
   good as your volatility guess.
3. **Confusing N(d1) and N(d2).** N(d1) is the hedge ratio (delta); N(d2) is the
   risk-neutral probability of exercise — *not* a real-world probability and not
   the delta.
4. **Confusing historical and implied volatility.** One looks backward, one
   forward. They are different numbers.
5. **Unit errors.** r, T, and σ must all be annualized and in decimals (0.05, not
   5). A 3-month option is **T = 0.25**, not 3; a 30-day option is `30/365 ≈ 0.08`.
6. **Quoting Greeks unscaled.** Vega is per 1.00 of vol (÷100 for "per 1%"); theta
   is per year (÷365 for per-day).
7. **Using simple interest for r.** The formula uses *continuous* compounding —
   that's why `e^(−rT)` appears, not `1/(1+r)^T`.
8. **Forgetting dividends.** The basic formula assumes none; with a dividend yield
   q, replace S with `S·e^(−qT)`. Otherwise you overprice calls and underprice
   puts.
9. **Applying it to American options directly,** or plugging *one* IV across all
   strikes — the smile says each strike has its own.
10. **Believing a delta-hedge is risk-free in practice.** It's only perfect under
    *continuous* rebalancing in theory; gamma, jumps, and transaction costs break
    it.
</Callout>

## Putting it to work

You don't need to be a hedge fund to use this:

1. **Get a Black–Scholes calculator** (most brokers have one; or build the
   spreadsheet — `=NORM.S.DIST()` is the only special function you need).
2. **Before buying any option, price it.** Feed in S, K, r, T, and a volatility
   estimate. Compare your fair value to the market's asking price.
3. **Read the implied volatility, not just the dollar price.** Solve the formula
   backwards (or read the IV your broker shows). If the market wants far more than
   your fair-value number, *you are paying up for high implied volatility* — ask
   whether that's justified before you buy.
4. **Respect the Greeks.** Know your delta (effective share exposure) and your
   theta (how fast time is eating your premium) before you put money down.
5. **Never trust the model in the tails.** Buy your crash protection knowing the
   formula underprices it — that's often *why* it looks cheap.

The deepest takeaway is conceptual and outlives any one formula: **price by
replication and no-arbitrage.** If you can build something that copies a payoff,
that payoff must cost what the copy costs. That principle now prices everything
from corporate bonds to employee stock options to mortgage securities — the entire
architecture of modern derivatives stands on it.
