## The one-sentence version

Alan Farley argued that markets don't move randomly — they cycle through a small set of repeating emotional stages, and the swing trader's job is to recognize which stage a stock is in, across several time frames at once, and act one step ahead of the crowd.

If you've ever bought a "breakout" that immediately reversed, or sold a "top" that kept climbing, the problem usually wasn't the pattern — it was that you didn't know *where in the cycle* the pattern appeared. Farley's whole framework, laid out in his 2000 book *The Master Swing Trader*, is built to fix exactly that. This lesson is for traders who already know basic chart patterns and want a structured way to organize them, with honest attention to where the method gets fuzzy.

<Callout type="key">

Farley's central claim: a pattern is only meaningful in context. The same triangle is a high-probability trade near the end of a basing phase and a trap near the end of a mature trend. "Pattern Cycles" is the system for reading that context.

</Callout>

## Who Alan Farley was

Alan S. Farley is a private trader, author, and educator who became prominent during the late-1990s online-trading boom. He is the publisher and editor of **Hard Right Edge** (hardrightedge.com), one of the earlier well-regarded web portals dedicated to trader education and short-term trading. He became a columnist for TheStreet.com / RealMoney around 2001 — the era of Jim Cramer's site — and has written widely for financial publications since, including educational articles for Investopedia and Benzinga.

His signature work is *The Master Swing Trader: Tools and Techniques to Profit from Outstanding Short-Term Trading Opportunities* (McGraw-Hill, 2000), with a later companion, *The Master Swing Trader Toolkit* (McGraw-Hill, 2010). The book arrived at a specific moment: retail traders suddenly had direct market access and intraday charts, and many were getting destroyed by treating short-term trading as faster long-term investing. Farley's pitch was that short-term trading is a *different discipline* requiring its own map.

<Callout type="note">

"Swing trading" here means holding positions for roughly a few days to a few weeks — longer than a day trade, shorter than position trading. Farley uses the term broadly and stresses that his framework is "time-frame independent": the same logic applies to a 5-minute chart and a weekly chart.

</Callout>

## The core idea: Pattern Cycles

Farley originally called his framework the **"Traders Wheel"** — a concept he traces to his early CompuServe trading materials — then renamed it **Pattern Cycles** to emphasize that markets repeat the same stages in an orderly process. The claim is that price moves "relentlessly from bottom to top and back again," cycling through these stages (drawn directly from the book's Chapter 3):

```
Bottoms → Breakouts → Rallies → Highs → Tops → Reversals → Declines → (back to Bottoms)
```

You may recognize the skeleton of Richard Wyckoff and the older accumulation/markup/distribution/markdown idea — Farley is explicitly in that lineage. His contribution is operational: he ties each stage to (a) the crowd emotion driving it and (b) the specific setups that work *in that stage and nowhere else*.

The organizing concept underneath the cycle is the **trend–range axis**. Markets are always doing one of two things: *trending* (directional movement) or *ranging* (sideways consolidation). Tradeable energy is released at the *transitions* — range giving way to trend (a breakout) or trend exhausting into a range (a top). Most of Farley's setups are bets on a specific transition.

<Callout type="key">

The practical upgrade over "just trade the pattern": before you take any setup, you first classify the stage (where in the cycle?) and the trend–range state (trending or ranging?). The setup only earns a trade if it agrees with that context.

</Callout>

## Convergence across multiple time frames

This is the part most beginners under-use, and it's where Farley's edge mostly lives. He calls it **cross-verification** (and his charting application of it "3D charting"): a signal is only as good as the number of independent things agreeing with it.

The strongest "independent thing" is *another time frame*. A bounce on the 15-minute chart is noise. A bounce on the 15-minute chart that occurs exactly at a 62% Fibonacci retracement (the 61.8% level, rounded) on the daily chart, into a weekly support shelf, is **convergence** — multiple time frames pointing at the same price for the same reason.

- **Convergence** = time frames and indicators agree → higher-probability, tighter-risk trade.
- **Divergence** = time frames disagree (daily says "up," weekly says "topping") → conflict, lower probability, stand aside or size down.

A practical way Farley operationalizes this is **multi–time-frame Fibonacci retracements**: draw retracements on weekly, daily, and intraday charts and look for prices where the levels stack. Where they cluster, the crowd's memory of price is concentrated, and reversals there are more reliable.

## The 7 Bells

The "**7 Bells**" are Farley's named, original setups — each is essentially "a recognizable structure that, *in the right stage*, signals a high-probability move." They are mnemonics for a principle, not mechanical buy/sell rules. Briefly:

| Bell | Principle |
|---|---|
| **Dip Trip** | A pullback against a strong trend tends to rebound sharply (buy the dip in an uptrend). |
| **Coiled Spring** | Tightly constricted price (a narrowing range / volatility squeeze) gives way to a directional move. |
| **Finger Finder** | A single sharp candle ("finger") flags a reversal that's visible in the next-smaller time frame. |
| **Hole-in-the-Wall** | A gap down after a strong rally signals a trend change (distribution beginning). |
| **Power Spike** | A high-volume event prints the likely future direction of price. |
| **Bear Hug** | A weak market rallies into resistance, then drops quickly — a short setup. |
| **3rd Watch** | A breakout through a triple top signals a major new uptrend. |

<Callout type="warning">

Don't trade the bells as a checklist of shapes. Each is only valid in its stage of the Pattern Cycle. A "Coiled Spring" near a mature top is a coin flip; the same spring at the end of a base is the high-probability version. The bell describes *what* to look for; the cycle tells you *whether to trust it*.

</Callout>

## Patterns are crowd psychology, drawn

Farley repeatedly frames technicals as a picture of mass emotion. His blunt formulation of the goal: the trader's purpose is "to take other people's money before they take ours," by recognizing setups one step ahead of the crowd and closing the position before the majority has even caught on.

That cynicism is the point. A pattern works because it maps a *predictable emotional sequence*: fear builds a base, hope launches a breakout, greed extends a rally, complacency forms a top, then denial and panic drive the decline. Support and resistance are simply price levels where the crowd's prior pain or pleasure is concentrated, so traders react there again. Reading the pattern is reading the crowd.

## A worked example (illustrative)

*The following numbers are illustrative, not a real trade.*

Suppose stock XYZ has been declining for months, then goes quiet near \$20 for several weeks (a possible **Bottom** stage; a trend→range transition). You check convergence:

- **Weekly:** \$20 is an old support shelf from a year ago. ✓
- **Daily:** price is coiling — successively narrower ranges (a **Coiled Spring**) on falling volume. ✓
- **60-minute:** a single **Power Spike** bar lifts off \$20 on volume 3x average. ✓

Three time frames converge on \$20–\$21 as the launch zone. Setup: buy the breakout above the coil, say \$21.20.

```

Stop:         19.80   (just under the base; structure breaks here)
Risk/share:   1.40
First target: 26.00   (prior congestion / measured move)
Reward/share: 4.80
Reward:risk = 4.80 / 1.40 ≈ 3.4 : 1
```

Farley insists on this arithmetic *before* entry. A setup whose reward:risk falls much below roughly 2.5–3:1 generally isn't worth taking, because even a good win rate can't overcome a poor payoff ratio. If the stop must sit far away (wrecking reward:risk), the trade is rejected regardless of how pretty the chart looks.

<Callout type="key">

Timing is the whole game. Farley wants entries right at the structural inflection — the breakout edge, the retracement level — so the stop can sit close behind structure. A late entry forces a wide stop, wrecks reward:risk, and turns a good setup into a bad trade.

</Callout>

## The Adam Theory critique

In *The Master Swing Trader*, Farley is openly critical of J. Welles Wilder's later **"Adam Theory of Markets"** (1987) — Wilder's claim that markets are essentially symmetrical and that you can forecast future moves by reflecting past price action, discarding the very indicators (RSI, ADX, Parabolic SAR) that Wilder himself had introduced in 1978 and made famous.

Farley's objection is on principle: there is no reliable symmetry to exploit, and a method that throws away confirmation tools in favor of a single mirror-image forecast contradicts the entire cross-verification philosophy. His stance is honest and worth internalizing — the lesson isn't "Wilder bad," it's that **single-tool, single–time-frame prediction is fragile**; robustness comes from convergence, not from one elegant rule.

## Honest criticisms and pitfalls

- **It resists backtesting.** Pattern Cycles, stage classification, and "convergence" require judgment. By Farley's own framing, the method demands concentration, discipline, and skilled execution — a polite way of saying it's discretionary and hard to verify statistically. Two skilled traders can label the same chart's "stage" differently.
- **Hindsight bias.** Stages and bells are obvious on historical charts and ambiguous in real time. A base only becomes a "Bottom" after price has left it.
- **The 7 Bells are heuristics, not edges.** None comes with a published win rate or expectancy. They organize attention; they don't guarantee profit.
- **Vocabulary overload.** Farley coins a lot of terms (Traders Wheel, 3D charting, Finger Finder…). Don't mistake fluency in the jargon for skill at the trades.
- **It's a product of its era.** Written for late-1990s equity markets; behavior in algorithm-dominated, high-frequency markets and in 24/7 crypto may differ, especially around gaps and volume signatures.

<Callout type="warning">

The most common beginner failure is skipping the context step: spotting a bell, falling in love with it, and ignoring the stage and the higher time frame. The framework's value is almost entirely in the discipline of classifying context *first*.

</Callout>

## How to apply it today

1. **Top-down, every time.** Start on the highest time frame you trade, classify the stage and trend–range state, then drill down. Only take setups that agree with the higher frame.
2. **Demand convergence.** Require at least two independent confirmations (for example, a multi–time-frame Fibonacci cluster plus a volume spike) before entering.
3. **Compute reward:risk before entry.** If you can't define a structural stop that yields roughly 3:1 or better, pass.
4. **Pick two or three bells and specialize.** Trying to trade all seven at once dilutes recognition skill. Master Dip Trip and Coiled Spring first.
5. **Keep a labeled journal.** Tag each trade with stage, bell, and convergence factors. Over time this is the only way to learn whether *your* stage-reading is actually predictive.
