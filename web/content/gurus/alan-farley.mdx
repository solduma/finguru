## The one-sentence version

Alan Farley argued that markets don't move randomly — they cycle through a small set of repeating emotional stages, and the swing trader's job is to recognize which stage a stock is in, across several time frames at once, and act one step ahead of the crowd only when multiple independent signals point at the same price.

If you've ever bought a "breakout" that immediately reversed, or sold a "top" that kept climbing, the problem usually wasn't the pattern — it was that you didn't know *where in the cycle* the pattern appeared, and you didn't have anything else agreeing with it. Farley's whole framework, laid out in his 2000 book *The Master Swing Trader*, is built to fix exactly that. This lesson is for traders who already know basic chart patterns (read [Charles Dow](/learn/gurus/charles-dow) first) and want a structured way to organize them, with honest attention to where the method gets fuzzy.

<Callout type="key">

Farley's two central claims:

1. **A pattern is only meaningful in context.** The same triangle is a high-probability trade near the end of a basing phase and a trap near the end of a mature trend.
2. **A signal is only worth trading when several independent signals agree at the same price.** One reason is a guess; three unrelated reasons stacking on one level is an edge.

"Pattern Cycles" is the system for reading the first; "convergence" is the discipline for the second.

</Callout>

## Who Alan Farley was

Alan S. Farley is an American private trader, author, and educator who became prominent during the late-1990s online-trading boom. He holds a bachelor's degree in **psychology from the University of Pittsburgh** — a background that shows up everywhere in his work, which treats charts as a picture of crowd emotion and insists that trader *discipline* matters more than market *knowledge*.

He is the publisher and editor of **Hard Right Edge** (hardrightedge.com), one of the earlier well-regarded web portals dedicated to trader education and short-term trading. (The name itself is a teaching point: the "hard right edge" of a chart is the most recent bar — the live decision point, where the future is blank and you actually have to commit. Everything to the left is easy in hindsight.) He wrote the long-running **"The Swing Shift"** column and **"The Daily Swing Trade"** newsletter for **TheStreet.com / RealMoney** for well over a decade, and has been a frequent contributor to **CNBC and Bloomberg TV**, with work featured in *Barron's, SmartMoney, Forbes,* and *Technical Analysis of Stocks & Commodities*.

His signature work is *The Master Swing Trader: Tools and Techniques to Profit from Outstanding Short-Term Trading Opportunities* (McGraw-Hill, 2000), with a later companion, *The Master Swing Trader Toolkit: The Market Survival Guide* (McGraw-Hill, 2010). The book arrived at a specific moment: retail traders suddenly had direct market access and intraday charts, and many were getting destroyed by treating short-term trading as faster long-term investing. Farley's pitch was that short-term trading is a *different discipline* requiring its own map — and that **survival (capital preservation) comes before profit**. His book famously opens with the line that discipline is more crucial than knowledge.

<Callout type="note">

"Swing trading" here means holding positions for roughly a few days to a few weeks — longer than a day trade, shorter than position trading. Farley uses the term broadly and stresses that his framework is "time-frame independent": the same logic applies to a 5-minute chart and a weekly chart.

</Callout>

## The core idea: Pattern Cycles

Farley originally called his framework the **"Traders Wheel"** — a concept he traces to his early CompuServe trading materials — then renamed it **Pattern Cycles** to emphasize that markets repeat the same stages in an orderly process. The claim is that price moves "relentlessly from bottom to top and back again," cycling through these stages:

```
Bottoms → Breakouts → Rallies → Highs → Tops → Reversals → Declines → (back to Bottoms)
```

In plain terms, the stages are: **Bottoms** (price stops falling and goes quiet, building a base), **Breakouts** (it finally pushes up out of that base), **Rallies** (the sustained climb), **Highs** (the strong, still-trending peak), **Tops** (the climb stalls and rolls into a sideways range — the trend is exhausting), **Reversals** (price turns down and confirms the trend has flipped), and **Declines** (the sustained fall, which eventually quiets down into the next Bottom). Note the deliberate split between *Highs* (still healthy, trend intact) and *Tops* (trend stalling) — that distinction is exactly the kind of context the whole framework hangs on.

You may recognize the skeleton of **Richard Wyckoff** and the older accumulation → markup → distribution (large holders quietly selling near the top) → markdown idea — Farley is explicitly in that lineage. His contribution is operational: he ties each stage to (a) the crowd emotion driving it and (b) the specific setups that work *in that stage and nowhere else*.

The organizing concept underneath the cycle is the **trend–range axis**. Markets are always doing one of two things: *trending* (directional movement) or *ranging* (sideways consolidation). Every Farley tactic is therefore one of two kinds — a **trend-following** move (ride the markup or markdown) or a **range/reversal** move (fade the edges of the base or top). The most tradeable energy is released at the *transitions*: range giving way to trend (a breakout) or trend exhausting into a range (a top). Most of Farley's setups are bets on a specific transition — so **before anything else you must decide which environment you're in**, because trend tactics fail inside ranges and range tactics fail inside trends.

## Step 1: Read the same stock on three time frames

This is the part most beginners skip, and it's where Farley's edge mostly lives. He insists you never look at a stock on a single chart. Instead you build a **trading screen of three time frames** and only trade when they agree. For a multi-day swing trade, a typical screen is:

| Time frame | Role | What it decides |
|---|---|---|
| **Long** (e.g. weekly) | The big picture | The dominant trend and major support/resistance — *am I allowed to be long at all?* |
| **Intermediate** (e.g. daily) | The setup chart | Where the actual pattern forms and where you place the order |
| **Short** (e.g. 60-minute) | The trigger | The precise entry timing |

The rule for a **long**: the higher time frame must be in an uptrend or basing (*not* in markdown), **and** the setup appears on the intermediate chart, **and** the short chart confirms the trigger. When the time frames conflict — the daily makes a higher high while the weekly is rolling over at resistance — that **divergence** is a signal to **stand aside or size down**, not to trade.

## Step 2: Build the convergence map

Farley calls the broader principle **cross-verification** (and his charting application of it "3D charting"): a signal is only as good as the number of *independent* things agreeing with it. On your intermediate (setup) chart you mark **every independent price level** you can find:

- Prior swing highs and lows (horizontal support/resistance).
- Key moving averages — Farley leans hard on the **200-day MA** as a primary strong/weak filter, plus shorter ones (the 20- and 50-day).
- **Fibonacci retracement** levels of the last major swing (38.2%, 50%, 61.8%).
- Trendlines and the **Bollinger Bands**.
- Round numbers and recent gaps.

Where several of these *stack at one price*, you have a **convergence zone** — your highest-probability reaction point. A bounce on the 15-minute chart is noise. A bounce that occurs exactly at a daily 61.8% Fibonacci level, on top of the 50-day MA, at an old support shelf the weekly chart remembers, is **convergence**: multiple independent reasons pointing at the same price for the same crowd-psychology reason.

**Convergence = high probability, tight risk.** The more independent reasons that point at one price, the better the odds and the closer you can place your stop (just beyond the zone). One reason alone — a lone Fibonacci level, a lone moving average — is not a trade.

</Callout>

## Step 3: Decide — reaction trade or execution trade?

Farley splits entries into two families, and knowing which one you're taking sets your whole risk plan:

- **Reaction trade** — you *buy a pullback into* a strong support convergence zone (or short a rally into resistance). You're buying the dip, fading the move back toward value. Lower risk, because the stop sits just beyond the zone, but you're catching a falling knife if the zone fails.
- **Execution / breakout trade** — you buy *as price breaks out* of a range or pattern, ideally on expanding volume. Higher momentum and you trade in the direction of the thrust, but breakouts fail often, so expect a lower hit rate.

The two demand different mindsets: a reaction trade rewards patience and a precise entry; an execution trade rewards confirmation (volume, a clean break) and tolerance for more false starts.

## Step 4: The volatility engine — contraction then expansion

Underneath every setup is one rhythm: the market alternates between **range contraction** (low volatility, tight bars, Bollinger Bands narrowing — energy *building*) and **range expansion** (a sudden wide-range bar, a breakout or breakdown — energy *releasing*). Because volatility mean-reverts, the highest-reward moment is the **transition from contraction to expansion**.

The core rule: **enter during, or just before, the expansion that follows a clear contraction — and never chase a move that has already fully expanded.** Buying after a stock has already made its big wide-range run is how beginners get the worst possible entry: a wide stop and a move that's about to revert.

## Step 5: Wait for one of the 7 Bells

The "**7 Bells**" are Farley's named, original setups — each is essentially "a recognizable structure that, *in the right stage*, signals a high-probability move." They are mnemonics for a principle, not mechanical buy/sell rules. Briefly:

| Bell | Principle | Best stage |
|---|---|---|
| **Dip Trip** | A pullback against a strong trend, bought as it reaches a support/Fibonacci convergence and resumes. | Rallies (trend continuation) |
| **Coiled Spring** | Tightly constricted price (a volatility squeeze, often an *NR7* — the narrowest daily range of the last 7 days) gives way to a directional move. | Bottoms / late ranges |
| **Finger Finder** | A single long-wick candle ("finger") pierces support and snaps back — a failed breakdown and reversal. | Bottoms / Reversals |
| **Hole-in-the-Wall** | A gap *down* after a strong rally punches through support — distribution beginning, a trend change. | Tops → Declines |
| **Power Spike** | A burst of unusually high volume reveals — and often kicks off — the next move. | Any (context decides up vs. down) |
| **Bear Hug** | A weak market rallies into resistance, fails, and drops quickly — a short. | Tops → Declines |
| **3rd Watch** | A breakout through a level tested three times (a triple top) tends to run on the third or fourth push. | Breakouts |

<Callout type="warning">

Don't trade the bells as a checklist of shapes. Each is only valid in its stage of the Pattern Cycle, **and** each still needs convergence behind it. A "Coiled Spring" near a mature top is a coin flip; the same spring at the end of a base, sitting on a Fibonacci-plus-MA cluster, is the high-probability version. The bell tells you *what* to look for; the cycle and the convergence map tell you *whether to trust it*.

</Callout>

## Step 6: Use time as a second dimension ("adverse time")

Most traders only watch *price*. Farley treats **time as a dimension equal to price**, and this gives him a second kind of stop:

- **Adverse time.** A setup that should react *fast* (a Coiled Spring, a Power Spike) but instead just sits there for several bars is failing — even if price hasn't hit your hard stop. The absence of the expected reaction is itself information. Farley advocates exiting on time, near breakeven, rather than waiting for the price stop to bleed you out slowly.
- **Fibonacci in time as well as price.** Beyond price retracements, he watches recurring time intervals and seasonality (his "Market Clock" — tendencies around certain weekdays and Fed-meeting days).

The practical takeaway for a beginner: decide *in advance* how many bars the setup is allowed to take to work. If it stalls past that window, you're out — a stalled trade is a wrong trade that simply hasn't cost you yet.

## Step 7: Manage risk (survival first)

Farley's first principle is capital preservation, and it's enforced with arithmetic done *before* the entry:

- Define **entry, hard stop, and target** before you click. The stop goes just beyond the convergence zone or pattern that would prove the idea wrong.
- Require a favorable **reward-to-risk ratio** — roughly **2.5–3:1 or better** is the practical floor. If a sensible structural stop forces a poor ratio, you reject the trade no matter how pretty the chart.
- **Size the position from the risk**, not the other way around: risk a fixed small fraction of the account (e.g. 1%) and let the distance to the stop set the share count.
- Scale out at Fibonacci or prior-resistance targets, and honor the *time* stop as well as the price stop.

## A worked example (illustrative)

*The following numbers are illustrative, not a real trade. They show the mechanics on a \$25,000 account willing to risk 1% (\$250) per trade.*

Suppose stock XYZ has been declining for months, then goes quiet near \$20 for several weeks (a possible **Bottom** stage; a trend→range transition). You run the full process:

- **Time frames.** Weekly: \$20 is an old support shelf from a year ago, and the multi-month decline is decelerating — *basing, not still in markdown,* so a long is allowed. ✓
- **Convergence map (daily).** Price is coiling into successively narrower ranges (a **Coiled Spring**, with the latest bar an NR7) on falling volume, and the coil sits right on the \$20 shelf. ✓
- **Trigger (60-minute).** A single **Power Spike** bar lifts off \$20 on volume ~3× average — the expansion following the contraction. ✓

Three independent signals converge on \$20–\$21 as the launch zone. Setup: an **execution (breakout) trade** — buy the breakout above the coil, say \$21.20.

The first target \$26.00 is the **prior congestion** — the price shelf near \$26 that the stock fell from before going quiet at \$20 (that earlier decline isn't on this zoomed-in chart, which starts at the base). The reasoning: a stock that reclaims its launch zone tends to run back toward the supply it last broke down from. (A related technique is the pure *measured move* — projecting the height of the base itself up from the breakout — but here we use the more conservative prior-resistance target.)

If instead price **gaps down through \$19.80 on volume**, that's a **Hole-in-the-Wall** — the setup has failed; you're out and you do *not* re-buy.

<Callout type="key">

Timing is the whole game. Farley wants entries right at the structural inflection — the breakout edge, the retracement level — so the stop can sit close behind structure. A late entry (chasing an already-expanded move) forces a wide stop, wrecks reward:risk, and turns a good setup into a bad trade.

</Callout>

## Patterns are crowd psychology, drawn

Farley repeatedly frames technicals as a picture of mass emotion — unsurprising for a psychology graduate. His blunt formulation of the goal: the trader's purpose is "to take other people's money before they take ours," by recognizing setups one step ahead of the crowd and closing the position before the majority has even caught on.

That cynicism is the point. A pattern works because it maps a *predictable emotional sequence*: fear builds a base, hope launches a breakout, greed extends a rally, complacency forms a top, then denial and panic drive the decline. Support and resistance are simply price levels where the crowd's prior pain or pleasure is concentrated, so traders react there again. Reading the pattern is reading the crowd.

## The Adam Theory critique

In *The Master Swing Trader*, Farley is openly critical of J. Welles Wilder's later **"Adam Theory of Markets"** (1987) — Wilder's claim that markets are essentially symmetrical and that you can forecast future moves by reflecting past price action, discarding the very indicators (RSI and ADX — the momentum and trend-strength gauges — and Parabolic SAR, a trailing-stop tool) that Wilder himself had introduced in 1978 and made famous.

Farley's objection is on principle: there is no reliable symmetry to exploit, and a method that throws away confirmation tools in favor of a single mirror-image forecast contradicts the entire cross-verification philosophy. His stance is honest and worth internalizing — the lesson isn't "Wilder bad," it's that **single-tool, single–time-frame prediction is fragile**; robustness comes from convergence, not from one elegant rule.

## Honest criticisms and pitfalls

- **It resists backtesting.** Pattern Cycles, stage classification, and "convergence" require judgment. By Farley's own framing, the method demands concentration, discipline, and skilled execution — a polite way of saying it's discretionary and hard to verify statistically. Two skilled traders can label the same chart's "stage" differently, and there is no published, backtested win rate or expectancy.
- **Hindsight / curve-fitting bias.** Stages and bells are obvious on historical charts and ambiguous in real time at the hard right edge. A base only becomes a "Bottom" *after* price has left it, and it's easy to "see" perfect setups in the past that weren't tradable when they were forming.
- **It's encyclopedic — almost a fault.** Reviewers note the book tries to pack nearly every short-term-trading concept into one volume, which can overwhelm a true beginner. And much of the underlying content (support/resistance, MAs, Bollinger, Fibonacci) is standard across the trading canon — Farley's real contribution is the *packaging* (Pattern Cycles, the named 7 Bells, the convergence discipline), not brand-new tools.
- **It's a product of its era.** Written for late-1990s/early-2000s equity markets; the 2010 *Toolkit* itself acknowledges decimalization, ECNs, and high-frequency trading changed market microstructure. Behavior of gaps and volume signatures in algorithm-dominated and 24/7 crypto markets may differ — Farley himself argues HFT can distort classic patterns, which somewhat undercuts pure pattern reliance.
- **The efficient-market / fractal challenge.** Scholars would question whether repeatable chart patterns carry a durable, exploitable edge after costs, and pattern-trading literature is prone to survivorship bias — winning examples are easy to cherry-pick while real breakout failure rates are high.

<Callout type="warning">

**Common beginner failures, ranked:**

1. **Trading a bell in isolation** — spotting a Coiled Spring with nothing else agreeing. The whole edge is *multiple* independent signals stacking at one price.
2. **Ignoring the higher time frame** — going long a daily setup while the weekly is in markdown. Conflicting time frames = stand aside.
3. **Chasing expansion** — buying after the move has already fully run instead of at the contraction→expansion transition.
4. **Misjudging the cycle stage** — applying trend tactics in a range (and vice-versa).
5. **Skipping the time stop** — holding a stalled "adverse time" position hoping the price stop saves you.
6. **No predefined stop / poor reward:risk** — entering before defining the invalidation level; demand ~3:1.
7. **Drowning in the encyclopedia** — trying to use all hundreds of the book's concepts at once instead of mastering two or three setups.
8. **Treating patterns as certainties** — they are probabilities; expect a meaningful fraction of breakouts to fail and size accordingly.

</Callout>

## How to apply it today

1. **Top-down, every time.** Start on the highest time frame you trade, classify the stage and trend–range state, then drill down to the setup chart and the trigger chart. Only take setups that agree with the higher frame.
2. **Build the convergence map first.** Mark swing highs/lows, the 200/50/20-day MAs, Fibonacci levels, and Bollinger Bands. Require at least two or three *independent* confirmations stacking at one price before entering.
3. **Name your trade type.** Reaction (buy the dip into a zone) or execution (buy the breakout)? It sets your stop logic.
4. **Compute reward:risk and position size before entry.** If a structural stop can't yield roughly 3:1, pass. Size from a fixed account-risk fraction.
5. **Set a time stop, not just a price stop.** Decide how many bars the setup gets to work; exit on adverse time.
6. **Pick two or three bells and specialize.** Master Dip Trip and Coiled Spring first; trying to trade all seven at once dilutes recognition skill.
7. **Keep a labeled journal.** Tag each trade with stage, bell, and convergence factors. Over time this is the only way to learn whether *your* stage-reading is actually predictive.
