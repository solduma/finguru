## The one-sentence version

Martin Pring's big idea is that momentum and the economic cycle are two sides of the same coin: if you measure how fast prices are changing across *several* time horizons at once, and you know roughly where you are in the boom-and-bust cycle, you can read the market's "bigger picture" instead of getting whipsawed (repeatedly stopped out by quick price reversals) by daily noise.

Most beginners meet momentum through a single indicator like RSI or a lone rate-of-change line, then get frustrated when it screams "overbought" for months during a strong trend. Pring's contribution was to *systematize* momentum: to combine multiple speeds into one cleaner signal (the KST), and to tie all of it back to a repeatable map of the business cycle. He is less a "guru with a secret" and more an educator who organized scattered ideas into a teachable framework. That is exactly why he is worth studying.

His personal mantra, the thing to carry out of this whole lesson: **don't predict — identify the trend and the cycle stage, then go with it, and only act when more than one piece of evidence agrees.**

## Who he was

Martin J. Pring is a British-born technical analyst who has worked in financial markets since the late 1960s. He is best known for *Technical Analysis Explained*, first published around 1980 and now in its **fifth edition (McGraw-Hill, 2014, ISBN 9780071825177)**, subtitled "The Successful Investor's Guide to Spotting Investment Trends and Turning Points." It is widely used as a study text by the **CMT Association** (the body that grants the Chartered Market Technician certification). He has written many other books, including *The Investor's Guide to Active Asset Allocation* (2006), which lays out his six business-cycle stages in detail.

Pring has been an educator first and foremost. He founded **Pring Research, Inc.**, publisher of the monthly *InterMarket Review* (which has run for 35+ years), and runs **PringU**, a 20-lesson interactive technical-analysis course. He co-founded the money-management firm **Pring Turner Capital Group** with Joe Turner, built around his business-cycle framework. In **2012**, Dow Jones Indexes back-tested his approach to 1955 and launched the **Dow Jones Pring Business Cycle Index** — described as its first proactively managed business-cycle index.

<Callout type="warning">

There are two "Martin Prings." The famous Wikipedia entry under that name is a 17th-century English explorer (Martin Pring, 1580–1626), **not** the technical analyst. If you go researching, look for Pring Research, *Technical Analysis Explained*, or the KST — not the Wikipedia biography.

</Callout>

<Callout type="note">

"Technical analysis" means studying price and volume history (charts) to judge probable future direction, as opposed to "fundamental analysis," which studies a company's earnings, assets, and the broader economy. Pring used both, but built his reputation on the technical side.

</Callout>

## The core idea: momentum measured properly

**Momentum** in technical analysis means the *rate* at which price is changing, not the price level itself. The simplest measure is **rate of change (ROC)**, the percentage difference between today's price and the price N bars ago:

```
ROC(n) = ((Close − Close_n_bars_ago) / Close_n_bars_ago) × 100
```

A rising ROC says the trend is accelerating; a falling ROC says it is losing steam even if price is still climbing. That early warning — momentum often peaks *before* price — is the whole point of studying it.

<Callout type="key">

**Worked ROC example.** A stock closed at **$50** twelve days ago and closes at **$55** today.
`ROC(12) = ((55 − 50) / 50) × 100 = +10%`.
A common rule of thumb treats roughly **+10 as overbought and −10 as oversold** (tune wider, ±15, for volatile names and tighter, ±5, for calm ones). So +10% says momentum is strong but *stretched* — a reason to look for price confirmation, not to chase. Remember: a market can stay oversold while it keeps falling. ROC extremes are warnings, never automatic buy/sell buttons.

</Callout>

The problem: any single ROC has a personality. A 10-day ROC is jumpy and full of false alarms; a 30-day ROC is smooth but slow. Pick the wrong length and you either get whipsawed or arrive late. Pring's insight was simple but powerful: **don't pick one, blend several.**

### RSI sits alongside ROC

Pring also teaches **RSI** (Wilder's Relative Strength Index) next to ROC. RSI is bounded 0–100: above 70 is "overbought," below 30 "oversold," with the most useful read being *divergence* (price makes a new high, RSI doesn't). Pring's caution is identical to his ROC caution: in a strong trend RSI can stay "overbought" or "oversold" for a long time, so use it for divergence and confirmation, **not** as a standalone trigger. (For the full mechanics, see the RSI lesson — Pring treats it as one vote among several.)

## The KST (Know Sure Thing)

The **KST** ("Know Sure Thing," a playful name) is a momentum oscillator that combines four smoothed rate-of-change calculations of different lengths into a single weighted line. Longer-term cycles get more weight, because Pring believed the dominant trend deserves the loudest vote.

Each component (Pring calls it an RCMA, short for Rate-of-Change Moving Average — an ROC that has been smoothed by a moving average) is an ROC, smoothed with a simple moving average (SMA — the plain average of the last N values), then multiplied by a weight:

| Component | ROC period | Smoothing SMA | Weight |
|-----------|-----------|---------------|--------|
| RCMA1 | 10 | 10 | 1 |
| RCMA2 | 15 | 10 | 2 |
| RCMA3 | 20 | 10 | 3 |
| RCMA4 | 30 | 15 | 4 |

```
KST = (RCMA1 × 1) + (RCMA2 × 2) + (RCMA3 × 3) + (RCMA4 × 4)
Signal line = 9-period SMA of KST
```

The shorthand for these daily defaults is **KST(10,15,20,30, 10,10,10,15, 9)**: four ROC lengths, four smoothing lengths, one signal length. Pring published different settings per horizon — for example a medium-term weekly **KST(10,13,15,20, 10,13,15,20, 9)** and a long-term monthly **KST(9,12,18,24, 6,6,6,9, 9)** (the monthly one is tied to the roughly four-year business cycle). Crucially, he changed the *settings*, not just the chart interval, when switching horizons.

<Callout type="key">

The KST is really four ROC opinions averaged into one. Short cycles make it responsive; the heavily weighted long cycle keeps it honest about the primary trend. That blend is why it produces fewer false reversals than a lone ROC line.

</Callout>

### Why the long cycle dominates: the 1-2-3-4 weighting

The weights aren't decoration — they decide whose vote counts. Even if all four RCMAs read the *same* raw value, RCMA4 contributes four times as much as RCMA1. To make this concrete, suppose (illustrative numbers) the four smoothed ROC components on a given day are RCMA1 = +0.5, RCMA2 = +0.8, RCMA3 = +1.2, and RCMA4 = +1.5. After weighting, their contributions to the KST are:

KST = 0.5 + 1.6 + 3.6 + 6.0 = **+11.7**. Notice the longest component starts only marginally larger than the shortest (+1.5 vs +0.5 raw), yet after weighting it towers over everything else. Even when the short-term piece is modest, the heavily weighted long cycle drags the KST solidly positive — meaning the broader momentum picture is bullish. That is the whole design philosophy in one chart: *the primary trend gets the loudest vote.*

### A second worked KST example: reading the turn

Numbers can also warn you *before* a cross. Suppose at a month-end the four monthly components are RCMA1 = +2.0, RCMA2 = +1.5, RCMA3 = −0.5, RCMA4 = +3.0:

```
KST = (2.0 × 1) + (1.5 × 2) + (−0.5 × 3) + (3.0 × 4)
    = 2.0 + 3.0 − 1.5 + 12.0
    = +15.5
```

KST is positive (bull bias) and dominated by that heavily weighted +12.0 long-term component. If last month's KST was +12.0, the rising 9-period signal line is below the current +15.5 — so KST is **above its signal line and rising → buy bias confirmed.** But watch what happens *next*: if KST then prints +18, +17, +14 while still above its signal line, that fading sequence warns upside momentum is *running out* — a caution flag. It does not become a sell until KST actually crosses below the signal line **and** price confirms by breaking a trendline or moving average.

### How to read the KST

- **Centerline:** above zero favors bulls, below zero favors bears.
- **Signal-line crossovers:** the most robust signal. KST crossing *above* its 9-period average is bullish; crossing below is bearish.
- **Direction vs. position:** a KST that is still negative but turning up shows downside momentum waning; a KST still positive but turning down shows upside momentum fading.
- **Trendline breaks on the KST itself:** rare but powerful, and they reinforce a crossover.
- **Divergence:** when price makes a new high but KST does not (a "negative divergence"), it can warn of exhaustion — but Pring stressed using only large, blatant divergences, ideally confirmed by a signal-line cross.

<Callout type="warning">

The KST is *unbounded* — it has no fixed 0-to-100 ceiling like RSI. That makes it poorly suited for "overbought/oversold" calls. In a strong uptrend it can stay positive for a very long time; a signal-line cross there often just flags slowing momentum, not a reversal. Use signal-line and zero-line crossovers and trendline breaks — never treat a "high" KST as a sell.

</Callout>

## Special K: momentum across the whole cycle

Pring later built **Special K**, which extends the KST idea to *twelve* ROC components spanning short-, intermediate-, and long-term cycles, again grouped in fours with weights 1-2-3-4 *within* each group. The structure is the KST's idea nested three times — three speed bands, each a little 1-2-3-4 ladder:

The design assumes prices revolve around a roughly **four-year business cycle**, so Special K aims to peak and trough at the same time as the primary price trend. It needs a lot of history — at least about 725 data points — to compute fully. Read it with trendline breaks (a violated trendline of nine months or more suggests the primary trend has reversed) and with a long, double-smoothed signal line (the common default is a 100-period SMA smoothed by another 100-period SMA). Note its honest limitations: in a long linear bull market it tends to *lead* turning points, and around sudden, sharply truncated moves like the 1987 crash (where the decline was largely over almost before momentum could turn down), it can arrive *late*.

## The bigger picture: the six business-cycle stages

Pring's other great theme is **intermarket rotation** — the observation that bonds, stocks, and commodities take turns leading the economy in a repeating relay. In a classic inflationary regime the sequence is: **bonds turn first, then stocks, then commodities** (and they weaken in the same order). Pring packaged this into six stages that loop endlessly — the economy never "finishes" the cycle, it just rolls into the next turn.

<Callout type="key">

Bonds bottom first and top first; commodities bottom last and top last; stocks sit in the middle. Knowing which asset is "supposed" to be leading tells you which stage you are likely in — and which sectors tend to outperform there. *Defensive* sectors (utilities, consumer staples, healthcare — things people buy in any economy) hold up best early in a recovery and during slowdowns; *cyclical* sectors (industrials, materials, consumer discretionary — sensitive to the business cycle) lead when the expansion is in full swing.

</Callout>

### Offense vs. defense: the allocation playbook

The reason the cycle matters to a real portfolio is that it tells you when to play **offense** (lean into stocks) and when to play **defense** (lean into bonds and cash):

- **Stage 1 (recession):** defensive — heavy bonds and cash to stabilize while stocks are still falling.
- **Stages 2–3 (accelerating economy):** offensive — maximum stock exposure; this is where the easy money is.
- **Stages 4–5 (overheating / late cycle):** progressively de-risk; commodities and inflation hedges can lead here.
- **Stage 6 (rolling into recession):** rotate back toward cash and bonds as the cycle hands off to the next Stage 1.

<Callout type="warning">

**Move gradually.** Cycles can skip a stage or "retrograde" back to a previous one, and the current stage is genuinely ambiguous in real time. Make a big allocation shift only when the evidence is overwhelming *and* the move isn't already priced in. Slow rotation beats abrupt all-or-nothing switches.

</Callout>

This is where momentum and macro connect: Pring applied his KST and Special K tools to bond, stock, and commodity indexes to gauge each market's primary trend, then read those turns against the six-stage map to estimate where in the cycle the economy sits.

## Trend confirmation: the discipline that ties it all together

Everything above feeds one rule, and it is the single most important thing to take from Pring: **never act on one indicator alone.** He called it the **"weight of the evidence"** approach. A momentum reversal becomes *actionable* only when it lines up across three independent layers:

In practice: a KST signal-line cross by itself is a *candidate*, not a trade. You wait for price to break a trendline or a moving average too, and you check that the cycle stage isn't fighting you. The discipline is dull on purpose — it filters out the lone-indicator whipsaws that cost beginners the most money.

## A worked example: walking the cycle relay

Imagine bond prices have been falling for two years, then their long-term KST turns up through its signal line. Bonds bottoming is the Stage 1 signal. A few months later the stock-index KST also crosses up: Stage 2, recovery is being anticipated. Later still, a commodity-index KST turns positive: Stage 3, broad expansion underway. A disciplined Pring follower would have rotated from cash and defensives toward cyclical stocks somewhere around Stages 2–3, then watched for bonds to weaken (Stage 4) as the first hint the expansion is maturing.

This isn't only theory. A real instance from Pring Turner's own commentary: in the 2018–19 cycle, **bonds bottomed in October 2018 (Stage 1)**, **stocks bottomed in December 2018 (Stage 2)**, and the next expected event was a **low in commodities (Stage 3)** — the bonds→stocks→commodities relay playing out in real time. None of these signals is exact, but the *order* gives a coherent storyline rather than isolated guesses.

## Honest criticisms

- **Cycle stages are clear only in hindsight.** Real cycles skip stages, retrograde, or stall, and the "current stage" is genuinely ambiguous live — Pring himself warns to move gradually.
- **Intermarket relationships are regime-dependent.** Bond/stock/commodity correlations flipped between the inflationary era (1970s–late 1990s, when bonds and stocks were positively correlated) and the deflationary era after about 1998 (when they often moved inversely). Central-bank intervention — QE, zero-rate policy — has repeatedly broken the textbook rotation.
- **The KST is curve-fit and parameter-heavy.** Nine parameters invite over-optimization; different timeframe settings can give conflicting signals; and because it's unbounded it gives no clean overbought/oversold read.
- **Momentum divergences fail often.** Both StockCharts and Pring note divergences fail to foreshadow reversals more often than not — they need price confirmation.
- **Lag is baked in.** KST is a smoothed average of averages, so it lags turns; in choppy markets signal-line crossovers whipsaw. Special K openly leads in some regimes and lags in others.
- **The broad academic critique of technical analysis** (the efficient-market view) holds that much of TA's edge is hard to demonstrate out-of-sample after trading costs.

## Common pitfalls and misconceptions

- **Trading a single indicator alone.** Pring's whole philosophy is weight of the evidence; acting on a KST cross without price *and* cycle confirmation is the number-one error.
- **Treating overbought/oversold as an automatic sell/buy.** Markets stay overbought in strong trends; ROC and RSI extremes need price confirmation.
- **Using the KST as an overbought/oversold gauge.** It is unbounded — use signal-line and zero-line crossovers and trendline breaks instead.
- **Mixing timeframes.** Applying daily KST settings to monthly cycle decisions (or vice versa). Match the *settings* to your horizon, not just the chart interval.
- **Forcing the current cycle stage.** Declaring "we're in Stage 3" prematurely; stages are confirmed by the three barometers (bonds, stocks, commodities) together, not by one chart.
- **Over-trusting divergences.** Chase only the large, blatant ones, and wait for a confirming cross.
- **Big, abrupt allocation switches.** Pring says shift gradually; cycles can retrograde.
- **Confusing the two Martin Prings** — the famous Wikipedia entry is a 17th-century explorer, not the analyst.

## How to apply it today

1. Plot KST on three horizons (daily, weekly, monthly) — with the *settings* matched to each — and trade in the direction of the longer ones.
2. Use signal-line crossovers as your primary trigger; act on divergences only when they are large and obvious.
3. Run the same KST on bond, stock, and commodity benchmarks to locate the cycle stage, then tilt toward offense or defense accordingly — gradually.
4. Apply the weight-of-the-evidence gate: never act until price, momentum, and the cycle backdrop agree. Combine KST with trend tools (moving averages, price patterns) and never bet on momentum alone.
