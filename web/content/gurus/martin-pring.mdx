## The one-sentence version

Martin Pring's big idea is that momentum and the economic cycle are two sides of the same coin: if you measure how fast prices are changing across *several* time horizons at once, and you know roughly where you are in the boom-and-bust cycle, you can read the market's "bigger picture" instead of getting whipsawed by daily noise.

Most beginners meet momentum through a single indicator like RSI or a lone rate-of-change line, then get frustrated when it screams "overbought" for months during a strong trend. Pring's contribution was to *systematize* momentum: to combine multiple speeds into one cleaner signal (the KST), and to tie all of it back to a repeatable map of the business cycle. He is less a "guru with a secret" and more an educator who organized scattered ideas into a teachable framework. That is exactly why he is worth studying.

## Who he was

Martin J. Pring is a British-born technical analyst who began working in financial markets in **1969**. He is best known for *Technical Analysis Explained*, first published in **1979** and now in its fifth edition; *Forbes* called it "the standard work for this generation of chartists," and *Barron's* described Pring himself as "a technician's technician." He has written many other books, including *Investment Psychology Explained*, *Martin Pring on Price Patterns*, and a multi-volume course on market momentum.

Pring has been an educator first and foremost. Since **1984** he has published the monthly *InterMarket Review*; he pioneered technical-analysis instructional videos in **1987**; and he has lectured everywhere from the Darden Business School to SDA Bocconi. He received the Market Technicians Association (now CMT Association) Annual Award in **2004** and was inducted into the Traders' Library Hall of Fame in **2007**. In **2012** he co-created the Dow Jones Pring U.S. Business Cycle Index with S&P Dow Jones Indices, and since **2015** he has served as Chief Technical Strategist for StockCharts.com.

<Callout type="note">

"Technical analysis" means studying price and volume history (charts) to judge probable future direction, as opposed to "fundamental analysis," which studies a company's earnings, assets, and the broader economy. Pring used both, but built his reputation on the technical side.

</Callout>

## The core idea: momentum measured properly

**Momentum** in technical analysis means the *rate* at which price is changing, not the price level itself. The simplest measure is **rate of change (ROC)**, the percentage difference between today's price and the price N bars ago:

```
ROC(n) = ((Price_today / Price_n_bars_ago) - 1) x 100
```

A rising ROC says the trend is accelerating; a falling ROC says it is losing steam even if price is still climbing. That early warning — momentum often peaks *before* price — is the whole point of studying it.

The problem: any single ROC has a personality. A 10-day ROC is jumpy and full of false alarms; a 30-day ROC is smooth but slow. Pick the wrong length and you either get whipsawed or arrive late. Pring's insight was simple but powerful: **don't pick one, blend several.**

## The KST (Know Sure Thing)

The **KST** ("Know Sure Thing," a playful name) is a momentum oscillator that combines four smoothed rate-of-change calculations of different lengths into a single weighted line. Longer-term cycles get more weight, because Pring believed the dominant trend deserves the loudest vote.

Each component is an ROC, smoothed with a simple moving average (SMA — the plain average of the last N values), then multiplied by a weight:

| Component | ROC period | Smoothing SMA | Weight |
|-----------|-----------|---------------|--------|
| RCMA1 | 10 | 10 | 1 |
| RCMA2 | 15 | 10 | 2 |
| RCMA3 | 20 | 10 | 3 |
| RCMA4 | 30 | 15 | 4 |

```
KST = (RCMA1 x 1) + (RCMA2 x 2) + (RCMA3 x 3) + (RCMA4 x 4)
Signal line = 9-period SMA of KST
```

The shorthand for these daily defaults is **KST(10,15,20,30, 10,10,10,15, 9)**: four ROC lengths, four smoothing lengths, one signal length. Pring published different settings per horizon — for example a medium-term weekly **KST(10,13,15,20, 10,13,15,20, 9)** and a long-term monthly **KST(9,12,18,24, 6,6,6,9, 9)**. Crucially, he changed the *settings*, not just the chart interval, when switching horizons.

<Callout type="key">

The KST is really four ROC opinions averaged into one. Short cycles make it responsive; the heavily weighted long cycle keeps it honest about the primary trend. That blend is why it produces fewer false reversals than a lone ROC line.

</Callout>

### A small illustrative calculation

Suppose (illustrative numbers) the four smoothed ROC components on a given day are:

| Component | Value | Weight | Contribution |
|-----------|------:|-------:|-------------:|
| RCMA1 | +0.5 | 1 | +0.5 |
| RCMA2 | +0.8 | 2 | +1.6 |
| RCMA3 | +1.2 | 3 | +3.6 |
| RCMA4 | +1.5 | 4 | +6.0 |

KST = 0.5 + 1.6 + 3.6 + 6.0 = **+11.7**. The longest, most heavily weighted component (+6.0) dominates, so even though the short-term piece is modest, the KST registers solidly positive — meaning the broader momentum picture is bullish.

### How to read the KST

- **Centerline:** above zero favors bulls, below zero favors bears.
- **Signal-line crossovers:** the most robust signals. KST crossing *above* its 9-period average is bullish; crossing below is bearish.
- **Direction vs. position:** a KST that is still negative but turning up shows downside momentum waning; a KST still positive but turning down shows upside momentum fading.
- **Divergence:** when price makes a new high but KST does not (a "negative divergence"), it can warn of exhaustion — but Pring stressed using only large, obvious divergences, ideally confirmed by a signal-line cross.

<Callout type="warning">

The KST is *unbounded* — it has no fixed 0-to-100 ceiling like RSI. That makes it poorly suited for "overbought/oversold" calls. In a strong uptrend it can stay positive for a very long time; a signal-line cross there often just flags slowing momentum, not a reversal.

</Callout>

## Special K: momentum across the whole cycle

Pring later built **Special K**, which extends the KST idea to *twelve* ROC components spanning short-, intermediate-, and long-term cycles, again grouped in fours with weights 1-2-3-4 within each group:

- Short-term: ROC(10, 15, 20, 30)
- Intermediate: ROC(40, 65, 75, 100)
- Long-term: ROC(195, 265, 390, 530)

The design assumes prices revolve around a roughly **four-year business cycle**, so Special K aims to peak and trough at the same time as the primary price trend. It needs a lot of history — at least about 725 data points — to compute fully. Read it with trendline breaks (a violated trendline of nine months or more suggests the primary trend has reversed) and with a long, double-smoothed signal line (the common default is a 100-period SMA smoothed by another 100-period SMA). Note its honest limitations: in a long linear bull market it tends to *lead* turning points, and around truncated events like the 1987 crash it can arrive *late*.

## The bigger picture: the six business-cycle stages

Pring's other great theme is **intermarket rotation** — the observation that bonds, stocks, and commodities take turns leading the economy in a repeating relay. In a classic inflationary regime the sequence is: **bonds turn first, then stocks, then commodities** (and they weaken in the same order). Pring packaged this into six stages.

| Stage | Bonds | Stocks | Commodities | Roughly where |
|-------|-------|--------|-------------|---------------|
| 1 | Bull | Bear | Bear | Deep contraction; rates falling, bonds bottom first |
| 2 | Bull | Bull | Bear | Stocks bottom, anticipating recovery |
| 3 | Bull | Bull | Bull | Conditions improve; commodities join as demand returns |
| 4 | Bear | Bull | Bull | Full expansion; bonds top first as inflation pressures rise |
| 5 | Bear | Bear | Bull | Stocks top; commodities still strong |
| 6 | Bear | Bear | Bear | Slowdown; commodities finally roll over |

<Callout type="key">

Bonds bottom first and top first; commodities bottom last and top last; stocks sit in the middle. Knowing which asset is "supposed" to be leading tells you which stage you are likely in — and which sectors (defensive vs. cyclical) tend to outperform there.

</Callout>

This is where momentum and macro connect: Pring applied his KST and Special K tools to bond, stock, and commodity indexes to gauge each market's primary trend, then read those turns against the six-stage map to estimate where in the cycle the economy sits.

## A worked example (illustrative)

Imagine bond prices have been falling for two years, then their long-term KST turns up through its signal line. Bonds bottoming is the Stage 1 signal. A few months later the stock-index KST also crosses up: Stage 2, recovery is being anticipated. Later still, a commodity-index KST turns positive: Stage 3, broad expansion underway. A disciplined Pring follower would have rotated from cash and defensives toward cyclical stocks somewhere around Stages 2-3, then watched for bonds to weaken (Stage 4) as the first hint the expansion is maturing. None of these signals is exact, but the *order* gives a coherent storyline rather than isolated guesses.

## Honest criticisms

- **Curve-fitting risk.** The KST and Special K periods and weights were chosen from years of market observation. Numbers tuned on past data can look better in hindsight than they perform live.
- **Lag is baked in.** Smoothing buys cleaner signals at the cost of timeliness. Special K openly leads in some regimes and lags in others.
- **The cycle is not a clock.** The six-stage model assumes a recognizable four-year-ish cycle and stable intermarket relationships. Those relationships shifted in the deflationary period after about 1998 — when stocks and bonds decoupled and often began moving inversely — and central-bank intervention can stretch or compress stages.
- **Complexity.** Twelve ROCs and a six-box matrix can create false confidence and over-fitting in your own head.

## Common pitfalls and misconceptions

- Treating KST as overbought/oversold like RSI. It is unbounded; do not.
- Acting on every signal-line cross in a strong trend. Many are momentum hiccups, not reversals.
- Forcing today's market into a stage. Stages overlap and sometimes skip; use them as a hypothesis, not a horoscope.
- Ignoring confirmation. Pring himself preached a "weight of the evidence" approach — no single indicator decides.

## How to apply it today

1. Plot KST on three horizons (daily, weekly, monthly) and trade in the direction of the longer ones.
2. Use signal-line crossovers as your primary trigger; act on divergences only when they are large and obvious.
3. Run the same KST on bond, stock, and commodity benchmarks to locate the cycle stage.
4. Combine with trend tools (moving averages, price patterns) and never bet on momentum alone.
