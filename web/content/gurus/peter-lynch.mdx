## The investor who beat Wall Street with the local mall

Most legendary investors tell you the market is too hard for amateurs. **Peter
Lynch said the opposite.** He argued that an ordinary person — someone who shops,
works, and lives in the real world — *sees* great businesses growing before the
analysts on Wall Street ever notice. The trick is knowing what to do with that
head start.

From **1977 to 1990**, Lynch ran Fidelity's **Magellan Fund** and earned roughly
**29% per year** — about double the market — turning a sleepy ~$18 million fund
into a ~$14 billion giant and the best-performing mutual fund in the world. Then,
at 46, he walked away to spend time with his family.

His method has a name — **GARP, "Growth At a Reasonable Price"** — and two
signature tools: the **PEG ratio** (a price check) and the **two-minute drill** (a
story check). By the end of this lesson you'll be able to apply both.

<Callout type="key">
Lynch's whole philosophy fits in one breath: **find a genuinely growing company
you understand, then buy it only if the price is sensible relative to that
growth.** Not growth at any price (that's how you overpay). Not cheapness for its
own sake (that's how you buy dying businesses). Growth *and* a fair price.
</Callout>

## Who he was

**Peter Lynch** was born January 19, 1944, in Newton, Massachusetts. He studied
**history, psychology, and philosophy** at Boston College — pointedly *not*
finance, which he later said was an advantage, because markets are driven as much
by human behavior as by spreadsheets. He earned an MBA from **Wharton** in 1968.

He got his foot in the door at **Fidelity** by caddying for the firm's president
at a country club, landing an internship in 1966 and a permanent job in 1969. He
served as Fidelity's **director of research** from 1974 to 1977, then took over
the small **Magellan Fund in May 1977.**

Over the next ~13 years Magellan returned about **29.2% annualized** versus roughly
**15.8%** for the S&P 500 — a staggering margin sustained over more than a decade.
At its peak Lynch held an enormous portfolio, reported at around **1,400 stocks**
by 1989, because he could research them full-time. He retired in 1990 and became a
part-time Fidelity vice chairman and a major philanthropist.

## The two famous slogans (and how they're misread)

### "Invest in what you know"

Everyday life is a research lab. You notice which store is always packed, which
product your kids won't stop asking for, which tool makes your job easier. Lynch's
favorite examples were **Dunkin' Donuts** and **McDonald's** — businesses anyone
could watch growing from the parking lot.

<Callout type="warning">
This is the **most misunderstood idea in all of investing.** "I like the product,
so I'll buy the stock" is **not** Lynch's advice — it's the trap he warned against.
The product is only a **lead**, a reason to start digging. Lynch was emphatic: the
observation is step *one*, never the buy signal. The homework (earnings,
valuation, debt) still has to be done. Lynch liked to admit his *own* failures
here — he noticed Apple's growth through his daughter's iPod but never bought it.
*Noticing is not a strategy.*
</Callout>

### "Ten-baggers"

A **ten-bagger** is a stock that rises to **10× your purchase price** — the term
comes from baseball (ten bases). Lynch's insight: in a portfolio of many stocks,
you don't need to be right often. **One or two ten-baggers can carry the entire
portfolio**, even if several other picks go nowhere. The math is asymmetric — a
loser can only fall 100%, but a winner can rise 1,000% or more. That's why he
hunted for big winners and held them patiently rather than selling after a quick
30% gain.

## The full method, in concrete steps

Lynch's process is sequential. A beginner can literally walk down this list.

<ConceptDiagram

  chart={`flowchart LR

  B --> C[Do the homework: earnings, debt, inventories]

### Step 1 — Notice candidates from real life

Spot a product, store, or service that is clearly good *and* growing. Write it
down as a candidate. Do **not** buy yet.

### Step 2 — Classify the company into one of six categories

Lynch sorted every stock into six buckets, because what you expect — and when you
sell — differs completely by type.

| Category | What it is | Why you'd own it |
|----------|-----------|------------------|
| **Slow growers** | Large, mature, growing ~2–4%/yr | Steady dividends (income) |
| **Stalwarts** | Big firms still growing ~10–12%/yr | Defense; sell after a moderate gain |
| **Fast growers** | Small, aggressive, ~20–25%+ growth | **Lynch's favorites** — the ten-baggers (and the riskiest) |
| **Cyclicals** | Earnings rise/fall with the economy (autos, airlines, steel) | Profit by timing the economic cycle |
| **Turnarounds** | Beaten-down firms that may recover | High risk/reward; uncorrelated with the market |
| **Asset plays** | Sitting on undervalued assets (real estate, cash, patents) | The market is ignoring hidden value |

<Callout type="note">
The category dictates the analysis. A **fast grower** lives or dies on its growth
rate. A **cyclical** lives or dies on where you are in the economic cycle. An
**asset play** is about the balance sheet, not earnings growth at all. Using one
checklist for all six is a common mistake (see pitfalls below).
</Callout>

It helps to *see* the six categories laid out by how fast they grow and how much
risk they carry — because that map is exactly why each demands different analysis.

### Step 3 — Do your homework

Read the actual financial statements. Lynch focused on the **business**, not on
predicting interest rates or the economy. His core checks:

- **Is the earnings growth real and sustainable?** (Not a one-time fluke.)
- **What is the P/E** versus the company's own history and its peers? (P/E =
  price-to-earnings, how many dollars you pay per $1 of annual profit.)
- **How much debt?** Lynch favored **low debt** — a debt-light company survives bad
  years.
- **Cash position** — money in the bank is a cushion.
- **Inventories** (for retailers/manufacturers) — piling-up inventory is a warning
  that products aren't selling.
- **Insider buying and buybacks** are positives — when the people who know the
  company best are putting their own money in, take note.

<Callout type="note">
Lynch's taste in companies was deliberately unglamorous: he *preferred* **boring,
simple, even ugly** businesses in dull industries — a funeral-services firm, a
bottle-cap maker — because they're under-followed and cheap. He was wary of "hot"
stocks and anything sold as "the next [famous company]." Excitement, in Lynch's
world, is usually already priced in.
</Callout>

### Step 4 — Apply the valuation discipline: the PEG ratio

This is the **heart of GARP**. The problem with judging a stock by P/E alone:
a P/E of 20 is *expensive* for a company growing 8% a year, but *cheap* for one
growing 25%. The **PEG ratio** fixes this by dividing the P/E by the growth rate.

```
PEG = (P/E ratio) ÷ (annual EPS growth rate, in %)
```

Lynch's rule of thumb, from *One Up on Wall Street*: *"The P/E ratio of any
company that's fairly priced will equal its growth rate."* In PEG terms:

- **PEG = 1** → fairly priced (P/E matches growth).
- **PEG `< 1`** → attractive — you're paying *less* than the growth justifies (≈0.5 is very cheap).
- **PEG `> 1`** → expensive — you're paying *more* than the growth justifies (≈2 is poor).

<Callout type="note">
**The dividend-adjusted PEG.** In *One Up on Wall Street* Lynch refines the rule
to credit a company for paying you cash while you wait, combining growth and
dividend yield against the P/E. Written in the same "lower is better" form as
above:

**PEG = P/E ÷ (EPS growth rate + dividend yield).**

A 3% dividend is added to the growth rate, so it effectively boosts the "return"
you're being paid for and lowers the PEG. (Lynch's own phrasing inverts this —
"add the growth rate to the dividend yield and divide by the P/E," where *higher*
is better and anything above 1.5 is good — but it is the identical idea, just
flipped.)
</Callout>

### Step 5 — Pass the two-minute drill

This is Lynch's final, almost foolproof gate — and the step beginners skip most.
Before you buy a single share, you should be able to explain the stock's **story
out loud, in plain English, in about two minutes.** No jargon, no charts. The
story has three parts:

<ConceptDiagram
  caption="The two-minute drill: a three-part story you must be able to tell before buying. If you can't fill all three boxes in plain language, you don't understand the company well enough to own it — and 'the price keeps going up' is NOT a valid answer to box A."
  chart={`flowchart TB
  A["A. Why am I buying it?<br/>(what makes the company attractive)"] --> B["B. What has to happen for it to win?<br/>(the growth driver / the story)"]
  B --> C["C. What could derail it?<br/>(the risks and obstacles)"]
  C --> D{Can you tell all three<br/>in two minutes?}
  D -->|Yes| E[You understand it — proceed]
  D -->|No| F[You don't — do more homework or pass]`}
/>

The drill is brutally effective because it exposes the most dangerous reason to
buy: *"the price is going up."* If that's your only story, you fail the drill. A
genuine Lynch story sounds like: *"This is a fast grower at a PEG of 0.8. It's
opening 200 new stores a year and is only in 15 states, so it has years of runway.
The risk is that expansion outpaces management or a recession hits discretionary
spending."* That's a story you can monitor — and **monitoring the story** is how
Lynch decided when to sell.

### Step 6 — Match the buy/sell rule to the category, then hold

Lynch was not a market-timer; he judged each holding by whether its *story is
still intact* — is the company still in an early "inning of the ball game"? Sell
when the **story breaks**, not when the market dips.

- **Stalwarts:** trim after a moderate gain (say 30–50%) and rotate into the next one.
- **Fast growers:** hold as long as the **growth story and the PEG both stay
  intact.** Sell when growth slows or the PEG balloons.
- **Cyclicals:** buy early in an economic recovery, sell as the cycle peaks.
- **Asset plays / turnarounds:** sell when the hidden value is finally recognized
  or the recovery has played out.

<Callout type="key">
**Don't "pull the flowers and water the weeds."** Lynch's vivid warning against
selling your winners to prop up your losers — the exact opposite of what works. A
loser can only cost you 100%; a winner left to run can return many times your
money. His other rule worth taping to your monitor: *"If you need the money in 1 or
2 years, you shouldn't be buying stocks."* Stocks reward patience; they punish
people who must sell on a fixed date.
</Callout>

## A worked example: the PEG test and the drill in action

*(Illustrative numbers, built straight from the formula.)*

You notice a popular, fast-growing coffee chain — call it **BrewCo** — packed every
morning. That's your *lead*. Now do the homework.

- Share price = **$40**; trailing EPS (earnings per share) = **$2.00**.
- **P/E = 40 ÷ 2 = 20.**
- Its history and analysts suggest sustainable EPS growth of about **25%/yr.**
- **PEG = 20 ÷ 25 = 0.80.** Since **0.80 `< 1`**, BrewCo is *reasonably priced for
  its growth* — a genuine GARP candidate.

Now compare **SteadyCorp**, a big mature stalwart, at the **same P/E of 20:**

- Price = $50, EPS = $2.50 → **P/E = 20**, but growth is only **8%/yr.**
- **PEG = 20 ÷ 8 = 2.5 → overpriced.** Same P/E, opposite verdict — *that's the
  power of PEG.*
- **Dividend twist:** if SteadyCorp also pays a **3% dividend**, the adjusted PEG =
  20 ÷ (8 + 3) = **1.8** — still expensive, but the dividend improves the picture.

**Now the two-minute drill for BrewCo:** *"It's a fast grower at a PEG of 0.8 —
cheap for 25% growth. It's only in 15 states and opening stores fast, so the runway
is long. The risks: a saturation point as it fills the map, and a recession denting
a $5 daily coffee habit. I'll hold while same-store growth stays strong and sell if
that story breaks."* That passes — there's an attraction, a driver, and a named
risk.

And the upside math that made Lynch patient: buy BrewCo at $40; if it grows into a
**ten-bagger** it reaches **$400** — a 900% gain. Even if four other $1,000
positions each *halved* (losing $2,000 in total), this single ten-bagger (+$9,000)
leaves the five-stock basket up **$7,000 on $5,000 invested.** That asymmetry — one
winner outweighing several losers — is exactly why Lynch tolerated owning hundreds
of names.

## The key books

- **One Up on Wall Street** (1989, with John Rothchild) — the foundational text:
  invest in what you know, the six categories, the two-minute drill, the PEG rule.
  Sold over a million copies. **Start here.**
- **Beating the Street** (1993, with John Rothchild) — case studies of his actual
  stock-picking process, with more on PEG and practical rules for amateurs.
- **Learn to Earn** (1995, with John Rothchild) — investing basics for absolute
  beginners.

<Callout type="note">
Lynch **popularized** the PEG ratio; he did not invent it. The PEG was first
described by **Mario Farina** in *A Beginner's Guide to Successful Investing in the
Stock Market* (1969). Lynch made it famous.
</Callout>

## Honest criticisms and limitations

Be skeptical — Lynch would want you to be.

- **His record may not be replicable.** Magellan's 29% run came with structural
  advantages: a small asset base early on, a favorable 1980s bull market, a
  far less-efficient market with much less competition for small-cap information,
  Fidelity's full-time research firepower, and the freedom to hold ~1,400 names. A
  retail investor cannot match that breadth or information flow.
- **A good company is not the same as a good stock.** This is the deepest critique
  of "invest in what you know": a wonderful business bought at a high PEG is still a
  bad investment. Lynch's *own* Apple miss — he saw the iPod and didn't buy —
  underscores that the slogan, taken alone, makes people skip the very homework that
  makes it work.
- **The "investors underperformed the fund" cautionary tale.** It's widely repeated
  that the *average dollar* in Magellan earned far less than the fund's 29%, because
  people piled in after run-ups and bailed in dips. Treat this as a *widely-cited
  illustration of bad investor timing*, not a hard, sourced statistic — the specific
  numbers are not well documented. The lesson (don't chase performance) is sound
  regardless.
- **PEG is a blunt tool.** It depends entirely on a **forecast growth rate**, which
  is uncertain. It breaks down for slow/no-growth, cyclical, and financial
  companies, ignores debt and capital intensity unless you check them separately,
  and is meaningless when earnings are negative.
- **GARP lags at extremes.** In a "growth-at-any-price" mania it trails pure growth;
  in a deep value rally it can trail value. GARP is a middle path — it rarely wins
  the sprint, which is the price of consistency.

## Legacy: GARP today

Lynch's discipline outlived his Magellan years — and you no longer have to build a
GARP portfolio stock by stock to use it. The same idea has been **productized as
smart-beta**, a rules-based index that mechanically screens for Lynch-style stocks.
The best-known is the **S&P 500 GARP Index** (launched in 2019, roughly 75
constituents), which ranks the S&P 500 on **growth, quality, and value** signals
and holds only the names that score well on all three — exactly the "real growth at
a reasonable price" recipe, automated. ("Quality" here means strong, low-debt,
profitable businesses — the same balance-sheet checks Lynch did by hand.)

<Callout type="key">
The practical takeaway for a beginner: you can **buy a GARP strategy off the shelf**
through a low-cost ETF that tracks the S&P 500 GARP Index, rather than picking
every stock yourself. That gives you Lynch's *framework* with index-level
diversification and no full-time research job — a sensible middle ground between
copying his 1,400-name portfolio and giving up on stock-picking entirely. (Exact
constituent counts and rules change at each rebalance; check the current index
factsheet before investing.)
</Callout>

## Common beginner pitfalls

<Callout type="warning">
1. **Buying on the product alone.** "I love the coffee, so I bought the stock" —
   skipping earnings, P/E/PEG, and debt. This is *the* classic Lynch misreading.
2. **Confusing a good company with a good stock.** Overpaying for quality (a high
   PEG) turns a great business into a poor investment.
3. **A low PEG built on a fantasy growth rate.** Garbage growth assumption → garbage
   PEG. Sanity-check the growth number against history before you trust it.
4. **Applying one rule to all six categories.** A cyclical or turnaround needs
   different analysis than a fast grower. PEG is most useful for fast growers and
   stalwarts; buying a cyclical at the top of its cycle and mistaking it for a
   stalwart is a classic trap.
5. **Skipping the two-minute drill.** If you can't tell the story — the attraction,
   the driver, and the risk — in plain language, you don't understand the company
   well enough to own it.
6. **"Diworsification"** (Lynch's own coined term) — copying his 1,400-stock breadth
   when you can't research them. Own as many as you can genuinely follow, and no more.
7. **Market timing and short horizons** — exactly what Lynch warned against. Don't
   buy stocks with money you need in 1–2 years.
8. **Pulling the flowers, watering the weeds** — selling winners early and clinging
   to losers. "Invest in what you know" never meant "hold forever no matter what";
   it meant hold *while the story holds*, and expect a few winners to carry the rest.
</Callout>

## Putting it to work

You can start this week with no special tools:

1. **Keep a watchlist** of 3–5 businesses you genuinely understand from daily life.
2. For each, **classify it** into one of the six categories.
3. **Pull the basics** — price, EPS, P/E, recent earnings growth, debt — from any
   free finance site.
4. **Compute the PEG.** Is it below 1? Is the growth rate believable?
5. **Run the two-minute drill** out loud: why you'd buy it, what has to go right,
   and what could go wrong.
6. **Buy only the ones that pass all three tests** — category fit, PEG, and drill —
   size them so no single name can sink you, and **hold while the story holds.**

Do the homework, demand a reasonable price for real growth, tell the story before
you buy, let your winners run, and ignore the people shouting about next quarter's
economy. That, in full, is Peter Lynch.
