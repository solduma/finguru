## The one-sentence version

W.D. Gann is the most mythologized figure in technical analysis: a Texas-born broker who claimed that markets move in predictable geometric, cyclical, and even astrological patterns, who left behind a toolkit — **Gann angles, the Square of Nine, time cycles** — still bolted onto every modern charting platform, and whose legendary trading riches almost certainly never existed.

Studying Gann well means doing two things at once: learning the genuinely useful parts of his tools, and staying clear-eyed about the parts that are numerology dressed up as physics. This lesson does both, and tells you which is which at every step.

<Callout type="warning">

Almost everything you read online about Gann's "$50 million in profits," a "98% win rate," or a famous month-long trading demonstration in 1909 is **unverified promotional folklore** (more on that below). Separating documented fact from legend is the single most important skill when studying Gann — so we flag the source quality of every claim.

</Callout>

## Who he was

**William Delbert Gann** was born **June 6, 1878, in Lufkin, Texas**, and died **June 18, 1955**, at age 77 ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Delbert_Gann)). He worked as a cotton broker, moved to **New York City in 1903**, and founded his own brokerage, **W.D. Gann & Company, in 1908**. From there he built a business around *forecasting* — running subscription newsletters (**The Supply and Demand Letter**, 1919; **The Busy Man's Service**, 1923) and selling expensive trading courses.

His real, documented career was as a **publisher and educator**, not a celebrated fund manager. His forecasting was, in Wikipedia's words, "purportedly based on geometry, astronomy, astrology, time cycle analysis, and other esoteric means." He wrote a long string of books and courses (full list under *Further reading*), of which the most cited are *Truth of the Stock Tape* (1923), the cryptic novel *Tunnel Thru the Air* (1927), and *45 Years in Wall Street* (1949).

Two firsthand accounts puncture the legend:

- **Alexander Elder** (*Trading for a Living*, 1993) reported interviewing Gann's son — a Boston bank analyst — who said his father **"could not support his family by trading"** and earned his living **selling instructional courses**. Gann's estate was valued at **slightly over $100,000** at death — comfortable for the era, but not the fortune of a market wizard.
- **Larry Williams** (*The Right Stock at the Right Time*, 2003) independently met Gann's son John and a former Gann promoter, **F.B. Thatcher**, who called Gann **"just a good promoter, not necessarily a good stock trader."**

Wikipedia summarizes the verdict on his trading record as **"sharply divided."**

<Callout type="warning">

**The 1909 demonstration is legend, not fact.** Trading lore says that in October 1909 a magazine (*The Ticker and Investment Digest*) watched Gann make ~286 trades in a month at a ~92% win rate. This story is repeated everywhere — but it **cannot be verified from any primary or encyclopedic source.** Treat it as marketing folklore, never as evidence.

</Callout>

<Callout type="note">

None of this makes Gann's tools worthless — plenty of useful ideas come from imperfect people. It means you should evaluate the tools **on their own merits**, not on a biography the evidence does not support.

</Callout>

## The core idea: price and time are units of the same thing

Gann's central, genuinely interesting claim is that **markets move in geometric and cyclical patterns, not randomly**, and that **price and time are linked partners** — the same proportions and cycles repeat. Everything else (angles, squares, cycles) is machinery built on three pillars:

1. **Price and time are equal partners.** A move "balances" when the amount of price travel equals the amount of time elapsed in matched units — Gann's "squaring price and time."
2. **Geometry governs trends.** Specific angles — above all the 45° "1×1" line — act as sliding support and resistance.
3. **Time cycles repeat.** Important highs and lows recur on anniversaries and at fixed intervals (≈30, 60, 90, 144, 360 days/degrees), so the calendar matters as much as the chart.

The whole game: pick a major high or low, then project both **where** (price geometry) and **when** (time cycles) the next turn should occur — and trust it most where the two agree.

Define the jargon up front:

- **Support / resistance**: price levels where buying (support) or selling (resistance) tends to stall or reverse a move.
- **Pivot**: a clear major high or low you anchor your tools to.
- **Squaring price and time**: the moment when a price move and an elapsed amount of time become "equal" in your chosen units — supposedly a point where a trend change becomes likely (numbers below).

### Gann angles and the 1×1 line

A **Gann angle** is a straight line drawn from an important pivot at a *fixed rate of price per unit of time*. Gann formally introduced the technique in *The Basis of My Forecasting Method*, a 33-page course written in **1935** ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gann_angles)).

The master angle is the **1×1**, which Gann also called the **45° line**: **one unit of price for one unit of time**. A stock rising along its 1×1 gains one point per bar. Steeper and shallower angles are labeled by their price-to-time ratio:

```
Ratio   Meaning (price units : time units)   Slope vs 1x1
8x1     8 price per 1 time                    very steep (very bullish)
4x1     4 price per 1 time                    steep
3x1     3 price per 1 time                    steep
2x1     2 price per 1 time                    moderately steep
1x1     1 price per 1 time                    45° baseline
1x2     1 price per 2 time                    shallow
1x3     1 price per 3 time                    shallower
1x4     1 price per 4 time                    shallower
1x8     1 price per 8 time                    very shallow (weak)
```

Drawn together from a single pivot, this family of lines forms a **Gann fan**. Rising angles fan out from a major bottom; falling angles (the mirror image) fan down from a major top.

The reading rule Gann gave:

<Callout type="key">

Price trading **above the 1×1** line is in a position of strength; price **below the 1×1** signals weakness. Above the 2×1 is stronger still. When price **breaks below an ascending angle, it tends to fall to the next-flatter angle below** — so each broken angle hands off to the next as the new support (the mirror holds in downtrends, where angles act as resistance).

</Callout>

### Squaring price and time — a worked example

This is Gann's titular idea, so let's put numbers on it. "Squaring" means a price move and an elapsed amount of time become **equal in your chosen units**. Pick a scale first — say **1 price point = 1 bar of time** — then watch for the bar where *points moved* equals *bars elapsed*.

```
Worked example (ILLUSTRATIVE):
Scale: 1 point = 1 bar
Major low = 100, made at bar 0

Bar elapsed   Price       Points moved from low   Squared?
30            120         20                       no  (20 != 30)
60            150         50                       no  (50 != 60)
90            190         90                       YES (90 points = 90 bars)
```

At **bar 90 the price has risen 90 points** — the move and the elapsed time are "equal," so Gann would say price and time have **squared out**, flagging that bar as a likely turning point. Notice this is just the **1×1 angle restated**: when a market rides its 1×1 line, price and time advance one unit each, so they are squared at every bar — "squaring" and "the 45° line" are the same idea in two languages. A market that has run far enough to square a major prior swing is, in Gann's framework, due for a reaction.

<Callout type="warning">

Squaring inherits the same flaw as everything else in Gann: it only works if your **unit scale is fixed in advance**. Choose "1 point = 2 bars" instead and the same chart squares at a completely different bar. The arithmetic is exact; the *choice that feeds it* is not.

</Callout>

### The Square of Nine

The **Square of Nine** is a spiral of numbers. You start with **1 at the center** and wind outward — 2, 3, 4, 5... — so the integers form concentric square "rings" around the center. This is exactly the **Ulam spiral** geometry: odd perfect squares (1, 9, 25, 49...) line up on one diagonal and even squares (4, 16, 36...) on the opposite one, so each full 360° turn adds a fixed quadratic step ([Ulam spiral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ulam_spiral)). The diagram shows the first two rings; picture it continuing outward forever.

Numbers along the **cardinal cross** (horizontal/vertical arms) and the **diagonal cross** (corners) are treated as significant, and Gann watched positions **90°, 180°, 270°, 360°** around the spiral from a given price as candidate turning levels. The way most modern traders actually compute those levels is a simple square-root rule:

```
Square-of-Nine formula (conventional practitioner method):
1. Take the SQUARE ROOT of a key price.
2. Add a fraction of a full turn:  +0.25 = 90°   +0.5 = 180°
                                   +0.75 = 270°  +1.0 = 360°
   (some traders also use +0.125 steps = 45° increments)
3. SQUARE the result to get the next projected resistance.
4. Subtract instead of add for downside support.
```

```
Worked example (ILLUSTRATIVE): significant low = 100
sqrt(100) = 10.0
+0.25 -> 10.25 -> 10.25^2 = 105.06   (90°)
+0.50 -> 10.50 -> 10.50^2 = 110.25   (180°)
+0.75 -> 10.75 -> 10.75^2 = 115.56   (270°)
+1.00 -> 11.00 -> 11.00^2 = 121.00   (360°  <- one full revolution, also 11^2)
downside:  -0.50 -> 9.50 -> 9.50^2 =  90.25   (180° below)
```

So from a low of 100 a Gann trader watches **~105, ~110.25, ~115.5, and 121** as resistance, with **121 — one full revolution, and also 11² — the major level**. Notice these are just squares of evenly spaced numbers: there is **no market data in this calculation at all**, which is precisely the criticism we get to below.

<Callout type="note">

This square-root rule is **conventional practitioner knowledge, not a rule Gann himself published** in this exact form, and the increment convention varies between authors (some write +0.5 for 90°, +1.0 for 180°, +2.0 for 360° — the same spiral, scaled differently). The takeaway is the *method* — root, step a fraction of a turn, re-square — not any one author's increment.

</Callout>

## A worked example: drawing a Gann fan

Imagine a stock makes a clear bottom at **$50** (illustrative numbers).

1. **Anchor** the fan at the $50 low (a significant, unambiguous pivot).
2. **Set the scale.** Decide that 1 unit of price = $1 and 1 unit of time = 1 day. This choice is everything (see warning below).
3. **Draw the 1×1**: from $50, rising $1/day. After 20 days it sits at $70.
4. **Draw the 2×1** ($2/day, reaching $90 in 20 days) and the **1×2** ($0.50/day, reaching $60 in 20 days).
5. **Read it.** While price stays above the 1×1, Gann would call the uptrend healthy. If price slices below the 1×1 and lands on the 1×2, the 1×2 becomes the new support; failure there implies the trend has flattened or turned.

<Callout type="warning">

**Scaling makes or breaks the whole method, and Gann never gave fixed rules for it.** "One point per day" is meaningless for an index near 40,000 or a stock at $3 — you have to invent a unit. Different analysts pick different price-per-bar units and get completely different "45° lines" on the same chart. Critics argue this makes angle placement *arbitrary and not scientific*: you can almost always find a scale where an angle "worked" after the fact ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gann_angles)). On a rescaled or log chart the 1×1 is no longer literally 45° — the *math* (1 price unit per 1 time unit), not the visual angle, is what matters.

</Callout>

## Time cycles and the esoteric side

Beyond geometry, Gann claimed markets repeat on **time cycles** tied to natural and calendar numbers. In practice that means projecting forward from major historical highs and lows by his favored counts — **30, 60, 90, 120, 144, 180, 270, 360** (days, weeks, or "degrees") — plus simple **anniversaries** (1, 2, 5, 10 years from a major turn) and watching those calendar dates for reversals.

<Callout type="key">

The strongest Gann signal is **confluence**: a time-cycle date that lands where a price angle or Square-of-Nine level also sits. When *when* and *where* point at the same spot, a Gann analyst pays attention. (This is also where the method is easiest to fool yourself — see pitfalls.)

</Callout>

This astrological side is not internet rumor. Wikipedia notes his forecasting was "purportedly based on geometry, astronomy, astrology, time cycle analysis, and other esoteric means," and that in a **letter to a student dated March 19, 1954**, Gann demonstrated using **planetary cycles** to forecast the coffee market ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Delbert_Gann)). His deliberately obscure writing — possibly meant to conceal his "real" method — has fueled endless speculation, a claim that is, by construction, unfalsifiable.

<Callout type="warning">

There is **no scientific mechanism** linking planetary positions to market prices, and no replicated evidence of an edge. Treat the astrological/planetary material as **historical curiosity, not a tradable signal** — it is the part of Gann that modern analysts reject outright.

</Callout>

## What is empirically defensible vs. mystical

| Gann concept | Defensible kernel | Problem |
|---|---|---|
| Price/time relationship | Trends do have measurable slopes; rate-of-change matters | "Squaring" requires arbitrary unit choices |
| Gann angles / 1×1 | A trendline at a fixed slope is a legitimate, testable construct | The specific 45° / fan ratios have no proven edge over ordinary trendlines |
| Square of Nine levels | Round numbers (50, 100, 500 — where traders cluster orders) and prior swing extremes really do act as S/R | The square's outputs use no market data; it is number theory, not analysis |
| Time cycles | Some markets show rough seasonality/periodicity | Cherry-picked anniversaries fit anything in hindsight |
| Astrology | None | No credible mechanism or replicated evidence |

The honest summary: Gann tools **conflict with even the weakest form of the Efficient Market Hypothesis** — the idea that all information in past prices is already reflected in the current price, so methods relying only on past prices cannot consistently beat the market ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Efficient-market_hypothesis)). No peer-reviewed study has shown his specific methods produce a real edge, and the Gann-angles article itself is **flagged as needing additional citations**, reflecting how thin the evidentiary base is. Much of their apparent success is **hindsight bias** — you remember the angles that lined up with a turn and forget the far more numerous ones that didn't. With enough angles, squares, and date counts, *something* will line up near any turning point.

## Common pitfalls and misconceptions

<Callout type="warning">

1. **Believing the legend.** The "92% win rate 1909 demonstration" and "$50 million" stories are unverified marketing. Treat them as folklore.
2. **Arbitrary scaling / curve-fitting the scale.** Adjusting price-per-bar until the 1×1 "fits" past data is hindsight, not prediction. Lock the scale by an objective rule *before* you draw.
3. **Overloading the chart.** A full fan + multiple Square-of-Nine levels + a dozen time counts guarantees a "hit" near any turn. That is confirmation bias, not forecasting.
4. **Confusing the 45° look with the 1×1 math.** On a rescaled or log chart the line is no longer literally 45°; the *math* (1 price unit per 1 time unit) is what matters.
5. **Confusing precision with accuracy.** A level of 110.25 *looks* scientific. It is just the square of an arbitrary increment.
6. **Taking astrology literally.** Planetary/cycle claims are historical curiosity, not an edge.
7. **No risk management.** Gann levels are *hypotheses* about support/resistance. Any single level fails often, so beginners must still use stops.

</Callout>

## How to apply it today (sensibly)

Modern platforms — TradingView, StockCharts, MetaTrader, NinjaTrader — ship **Gann Fan**, **Gann Square**, and **Gann Box** tools. To use them as a disciplined trader rather than a believer:

1. **Anchor angles only at unambiguous, significant pivots** (major swing highs/lows).
2. **Fix your scaling rule in advance and never change it mid-analysis.** Write it down.
3. **Treat the 1×1 like any trendline** — a dynamic support/resistance reference, confirmed by price action or volume, not a prophecy.
4. **Use Square-of-Nine outputs only where they coincide** with independently identified levels (prior swings, round numbers, volume profile). Confluence — several independent signals at one spot — is the signal, not the square alone.
5. **Backtest before you trust.** Run the rule over years of past price data and check whether it would actually have made money. If a Gann rule has an edge, you can measure it. If you can't define it precisely enough to test, you can't trade it rationally.
6. **Always use a stop.** Every level is a hypothesis that can fail.

<Callout type="key">

The defensible takeaway from Gann is the discipline of watching *slope* (price per unit time) and *symmetry between price moves and elapsed time*. Strip away the mysticism and you are left with structured trendlines and round-number levels — useful, but not magic, and not unique to Gann.

</Callout>
