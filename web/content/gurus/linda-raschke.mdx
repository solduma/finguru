## The one-sentence version

Linda Raschke built a career on a deceptively simple idea: you don't need to predict the market, you need to read which *regime* it is in — trending or ranging — apply the matching setup, and risk so little on each trade that no single loss can hurt you.

If you have read a few trading books, you have probably met traders who sell mystique. Raschke sells the opposite: a small toolkit of well-defined setups, a relentless daily routine, and an almost boring insistence that the real "Holy Grail" of trading is risk control, not any indicator. She was a floor options market maker, a top-ranked fund manager, and the first woman profiled in Jack Schwager's *The New Market Wizards*. Her manual *Street Smarts*, co-written with Larry Connors, is still passed around trading desks because the setups inside it are concrete enough to code and test. This lesson walks through who she is, her process, four signature setups, a fully worked example with position sizing, and an honest look at where the methods break down.

## Who she is

Linda Bradford Raschke was born in 1959 in Pasadena, California, and earned dual bachelor's degrees in economics and music composition from Occidental College in 1980. She began trading professionally in **1981 as a market maker in equity options**, holding memberships on the Pacific Coast Stock Exchange and later the Philadelphia Stock Exchange. Floor market making — quoting two-sided prices and capturing the bid-ask spread while managing inventory risk — taught her volatility, arbitrage, and risk control from the inside out. She has been candid that she "went bust early" on the floor, an experience that hardwired her focus on consistency and survival.

A *market maker* is a trader who continuously offers to both buy (the bid) and sell (the ask), earning the spread between them while holding inventory; the skill is managing the risk of that inventory as prices move.

When she left the floor around 1987 and moved "upstairs" to screen trading, she told a story she still repeats as a warning: she had **45 consecutive profitable weeks** reading a bare price-quote screen, then **lost money for three months** the moment she got her first charting software — "It was a distraction… like a tennis player who's always played on clay switching to grass." The lesson she drew was that *more* tools can make you worse, not better.

In 1992 she registered as a Commodity Trading Advisor and founded LBRGroup. She launched the Watermark Fund in 1998 and, after establishing LBR Asset Management in 2002, ran the Granat Fund trading global futures markets; the Granat Fund was reportedly ranked 17th out of roughly 4,500 hedge funds for five-year performance by BarclayHedge. She was profiled in Schwager's *The New Market Wizards* (1992) — the first woman featured in the *Market Wizards* series — served two terms as president of the AAPTA, sat on the board of the Market Technicians Association (now the CMT Association), and received a Lifetime Achievement Award from the International Federation of Technical Analysts (IFTA) in 2024. She also wrote the memoir *Trading Sardines* (2018).

<Callout type="key">

Raschke's edge was never a secret indicator. It was the combination of regime-reading, pattern recognition, strict risk control, and the emotional flatness of a professional who treats trading as a probabilistic process, not a series of predictions.

</Callout>

## The core philosophy: momentum and mean reversion

Markets do two things: they trend, and they revert. Raschke's central skill is reading **which regime is active** and then applying the matching tactic. Her setups split cleanly along that line.

- **Momentum / trend-continuation setups** assume an existing trend will resume after a pause. You buy a pullback *in the direction of* the trend. The **Holy Grail** and the **Anti** live here.
- **Mean-reversion / failure setups** assume a move has overextended and will snap back. You fade a breakout or extreme that fails. **Turtle Soup** and the **80-20** live here.

*Mean reversion* is the tendency of price to return toward an average or prior level after stretching too far from it. *Fading* a move means betting against it.

<Callout type="warning">

**Using the wrong tool in the wrong regime is the core failure mode.** Momentum setups bleed money in choppy ranges; mean-reversion setups get run over in strong trends. Before you ask "what's the setup?", ask "what regime am I in?"

</Callout>

Crucially, every setup has a defined **trigger** (the condition that puts you in), a **stop** (the price that proves you wrong and gets you out), and an **exit plan**. That structure — not the indicator settings — is the real lesson. Importantly, Raschke says she does **not** run fully mechanical systems. She keeps notebooks of statistical *tendencies* she uses as "signposts" for discretionary decisions, not as auto-fire buttons.

## Her daily process (the actual foundation)

Before any setup, there is a routine. Raschke insists the work that matters happens **after the close**, when the market is quiet: "You'll always see things most clearly when the markets are closed — you're at your most objective then." Her professional habits, which a beginner can copy directly:

- **Do homework after the close, not during the session.** Plan the next day when you are calm.
- **Log trades and market data by hand.** "Writing things down helps burn them into my brain." Run trading "like you would any other business."
- **Watch market internals**, not just the chart: breadth (advancers vs. decliners), the **TICK** (net up-ticking vs. down-ticking stocks), and the put-call ratio (she watches a 5-period average) to judge whether the broad market supports a trade.
- **Keep the toolkit small.** A typical dashboard: 14-period ADX (trend strength), a 20-period EMA (pullback reference), the **3-10 oscillator** (momentum), Keltner Channels, and a 2-period rate-of-change. That is roughly it — remember the charting-software story.

### The 3-10 oscillator (her signature momentum gauge)

She has used the same momentum tool since 1981. It is simply a **MACD built from *simple* moving averages**:

```
Fast line   = SMA(3) of close  −  SMA(10) of close
Signal line = SMA(16) of the fast line
```

Practically: take a standard MACD and switch the moving-average type to *simple* with lengths 3, 10, and 16. You read it like any momentum oscillator — a **new momentum high/low confirms** the trend, while a **price-vs-oscillator divergence** (price makes a new high but the oscillator does not) warns that a swing is ending. It is the engine behind the Anti and a confirming filter for the Holy Grail.

## Setup 1 — The Holy Grail (ADX + 20-period MA)

The name is a tongue-in-cheek nod to how reliable it can look in a strong trend, not a claim of perfection. It uses two tools:

- **ADX (Average Directional Index)**, a Welles Wilder indicator that measures trend *strength* (not direction) on a 0–100 scale, with a 14-period setting.
- A **20-period moving average**, used to mark where the first pullback in a trend tends to find support or resistance.

```
LONG setup (mirror for shorts):
  1. 14-period ADX is above 30 AND rising  -> a strong trend is in force
     (best setups also have the 3-10 oscillator at a NEW momentum high)
  2. Price makes its FIRST pullback and touches the 20-period MA
     (first pullback is best; a second is acceptable)
  3. ENTRY: buy stop just above the high of the bar that touched the MA
  4. STOP:  just below the recent swing low
  5. EXIT:  trail a stop, or exit on a push to new highs (hold for the move)
  6. If the bar is not triggered, roll the entry down to the next lower bar's high
  -> If ADX drops below 30 during the pullback, ABANDON the setup
```

A *buy stop* is an order that fires only once price trades up through your chosen level — so you are bought in only if the bar confirms the move; a *sell stop* is the mirror, triggering when price falls down through a level. Because a stop becomes a market order the moment it fires, both setups depend on the move actually happening, never on price sitting still.

The logic: a high, rising ADX confirms the trend has real momentum; the *first* pullback to the 20-period MA is where dip-buyers re-enter; the stop sits where the pullback thesis is clearly wrong. Raschke notes the pullback often **cascades across timeframes** (15-minute → 30 → 60 → 120). "By the time it reaches the 120-minute chart, you should be looking for a top" — the move is maturing.

<Callout type="note">

Sources disagree on the exact moving average. Many write-ups (and Raschke's own examples) use a **20-period EMA**; others use a 20-period SMA. The behavior is nearly identical; pick one and test it consistently rather than agonizing over the choice.

</Callout>

## Setup 2 — Turtle Soup (fading failed breakouts)

The name mocks the famous "Turtle" traders who bought 20-day breakouts. Turtle Soup eats those breakouts when they fail — breakout buyers "become soup."

```
LONG Turtle Soup (mirror for shorts):
  1. Today price makes a NEW 20-day low
  2. The PRIOR 20-day low must be at least 3-4 trading sessions old
  3. ENTRY: buy stop 5-10 ticks above the previous 20-day low (valid next day only)
     (a tick is the smallest price increment an instrument trades in -- often a cent for stocks)
  4. If filled, place a sell stop a few ticks below today's low
     (or use an ATR-based stop, e.g. a fraction of the 20-period ATR)
  5. EXIT: trail (e.g. a 1R trailing stop) or use a channel exit; re-entry allowed if quickly stopped
```

The "at least 3–4 sessions old" filter is important: it ensures the new low is a genuine probe past an *established* level — the kind that traps breakout buyers. There is a variant, **Turtle Soup Plus One**, that waits one extra day for the reversal to confirm before entry.

## Setup 3 — The 80-20

Borrowed from the rhythm ideas of trader George Taylor (author of *The Taylor Trading Technique*), this is a reversal built on a prior day that opens at one extreme of its range and closes at the other.

```
LONG 80-20 (Street Smarts version):
  - Prior day OPENS in the bottom 20% of its range
  - and CLOSES in the top 80% of its range
  - Next session, if price trades BELOW the prior day's low (a few ticks)
    and then REVERSES back up -> buy on the failure, entering back through the prior low
  - STOP: beyond the day's extreme.  EXIT: trail a protective stop.
SHORT 80-20: mirror -- prior day opens in top 20%, closes in bottom 80%,
    next session price probes above the prior high and reverses down.
```

The premise is that a market opening at one extreme and closing at the other shows a one-sided session; the next day's probe past the prior extreme flushes out late breakout traders and then reverses.

<Callout type="note">

The statistical backbone, in her own words: an S&P bar that closes in the **top 20% of its range** has **~75% odds the next bar trades above its high** — but only about 50% odds it *closes* above. So the tendency forecasts a **probe, not a trend**. That is exactly why the 80-20 fades the probe rather than chasing it. (Some write-ups describe the pattern with the extremes swapped — the constant is an extreme-to-extreme day followed by a failed probe that signals the snap-back.)

</Callout>

## Setup 4 — The Anti

A trend-continuation setup built on Raschke's oscillators. It is the **first pullback after a fresh momentum thrust or trend change** — the oscillator-based cousin of the Holy Grail. Her original used a stochastic; many traders use the fast line of the 3-10 oscillator.

```
The "Anti" (long version):
  1. A sharp impulsive move establishes a NEW up-trend (the thrust)
  2. The FAST oscillator (or fast 3-10 line) makes a small COUNTERTREND dip
     while the dominant trend is still up
  3. ENTRY when the fast line "hooks" back UP, in the direction of the new trend
  4. STOP below the pullback low; ride the trend resumption
```

A *stochastic oscillator* measures where price closes within its recent range (0–100); fast and slow versions just smooth it differently. The Anti trades the moment a short-term pullback ends and the dominant trend reasserts itself.

<Callout type="note">

The Anti's exact entry mechanics vary across sources; treat the rules above as Raschke's *general* oscillator-pullback technique rather than a single canonical recipe. The definitive write-up is in *Street Smarts* (1995).

</Callout>

## A worked example — Holy Grail long, with position sizing

This follows Raschke's own documented **December 2003 S&P** example, with explicit numbers so a beginner can see every step, including the sizing math that is the real point.

- **Setup (Dec 18):** S&P futures break out of a two-week range. The **14-bar ADX crosses above 30** and the **3-10 oscillator prints a new momentum high** → strong uptrend confirmed.
- **Pullback (Dec 19):** price pulls back to the **20-bar EMA**, with the touching bar's **low = 1,075** and **high = 1,080**.
- **Entry:** buy stop one tick above the touching bar's high → fill at **1,080**.
- **Stop:** below the pullback swing low → **1,073**. Risk = 1,080 − 1,073 = **7 points**.
- **Position sizing:** account = **$50,000**; risk budget = **1% = $500**. At roughly **$50 per point** (e-mini scale), 7 points = **$350 risk per contract**. So $500 ÷ $350 ≈ **1 contract** — *round down, never up*.
- **Exit:** trail / sell on a push to new highs. Price runs to **1,094** → +14 points = **+$700**.
- **Result:** reward/risk ≈ **2:1** (14 points gained vs. 7 risked); +1.4% on the account from a 1% risk.

| Item | Value |
|---|---|
| 14-bar ADX (Dec 18) | above 30, rising; 3-10 at new high |
| Pullback bar low (at 20-EMA) | 1,075 |
| Pullback bar high | 1,080 |
| Entry (buy stop above bar high) | 1,080 |
| Stop (below swing low) | 1,073 |
| Risk per contract (7 pts × $50) | $350 |
| Account / risk budget (1%) | $50,000 / $500 |
| Position size ($500 ÷ $350, round down) | 1 contract |
| Exit (new highs) | 1,094 (+14 pts = +$700) |
| Reward : risk | ~2 : 1 |

Notice what drives the decision: a pre-defined trigger, a stop that defines exact risk, and position size *derived from* that risk (one "R" = the entry-to-stop distance). The setup is just the entry trigger. **Risk management is the strategy** — and Raschke calls risk control the real "Holy Grail."

</Callout>

## Risk control — the actual "Holy Grail"

Raschke is emphatic that survival, not brilliance, made her career.

- **Define your maximum loss before entry**, and risk a small fixed fraction per trade (commonly cited as **1–2%**). Size from the stop, never by a round share/contract count.
- **Trade small and consistent.** "Home runs are the exception, not the rule." It took her **11 years** to hit her first big home run.
- **Don't trade scared.** "It's very difficult to trade when you're freaking out about how you're going to pay the rent." Keep income while you learn.
- **Managing the trade is harder than entering it.** "Initiating a trade is the easy part… it's a much more active process once you have a trade on."

## Modern implementation and legacy

*Street Smarts* setups are popular partly because they are **codeable**: ADX/MA touches, new-N-day-low conditions, and open-vs-range rules all appear as TradingView Pine scripts, MQL5 Expert Advisors, and backtest studies. But Raschke herself layers discretion on top — internals, regime context, and screen-read judgment. Her professional process emphasized a pre-market routine, a small set of setups traded repeatedly, hand journaling, and treating drawdowns (a drawdown is a peak-to-trough drop in account value, not a single trade's loss) as a cost of doing business.

## Honest criticisms and controversies

- **Edge decay.** *Street Smarts* dates to 1995. After 30 years of publication and algorithmic markets, practitioners widely report that several setups (the daily 80-20, classic Turtle Soup, Momentum Pinball) have a thinner edge now; the Holy Grail pullback is the most-cited survivor. One independent backtest (Oxford Strategies, 42 futures, 1980–2011) graded Turtle Soup only a **"D."**
- **It is discretionary, not mechanical.** Raschke says she does *not* run rule-complete systems; her notebooks are "signposts." That makes results hard to reproduce and dependent on judgment, screen time, and experience — a beginner cannot simply "copy the rules."
- **Indicator pitfalls.** A *very* high ADX can mean a trend is already overdone and about to reverse; and slow, grinding trends may never push ADX above 30, so the Holy Grail **misses** them. ADX is also slow to register a turn, and the 20-MA generates many false touches (whipsaws — quick reversals that stop you out repeatedly) in choppy markets.
- **Regime dependence.** Momentum setups bleed in ranges; mean-reversion setups get run over in trends. The wrong tool in the wrong regime is the central way these methods lose money.
- **Survivorship and authority bias.** "Market Wizards" fame makes the methods sound more certain than the live edge supports; we rarely see equally disciplined traders who used similar methods and still failed.

<Callout type="warning">

Do not trade any of these setups live until you have personally backtested and forward-tested them on your instrument and timeframe with realistic commissions and slippage (slippage is the gap between the price you expected and the price you actually got, especially in fast markets). A setup that "looks great" on a hand-picked chart is the single most common way beginners lose money.

</Callout>

## Common pitfalls and misconceptions

- **Treating setups as mechanical signals.** They are conditional *tendencies*; without regime context (trend vs. range) they misfire.
- **Skipping the trend filter.** Buying Holy Grail pullbacks with ADX below 30 is buying a pullback in a non-trend — exactly when Raschke says she is "wrong 90% of the time" guessing direction. The 3–4-session rule in Turtle Soup is equally non-optional.
- **Chasing the breakout instead of fading it** on Turtle Soup / 80-20 — beginners reflexively do the opposite of the setup.
- **No predefined stop or size.** Every Raschke setup names the exact bar that invalidates it. If you cannot state your stop *and* your risk-based size before entering, you do not have the trade.
- **Mismanaging the live trade** — moving stops, cutting winners early, holding losers — when "managing the trade is harder than entering."
- **Indicator overload and over-optimization.** She *lost money for months* when software first distracted her. Don't curve-fit; master a few tools.
- **Trading under financial pressure**, or **expecting home runs** instead of stacking small, high-probability, consistent trades.

## How to apply it today

1. **Read the regime first.** Trend (ADX up, price respecting the 20-MA) → momentum setups (Holy Grail, Anti). Range / failed extremes → mean-reversion (Turtle Soup, 80-20).
2. Pick **one** setup that matches your temperament and write it down as code-level conditions: trigger, stop, exit, filters.
3. Backtest on at least a few hundred occurrences across different regimes; record win rate, average win, average loss, and worst drawdown.
4. Forward-test on a demo or tiny size for several weeks.
5. Size every trade by **fixed fractional risk** (e.g., 0.5–1% of account), computed from the stop — never a fixed share/contract count.
6. **Do your homework after the close, journal every trade by hand, and review weekly.** Raschke's real "secret" was relentless review and risk control.
