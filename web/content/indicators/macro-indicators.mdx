## Why this matters

Every month the economy throws out a blizzard of numbers — jobs, prices, factory
orders, housing starts, interest rates. A beginner sees noise. A macro trader
sees a **clock**.

The trick that turns the blizzard into a clock is one idea: not all data arrives
at the same time relative to the **business cycle** — the economy's repeating
rhythm of expansion (growing) and recession (shrinking). Some numbers turn
*before* the economy does, some turn *with* it, and some turn *after*. Sort every
indicator into those three buckets and you stop asking the impossible question
("what is the economy doing right now?") and start asking three answerable ones:
*where is it heading, where is it now, and has the trend been confirmed?*

That three-bucket scheme — **leading, coincident, lagging** — is the oldest and
most durable framework in macro. This lesson teaches you to build the read.

## The one-sentence version

> Leading indicators tell you where the economy is *going*, coincident
> indicators tell you where it *is*, and lagging indicators *confirm* where it has
> *been* — and the skill is reading the three in sequence, not betting on any one.

## The three buckets, defined

The whole framework rests on **timing relative to the business cycle's turning
points** — the peaks (where expansion flips to recession) and troughs (where
recession flips to recovery).

- **Leading indicators** — turn *before* the economy does. They are your early
  warning system: the things that soften or strengthen first because they sit at
  the front of the economic chain (decisions to order, build, lend, and hire).
- **Coincident indicators** — turn *with* the economy. They *define* the cycle's
  current state. When economists say "we are in a recession," these are the
  numbers they are looking at.
- **Lagging indicators** — turn *after* the economy. They are slow to move, so
  they don't predict — they **confirm**. A lagging signal tells you a trend was
  real, after the fact.

<Callout type="key">

Think of it like a long freight train. The **locomotive** (leading) moves first.
The **middle cars** (coincident) move as the train as a whole gets going. The
**caboose** (lagging) is still sitting still when the engine has already pulled
away — and is still rolling after the engine has stopped. To know what the train
is doing, you watch all three cars and read the *sequence*.

</Callout>

## Where the framework comes from: the Conference Board indexes

This isn't folk wisdom. It was built into formal statistics. Economists **Wesley
Mitchell and Arthur Burns** at the **National Bureau of Economic Research
(NBER)** pioneered the classification of indicators by cycle timing in **1938**.
The US Commerce Department later published composite indexes, and since **1996**
**The Conference Board** — a private research nonprofit — has produced the
canonical three:

- **LEI** — the Leading Economic Index (10 components)
- **CEI** — the Coincident Economic Index (4 components)
- **LAG** — the Lagging Economic Index (7 components)

Each is a single number (set to a base of **2016 = 100**) that blends many
underlying series into one composite, so a rise or fall in the index summarizes
the whole bucket. Bundling many indicators is deliberate: any one series is
noisy and gives false signals, but a *composite* smooths out the noise.

### The LEI — the locomotive (10 components)

The LEI is the headline number markets watch. The Conference Board's own framing
is that **the LEI tends to lead cycle turning points by about 7 months** — that
~7-month figure is the single most-citable number in this lesson, and it is why
the index exists at all.

Its ten components, each chosen because it sits early in the economic chain:

1. Average weekly **manufacturing hours** (firms adjust hours before headcount)
2. Average weekly **initial jobless claims** (the fastest-moving labor signal)
3. Manufacturers' **new orders** for consumer goods and materials
4. The **ISM Index of New Orders** (a factory survey, explained below)
5. Manufacturers' **new orders** for nondefense capital goods (ex-aircraft) — a
   proxy for business investment plans
6. **Building permits** for new private housing (housing turns early)
7. The **S&P 500** stock index (markets price the future)
8. The **Leading Credit Index** (a blend of credit-market conditions)
9. The **interest-rate spread**: the 10-year Treasury yield minus the fed funds
   rate (the yield-curve signal, the star of this show)
10. Average **consumer expectations** for business conditions

<Callout type="note">

Notice the logic. Hours change before hiring. Orders change before production.
Permits change before construction. Stock prices and the yield curve are
*forward-looking by nature*. Every component is something that moves at the
*front* of the chain — that is what makes the bundle "leading."

</Callout>

### The CEI — the middle cars (4 components)

The Coincident Economic Index is built from the four series that, together,
*are* the economy's current state:

1. **Nonfarm payroll employment** (total jobs)
2. **Personal income** less transfer payments (earned income)
3. **Manufacturing and trade sales**
4. **Industrial production**

These four are, not by accident, essentially the variables the **NBER's
business-cycle dating committee** watches when it officially declares the start
and end of recessions. If the CEI is the definition of "now," these are the
words it's written in.

### The LAG — the caboose (7 components)

The Lagging Economic Index moves last, and its job is **confirmation**. Its seven
components (per the Conference Board's *Description of Components*) are:

1. Average **duration of unemployment** (how long the jobless stay jobless —
   inverted, because it falls as the economy improves)
2. The **inventories-to-sales ratio** for manufacturing and trade
3. Change in **unit labor costs** (manufacturing)
4. The average **prime rate** charged by banks
5. **Commercial and industrial loans** outstanding
6. The **consumer-installment-credit-to-income ratio**
7. The change in the **CPI for services** (services inflation)

<Callout type="warning">

The LAG component list is the single most mis-stated fact in popular write-ups of
this topic — many sources swap items in or out. The seven above are the
Conference Board's official set. If you ever quote them, verify against the
Conference Board's own *Description of Components* document rather than a
secondhand summary.

</Callout>

### A snapshot of the three together

To make the three concrete, here is a recent reading (Conference Board, May 2026
data — illustrative of the *levels*, not a forecast): LEI **99.3** (+0.1% on the
month), CEI **114.6** (+0.2%), LAG **120.5** (−0.1%). Note that the LEI's six- and
twelve-month growth rates were still negative even as the monthly figure ticked
up — exactly the kind of mixed signal that makes reading the *sequence* matter.

## The recession-signal rule: the "3 Ds"

A single month of a falling LEI means little. To filter noise, the Conference
Board uses a rule built on three properties — the **"3 Ds": Duration, Depth, and
Diffusion.**

- **Duration** — is the decline lasting, not a one-month blip?
- **Depth** — is it large enough to matter?
- **Diffusion** — is the weakness *spread across* many components, or just one?

The mechanical trigger: the recession signal fires when the **six-month diffusion
index is at or below 50** (meaning *most* components are weakening, not just a
few) **and** the **six-month annualized growth rate falls below about −4.3%**.
Both conditions — breadth *and* depth, sustained over six months — must hold. The
rule is conservative on purpose: it would rather miss a wobble than cry wolf.

<Callout type="key">

The deepest lesson of the "3 Ds" is **diffusion**. One weak number is noise. Weak
numbers *spread across the whole bucket* is signal. Whenever you read macro data,
ask not just "is this number bad?" but "**how broad** is the weakness?" Breadth is
what separates a scare from a turn.

</Callout>

## The single most-cited leading signal: the yield curve

If you learn one individual indicator, learn this one.

The **yield curve** plots the interest rate (yield) on government bonds against
how long until they mature. Normally it slopes **up**: longer loans pay more,
because you're tying up money for longer. An **inverted** yield curve — when
short-term rates rise *above* long-term rates — has preceded essentially every
modern US recession, which is why it's the most-watched recession bellwether on
the board.

Two versions get quoted:

- **10-year minus 3-month** — the spread popularized by economists **Arturo
  Estrella and Frederic Mishkin** at the New York Fed in the mid-1990s, and the
  one many academics favor as the cleanest signal.
- **10-year minus 2-year** — the version markets and financial media quote most
  often.

When either spread goes **negative** (the line inverts), the clock starts. The
lead time is commonly stated as anywhere from **~7 to 24 months** before a
recession begins — wide, which is the first hint that this is a *warning light*,
not a *timer*.

### Why an inversion is a warning at all

The intuition: short-term rates are set close to the central bank's policy rate.
When the central bank jacks rates up to fight inflation, the short end shoots up.
Long-term rates reflect the market's view of *future* growth and rates — and if
investors expect the economy to slow (forcing rate *cuts* later), they pile into
long bonds, pushing long yields *down*. High short rates plus low long rates =
inversion. So an inverted curve is the market collectively saying: *policy is
tight now, and we expect it to hurt.*

## A worked read: where in the cycle am I?

Here is how the buckets line up across a stylized cycle. This is the mental model,
not a calendar:

| Cycle phase | Leading | Coincident | Lagging |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Late expansion (peak nearing)** | Already softening — claims tick up, permits fall, curve inverts | Still strong (jobs, production high) | Still rising (inflation, unemployment duration low) |
| **Recession** | Bottoming, may start to recover | Falling — the recession *is* the CEI dropping | Still deteriorating (unemployment duration rises, services CPI sticky) |
| **Early recovery** | Rising clearly | Turning up | Still poor — lagging is last to heal |

Read across any row and the sequence is obvious: leading moves first, lagging
moves last. The macro trader's edge is **not** in any one cell — it's in noticing
when the cells *disagree*. When leading indicators roll over while coincident
data still looks great, that gap is the most valuable information the framework
produces.

## How a macro trader builds the read

The synthesis — the actual skill — is cross-checking the **sequence**:

1. **Scan the leading bucket for an early turn.** Watch the LEI's six-month
   growth rate *and* its diffusion (breadth), plus the highest-frequency raw
   signals: weekly initial jobless claims and the monthly ISM new-orders survey
   (released the first business day after month-end; a reading above 50 means
   manufacturing is expanding, below 50 contracting). These are your tripwires.
2. **Confirm with coincident data.** Don't act on a leading signal alone. Check
   whether payrolls, income, sales, and industrial production are actually
   bending. The gap between a leading warning and coincident confirmation is the
   window you trade in.
3. **Use lagging data to grade your call, not to make it.** When unemployment
   duration finally lengthens and services inflation cools, the trend you spotted
   months earlier is confirmed — useful as validation, useless as a trigger.
4. **Watch the curve's *steepening*, not just its inversion.** A subtle but
   crucial point covered next.

<Callout type="note">

Notice how the data release calendar *itself* reflects the buckets. Initial
claims come **weekly** (least lagged). ISM lands the first business day after
month-end. Payrolls come the first Friday; industrial production mid-month. CPI
arrives mid-month for the *prior* month — genuinely lagging data about the past.
Faster data tends to be more leading; slower data more lagging. The calendar is
the framework in disguise.

</Callout>

## Honest failure modes — read this twice

The framework is powerful and the yield curve is the best single recession signal
we have. But "best available" is not "reliable." The honest record:

<Callout type="warning">

**The 2022–2024 inversion was the big false alarm.** The 10y–3m and 10y–2y curves
inverted from around July 2022 — described as the longest and deepest inversion on
record — and stayed inverted until roughly September 2024. **No recession
materialized** in that window. This is the headline cautionary case of the modern
era: do *not* describe the 2022 inversion as a successful recession call. The
signal that has "never been wrong" was, in the most-watched real-time test in a
generation, at minimum badly early and arguably wrong.

</Callout>

- **"It has predicted nine of the last five recessions."** The canonical joke
  (aimed originally at the stock market, often attributed to economist Paul
  Samuelson) captures the core hazard: **false positives.** Leading indicators
  flash warnings that don't pan out. Every leading signal trades earliness for
  accuracy.
- **Reflexivity (the Goodhart problem).** Once *everyone* watches a signal, its
  behavior can change. Analysts at Deutsche Bank have noted that in prior cycles
  the curve actually **re-steepened *before* the recession began** — meaning the
  *end* of an inversion (not its onset) can be the real warning. A signal everyone
  front-runs is a signal that may stop working as advertised.
- **Term-premium distortion.** Years of central-bank bond buying and selling
  (quantitative easing and tightening) can flatten or steepen the curve for
  reasons that have nothing to do with recession risk. The NY Fed's own "signal or
  noise?" research warns the curve can be pushed around by policy, not the cycle.
- **Data revisions.** Payrolls and several LEI components are revised — sometimes
  substantially — after first release. The clean historical chart traders study
  later is *not* what was visible in real time. The signal you act on is fuzzier
  than the signal you backtest.

<Callout type="key">

Put the failure modes together and you get the one rule that protects you: **never
trade a single indicator.** The yield curve's 2022 false alarm, the
"nine-of-five" false-positive problem, reflexivity, policy distortion, and
revisions are *all* reasons the buckets exist as a *system*. Leading flags it,
coincident confirms it, lagging validates it. The discipline is the cross-check.

</Callout>

## Key takeaways

- **Sort by timing, not topic.** Every indicator belongs to one of three buckets:
  **leading** (turns before — where it's going), **coincident** (turns with —
  where it is), **lagging** (turns after — confirmation).
- **The Conference Board formalized it** into three composites: **LEI** (10
  components), **CEI** (4), and **LAG** (7). The **LEI leads cycle turns by about
  7 months** — the number worth remembering.
- **Composites beat single series** because bundling smooths the noise that makes
  any one indicator unreliable.
- **The "3 Ds" — Duration, Depth, Diffusion** — filter noise from signal. The
  recession trigger needs a sustained, broad, deep decline (six-month diffusion
  `≤ 50` and six-month annualized growth below about `−4.3%`). **Breadth
  (diffusion) is the underrated half.**
- **The yield curve is the single most-cited leading signal** (10y–3m per
  Estrella–Mishkin, or 10y–2y in the markets), with a wide lead of roughly 7 to 24
  months — a warning light, not a timer.
- **It fails, and recently.** The 2022–2024 inversion produced *no* recession.
  Reflexivity, term-premium distortion, and data revisions all erode any single
  signal.
- **The skill is the sequence, not the number.** Leading flags, coincident
  confirms, lagging validates. The most valuable information appears when the
  buckets *disagree* — when leading indicators soften while coincident data still
  looks strong.
- This is educational material, not financial advice. Indicators describe
  probabilities, not certainties; size and risk decisions are your own.
