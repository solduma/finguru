## The one-sentence version

When you buy a **stock**, you own a slice of a company. When you buy a **bond**, you are not an owner at all — you are the **lender**. You hand a government or a company a pile of money, they promise to pay you interest along the way and return your money on a fixed date, and that promise is the whole product.

That difference — owner versus lender — is why bonds behave so differently from stocks, and why a diversified, all-weather portfolio holds them. Stocks are the engine: they provide most of the long-run growth, but they lurch. Bonds are the **ballast** — the heavy, boring weight low in a ship's hull that keeps it from capsizing when the engine's power throws it around. This lesson explains what a bond really is, the counterintuitive seesaw between bond prices and interest rates, and the honest limits of the ballast metaphor — including the year the ballast leaked.

<Callout type="key">

A bond is an **IOU you can trade.** You lend money; the borrower promises fixed interest plus your money back on a set date. Because the interest is fixed, the whole family is called **fixed income.** Its job in a portfolio is not to make you rich — it's to keep you steady while your stocks do the growing.

</Callout>

## What a bond actually is (you are the lender)

Governments and companies constantly need to borrow. Rather than visit a bank, they borrow from thousands of investors at once by **issuing bonds** — and each bond is a chunk of that loan that you can buy, hold, and later sell to someone else.

So the roles flip. As a shareholder you're an owner hoping the business grows. As a **bondholder** you're a creditor. You don't share in the company's success beyond your agreed interest — but you also stand *ahead* of shareholders if things go wrong. If a company goes bankrupt, bondholders get paid out of what's left before shareholders see a cent. You gave up the upside in exchange for a stronger, more predictable claim.

## The anatomy of a bond

Every bond is defined by a handful of terms. Meet them once and the rest of the lesson clicks into place.

- **Face value (par):** the amount the issuer promises to repay you at the end — the loan's principal. It's conventionally quoted as **$1,000** per bond. Think of it as the sticker on the IOU.
- **Coupon:** the fixed interest the bond pays, quoted as an annual percentage of face value. A 5% coupon on a $1,000 bond pays **$50 a year**, usually split into two $25 payments. (The name is literal: old paper bonds had little coupons you clipped off and mailed in to collect your interest.)
- **Maturity:** the date the loan ends and the issuer repays the face value. It can be months away (a short-term bond) or 30 years out (a long-term bond).
- **Issuer:** who's borrowing — and therefore who has to pay you back. This is the single biggest driver of how safe the bond is.

On the issuer, three broad families matter:

- **Government bonds** — a national government borrowing in its own currency (in the US, **Treasuries**). Generally treated as the safest borrower in that currency, because a government can tax and, in the last resort, print money to pay. This is the classic "ballast" bond.
- **Corporate bonds** — a company borrowing. Riskier than a government of the same country, because companies can go bankrupt. To compensate, they pay **more** interest.
- **Municipal bonds** — in the US, bonds issued by state or local governments (to build a school, a bridge, a water system). Their interest is often free of federal income tax, which is their main draw.

<Callout type="note">

A bond bought at issue and held to maturity is genuinely simple: you collect the coupons, then get your face value back. All the confusing stuff — prices bouncing around, "yields" that differ from the coupon — comes from the fact that bonds also **trade** in between. The moment you might sell before maturity, or buy someone else's bond secondhand, price and yield start to matter.

</Callout>

## Coupon vs. yield — the number that actually tells you your return

Beginners mix up two numbers that sound alike. The **coupon** is fixed forever — a 5% bond always pays $50 a year. The **yield** is what you actually earn *given the price you pay today*, and it moves.

The simplest version, **current yield**, is the annual coupon divided by the bond's current market price:

```
current yield = annual coupon ($) / current price ($)  × 100
```

Say a $1,000 bond has a 5% coupon, so it pays $50 a year. If you can buy it today for exactly $1,000, your yield is `50 / 1000 = 5%` — same as the coupon. But bonds don't always trade at face value. If nervous sellers dump this bond and you scoop it up for **$800**, you still collect the same $50 a year — so your yield is `50 / 800 = 6.25%`. You paid less for the same fixed stream, so you earn a higher rate.

That's the whole secret hiding behind the jargon: **the coupon is fixed in dollars, so the only way the market can make an old bond pay a competitive rate is to change its price.** Hold that thought — it's the seesaw.

<Callout type="note">

Professionals mostly quote **yield to maturity (YTM)**, a fuller number that also folds in the gain or loss between today's price and the $1,000 you'll get back at maturity, spread over the bond's remaining life. The exact YTM math needs a calculator, but the intuition is identical to current yield: **pay less than face value and your yield rises above the coupon; pay more and it falls below.** For a beginner, "yield moves opposite to price" is the load-bearing idea — not the formula.

</Callout>

## The core seesaw: prices move opposite to rates

Here is the one fact that surprises every beginner, and the reason bonds feel weird: **when interest rates rise, existing bond prices fall — and when rates fall, existing bond prices rise.** They sit on opposite ends of a seesaw.

Why? Because a bond's coupon is locked in, but the world's interest rates are not. Work through it slowly with real numbers:

1. You buy a brand-new $1,000 bond with a **5% coupon** → it pays $50 a year. Fair deal, because new bonds are being issued at 5%.
2. Six months later, interest rates in the economy climb. Now the government issues **new** $1,000 bonds paying a **6% coupon** → $60 a year.
3. You need to sell your old bond. But why would anyone pay $1,000 for your $50-a-year bond when a fresh $1,000 bond pays $60? They wouldn't.
4. So your bond's **price has to drop** until its $50-a-year payment represents a competitive yield. Roughly, it has to fall toward the point where $50 on the lower price works out near 6%. Your bond is now worth *less than* $1,000 — even though nothing about the bond itself changed.

The reverse is just as true. If rates *fall* to 4%, your 5%-coupon bond suddenly looks generous, and buyers will pay *more than* $1,000 to get it. The bond didn't get better; the alternatives got worse.

<Callout type="key">

The seesaw in one line: **a bond's coupon is fixed, so when the going rate changes, the bond's *price* is the only thing that can move to keep it competitive.** Rates up → old bonds worth less. Rates down → old bonds worth more. This is why "interest rates" and "bonds" are always mentioned in the same breath.

</Callout>

<Callout type="warning">

The beginner trap: "bonds are safe, so their price can't fall." Not true. A **government** bond has almost no risk that the issuer *fails to pay you back* — but its market **price still swings** as rates move. If you're forced to sell a long-term bond after rates have jumped, you can lose real money on a bond that will, if held to maturity, pay every promised cent. Safe from default is not the same as safe from price moves.

</Callout>

## Duration — how hard the seesaw swings

If every bond reacted to rate changes by the same amount, that would be the end of it. But they don't. A 30-year bond gets slammed by a rate move; a 1-year bond barely flinches. The measure of that sensitivity is **duration.**

Plain-English duration: **roughly how much a bond's price moves, in percent, for each 1-percentage-point change in interest rates.** A rule of thumb that's close enough for intuition: a bond with a duration of 8 loses about 8% of its price if rates rise 1%, and gains about 8% if rates fall 1%.

Why does a longer maturity mean a bigger swing? Because a long bond locks you into its now-uncompetitive coupon for *many more years*. If your bond underpays by $10 a year, one more year of that is a small annoyance; twenty more years of it is a real loss — so the price has to drop much further to make up for two decades of shortfall.

**Worked example (illustrative).** Rates rise 1 percentage point. Compare two bonds:

- A **2-year** bond has a duration of roughly **2**. Its price falls about **2%** — a $1,000 bond drops to about **$980**. Annoying, but small; and it matures soon, so you get your $1,000 back before long anyway.
- A **10-year** bond has a duration of roughly **9**. Its price falls about **9%** — a $1,000 bond drops to about **$910**. Same 1-point rate move, more than four times the price hit.

<Callout type="note">

This cuts both ways, and it's the honest reason long bonds exist. The same duration that hurts you when rates rise **helps** you when rates fall: that 30-year bond that drops ~19% on a 1-point rate rise would gain roughly that much on a 1-point rate *cut*. Long bonds are the strongest cushion when rates plunge in a crisis — and the most painful thing to hold when rates spike. Duration is the dial that sets how much of that ride you're signing up for.

</Callout>

## The yield curve, in one plain paragraph

If you plot the yield of government bonds against how long until they mature — 3-month, 2-year, 10-year, 30-year — you get the **yield curve.** Normally it slopes **upward**: lenders demand more yield to tie up their money for longer, because a lot can go wrong over 30 years, so long-term rates sit above short-term rates. Sometimes, though, the curve **inverts** — short-term yields rise *above* long-term ones. That's unusual and it makes headlines because, historically, an inverted yield curve has often (not always) preceded a recession: it signals that investors expect the central bank to cut rates in the future to rescue a slowing economy, which is a collective bet that trouble is coming. Treat it as a widely-watched *warning light*, not a guarantee — it has been early, and it has been wrong.

## Credit risk — you get paid more to lend to a riskier borrower

The seesaw so far assumed the borrower always pays you back. For governments in their own currency, that's a fair assumption. For companies, it isn't. **Credit risk** is the risk that the issuer **defaults** — fails to make its promised payments. A default can mean late payments, reduced payments, or, in a bankruptcy, getting back only a fraction of your money.

To help investors judge this, agencies (Moody's, S&P, Fitch) assign **credit ratings** — letter grades for how likely a borrower is to pay. They split into two big buckets:

- **Investment-grade** (roughly BBB/Baa and above): borrowers judged reliable. Lower yields, because lending to them is safer.
- **High-yield**, bluntly nicknamed **junk** (below investment-grade): borrowers judged shaky. Higher yields, because you're being paid to accept a real chance of not being repaid.

<Callout type="key">

The iron law of fixed income: **higher yield is compensation for higher risk, not a free lunch.** A bond paying noticeably more than a Treasury of the same maturity is paying you extra because something is riskier — longer duration, weaker credit, or both. The extra yield a corporate bond pays over a safe government bond is called the **credit spread**, and it's literally the market's price tag on that borrower's risk of not paying you back.

</Callout>

<Callout type="warning">

Ratings are opinions, not guarantees, and they can lag reality badly. In the 2008 crisis, piles of mortgage bonds carried top **AAA** ratings right up until they collapsed. A rating is a useful starting filter — it is not a promise. And "high-yield" funds can behave a lot like stocks in a crash: when the economy scares investors, junk bonds fall *with* equities rather than cushioning them, precisely when you wanted a cushion.

</Callout>

## Why a portfolio holds bonds: the ballast

Now the payoff. Why would a growth-seeking investor deliberately hold something as un-exciting as a government bond? Because of how bonds behave **when stocks fall.**

For much of modern history, high-quality government bonds have tended to **rise when stocks crash.** The mechanism is intuitive: when a recession or panic hits, frightened investors sell stocks and pile into the safest thing they can find — government bonds — pushing bond prices up. On top of that, central banks usually *cut interest rates* to fight a downturn, and (via the seesaw) falling rates push bond prices up further. So the same event that guts your stocks can lift your bonds.

The cleanest real example is **2008.** US stocks fell roughly 37% for the year, while high-quality US Treasury bonds *gained* — a broad Treasury index rose in the double digits. An investor holding both felt far less pain than one holding stocks alone: the bonds were the ballast, steadying the ship while the stock engine sputtered.

The ballast idea: stocks and safe bonds often move in **opposite directions** during a crisis, so mixing them smooths the ride. You give up some upside in booming years, but you avoid the stomach-churning full-depth crashes — and, crucially, you're less likely to panic-sell at the bottom. The classic expression of this is the **60/40 portfolio**: 60% stocks for growth, 40% bonds for ballast. (Educational, not a recommendation — the right mix depends entirely on the individual.)

</Callout>

## The honest caveat: the year the ballast leaked

The ballast metaphor is powerful, and the marketing around "safe bonds" leans on it hard. But it rests on an assumption that is not a law of physics: that bonds and stocks move *oppositely.* They usually do — but not always. And when the assumption breaks, it breaks at the worst possible time.

**2022** is the cautionary tale every honest bond lesson has to tell. That year, inflation surged and central banks raised interest rates rapidly to fight it. Stocks fell — and bonds, instead of cushioning the blow, **fell too.** Via the seesaw, sharply rising rates crushed bond prices, and longer-duration bonds got hit hardest. A US total bond index lost around **13%** — its worst year in modern history — at the same time stocks dropped roughly 18%. The classic 60/40 portfolio had one of its ugliest years ever, because *both* halves fell together. The ballast leaked.

<Callout type="warning">

The pattern to internalize: bonds cushion stock crashes **that are driven by recession or panic** (rates fall, bonds rise — 2008). They do **not** cushion — and can amplify — a crash **driven by inflation and rising rates** (2022). Diversification is a strong tendency, not a promise. Anyone who tells you bonds "always" protect you is selling the metaphor, not the reality.

</Callout>

## How individuals actually own bonds

You *can* buy an individual bond and hold it to maturity — collect the coupons, get your face value back, ignore the price wiggles in between. That's the most predictable way to own fixed income, and it's popular with people who want a known payment on a known date.

But most people, especially beginners, own bonds through a **bond fund** or **bond ETF** — a single fund that holds hundreds or thousands of individual bonds. The trade-offs:

- **Individual bonds:** you know your exact payments and your exact maturity date, so if you hold to maturity, price swings don't cost you real money. The downsides: it takes a lot of money to buy enough different bonds to be diversified, and selling one early can be clumsy and expensive.
- **Bond funds / ETFs:** instant diversification across many issuers, low minimums, easy to buy and sell like a stock. The catch beginners miss: a bond fund **never matures.** It continually replaces maturing bonds, so it always has a duration — and its share price rises and falls with rates *permanently.* There's no "just hold to maturity" escape hatch; you're exposed to the seesaw for as long as you own it.

<Callout type="note">

A bond fund's **duration** (usually listed right on its fact sheet) tells you its rate sensitivity at a glance — the same rule of thumb applies: duration 6 means roughly a 6% price move per 1-point rate change. When you pick a bond fund, you're really choosing two dials: **how much credit risk** (government vs. corporate vs. junk) and **how much duration** (short-term vs. long-term). Those two choices explain most of how any bond fund will behave.

</Callout>

## Key takeaways

- A **bond** makes you the **lender**, not an owner: fixed interest plus your money back on a set date. The whole family is **fixed income.** Its portfolio job is stability, not growth.
- Anatomy: **face value** (par, ~$1,000, repaid at the end), **coupon** (fixed interest, e.g. 5% = $50/yr), **maturity** (the end date), and **issuer** (government = safest, corporate = riskier/higher-paying, municipal = often tax-advantaged).
- **Yield** is what you actually earn given today's price, and it moves even though the coupon is fixed — buy below face value and your yield rises above the coupon.
- The **core seesaw:** bond prices move **opposite** to interest rates. Rates up → existing bonds worth less; rates down → worth more. A fixed coupon can only stay competitive by changing its price.
- **Duration** measures how hard the seesaw swings: ~duration % price move per 1-point rate change. **Longer maturity = bigger swing** — a 10-year bond can fall ~9% on a 1-point rate rise while a 2-year barely dips ~2%.
- The **yield curve** normally slopes up (longer = higher yield); an **inversion** (short above long) is a watched — but imperfect — recession warning.
- **Credit risk** is the risk of **default.** Ratings run from **investment-grade** down to **junk/high-yield**; you're paid a higher yield (the **credit spread**) to lend to riskier borrowers — never a free lunch.
- Bonds are portfolio **ballast** because high-quality government bonds often **rise when stocks crash** (2008). But this is a tendency, not a law: in **2022**, inflation and rising rates made stocks **and** bonds fall together.
- Most people own bonds via **funds/ETFs** (diversified, but permanently exposed to the seesaw — a fund never matures) rather than **individual bonds** (predictable if held to maturity, but harder to diversify).
- This is educational material, not financial advice. The reliable claim isn't "bonds are safe" — it's "bonds behave differently from stocks, and that difference is what makes them useful."
