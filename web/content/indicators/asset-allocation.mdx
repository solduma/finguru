## The one-sentence version

The decision that shapes your investing results more than any other is not *which* stocks or funds you buy — it is **how you divide your money among the big buckets**: stocks, bonds, and cash. That split is called your **asset allocation**, and getting it roughly right matters far more than getting any single pick exactly right.

Most beginners pour all their energy into stock-picking — the most glamorous and least decisive part of investing. This lesson is about the part the professionals obsess over instead: the **allocation**. We'll cover the famous evidence (and the way that evidence is constantly misquoted), the simplest portfolio that actually works — the **three-fund portfolio** — the age-based rules of thumb, why **rebalancing** quietly enforces "buy low, sell high," and the strange pull of **home bias**.

<Callout type="key">

This is educational content, not financial advice. The numbers in the charts are illustrative teaching aids, not forecasts or recommendations. The aim is to make you understand *how allocation works* so you can think clearly about your own situation.

</Callout>

## What "asset allocation" actually means

**Asset allocation** is simply the mix of broad **asset classes** in your portfolio. An asset class is a family of investments that behaves in a broadly similar way:

- **Stocks (equities)** — ownership slices of companies. Higher long-run return, but they swing hard and can fall 30–50% in a bad year.
- **Bonds (fixed income)** — loans to governments or companies that pay interest. Lower return, far calmer, and they often hold up (or rise) when stocks fall.
- **Cash** — money-market funds, savings — almost no return, almost no risk, instant access.

Your allocation might be "70% stocks, 30% bonds." That one line determines the *character* of your portfolio: how much it grows over decades, and how violently it lurches along the way. Picking the exact stocks inside the stock bucket is a detail by comparison.

## The famous evidence — and the stat everyone gets wrong

In **1986**, three researchers — **Gary Brinson, Randolph Hood, and Gilbert Beebower** (universally abbreviated **BHB**) — published *"Determinants of Portfolio Performance"* in the *Financial Analysts Journal*. They studied **91 large US pension funds** over 1974–1983 and found a startling result: a fund's **asset-allocation policy explained 93.6% of the variance** of its quarterly returns. A **1991 follow-up** (Brinson, Singer & Beebower) put the figure at **91.5%**.

That "93.6%" became the most-quoted — and most-mangled — statistic in all of personal finance. You will constantly hear it stated as *"asset allocation determines 94% of your returns."* **That is wrong.** It is worth slowing down to understand why, because the correct version is genuinely useful and the wrong version leads people astray.

<Callout type="warning">

The BHB 93.6% figure measures **how much of the bumpiness of a fund's returns over time** is explained by its allocation policy. It does **not** mean "94% of how much money you make comes from allocation." Variance of returns over time is a completely different question from how much you end up with.

</Callout>

The cleanup came from **Roger Ibbotson and Paul Kaplan** in a wonderfully-titled **2000** paper: *"Does Asset Allocation Policy Explain 40, 90, or 100 Percent of Performance?"* Their answer: it depends which question you ask, and there are three different answers, all true at once.

- **~90%** — how much of the **variability of one fund's returns over time** allocation explains. (This is the BHB number, correctly framed.)
- **~40%** — how much of the **difference between funds** allocation explains. The rest of why funds differ from each other is security selection, timing, fees, and luck.
- **~100%** — on average, allocation explains roughly **the entire level of returns** a typical fund earns, because the average fund's active bets net out to roughly zero (and then cost fees).

To drive the point home, **Meir Statman** showed in 2000 that an imaginary advisor with *perfect* market-timing skill could beat the benchmark by about **8.1% per year** — an enormous edge — and yet allocation policy would *still* "explain" 89.4% of that portfolio's variance. In other words, "explaining 90% of the variance" is fully compatible with timing adding (or subtracting) a fortune. **Explaining variance is not the same as explaining performance.**

<Callout type="note">

The honest takeaway from all this: your allocation is the **single most important decision** you control, *and* the famous statistic is routinely overstated. Both things are true. Use the three framings above and you'll never be fooled by a headline that says "studies prove allocation is 94% of investing."

</Callout>

## The three-fund portfolio: maximum simplicity

If allocation is what matters, what's the simplest way to implement a sensible one? The answer popularized by **Taylor Larimore** — affectionately called the "King of the Bogleheads," the online community built around **John Bogle's** index-investing philosophy — is the **three-fund portfolio**.

You own exactly three low-cost **index funds** (funds that mechanically hold an entire market rather than trying to beat it):

1. **Total US stock market** — every investable US company at once.
2. **Total international stock market** — companies in the rest of the world.
3. **Total US bond market** — a broad basket of high-quality bonds.

That's the whole portfolio. With three funds you own *thousands* of underlying securities across the planet. The advantages compound:

- **Ultra-low cost.** Index funds charge a tiny fraction of what active managers do. Cost is the one thing in investing that's guaranteed, and lower cost reliably means more of the return stays yours.
- **Broad diversification.** Spreading across thousands of holdings means no single company's collapse can sink you.
- **Simplicity.** Three lines on a statement. You can understand and maintain it forever.
- **Tax efficiency.** Index funds trade rarely (low **turnover** — how often holdings are bought and sold), which means fewer taxable events.
- **No manager or style risk.** You're not betting that a particular fund manager stays hot, because there's no stock-picking bet to lose.

Sample splits you'll see quoted — things like 50/30/20 or 60/20/20 (US stock / international / bond) — are **illustrative starting points, not sacred numbers.** The right split depends on your age, goals, and stomach for volatility. The power of the idea is the *structure*, not any specific percentage.

<Callout type="note">

A bit of history worth knowing: **John Bogle founded Vanguard in 1974** and launched the first index mutual fund for ordinary investors in **1975** — derided at the time as "Bogle's Folly." The First Index Investment Trust (1976) became today's Vanguard 500. Bogle argued in his writing for holding a **minimum of 20% bonds and a minimum of 20% stocks**, increasing the bond share as you age. The snappy "**age in bonds**" maxim (hold your age as a percentage in bonds) is *commonly attributed* to him, but treat it as folk wisdom rather than a verified Bogle quote.

</Callout>

## Age and horizon: the rules of thumb (and where they break)

How much should be in stocks versus bonds? The oldest rule of thumb is **"100 minus your age"** = the percentage in stocks. A 30-year-old holds 70% stocks; a 70-year-old holds 30%. The logic: the longer your **time horizon** (years until you need the money), the more short-term volatility you can ride out, so the more stocks you can afford.

Because people now live longer and bonds have at times yielded very little, the rule got nudged upward to **"110 minus age"** and even **"120 minus age,"** which keep more in stocks for longer.

<Callout type="warning">

These rules ignore almost everything that actually matters: your **risk tolerance** (how much loss you can endure without panic-selling), other income like a pension or salary, your total wealth, and your specific goals. A rule of thumb is a conversation-starter, not an answer. **Target-date funds** — which automatically shift from stocks to bonds as a retirement date approaches along a preset "glide path" — are the packaged, automated version of this same idea.

</Callout>

## Rebalancing: contrarian discipline on autopilot

Once you pick an allocation, the market won't leave it alone. If stocks surge, your 70/30 portfolio drifts to maybe 78/22 — quietly becoming riskier than you chose. **Rebalancing** is the act of selling some of what grew and buying more of what lagged, returning the portfolio to its target mix.

Notice what that mechanically forces you to do: **sell high (trim the winners) and buy low (top up the losers).** It's a built-in **contrarian** discipline that overrides the human urge to chase whatever just went up.

There are two common methods:

- **Calendar rebalancing** — check on a fixed schedule, e.g. once a year. Easy to remember and to stick with.
- **Threshold (tolerance-band) rebalancing** — rebalance only when an asset drifts more than, say, **5%** away from its target. Larimore's widely-cited **"5/25 rule"** is a band heuristic of this type (a common rule from the Bogleheads community — verify the exact form before adopting it).

Research suggests the *exact* frequency is "probably not too important... as long as it's performed consistently." Consistency beats cleverness here.

<Callout type="warning">

Don't over-promise yourself a "rebalancing bonus." The idea that rebalancing reliably *adds* return (explored by William Bernstein in 1996, and pushed further by Rob Arnott, who suggested up to ~2%/year of extra return in favorable conditions) only holds under **specific conditions** — when assets are negatively correlated and **mean-reverting** (tending to swing back toward an average). In other periods, rebalancing can actually *reduce* returns by trimming a long, sustained winner too early. Rebalance to **control risk**, and treat any return bonus as a maybe, not a guarantee.

</Callout>

## Home bias: the comfort of the familiar

The "international" fund in the three-fund portfolio exists to fight a deeply human tendency. Investors all over the world overweight their *own* country's stocks far beyond what its share of the global market would justify — the **equity home bias puzzle**. It was first studied academically by **Kenneth French and James Poterba (1991)** in *"Investor Diversification and International Equity Markets."* (The famous claim that US investors held roughly **94%** of their equity at home traces to this paper — a widely-quoted figure worth confirming against the source before you repeat it.)

Modern magnitudes are still striking: home-equity bias has been estimated around **78% in the US, 78% in Japan, and 19% in the UK** (a far more globally-diversified market). The US foreign-asset share did rise over time — from roughly 12% in 2001 to about 28% in 2010 — but the pull of the familiar remains powerful. Researcher **Karen Lewis** found that a minimum-volatility mix sat near **61% domestic / 39% foreign** — a useful reminder that *some* home tilt can be reasonable, but a 90%+ home concentration usually isn't.

The lesson: a globally-diversified stock allocation reduces the risk that your portfolio's fate is chained to one country's economy. That's exactly why the three-fund portfolio splits stocks into "US" and "international."

## Don't confuse this with risk parity

As you read more, you'll meet a more advanced cousin called **risk parity**, and it's worth drawing a clean line so you don't mix them up. The three-fund portfolio allocates by **dollars** — a beginner-friendly, unleveraged approach. Risk parity allocates by **risk contribution** instead.

Here's the key fact risk parity is built on: a "balanced" **60/40** portfolio (60% stocks, 40% bonds by money) actually carries roughly **90% of its risk in the stocks**, because stocks swing several times harder than bonds. Risk parity equalizes the risk each asset contributes, then typically **uses leverage** (borrowing, especially against the bond sleeve) to lift the calmer portfolio's returns back up.

<Callout type="warning">

Risk parity is a **leveraged, advanced** strategy — associated with Bridgewater's **All Weather** fund (launched 1996) and the term coined by **Edward Qian** (PanAgora, 2005). Its reliance on **leverage** and on **bonds** behaving well are its central risks, and it had a hard time in the disorderly rising-rate environment of **2022**, when stocks and bonds fell together. It is **not** a drop-in alternative to a beginner three-fund portfolio. Treat it as a separate, more complex topic. (There's a dedicated lesson on it.)

</Callout>

## Common beginner pitfalls

<Callout type="warning">

1. **Mistaking the BHB stat for "94% of returns."** It's 94% of *variance over time*, not 94% of how much you make. Use Ibbotson & Kaplan's three framings (90% / 40% / 100%).
2. **Obsessing over picks, ignoring the split.** The allocation is the decision that dominates your experience — start there.
3. **Treating a rule of thumb as an answer.** "100 minus age" ignores your risk tolerance, goals, and other wealth. It's a starting point.
4. **Never rebalancing.** Drift quietly turns your chosen risk level into a bigger stock bet than you signed up for.
5. **Expecting a guaranteed rebalancing bonus.** It only exists under specific conditions; rebalance to manage risk, not to chase a sure return.
6. **Extreme home bias.** Holding 90%+ of your stocks in one country concentrates your fate; broad international exposure is cheap insurance.
7. **Confusing this with risk parity.** That's leveraged and advanced — don't blend the two.

</Callout>

## Putting it to work

1. **Decide your stock/bond split first.** Use an age rule as a *starting point*, then adjust honestly for how much loss you could stomach without selling at the bottom.
2. **Implement it simply** — three total-market index funds get you a globally-diversified, ultra-low-cost portfolio in three lines.
3. **Split your stocks across US and international** to push back on home bias.
4. **Write down a rebalancing rule** — calendar (once a year) or a tolerance band (e.g. ~5% drift) — and follow it mechanically.
5. **Keep costs and turnover low**, and leave it alone between rebalances. Boring is the point.

The enduring idea: **the mix is the decision.** Once you internalize that allocation drives your ride far more than any single pick, you'll spend your energy where it actually matters — and you'll be free to keep the rest of your portfolio refreshingly simple.

## Key takeaways

- **Asset allocation** — your split across stocks, bonds, and cash — is the single decision that most shapes your investing outcome, and it's one you fully control.
- The famous **BHB "93.6%"** figure means allocation explains ~90% of the **variability of returns over time** — *not* 94% of how much money you make. **Ibbotson & Kaplan** untangled it into three answers: ~90% (variability over time), ~40% (differences between funds), ~100% (average level of return).
- The **three-fund portfolio** (total US stock + total international stock + total US bond) delivers broad diversification, ultra-low cost, tax efficiency, and simplicity — popularized by Taylor Larimore from John Bogle's index philosophy.
- Age **rules of thumb** ("100/110/120 minus age" in stocks) are useful starting points but ignore risk tolerance, other income, and goals; **target-date funds** automate the glide path.
- **Rebalancing** restores your target mix, mechanically forcing "sell high, buy low." Do it consistently for **risk control** — don't bank on a guaranteed return bonus.
- **Home bias** leads investors to over-concentrate in their own country; broad international exposure is cheap diversification.
- **Risk parity** (allocate by risk, then leverage) is a separate, advanced, leveraged strategy — *not* a beginner alternative to the three-fund portfolio.
