## The one-sentence version

A moving average takes the noisy, jittery price of a stock and replaces it with a smooth running average, so the underlying *trend* stops hiding behind the daily wiggles.

Price charts are loud. A stock can close up 1%, down 2%, then up 0.5% in three days while quietly trending higher the whole time. Your eye gets fooled by that noise. The **moving average (MA)** — one of the most widely used tools in all of technical analysis — is the volume knob. Turn it up and the noise fades; the direction the price is actually heading becomes visible. In this lesson you'll learn the two workhorse versions (the SMA and the EMA), exactly how they're calculated, how to read them, and — just as important — the one tradeoff that no moving average can ever escape.

<Callout type="key">

A moving average is a *lagging, trend-following* tool. It tells you what the trend has been, not what it will be next. Everything good and bad about it flows from that fact.

</Callout>

## The intuition

Imagine you wanted to know whether a stock is "generally going up" this month. You wouldn't stare at today's price alone — that's just one data point, possibly an outlier. Instead you'd average the last, say, 20 days. Tomorrow you'd drop the oldest day and add the newest, recomputing the average. That rolling, re-computed-every-day average is exactly what "moving" means. Plotted on the chart, it becomes a smooth line that glides under (or over) the price.

The longer the window you average, the smoother the line — but the more sluggishly it reacts to fresh news. That tension is the heart of everything below.

## The math, precisely

### Simple Moving Average (SMA)

The **SMA** is the plain, unweighted average of the last *N* closing prices. Every price in the window counts equally.

```
SMA = (P1 + P2 + ... + PN) / N

where P1..PN are the last N closing prices and N is the "period" or "length"
```

Worked example — a 5-day SMA (illustrative numbers):

```
Closing prices: 11, 12, 13, 14, 15, 16, 17

Day 5: (11+12+13+14+15) / 5 = 13
Day 6: (12+13+14+15+16) / 5 = 14   <- dropped 11, added 16
Day 7: (13+14+15+16+17) / 5 = 15   <- dropped 12, added 17
```

Notice the SMA (13, 14, 15) rises more gently than the raw price (15, 16, 17). That's smoothing in action. Also notice the SMA "lags" — it sits below a steadily rising price.

### Exponential Moving Average (EMA)

A complaint about the SMA: it treats a price from 20 days ago as just as important as today's. The **EMA** fixes this by weighting recent prices more heavily, with weights that fade exponentially into the past (older data never quite reaches zero weight, but gets close). This makes the EMA react faster to new information.

The EMA is built in three steps:

```
1. Seed it: the first EMA value is just an SMA of the first N prices.

2. Smoothing multiplier (also called alpha):
       multiplier = 2 / (N + 1)

3. Each new value:
       EMA_today = (Close_today - EMA_yesterday) * multiplier + EMA_yesterday

   Equivalently:
       EMA_today = multiplier * Close_today + (1 - multiplier) * EMA_yesterday
```

The multiplier `2/(N+1)` is the standard convention ([StockCharts](https://chartschool.stockcharts.com/table-of-contents/technical-indicators-and-overlays/technical-overlays/moving-averages-simple-and-exponential); [Wikipedia: Moving average](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moving_average)). Worked numbers:

- 10-period EMA: `2 / (10 + 1) = 0.1818` → today's close gets about 18% weight.
- 20-period EMA: `2 / (20 + 1) = 0.0952` → today's close gets about 9.5% weight.

The weighting drops by roughly half every time you double the period. The bigger *N* is, the smaller the multiplier, the more weight sits on history, and the smoother (and slower) the line.

<Callout type="note">

The EMA technically depends on *all* past prices, not just the last *N*. Practical platforms approximate it by seeding with an SMA and iterating forward; StockCharts notes that with only ~30 bars the value is rough, so their charts use at least 250 periods to converge to within a fraction of a cent. The "period" of an EMA just sets the multiplier — it isn't a hard cutoff like the SMA window.

</Callout>

## SMA vs EMA at a glance

| | SMA | EMA |
|---|---|---|
| Weighting | Every bar equal | Recent bars weighted more |
| Reaction speed | Slower | Faster |
| Smoothness | Smoother, fewer false signals | Choppier, more signals |
| Best for | Big-picture trend, support/resistance | Catching turns sooner, shorter-term trading |
| Weakness | More lag | More whipsaws (false flips) |

Neither is "better." Faster (EMA) means you catch real moves earlier *and* get faked out more often. Slower (SMA) means fewer fake-outs *but* you're later to every party. This is the lag/whipsaw tradeoff, and it's unavoidable.

## Common periods

Three lengths dominate real-world charts:

- **20** (or 21) — short-term trend, popular with active and swing traders. (Historically, an even shorter 10-day average was the classic short-term gauge.)
- **50** — the intermediate trend; a classic "is this still a healthy uptrend?" gauge.
- **200** — the long-term trend. The 200-day SMA is among the most-watched lines in all of finance; many institutions treat "price above the 200-day" as shorthand for "bull market."

These aren't magic — they're conventions that became self-reinforcing because so many people watch them.

## How to read a moving average

**1. Slope = trend.** Rising MA → uptrend. Falling → downtrend. Flat → range/chop.

**2. Price vs. MA.** Price above the MA is generally bullish; below is generally bearish. A *price crossover* (price crossing through the line) is the simplest signal.

**3. Dynamic support and resistance.** In an uptrend, pullbacks often stall and bounce near a rising MA (commonly the 20 or 50) — the MA acts as a moving floor, or **support**. In a downtrend it acts as a moving ceiling, or **resistance**. Treat these as *zones*, not exact prices; markets routinely overshoot by a bit before turning ([StockCharts](https://chartschool.stockcharts.com/table-of-contents/technical-indicators-and-overlays/technical-overlays/moving-averages-simple-and-exponential)).

## Crossover systems: golden cross & death cross

Use two MAs of different lengths and watch where they cross.

- **Golden cross** — a shorter MA crosses *above* a longer MA. Bullish. The famous version is the 50-day crossing above the 200-day.
- **Death cross** — the shorter MA crosses *below* the longer one. Bearish (50-day below 200-day).

Worked logic (illustrative): if the 50-day is at 102 and climbing while the 200-day sits at 100, the moment the 50-day pokes above the 200-day you have a golden cross — a signal that the medium-term trend has turned up relative to the long-term trend.

A complete **crossover trading system** is just: *go long when the fast MA crosses above the slow MA, exit or short when it crosses below.* Simple, mechanical, and the basis of countless strategies.

<Callout type="warning">

Crossovers can be highly profitable in strong, sustained trends — and brutal in sideways markets, where the two lines tangle and produce a string of **whipsaws** (signals that reverse almost immediately, each one a small loss). Golden and death crosses are also *late by construction*: by the time the 50-day crosses the 200-day, a large part of the move has already happened. They confirm trends; they don't predict them.

</Callout>

## The fundamental tradeoff: lag

Every moving average lags because it's built from past prices. An SMA lags the latest data by roughly *half its window* — a 200-day SMA is, loosely, "centered" about 100 days back ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moving_average)). The EMA reduces but never eliminates this delay.

You cannot tune lag away. Shorten the period to react faster and you import noise (more whipsaws). Lengthen it for smoothness and you react slower (more lag). Choosing a period *is* choosing your point on that spectrum.

<Callout type="key">

Do not expect a moving average to sell you the top or buy you the bottom — it mathematically cannot. Its job is to keep you on the right side of a *sustained* trend and to filter out noise, accepting that you'll always give up a slice at each end.

</Callout>

## Combining MAs with other tools

Because MAs are lagging trend tools, the standard move is to pair them with a non-lagging or "leading" indicator:

- **MA + RSI (Relative Strength Index).** Use the MA to define the trend's direction, then use RSI (a momentum oscillator) to time entries on pullbacks — e.g., buy when price dips to a rising 50-day MA *and* RSI is recovering from oversold.
- **Multiple MAs (a "ribbon").** Stacking 20/50/200 lets you read trend strength at a glance: cleanly fanned out and in order = strong trend; tangled = no trend, stand aside.
- **MA + support/resistance + volume.** A bounce off the 50-day on rising volume is more convincing than one on thin volume.
- **MACD**, one of the most popular indicators, is *literally* built from EMAs (the difference between a 12- and 26-period EMA), so it's a natural companion.

## Common beginner mistakes

- **Trading crossovers in a flat market.** This is the number-one account-killer. Add a filter — require price to also be making higher highs, or only take signals when the longer MA is clearly sloped.
- **Treating the MA as an exact price.** It's a zone. Don't put a stop-loss one cent below the 50-day and expect it to hold.
- **Optimizing the period to fit the past.** A 47-day MA that "worked great" on last year's chart is curve-fitting. Stick to robust, round conventions (20/50/200).
- **Forgetting MAs need a trend.** In a range, they're worse than useless — they generate maximum whipsaw exactly when there's no trend to follow.
- **Confusing "fast = better."** A faster EMA isn't superior; it's just a different lag/noise tradeoff.

## Practice suggestions

1. By hand, compute a 5-day SMA on ten closing prices from any stock. Then compute a 5-day EMA using the steps above. Watch how the EMA hugs the recent prices more tightly.
2. Pull up a daily chart with the 20, 50, and 200 SMAs. Mark every golden and death cross over the last two years and ask: how late was each one? How many were whipsaws?
3. Switch the 20 between SMA and EMA and watch how many more times the faster EMA flips.
