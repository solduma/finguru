## The one-sentence version

If you only ever learn one thing about reading a price chart, learn this: **prices have memory.** Levels where buyers and sellers fought before tend to matter again — and the shapes price traces around those levels (the "chart patterns") are just stories about who is winning that fight.

Support and resistance is the most fundamental idea in technical analysis. Almost every other tool — trendlines, breakouts, chart patterns, even many indicators — is built on top of it. Get this lesson right and the rest of charting becomes far easier. Get it wrong and you'll spend years drawing lines that mean nothing.

## What support and resistance actually are

Let's define the two core terms.

- **Support** is a price level where buying demand is expected to be strong enough to stop a falling price from falling further. Think of it as a *floor*.
- **Resistance** is a price level where selling pressure is expected to be strong enough to stop a rising price from rising further. Think of it as a *ceiling*.

A useful way to picture it: price is a tug-of-war between **bulls** (buyers, who want higher prices) and **bears** (sellers, who want lower prices). When the rope sits still, you get a sideways "range." Support is the line bulls defend; resistance is the line bears defend ([StockCharts](https://chartschool.stockcharts.com/table-of-contents/chart-analysis/support-and-resistance)).

Support and resistance are *zones*, not exact lines. Real markets are noisy. Treat "support at 100" as roughly "the 98–101 area," not a single magic number.

</Callout>

## Why levels form

Three forces create these levels, and understanding them stops you from treating charting as superstition.

**1. Memory and trapped traders.** Imagine a stock that rose to $18, fell to $14, and is now climbing back. Everyone who bought near $18 is sitting on a loss. As price returns to $18, many of them sell just to "get out at breakeven." That wave of selling is a *supply overhang* — it creates resistance at $18 ([StockCharts](https://chartschool.stockcharts.com/table-of-contents/chart-analysis/support-and-resistance)).

**2. Psychology near round numbers.** Humans cluster orders at clean figures — $50, $100, or 1.3000 on a currency pair (an exchange rate — here, 1.30 dollars per euro). Big stop-loss (an order that exits a losing trade) and take-profit (an order that closes a winning trade at a target price) orders pile up there, so price often pauses or reverses at these *psychological levels* ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Support_and_resistance)).

**3. Repeated testing builds significance.** The more often a level is touched and holds, the more traders watch it, and the more self-fulfilling it becomes. As one reference puts it, "the more often a support/resistance level is 'tested' (touched and bounced off by price), the more significance is given to that specific level" ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Support_and_resistance)).

## Role reversal: the single most useful trick

Here is the idea that separates beginners from competent chart readers:

<Callout type="key">

When a support level breaks, it tends to become resistance. When a resistance level breaks, it tends to become support.

</Callout>

Why? Because the supply/demand balance has flipped. If price punches *up* through resistance, demand just overwhelmed supply. On a later pullback to that old ceiling, buyers who missed the move step in — so the old ceiling becomes a new floor ([StockCharts](https://chartschool.stockcharts.com/table-of-contents/chart-analysis/support-and-resistance)).

*Illustrative example:* An index breaks above resistance at 935. It later pulls back, touches 935 twice, and bounces. The 935 ceiling has become a 935 floor. Traders call this a *retest*, and a successful retest is one of the higher-confidence entries in technical analysis.

## Trendlines and channels

A horizontal line isn't the only kind of support/resistance.

- A **trendline** is a straight line connecting a series of rising lows (local troughs) — an *uptrend line*, acting as diagonal support — or falling highs (local peaks) — a *downtrend line*, acting as diagonal resistance. You need at least two points to draw one and a third touch to "confirm" it.
- A **channel** is two parallel trendlines bracketing price — one along the lows, one along the highs. Price tends to bounce back and forth between them.

<Callout type="note">

A trendline drawn through only two points is a guess. The third touch that holds is what makes it tradeable. Beware of forcing a line through the data — it's easy to fool yourself.

</Callout>

## Classic chart patterns

Chart patterns are recognizable shapes price makes around these levels. They fall into two families: **reversal** patterns (the trend is likely to flip) and **continuation** patterns (the trend is likely to resume after a pause). Each "completes" only on a **breakout** — a decisive close beyond the pattern's key level.

### Reversal patterns

**Head and shoulders (top).** A bearish topping pattern with three peaks: a *left shoulder*, a higher *head*, and a lower *right shoulder*. The line connecting the lows between them is the **neckline**. The pattern is not complete until price closes below the neckline ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Head_and_shoulders_(chart_pattern))). Volume typically diminishes from left shoulder to head to right shoulder — buyers are running out of fuel.

**Double top / double bottom.** A double top is two peaks at roughly the same price, confirmed when price falls below the valley between them (the neckline). A double bottom is the mirror image, confirmed on a break above the intervening peak ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_top_and_double_bottom)). A **triple top/bottom** is the same idea with three touches — three peaks (or troughs) at roughly the same price instead of two.

### Continuation patterns

**Triangles.** Converging trendlines as price range contracts. *Ascending* (flat top, rising lows — usually bullish), *descending* (flat bottom, falling highs — usually bearish), and *symmetrical* (both lines converging). Importantly, "of more importance is the direction that the market moves when it breaks out of the triangle" — don't assume the bias, wait for the break ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle_(chart_pattern))).

**Flags and pennants.** Both follow a sharp move called the **flagpole**, then a brief pause (price drifts sideways in a tight range — it "consolidates"). A *flag* consolidates between two parallel lines; a *pennant* between two converging lines. They are otherwise identical in meaning — a "breather" before the trend resumes ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flag_and_pennant_patterns)).

## Measured-move targets

Most patterns give a rough price *target* by projecting the pattern's height from the breakout point. All quantities below are in price units (dollars in the worked example).

```
Head & shoulders top target:
  height  = head peak − neckline (at the breakdown point)
  target  = neckline − height        (projected downward)

Flag / pennant target:
  target  = breakout price ± flagpole height

Double bottom target:
  height  = neckline (intervening peak) − the two lows
  target  = neckline + height        (projected upward)
```

*Illustrative worked example.* A stock forms a head and shoulders top. The head peaks at **$120**, the neckline sits at **$100**. Height = 120 − 100 = **$20**. Price breaks below the neckline at $100, so the measured-move target = 100 − 20 = **$80**. This is a *minimum* objective, not a promise — if the prior advance was small, the fall may be small too ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Head_and_shoulders_(chart_pattern))).

## The role of volume

**Volume** is the number of shares or contracts traded. It's the lie detector for breakouts.

<Callout type="key">

A genuine breakout usually comes with a surge in volume. A breakout on thin, shrinking volume is suspect — there isn't enough conviction behind it.

</Callout>

In a cup and handle, for instance, volume should fall as the cup forms and then increase rapidly as price breaks out of the handle ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cup_and_handle)).

## False breakouts (fakeouts)

A **false breakout** is when price pokes through a level — triggering breakout traders' entries and stops — then snaps back into the range. They're painfully common, partly because large players know exactly where stop-loss orders cluster (just beyond obvious levels) and price gets pulled there.

Two defenses:

1. **Wait for a confirmed close** beyond the level (e.g., a daily close, not just an intraday spike — a brief move during the day that doesn't last to the close), ideally on strong volume.
2. **Wait for the retest** — let price break, pull back to the old level, and hold, before entering.

<Callout type="warning">

The "obvious" level everyone sees is exactly where fakeouts happen most. Treating a single wick (the thin line above or below a candle's body, showing the highest/lowest price traded) beyond support as a breakdown is one of the most expensive beginner mistakes.

</Callout>

## Strengths and common beginner mistakes

**Strengths:** Support/resistance is intuitive, works across all timeframes and markets, and gives you concrete, definable places to put a **stop-loss** and a target — which makes risk management possible.

**Mistakes to avoid:**

- **Treating lines as exact prices.** They're zones.
- **Hindsight pattern-spotting.** Patterns look obvious *after* they complete. In real time they're ambiguous. Honest research (e.g., Thomas Bulkowski's pattern statistics) shows even "good" patterns fail a meaningful fraction of the time. Patterns shift probabilities; they don't guarantee outcomes.
- **Subjectivity and confirmation bias.** Give five traders the same chart and you'll get five different trendlines. Be aware you can always find *a* pattern if you look hard enough.
- **Forgetting the bigger picture.** A pattern on a 5-minute chart means little against a strong daily trend.

## How to combine it with other tools

Support/resistance is a foundation, not a complete system. Layer it with:

- **Volume** to confirm breakouts.
- **Moving averages** (e.g., the 50- and 200-day) — these often *act* as dynamic support/resistance themselves.
- **Momentum oscillators** like RSI to spot exhaustion as price reaches a level.
- **Multiple timeframes** — a level that shows up on the daily *and* weekly chart is stronger.

## Practice suggestions

1. Open a charting tool (TradingView is free) and mark every clear swing high and low (a swing high is a local peak — a high with lower highs on either side; a swing low is the opposite, a local trough) on a daily chart. Notice how often old levels get retested.
2. Find three historical examples of role reversal (support becoming resistance).
3. Identify one of each pattern in *past* data, then practice the measured-move math.
4. Forward-test on paper: predict the breakout direction *before* it happens, log your guesses, and review your hit rate honestly.
