## The one-sentence version

A moving average tells you *where* price has been, but it can't tell you whether the market is **trending hard enough to follow** or **chopping sideways enough to fade** — and that single distinction is the difference between a strategy that works and the same strategy bleeding you dry. Welles Wilder's three tools — the **Directional Movement Index (DMI)**, the **Average Directional Index (ADX)**, and the **Average True Range (ATR)** — exist to answer exactly that: *Is there a trend? How strong is it? And how much room do I need to give my stop?*

These all come from one source: J. Welles Wilder Jr.'s 1978 book *New Concepts in Technical Trading Systems*, the same book that gave us the RSI and Parabolic SAR. They were designed for commodities, which gap and move violently — and that origin shapes how they work.

<Callout type="key">

ADX measures trend **strength only**, never direction. A high ADX can mean a roaring uptrend *or* a collapsing downtrend. Direction is the job of the +DI and −DI lines; strength is the job of ADX.

</Callout>

## Part 1: The building block — True Range and ATR

Everything here starts with one idea: a single bar's high-minus-low range *understates* volatility when price gaps. If a stock closes at \$50 and opens the next day at \$55, the high-low range of that new day might be tiny, yet \$5 of movement just happened overnight. Wilder fixed this with **True Range (TR)**.

**True Range** is the *greatest* of these three distances:

```
TR = max of:
  1. Current High  -  Current Low
  2. | Current High  -  Previous Close |
  3. | Current Low   -  Previous Close |
```

The previous close is used in (2) and (3) precisely to capture gaps — when yesterday's close sits outside today's range, one of those absolute values becomes the largest number. A compact equivalent: `TR = max(High, PrevClose) − min(Low, PrevClose)`.

**Average True Range (ATR)** is just a smoothed average of TR. Wilder's default period is **14**. The first value is a simple average of the first 14 TRs; after that he applies his own smoothing (a flavor of exponential moving average with a smoothing factor of 1/14):

```
First ATR = average of the first 14 TR values
Subsequent ATR = [ (Prior ATR × 13) + Current TR ] / 14
```

**Worked micro-example (illustrative).** Suppose yesterday's close was 100, and today High = 104, Low = 99.

```
1. High − Low          = 104 − 99   = 5
2. |High − PrevClose|  = |104 − 100| = 4
3. |Low  − PrevClose|  = |99  − 100| = 1
TR = max(5, 4, 1) = 5
```

If the prior 14-period ATR was 3.0, the new ATR = (3.0 × 13 + 5) / 14 ≈ **3.14**.

<Callout type="note">

ATR is in **absolute price units** (dollars, ticks, pips), not a percentage. An ATR of 3 means very different things for a \$20 stock versus a \$2,000 one. To compare across instruments, normalize it: `ATR / Close × 100` gives an ATR-as-percent figure.

</Callout>

## Part 2: Directional Movement — the +DI and −DI lines

Wilder asked a simple question each bar: did we make a *more extreme high* or a *more extreme low* than yesterday? That excess is **Directional Movement (DM)**.

```
UpMove   = Current High − Previous High
DownMove = Previous Low − Current Low

+DM = UpMove   if (UpMove > DownMove and UpMove > 0),   else 0
−DM = DownMove if (DownMove > UpMove and DownMove > 0), else 0
```

Only the *larger* move counts — a bar can contribute to +DM or −DM, but not both. An "inside day" (lower high *and* higher low than yesterday) produces zero for both.

We then smooth +DM, −DM, and TR over 14 periods (using the same Wilder smoothing as ATR) and turn them into the **Directional Indicators**, expressed as percentages:

```
+DI = 100 × (Smoothed +DM / Smoothed TR)
−DI = 100 × (Smoothed −DM / Smoothed TR)
```

Reading them is intuitive: when **+DI is above −DI**, buyers have the edge; when **−DI is above +DI**, sellers do. A classic signal is a *crossover* — +DI crossing above −DI is bullish, the reverse is bearish.

## Part 3: From DI to ADX — distilling strength

The two DI lines tell you who's winning. To measure *how decisively*, Wilder computed the **Directional Index (DX)**:

```
DX = 100 × ( | +DI − −DI |  /  ( +DI + −DI ) )
```

If +DI = 30 and −DI = 10, then DX = 100 × (20 / 40) = **50** — a wide spread, meaning one side dominates. If +DI = 22 and −DI = 18, DX = 100 × (4 / 40) = **10** — the sides are evenly matched, no real trend.

DX is jumpy, so **ADX is DX smoothed over 14 periods**:

```
First ADX = 14-period average of DX
Subsequent ADX = [ (Prior ADX × 13) + Current DX ] / 14
```

Because ADX is the absolute *difference* between the DIs, it ignores direction entirely — that's why it rises in strong up- *and* down-trends alike.

### Reading the ADX scale

ADX ranges from 0 to 100, but in practice you'll rarely see it above 60. Common interpretation (thresholds are conventions, not laws):

| ADX reading | Interpretation |
|---|---|
| Below 20 | Weak or no trend — ranging / choppy |
| 20–25 | "Gray zone" — trend may be forming |
| Above 25 | Trend is present and worth following |
| Above 40 | Strong trend |
| Above 50 | Very strong trend |

Wilder himself treated **25** as the threshold for a present trend and **20** as the line below which there is no trend; many analysts simply use 20. What matters as much as the level is the **slope**: a *rising* ADX means the trend is strengthening; a *falling* ADX means it's losing steam (even if price still drifts the same way). Note that a steady, constant-slope trend will make ADX *flatten out* — ADX reacts to *changes* in trend strength.

<Callout type="warning">

ADX is a heavily smoothed, **lagging** indicator. Wilder's double smoothing means it can take around **150 periods** of data before ADX settles to consistent "true" values, and crossovers and threshold crosses arrive late. Never trade ADX in isolation — use it as a *filter*, not a *trigger*.

</Callout>

## Part 4: The killer application — choosing your strategy

The single most valuable use of ADX is as a **regime filter**: it tells you which *kind* of strategy the market currently rewards.

- **ADX above ~25 (and rising):** trend regime. Trend-following logic works — buy pullbacks, ride breakouts, trail stops. Mean-reversion will get run over.
- **ADX below ~20:** range regime. Mean-reversion works — fade overbought/oversold extremes (e.g., RSI), sell resistance, buy support. Breakout and trend systems will get chopped to death by false signals.

This solves a problem that destroys many beginners: applying a trend strategy in a range, or a fade strategy in a trend. The same RSI "overbought" signal that's a great short in a quiet market is a *terrible* short in a runaway uptrend — ADX is how you tell the two apart.

### ATR for stops and position sizing

ATR answers the practical question: *how much noise do I need to tolerate before admitting I'm wrong?* Setting a stop at a fixed percentage ignores how volatile the instrument currently is. ATR adapts.

A popular volatility stop is the **Chandelier Exit** (created by Charles Le Beau and featured in Alexander Elder's books):

```
Chandelier Exit (long)  = Highest High over 22 periods − (ATR(22) × 3)
Chandelier Exit (short) = Lowest Low over 22 periods   + (ATR(22) × 3)
```

The stop "hangs" three ATRs below the recent peak and ratchets up as price rises — never down. Defaults are **22 periods, 3× multiplier**; volatile names may need a larger multiplier (e.g., 5) to avoid whipsaws.

ATR also drives **volatility-based position sizing**. If you risk a fixed dollar amount per trade, your share count should scale *inversely* with volatility:

```
Position size (shares) = (Account risk in $) / (ATR × stop multiplier)
```

**Illustrative example.** Risk \$500 per trade, stop = 2 × ATR, ATR = \$2.50. Stop distance = \$5.00, so size = 500 / 5 = **100 shares**. If ATR jumps to \$5, the same \$500 risk buys only 50 shares — automatically smaller positions in wilder markets.

## Strengths, weaknesses, and beginner traps

**Strengths.** ADX cleanly separates "trend vs. no-trend," a genuinely hard thing to eyeball. ATR is one of the few volatility measures that survives price gaps and back-adjusted futures contracts, since it works on *differences* of price.

**Honest weaknesses.** ADX lags badly and its thresholds (20/25) are conventions, not fixed truths — they vary by market and timeframe, and you should backtest them rather than trust them. ATR's absolute scale makes cross-asset comparison meaningless without normalizing, and its value depends on how far back your data starts.

<Callout type="warning">

Common beginner mistakes: (1) reading a high ADX as *bullish* — it isn't directional. (2) Treating an ADX cross above 25 as a buy signal — it only says "a trend exists," so check the DI lines for direction. (3) Comparing raw ATR across two different-priced stocks. (4) Setting a too-tight ATR-stop multiplier (under ~1.5) and getting whipsawed out of good trades by normal noise.

</Callout>

## How to practice

1. Put ADX, +DI, −DI, and ATR on a chart you know. For each big move, check whether ADX was above 25 *before* the move ran — build intuition for the filter.
2. Pick one ranging period and one trending period. Apply an RSI mean-reversion rule to each and see how the ADX regime predicted which one worked.
3. Manually compute TR and a 14-period ATR for ~20 bars in a spreadsheet, then compare to your platform's value. Discrepancies teach you how the smoothing and start-point work.
4. Backtest your own ADX threshold — try 18, 22, 25, 30 — instead of assuming 25 is optimal for your market.
