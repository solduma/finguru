## The one-sentence version

Most of what you can buy in a brokerage account is **paper** — a share is a claim on a company, a bond is a promise to be repaid. A **real asset** is the physical thing itself: a building, a highway, a pipeline, a bar of gold. People hold real assets because they tend to hold their value when prices rise, they often throw off steady cash, and they don't move in perfect lockstep with the stock market.

The problem is that owning real assets directly is a hassle — you can't buy half a warehouse, and being a landlord is a job. The elegant solution is the **REIT (real estate investment trust)**: a company that owns income-producing property and trades on the stock exchange like any share. Buy one share and you own a sliver of a portfolio of malls, offices, warehouses, or apartments — no tenants to call, no toilets to fix.

This lesson explains what real assets are and why people want them, how a REIT works, the one rule that gives REITs their famously fat dividends, and — most importantly — the single beginner mistake that this whole learning path is built to fix: **you cannot judge a REIT by its ordinary earnings.** You have to learn a different number called FFO. That reveal is the heart of what follows.

## Paper assets vs. real assets

Start with the distinction, because the words get thrown around loosely.

- A **paper asset** (also called a *financial asset*) is a claim or a promise written on paper (or, these days, in a database). A **share** is a claim on a company's profits. A **bond** is a promise to repay borrowed money with interest. Cash in a bank is a claim on the bank. None of these is a physical object — they're contracts. Their value depends on someone else honoring the claim.
- A **real asset** (also called a *tangible* or *physical asset*) is the actual thing you could stub your toe on: land, buildings, roads, bridges, machinery, farmland, timber, oil in the ground, and metals like gold and silver. Its value comes from what it *is* and what it *does*, not from a promise.

Real assets usually fall into three big buckets:

- **Property (real estate)** — apartments, offices, shopping centers, warehouses, hotels, data centers. This is the biggest and most accessible category, and the main focus of this lesson.
- **Infrastructure** — the physical backbone of the economy: toll roads, airports, pipelines, cell towers, power grids, water systems. These often earn regulated or contracted fees that rise with inflation, which is why big pension funds love them.
- **Commodities and precious metals** — oil, copper, wheat, and especially **gold**, which people have treated as a store of value for thousands of years. (Whether gold belongs in a portfolio is genuinely debated — it pays no income — but it's the classic "hold-a-real-thing" asset.)

<Callout type="key">

The core intuition: a paper asset is a *promise* about value; a real asset *is* value in physical form. That difference is exactly why investors reach for real assets in the moments when they trust promises the least — during inflation, financial stress, or currency worries.

</Callout>

## Why people hold real assets

Three reasons come up again and again. Understand them and you understand the whole "real assets" investment case.

**1. Inflation hedge.** **Inflation** is the general rise in prices over time — the reason a coffee that cost $2 a decade ago costs $4 now. Inflation quietly erodes the value of cash and fixed bond payments (a bond that pays you $50 a year buys less and less as prices climb). Real assets tend to *rise with* inflation instead, because the thing itself gets more expensive: rents go up, replacement building costs go up, commodity prices go up. So real assets act as a partial shield — a **hedge** (a position taken to offset a specific risk) against inflation.

**2. Income.** Property collects **rent**; infrastructure collects **tolls and fees**. That's real cash coming in month after month, largely independent of whether the stock market is up or down that week. For an investor who wants income rather than just price gains, that steady stream is the appeal.

**3. Diversification.** **Diversification** means spreading your money across things that don't all move together, so one bad event doesn't sink everything. Real assets — especially property and infrastructure — often march to a somewhat different drummer than the stock market. Adding them can smooth out your overall ride. (The honest caveat, which we'll return to: this diversification benefit weakens exactly when you'd most want it, in a full-blown crisis.)

<Callout type="note">

"Inflation hedge" is a *tendency over long stretches*, not a monthly guarantee. Over years and decades, real assets have broadly kept pace with rising prices. But in any given quarter — especially when interest rates are spiking — real assets can fall hard even while inflation is high. Don't read "hedge" as "can't lose money when prices rise." It means "tends to hold real value over the long run."

</Callout>

## Enter the REIT: real estate for the price of a share

Owning a building directly is the classic real asset — and the classic headache. You need a huge amount of money, you can't sell half of it if you need cash, and you become responsible for tenants, repairs, and vacancies. Most people can't and shouldn't do it.

A **REIT (real estate investment trust)** solves this. A REIT is a **company that owns (and usually operates) income-producing real estate** — and whose shares trade on a stock exchange just like any other stock. When you buy a share of a REIT, you own a tiny slice of its entire property portfolio, and you receive a proportional slice of the rent it collects, paid out as dividends.

The magic is that it converts an illiquid, lumpy, hands-on asset into something you can buy with one click:

- **Small entry price.** One share might cost $30 or $80 — versus millions for an actual building.
- **Liquidity.** **Liquidity** is how easily you can turn an asset into cash without moving its price. A share of a REIT can be sold in seconds during market hours; an actual office tower can take a year to sell. REIT shares are highly liquid; direct property is deeply illiquid.
- **No landlord duties.** Professional managers handle tenants, leases, and maintenance. You just own the shares.
- **Instant diversification.** A single REIT might own hundreds of properties across many cities, so you're not betting everything on one building.

REITs specialize by property type, and the mix has changed a lot over the decades. Common sectors include:

- **Retail** — shopping malls and strip centers.
- **Office** — office towers (a troubled sector since 2020; more on that below).
- **Industrial / logistics** — warehouses and distribution centers, boosted by e-commerce.
- **Residential** — apartment complexes and single-family rentals.
- **Data centers** — the buildings full of servers that power the internet and AI (a fast-growing category).
- **Specialty** — cell towers, self-storage, hospitals, timberland, even billboards.

<Callout type="note">

The structure was created by U.S. Congress in **1960** specifically to let ordinary people invest in large-scale, income-producing real estate the way they'd invest in a company's stock — pooling small amounts into professionally managed property. Many other countries later adopted their own REIT rules. So the whole point of a REIT, from day one, was democratizing access to real assets.

</Callout>

## The defining rule: the ~90% payout

Here's what makes a REIT special — and what produces those eye-catching yields you've seen. To qualify as a REIT and get its special tax treatment, a company must follow strict rules, and the headline one is this:

**A REIT must distribute at least ~90% of its taxable income to shareholders as dividends each year.**

An ordinary company can keep its profits and reinvest them (or pay a modest dividend of its choosing). A REIT is legally required to hand almost all of its taxable profit back to you. In exchange, the REIT itself generally pays little or no corporate income tax on the profits it distributes — avoiding the usual "double taxation" where a company is taxed and then the shareholder is taxed again on dividends.

That mandatory payout is *why* REITs typically carry higher dividend yields than the average stock. It's not generosity; it's the law.

<Callout type="warning">

That high yield comes with a catch you met in the dividends lesson: REIT dividends are **generally taxed as ordinary income**, at your full income-tax rate, not at the lower "qualified dividend" rate that most stock dividends enjoy. This is why many investors deliberately hold REITs inside a tax-advantaged retirement account rather than a regular taxable one. The yield looks fat; the after-tax yield in a taxable account is meaningfully thinner. (Educational, not tax advice — confirm current rules for your situation.)

</Callout>

## Direct property vs. REIT funds — the honest trade-offs

You now have three broad ways to own real estate. It helps to line them up:

- **Direct ownership** (buy an actual property): maximum control and you keep all the rent — but it demands a lot of money, a lot of effort, terrible liquidity, and concentrated risk (one building, one location).
- **A single REIT** (buy shares of one real-estate company): cheap, liquid, hands-off, and diversified across that company's properties — but you're still exposed to that one company's sector and management.
- **A REIT fund or REIT ETF** (buy a fund holding many REITs): the broadest diversification — dozens or hundreds of REITs across every property type in one purchase — at the cost of a small fund fee and no control over the individual holdings.

<Callout type="key">

The trade-off in one line: **direct property gives you control and effort; REITs give you liquidity and convenience; REIT funds give you diversification on top.** As you move from direct ownership toward funds, you give up control and upside concentration in exchange for ease, liquidity, and spread-out risk. None is "best" — they suit different goals. (Educational, not a recommendation to buy any of them.)

</Callout>

## The crucial reveal: why net income lies about a REIT

This is the single most important idea in the lesson, and the reason this whole learning path exists. If you take one thing away, take this.

In the dividends lesson you learned the **payout ratio** — dividends divided by earnings (net income). A payout ratio over 100% is usually a red flag: the company is paying out more than it earns. So a beginner looking at a REIT sees something alarming: **REIT payout ratios are frequently well over 100%.** By the ordinary rule, that should scream "the dividend is about to be cut!"

It almost never is. The ordinary rule is simply *wrong for REITs*, and here's exactly why.

### Depreciation: the huge fake expense

**Depreciation** is an accounting expense that spreads the cost of a long-lived asset over its useful life. If a company buys a machine for $100,000 that lasts 10 years, accountants deduct roughly $10,000 of "depreciation" each year as an expense — even though no cash left the building in years 2 through 10. It's a **non-cash charge**: a bookkeeping entry, not money going out the door.

For most companies, depreciation is a modest line item. For a REIT it is enormous, because a REIT's assets are *buildings* — massive, expensive, long-lived things. Accounting rules force a REIT to depreciate its buildings year after year, subtracting a giant non-cash expense from its reported profit.

Here's the twist that breaks the ordinary ratio: **in the real world, well-located real estate often holds or rises in value over time, even as the accounting statements pretend it's steadily wearing out to zero.** So depreciation, for a REIT, is largely a fiction — a paper charge that crushes reported net income far below the actual cash the properties generate.

That's why net income is a lousy measure of a REIT's health, and why the industry uses a different number.

### FFO — funds from operations

To undo the distortion, REITs report **FFO (funds from operations)**. The idea is simple: start from net income and **add back the fake, non-cash depreciation** (plus a couple of other adjustments) to get a truer picture of the cash the properties actually threw off.

```
FFO = Net income
      + Depreciation & amortization (the non-cash charges)
      - Gains on property sales   (+ back any losses)
```

(We subtract one-time gains from *selling* buildings because FFO is meant to capture recurring operating cash, not lumpy sale profits.)

**Worked example (illustrative).** A REIT reports:

- Net income: **$20 million**
- Depreciation on its buildings: **$50 million**
- Gains from selling one property this year: **$5 million**

```
FFO = $20M + $50M - $5M = $65 million
```

Look at what happened. Reported net income was a measly **$20M**, but the real cash-generating power was **$65M** — more than three times larger. The $50M of depreciation was a paper charge, not lost cash. If this REIT paid out, say, **$40M** in dividends, the ordinary payout ratio would be $40M / $20M = **200%** (terrifying!), but the FFO payout ratio would be $40M / $65M ≈ **62%** — perfectly healthy.

<Callout type="key">

**Never judge a REIT by its net-income payout ratio.** Depreciation is a giant non-cash charge that pushes reported earnings far below true cash flow. Judge a REIT by its **FFO** (and AFFO, below) instead. A REIT paying out "200% of earnings" can be entirely safe when measured against FFO — and a REIT that looks fine on earnings can be stretched on FFO. This one substitution is the whole game.

</Callout>

### AFFO — the stricter cousin

FFO adds back *all* depreciation as if buildings never need a cent spent on them. But real buildings do need maintenance — roofs, elevators, parking lots, tenant improvements. **AFFO (adjusted funds from operations)** refines FFO by subtracting the recurring **capital expenditures** ("capex" — the ongoing cash spent to keep the properties rentable) and smoothing a few other items.

```
AFFO ≈ FFO - recurring maintenance capex (and other adjustments)
```

Think of it this way: **FFO** is the cash the properties generate; **AFFO** is the cash actually left over *after* keeping them in shape — which is closer to what the REIT can truly afford to pay you sustainably. There's no single official formula for AFFO (companies calculate it slightly differently), so it's less standardized than FFO, but many analysts consider it the better measure of *sustainable* dividend-paying capacity.

## The honest risks — what the marketing skips

REITs are pitched as "steady income from real estate." The income part is often true, but "steady" hides some sharp risks. A professional knows all three.

**1. Interest-rate sensitivity — the big one.** This surprises beginners most. REITs are unusually sensitive to **interest rates** (the cost of borrowing money, largely set by central-bank policy). Two reasons:

- REITs borrow heavily to buy buildings, so when rates rise, their interest costs rise and their profits get squeezed.
- REITs compete with bonds for income investors. When safe bonds suddenly pay 5% instead of 1%, a REIT yielding 4% looks less attractive, so its price falls until its yield is competitive again.

The clearest recent illustration: in **2022**, when central banks raised interest rates sharply to fight inflation, REITs fell hard as a group — often worse than the broad stock market — even though the underlying buildings were still collecting rent. Rising rates hit REIT *prices* directly.

<Callout type="warning">

The beginner trap here is subtle: REITs are sold as an "inflation hedge," yet in 2022 — a burst of high inflation — many REITs *dropped sharply*. Why? Because inflation triggered fast **rate hikes**, and in the short run the rate-hike pain overwhelmed the inflation-hedge benefit. "Inflation hedge" is a long-run tendency; "rate-sensitive" is a short-run reality. Both are true at once. Don't expect a REIT to rise the moment inflation prints high.

</Callout>

**2. Sector concentration.** A REIT focused on one property type lives or dies by that type's fortunes. The textbook case is **office REITs after 2020**: the shift to remote and hybrid work gutted demand for office space, and office-heavy REITs suffered badly with high vacancies and falling property values — even as, say, industrial (warehouse) and data-center REITs boomed. Owning "a REIT" is not owning "real estate broadly"; it's a bet on that REIT's specific corner of the market. This is a strong argument for a diversified REIT fund over a single specialized name.

**3. Leverage.** **Leverage** is the use of borrowed money to buy more assets than your own cash alone could. REITs use a lot of it — buildings are expensive, and debt is how you buy them. Leverage magnifies both gains and losses: when property values rise, a leveraged REIT gains more; when values fall or rates spike, the debt becomes a heavy burden that can force a REIT to cut its dividend, sell buildings at bad prices, or struggle to refinance. A REIT with too much debt is fragile precisely when times get hard.

<Callout type="note">

Notice how these three risks interlock. Rising rates make a REIT's *leverage* more expensive to service, which is most dangerous for the *sector* that's already struggling. That's why the real test of a REIT isn't the headline yield — it's whether its **FFO/AFFO comfortably covers the dividend** even after accounting for its debt costs. A fat yield on top of heavy leverage and thin FFO coverage is a dividend cut waiting to happen.

</Callout>

## What comes next: the FFO coverage test

This lesson is step one. The rest of the real-assets path builds directly on the reveal above. In the **company-REIT lab**, you'll take a real REIT's financials and do the thing that separates a beginner from someone who understands real assets: compute **FFO and AFFO from the bottom up**, then check whether they actually cover the dividend — the **FFO-based coverage** test.

That coverage test is to a REIT what the earnings-based coverage ratio was to an ordinary dividend stock in the last lesson: the honest answer to "can this company really afford what it's paying me?" Preview the mindset now — when you see a REIT's tempting yield, your reflex should not be "great income!" but "let me see the FFO coverage." That reflex is the whole point of what you're learning.

## Key takeaways

- A **paper asset** is a claim or promise (stock, bond, cash); a **real asset** is the physical thing itself (property, infrastructure, commodities, gold). Real assets *are* value; paper assets are promises about value.
- People hold real assets for three reasons: **inflation hedge** (they rise with prices over the long run), **income** (rent and fees), and **diversification** (they don't move in perfect lockstep with stocks).
- A **REIT** is a company that owns income-producing property and trades like a stock — letting you own real estate for the price of one share, with liquidity, no landlord duties, and built-in diversification.
- REITs must distribute **~90% of taxable income** as dividends, which is why their yields are high — but those dividends are usually taxed as **ordinary income**, so many investors hold REITs in tax-advantaged accounts.
- **Direct property** = control + effort; **a single REIT** = liquidity + convenience; **a REIT fund** = broad diversification. Different tools for different goals.
- The crucial reveal: **do not judge a REIT by its net-income payout ratio.** **Depreciation** is a giant non-cash charge that crushes reported earnings, so REITs report **FFO** (net income + depreciation − property-sale gains) and **AFFO** (FFO − maintenance capex) as truer measures of cash. A REIT "paying out 200% of earnings" can be perfectly safe on FFO.
- Honest risks: **interest-rate sensitivity** (2022's rate spike hit REITs hard, despite the "inflation hedge" label), **sector concentration** (office REITs post-2020), and **leverage** (heavy debt turns fragile when rates rise).
- The real test of a REIT's dividend is **FFO/AFFO coverage**, not the headline yield — which is exactly what the company-REIT lab will have you compute.
- This is educational material, not financial advice, and not a recommendation to buy any specific security. The discipline reduces to one habit: for a REIT, always check whether FFO covers the payout before you trust the yield.
