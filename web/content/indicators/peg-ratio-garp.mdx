## Why this matters

Imagine two stocks. The first trades at a **P/E ratio of 30** — the price-to-earnings ratio, meaning you pay 30 dollars for every 1 dollar of the company's annual profit. The second trades at a P/E of 15, half as much. The cheap-looking one is the second, right?

Not so fast. What if the first company's profits are growing 30% a year and the second's are barely growing at all? Suddenly the "expensive" stock might be the better deal, because you're paying a high price for a profit stream that's expanding fast. A bare P/E can't settle this. It tells you how expensive a stock is *today* but says nothing about how fast the underlying earnings are climbing.

The **PEG ratio** was built to close that gap. It takes the P/E and divides it by the growth rate, producing a single number that lets you compare a racehorse and a tortoise on the same scale. It is the signature screen of an investing style called **GARP — Growth At a Reasonable Price** — which refuses to overpay even for a wonderful growth story. This lesson explains the formula, the famous "1 = fair value" rule of thumb, who really invented it (not who you think), and — most importantly — the long list of ways the PEG ratio can quietly mislead you.

<Callout type="key">

This is educational content, not financial advice. The numbers in the charts and examples are illustrative teaching aids, not forecasts or recommendations. The goal is to make you understand *how* the PEG ratio works — and where it breaks — so you can think clearly for yourself.

</Callout>

## The formula

The PEG ratio is one short division:

`PEG = (P/E ratio) ÷ (annual EPS growth rate)`

A few definitions, since every term matters:

- **P/E ratio (price-to-earnings)** — the share price divided by **earnings per share (EPS)**, the company's annual profit chopped up per share. A P/E of 30 means the price is 30 times one year's per-share profit.
- **EPS growth rate** — how fast that per-share profit is expected to grow each year, expressed as a percentage.

The one catch that trips up beginners: you enter the growth rate as a **plain number, not a decimal**. A company growing 20% a year is divided by **20**, not by 0.20. Get this wrong and your PEG comes out 100 times too small.

Let's work two examples:

- A stock with a **P/E of 30** growing **30%** a year: `30 ÷ 30 = 1.0`.
- A stock with a **P/E of 15** growing **30%** a year: `15 ÷ 30 = 0.5`.

The second stock has the same growth but half the price tag relative to that growth — a PEG of 0.5 versus 1.0. By the PEG yardstick, it's the cheaper buy. Notice how the ratio rescued us: judged on P/E alone we'd just say "15 is cheaper than 30," but the PEG quantifies *how much growth you get for the price* in both cases.

## How to read it: the "≈ 1" heuristic

The rule of thumb that made the PEG famous is beautifully simple:

- **PEG ≈ 1 → roughly fairly valued.** The price you pay for growth is about right.
- **PEG `< 1` → potentially undervalued.** You're paying *less* than one unit of P/E per unit of growth — possibly a bargain.
- **PEG `> 1` → potentially overvalued.** You're paying *more* than the growth seems to justify.

When you're comparing similar stocks, **lower is generally better** — it means more growth per dollar of valuation.

But you must hold this rule loosely. The "PEG = 1 means fair" line is **deliberately a rough rule of thumb, not a law of nature.** Wikipedia, summarizing the convention, flatly calls the choice of 1 as the fair-value threshold "somewhat arbitrary." There is no deep theory that says 1 is exactly fair; it's a convenient, memorable round number that works *approximately* and only under certain assumptions.

<Callout type="warning">

Treat the magic number "1" as a conversation-starter, not an answer. The threshold depends on interest rates, the company's quality and durability, and how trustworthy the growth forecast is. A high-quality compounder at PEG 1.2 can easily be a better buy than a fragile one at PEG 0.7. Use the PEG to *rank and question*, never to mechanically buy or sell.

</Callout>

## Who really invented it (commonly misquoted — flag)

Almost everyone "knows" the PEG ratio belongs to **Peter Lynch.** Almost everyone is wrong about who *invented* it.

The PEG ratio was originated by **Mario Farina** in his **1969** book *A Beginner's Guide to Successful Investing in the Stock Market*. Lynch came two decades later. What Lynch did was **popularize** it — so thoroughly that the world now calls it "Lynch's PEG ratio." That's a credit for marketing, not for invention. It's a useful reminder that in finance, the person who explains an idea memorably often gets remembered over the person who first had it.

What Lynch actually contributed is the canonical one-liner. In *One Up on Wall Street* (1989), at page 199, he wrote:

> "The P/E ratio of any company that's fairly priced will equal its growth rate."

Read that carefully: "P/E equals growth rate" is exactly the same as saying **PEG = 1.** Lynch's sentence *is* the "≈ 1" heuristic, stated in plain English before anyone was calling it PEG.

### Who Peter Lynch was

Lynch ran **Fidelity's Magellan Fund from 1977 to 1990** — thirteen years. Over that stretch the fund is widely cited as averaging **about 29% annually** (the standard figure is 29.2%, sometimes rounded to ~28–29%), and it grew from roughly **$18 million to about $14 billion** in assets. He wrote *One Up on Wall Street* (1989) and *Beating the Street* (1993), and coined the term **"ten-bagger"** for a stock that rises tenfold. His investing philosophy — buy understandable businesses you encounter in everyday life, but only at a sensible price relative to their growth — is the spiritual home of GARP.

<Callout type="note">

The 29% figure is the standard citation, but treat any single headline return number with mild caution: averages can be computed several ways, and Magellan's assets ballooned over time, which makes the *typical investor's* experience differ from the headline fund return. The honest framing is "about 29% a year over 1977–1990" — an extraordinary record by any measure.

</Callout>

## GARP: the style the PEG belongs to

**GARP — Growth At a Reasonable Price** — is a hybrid. It borrows the **value investor's discipline** (never overpay; demand a margin of safety) and marries it to the **growth investor's appetite** (favor companies whose earnings are expanding faster than average). It sits squarely **between deep value and pure growth**:

- A **deep-value** investor buys cheap, slow, even unloved businesses, betting on a re-rating.
- A **pure-growth** investor buys the fastest growers and worries about price later — sometimes not at all.
- A **GARP** investor wants growth *and* refuses to pay any price for it.

The PEG is the **signature GARP screen**, with **PEG ≤ 1** a common target threshold. It's the perfect tool for the style because it bakes both halves into one number: the P/E (the "price" discipline) sits on top, the growth rate (the "growth" appetite) sits on the bottom.

<Callout type="warning">

A modern caveat: in recent years, with technology valuations elevated, a PEG below 1 has become genuinely rare. Many analysts now treat the strict "`< 1`" bar as **dated and context-dependent** rather than an absolute rule — the *relative* comparison (this stock's PEG versus its peers and its own history) often matters more than the raw number clearing some fixed line.

</Callout>

## The honest limitations — where the PEG lies to you

The PEG ratio's elegance hides a long list of failure modes. A beginner who treats it as a truth machine will get burned. Here are the big ones.

### 1. Garbage in, garbage out — it's only as good as the growth forecast

The entire denominator is a **forecast**. PEG hinges on a guess about future growth, and five-year analyst estimates are notorious for **over-optimistic growth projections.** If the forecast is too rosy, the PEG looks deceptively low and "cheap." Worse, the reverse can fool you too: a low PEG sometimes simply means **the market distrusts the forecast** — it's pricing the stock as if the rosy growth won't actually arrive. A bargain PEG and a value trap can look identical on the screen.

### 2. There's no standard recipe — the same stock has many PEGs

This is the trap that catches even professionals. Nothing is standardized:

- The **P/E** might be **trailing** (last 12 months' earnings) or **forward** (next year's estimate).
- The **growth rate** might be **1-year, 3-year, or 5-year.**

Mix and match, and the *same stock* yields wildly different PEGs. A widely-cited example: **Meta's** PEG has been quoted as **1.74 on a 4-year basis versus 0.86 on a 3-year basis** — one says "overvalued," the other says "bargain," for the identical company on the identical day. An older illustration: on **August 9, 2008**, IBM's PEG was reported as **1.26 by Yahoo Finance and 1.148 by NASDAQ** — same stock, same day, different methods.

**Always state the horizon and the P/E type.** A PEG quoted without saying "forward P/E, 5-year growth" is close to meaningless — you can't compare it to anything, and you can't know whether someone cherry-picked the inputs to make a stock look cheap.

</Callout>

### 3. Negative PEG is meaningless

If a company has **negative earnings** (a loss) or **declining/negative growth**, the formula spits out a negative number. A negative PEG tells you nothing useful — it's not "extra cheap." Just discard it; the ratio simply doesn't apply to unprofitable or shrinking companies.

### 4. It ignores most of the balance sheet and the business

The PEG looks only at price, earnings, and growth. It is **blind to**:

- **Debt** — two companies with identical PEGs can have wildly different risk if one is drowning in borrowings.
- **Cash flow** — earnings can be massaged; cash is harder to fake.
- **Dividends** — a dividend-paying stock looks artificially "expensive" on PEG because the payout isn't credited. (A variant called **PEGY** — PEG adjusted for dividend yield — exists to patch this.)
- **Return on equity (ROE)** and the **required rate of return.**

This makes the PEG a **poor fit for stable, slow-growing, dividend-paying firms** — utilities, mature consumer staples — where the growth denominator is tiny and the dividend, which PEG ignores, is much of the point.

### 5. A suspiciously smooth growth rate can be a warning, not a comfort

Because the PEG rewards steady, predictable growth, it can lull you toward businesses whose numbers look *too* perfect. A growth rate that never wobbles, year after year, is sometimes a **fraud signal** rather than a sign of quality — **Bernie Madoff's** impossibly smooth "returns" are the cautionary example. Real businesses are lumpy; suspiciously smooth ones deserve more scrutiny, not less.

## "Growth at any price": the dot-com lesson

GARP exists as a reaction to its opposite — **"growth at any price"** — the belief that growth itself justifies whatever multiple the market demands. The dot-com bubble is the textbook case of where that thinking leads, and it's the single best argument for the "reasonable price" half of GARP.

The **Nasdaq Composite peaked on March 10, 2000, at 5,048.62**, having risen roughly **600% from 1995** to that top. Valuations had decoupled from earnings entirely: the **Nasdaq's P/E reached around 200** — for comparison, Japan's Nikkei was near a P/E of ~80 at its own 1991 bubble peak. Many companies went public with **no profits at all**, which means no earnings, which means no meaningful P/E and no meaningful PEG. The growth story *was* the entire investment thesis.

Then it broke. The index **fell about 78%** to its trough by **October 2002** (the Nasdaq-100 sat near 1,114 on October 9, 2002), erasing roughly **$5 trillion** in market value.

The individual stories show the mechanism. **Qualcomm** rose **+2,619% in 1999** alone. **MicroStrategy** ran from about **$7 to roughly $333**, then lost about **62% in a single day** after a March 2000 revenue restatement. **Cisco** lost about **80%** of its value. Pure failures abounded — **Pets.com, Webvan, Boo.com** — and only about **48% of dot-com firms survived to 2004.**

<Callout type="key">

The core lesson connects straight back to the PEG: **when a stock prices in flawless growth — a very high multiple, a PEG well above 1 — any earnings miss triggers a double blow.** First the earnings get cut. Then the *multiple itself* compresses, because the market stops believing the perfect-growth story. The damage multiplies. Paying a reasonable price (a PEG near or below 1) is what gives you a cushion when reality disappoints. That cushion is the entire point of GARP.

</Callout>

## Putting it to work

1. **Use the PEG to rank, not to rule.** It's a fast first-pass screen for "am I overpaying for this growth?" — not a buy/sell button.
2. **Always state your inputs.** Write down "forward P/E, 5-year growth" (or whatever you used). An unlabeled PEG is untrustworthy.
3. **Interrogate the denominator.** Where does the growth forecast come from? Is it analyst optimism? Is a *low* PEG actually the market telling you it doesn't believe the forecast?
4. **Discard negative PEGs** and don't lean on PEG for stable, dividend-heavy firms — reach for other tools there.
5. **Check what PEG ignores by hand** — debt, cash flow, ROE, dividends, durability — before you act on any ratio.
6. **Remember the dot-com lesson.** A PEG far above 1 means the price already assumes everything goes right. Demand a reasonable price so a stumble doesn't become a catastrophe.

## Key takeaways

- **PEG = P/E ÷ growth rate**, with the growth rate entered as a plain number (20% growth → divide by 20). It normalizes the P/E so fast and slow growers can be compared on one scale.
- The famous heuristic: **PEG ≈ 1 is roughly fair, `< 1` potentially cheap, `> 1` potentially expensive** — but the "1" threshold is openly **arbitrary**, a rule of thumb, not a law.
- It was **invented by Mario Farina (1969)**, not Peter Lynch. **Lynch popularized it** in *One Up on Wall Street* (1989, p. 199): *"The P/E ratio of any company that's fairly priced will equal its growth rate"* — which simply restates PEG = 1.
- **GARP (Growth At a Reasonable Price)** blends value discipline with growth appetite; the PEG (target **≤ 1**) is its signature screen, though a sub-1 PEG has grown rare in today's elevated valuations.
- The PEG's weaknesses are serious: it depends entirely on an **optimistic-prone growth forecast**, has **no standard inputs** (same stock, many PEGs — Meta 0.86 vs 1.74), is **meaningless when negative**, and **ignores debt, cash flow, dividends, and ROE**. A *too*-smooth growth rate can even be a fraud flag.
- The **dot-com bust** (Nasdaq peak 5,048.62 in March 2000, ~78% crash, ~$5 trillion lost, P/E near 200) is the cautionary tale: pay a reasonable price for growth, because a stock priced for perfection gets hit twice — lower earnings *and* a collapsing multiple — when reality disappoints.
