## Read this first

## A company, a stock, and a share

A **company** is a business — it sells things, earns money, owns stuff, owes debts. To raise money, a company can slice ownership of itself into many small, equal pieces and sell them to the public.

- A **stock** is ownership in a company. If you own its stock, you own a slice of that actual business — a claim on its assets and its future profits.
- A **share** is one unit of that stock. "I own 10 shares of Apple" means you own ten of the little equal slices Apple was divided into. (People say "stock" for the idea and "shares" for the countable units, but in everyday talk they blur together.)

The everyday picture: a company is a giant pizza. Shares are the slices. Buy a slice and you're a part-owner of the whole pizza — you don't get to walk into headquarters, but if the pizza grows, your slice is worth more, and you get a say (a tiny vote) in big decisions.

## Ticker, exchange, and price

- A **ticker** (or ticker symbol) is a company's short code for trading — Apple is `AAPL`, Samsung Electronics is `005930`, Coca-Cola is `KO`. It's just a nickname so buyers and sellers know exactly which stock they mean.
- A **stock exchange** is the marketplace where shares are bought and sold — the New York Stock Exchange, Nasdaq, the Korea Exchange (KRX). You never go there in person; it's a computer system matching buyers with sellers.
- The **price** of a share is simply whatever a buyer and a seller most recently agreed on. It moves all day as opinions and news change. Price is set by the crowd, second by second — not by the company.

<Callout type="note">
A share's price on its own tells you almost nothing about whether a company is "expensive" or "cheap." A $500 stock is not more expensive than a $5 stock in any meaningful way — it depends on how many shares exist and how much the business earns. Later lessons make this precise; for now, just know that **price ≠ value**.
</Callout>

## A brokerage account: how you actually buy

You can't call a company and buy its shares directly. You go through a middleman called a **broker** (or **brokerage**) — a licensed firm that holds your account and places your buy/sell requests on the exchange for you. Think of it as a bank account that can hold investments instead of just cash. In the US these are firms like Fidelity, Schwab, or Vanguard; in Korea, 키움증권, 토스증권, 한국투자증권, and others.

Opening one is a lot like opening a bank account: you sign up, verify your identity, and link your bank so you can **fund** the account (move money in). Once there's cash in it, you can buy shares. When you sell, the cash lands back in the account, and you can transfer it out to your bank.

<Callout type="note">
This lesson is only the vocabulary. The full walk-through of *choosing* a broker, funding, and placing your very first order lives in its own lesson later in the path — you don't need it yet.
</Callout>

## An order: telling the broker what to do

An **order** is your instruction to the broker. The two you'll meet first:

- A **market order** says "buy (or sell) right now, at whatever the going price is." Fast and simple; you take the current price.
- A **limit order** says "buy (or sell) only at this price or better." You control the price but the trade only happens if the market reaches it.

The picture: a market order is grabbing the item off the shelf and paying the sticker price. A limit order is telling the shop "hold it for me — I'll buy only if it drops to $40."

## Dividends: getting paid to hold

Some companies share their profits with owners by paying out cash periodically — a **dividend**. If you own 100 shares and the company pays a $1 dividend per share, $100 lands in your account, just for holding. Not every company pays one (many young, fast-growing companies reinvest everything instead), and dividends are never guaranteed — a company can cut them in a bad year.

The picture: if shares are slices of a pizza *business*, a dividend is the business handing each slice-owner a small slice of this year's *profit*, in cash.

## Bonds: lending instead of owning

A **stock** makes you an *owner*. A **bond** makes you a *lender*. When you buy a bond, you're lending money — to a government or a company — in exchange for regular interest payments and the promise to return your original money on a set date.

- Bonds are generally **steadier** than stocks: as a lender you get paid before owners do, and you know the schedule in advance.
- In exchange for that safety, bonds usually **grow your money more slowly** than stocks over the long run.

The picture: owning stock is being a part-owner of the restaurant (big upside if it booms, you lose if it fails). Owning a bond is being the bank that lent the restaurant money (you get steady interest and your loan back, but you don't share in the boom).

<Callout type="key">
The single most important pair on this whole site: **stocks = ownership, higher risk, higher long-run growth. Bonds = lending, steadier, lower growth.** Almost every strategy is some thoughtful mix of these two ideas.
</Callout>

## Funds, index funds, and ETFs: buying a basket

Picking individual companies is hard. So most people buy a **fund** — a single product that pools many investors' money and holds *many* stocks (or bonds) at once. Buy one share of the fund and you instantly own a tiny piece of everything inside it. That's **diversification**: not betting everything on one company.

- An **index** is just a *list* that measures a slice of the market — the S&P 500 tracks 500 large US companies, for example. You can't buy an index directly; it's a yardstick, like a thermometer.
- An **index fund** is a fund that simply owns whatever is on that list, in the same proportions — no one trying to pick winners, so its fees are tiny.
- An **ETF** (exchange-traded fund) is a fund you buy and sell on an exchange all day, by ticker, exactly like a single stock. Most index funds beginners buy today are ETFs.

The picture: an index is the *recipe*, a fund is the *meal* cooked from it, and "ETF vs. mutual fund" is just the *container* it's served in. (There's a whole lesson — *Index Funds & ETFs* — waiting for you later in the path once you want the details.)

## Two words you'll see constantly

- **Portfolio** — everything you own, taken together. Your mix of stocks, bonds, and funds *is* your portfolio.
- **Risk** — in investing, this mostly means how much your money can drop in value along the way, and how sure you are of the outcome. More potential growth almost always comes with more risk. Managing risk — not chasing the highest return — is what separates investors who last from those who don't.

## Key takeaways

- **Stock/share** = ownership of a company, sliced into units. **Ticker** = its trading code. **Exchange** = the marketplace. **Broker** = the firm that holds your account and places your trades.
- **Order**: *market* = buy/sell now at the going price; *limit* = only at a price you set.
- **Dividend** = cash a company pays owners from profits (optional, not guaranteed).
- **Bond** = lending your money for steady interest — steadier than stocks, slower-growing.
- **Fund / index fund / ETF** = one product holding many investments, so you diversify instantly. An **index** is the list it follows.
- **Portfolio** = all you own; **risk** = how much it can fall and how uncertain the outcome. More return normally means more risk.
