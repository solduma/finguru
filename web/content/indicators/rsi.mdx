## The one-sentence version

RSI asks a simple question — *"of all the price movement recently, how much of
it was upward?"* — and squeezes the answer onto a scale from 0 to 100.

That's it. Everything else is learning to read that number honestly.

## Origin

The **Relative Strength Index** was created by **J. Welles Wilder Jr.**, a
mechanical engineer turned technical analyst, in his landmark 1978 book *New
Concepts in Technical Trading Systems*. That one book also gave us **ATR**,
**ADX/DMI**, and **Parabolic SAR** — so RSI was designed from the start to live
alongside other tools, not alone.

Wilder's default lookback is **14 periods**. "14-day" is just shorthand: RSI
works on any timeframe — 14 hourly bars, 14 weekly bars, whatever your chart
shows.

<Callout type="warning">
**RSI is not "relative strength."** Despite the name, RSI has nothing to do with
comparing two assets.

- **RSI** measures *one* security's own momentum, bounded 0–100.
- **Relative strength** (the RS *comparison line*) divides one asset's price by
  another — e.g. AAPL vs. the S&P 500 — and is used for ranking and rotation.

They share a word and nothing else. Beginners conflate them constantly.
</Callout>

## The math

Two formulas sit at the core, and they're identical across every authoritative
source:

```
RS  = Average Gain / Average Loss     (over N periods)

RSI = 100 − ( 100 / (1 + RS) )
```

Each bar contributes either a **gain** or a **loss**, both written as positive
numbers:

- Up bar → Gain = (close − prior close), Loss = 0
- Down bar → Gain = 0, Loss = (prior close − close)

### Wilder's smoothing — the part beginners get wrong

The averages aren't simple averages after the first step. Wilder uses a
**two-stage** process:

- **First value (initialization)** — a *simple* average of the first N gains and
  losses:
  - `First Avg Gain = (sum of gains over 14 periods) / 14`
  - `First Avg Loss = (sum of losses over 14 periods) / 14`
- **Every value after that** — a *recursive smoothing*:
  - `Avg Gain = (prev Avg Gain × 13 + current Gain) / 14`
  - `Avg Loss = (prev Avg Loss × 13 + current Loss) / 14`

The general form is `new = (prev × (N−1) + current) / N`.

<Callout type="note">
**Why this matters:** Wilder's smoothing is a *modified exponential moving
average* with a small smoothing factor (α = 1/N ≈ 0.071 for 14 periods). It
reacts more slowly and smoothly than a plain 14-period average, carrying a long,
gently-decaying memory of past data. It also explains why two charting platforms
can show slightly different RSI values on the first few bars — they converge as
more data accumulates.
</Callout>

### A worked numeric example

*(Illustrative numbers, built straight from the formula.)*

Say over the first 14 periods, gains sum to **7.0** and losses sum to **3.5**:

- First Avg Gain = 7.0 / 14 = **0.500**
- First Avg Loss = 3.5 / 14 = **0.250**
- RS = 0.500 / 0.250 = **2.0**
- RSI = 100 − (100 / (1 + 2.0)) = **66.7**

Now period 15 closes **up** by 0.80 (Gain = 0.80, Loss = 0):

- Avg Gain = (0.500 × 13 + 0.80) / 14 = **0.5214**
- Avg Loss = (0.250 × 13 + 0) / 14 = **0.2321**
- RS = 0.5214 / 0.2321 = **2.246**
- RSI = 100 − (100 / 3.246) = **69.2**

The up bar lifted RSI from 66.7 toward overbought — but smoothing kept the step
measured rather than abrupt.

**Edge cases:** no losses → RSI = 100; no gains → RSI = 0; equal gains and losses
→ RSI = 50.

## How to read it

### Overbought and oversold

Wilder's original thresholds:

- Above **70** → "overbought"
- Below **30** → "oversold"

In strong trends, many traders tighten to **80 / 20** to filter for only the
strongest momentum and reduce signal frequency.

### The centerline (50)

The 50 level is a trend-bias divider. Persistently above 50 = bullish momentum
bias; persistently below 50 = bearish bias; hovering near 50 = no real trend.

### Divergence

- **Bullish divergence** — price makes a *lower low* while RSI makes a *higher
  low.* Momentum is quietly strengthening; a reversal up may be coming.
- **Bearish divergence** — price makes a *higher high* while RSI makes a *lower
  high.* Momentum is fading.

<Callout type="warning">
Divergence is **unreliable on its own in a strong trend.** A powerful uptrend can
flash bearish divergence many times before it finally tops. Divergence is most
trustworthy *after* an overbought/oversold reading, and confirmed by price.
</Callout>

### Failure swings (Wilder's own signal — often overlooked)

These depend only on RSI's own shape, not on price:

- **Bullish failure swing:** RSI drops below 30 → bounces above 30 → pulls back
  but **holds above 30** → then breaks above its prior RSI peak.
- **Bearish failure swing:** RSI rises above 70 → drops below 70 → bounces but
  **fails to reach 70 again** → then breaks below its prior RSI low.

### The biggest pitfall: trends vs. ranges

Overbought/oversold logic works best in **sideways, ranging markets.** In a
strong trend it betrays you: **RSI can stay "overbought" for weeks in an uptrend**
(and "oversold" for weeks in a downtrend). An extreme reading alone is *not* a
reversal signal — in a trend it can be a sign of strength.

## Settings

**Period.** The default is 14. Shorter (7–9) = more sensitive, more signals, more
noise. Longer (21–25) = smoother, fewer signals. Short-term traders sometimes use
a **2-period RSI** with 90/10 extremes.

**Thresholds aren't sacred.** More volatile instruments and shorter timeframes
often justify wider 80/20 bands.

### RSI's range shifts between bull and bear markets

A professional-level insight: oscillators don't actually travel the full 0–100 —
they live in narrower, trend-dependent bands.

- **Constance Brown** (*Technical Analysis for the Trading Professional*):
  - Bull market: RSI roughly **40–90**, with **40–50 acting as support.**
  - Bear market: RSI roughly **10–60**, with **50–60 acting as resistance.**
- **Andrew Cardwell** (Brown's mentor) framed it as uptrends trading ~**40–80**
  and downtrends ~**20–60.**

The exact bands differ by author, but the practical lesson is the same and it's
huge: **in an uptrend, RSI rarely reaches the classic "30."** Pullbacks bottom
near 40–50. So waiting for "oversold 30" in a bull market means **missing every
entry.**

## Strengths and common mistakes

**Strengths:** normalized 0–100 (comparable across instruments and timeframes),
catches momentum shifts early, and can even form its own trendlines and patterns
before price does.

<Callout type="warning">
**The classic beginner mistakes:**

1. **Blindly shorting "overbought" (or buying "oversold").** The single most
   expensive error. RSI stays pinned at extremes throughout strong trends.
2. **Using rigid 30/70 in a trending market.** See Brown/Cardwell above — the
   operating range shifts.
3. **Trusting divergence alone in a strong trend.**
4. **Treating RSI as a standalone system.** It's a confirmation tool.
</Callout>

## Combining RSI with other tools

- **With trend (moving averages):** the canonical fix for the overbought trap.
  Use a long MA (e.g. the **200-day**) to set trend direction, then take only
  trend-aligned signals — in an uptrend, buy oversold RSI pullbacks and *ignore*
  overbought readings.
- **With support/resistance:** an RSI extreme matters far more when price is also
  at a key level. Confluence beats any single signal.
- **With price action:** confirm RSI signals with candlestick reversals or
  breakouts before acting.
- **With other Wilder tools:** use **ADX** to judge whether the market is
  trending (use trend logic) or ranging (use 30/70 logic), and **ATR** to size
  stops.

## Practice

- Overlay 9-, 14-, and 25-period RSI on one chart to *feel* the
  sensitivity-vs-smoothness trade-off.
- Pull up one ranging period and one strong trending period; watch how 30/70
  behaves in each until the difference is instinct.
- **Build RSI yourself in a spreadsheet** with the two-stage smoothing, then
  reconcile it against a charting platform — this cements the math.
- In an uptrend, draw the 40 line by hand and watch pullbacks respect it.
- Journal real failure swings and divergences: which resolved, which failed, and
  what the trend context was.
