## The last mile

Every other lesson ends at a *decision* — buy this index fund, hold this dividend stock, size this position. This one covers the part almost no course teaches: turning that decision into shares you actually own, without expensive rookie mistakes. It's the least glamorous lesson and the one most beginners get wrong.

<Callout type="warning">
This is **educational, not financial advice, and not a recommendation** to open any specific account or buy any security. Product names appear only as widely-known examples so the steps are concrete. Rules, fees, and account types change and vary by country and by person — verify the current details with the provider and, for anything tax-related, a qualified professional before you act.
</Callout>

## Step 1 — Choose a broker (the account, not the app)

A **broker** is the licensed firm that holds your account and routes your orders to the exchange. For a long-term investor buying funds and stocks, you're choosing on a short list of criteria — not on which app has the flashiest screen:

- **Cost** — commissions on stock/ETF trades are **$0 at most major brokers now**, so the fee that actually matters is the **expense ratio of the funds you'll buy** (that's the lesson from *Index Funds & ETFs* and the cost-drag lab), plus any account or FX fees.
- **What it lets you hold** — for most beginners, plain stocks and ETFs. You do **not** need options or margin approval, and you should not enable them by default.
- **The tax-advantaged accounts it offers** — see Step 3; this often matters more than any feature.
- **Trustworthiness** — a regulated, established broker with proper investor protection in your country. Boring is good here.

<Callout type="note">
**Korea (KR):** major retail brokers include 키움증권, 토스증권, 미래에셋증권, 삼성증권, and 한국투자증권. For US or global stocks you'll typically use the broker's *해외주식(overseas stock)* service. **US:** Fidelity, Charles Schwab, and Vanguard are the common low-cost, full-feature choices. These are examples for orientation, not endorsements.
</Callout>

## Step 2 — Open and fund the account

Opening a brokerage account is close to opening a bank account, and mostly done in-app:

1. **Verify your identity** — a government ID and, in Korea, typically a linked bank account for 계좌 인증. (KR minors and foreign residents have extra steps.)
2. **Link a bank account** so you can move cash in and out.
3. **Fund it** — transfer the amount you've decided to invest. Start with an amount you can leave invested for years; don't move your emergency fund.
4. **For overseas stocks (KR → US):** you'll **exchange KRW into USD** (환전) inside the broker, which carries a small spread; some brokers offer 통합증거금 (integrated margin) that auto-converts. That FX cost is a real, recurring friction — factor it in.

<Callout type="key">
Fund the account with money that has a **job and a time horizon** you set *before* you log in. The account is just plumbing; the decision of *how much* and *for how long* was made in the risk-management and allocation lessons — not in the heat of a rising market.
</Callout>

## Step 3 — Use the right *account type* (this quietly beats stock-picking)

*Which account* you buy inside often matters more to your final wealth than *which fund* you pick, because tax-advantaged accounts let more of your money compound. This is the highest-leverage, most-overlooked decision for a beginner.

- **Korea (KR):**
  - **ISA (개인종합자산관리계좌)** — a wrapper that shelters investment gains up to a limit; good general-purpose tax-advantaged bucket.
  - **연금저축 / IRP (개인형퇴직연금)** — retirement accounts with a **year-end tax deduction (세액공제)** on contributions; the trade-off is your money is locked for retirement.
  - A plain **위탁계좌 (general brokerage account)** for everything else.
- **United States (US):**
  - **401(k)** — employer plan, often with a **matching contribution** (free money — capture the full match first).
  - **IRA / Roth IRA** — individual retirement accounts; traditional defers tax, Roth grows tax-free.
  - A plain **taxable brokerage account** for goals before retirement.

<Callout type="note">
General rule of thumb, not personalized advice: capture any **employer match** first, then fill tax-advantaged retirement space, then use a taxable/general account for the rest. The lock-up on retirement accounts is the price of the tax break — match it to money you truly won't need until then.
</Callout>

## Step 4 — Place the order without fat-fingering it

This is the moment of truth. On the order screen you'll set a handful of fields. Go slowly the first time.

- **Ticker / symbol** — the exact code for what you're buying (e.g. `VOO`, `069500` for a KOSPI 200 ETF). Double-check it; similar tickers are a classic mistake.
- **Buy or sell**, and **quantity** — number of shares (some brokers allow **fractional shares**, useful when one share costs more than you want to commit).
- **Order type** — the important choice:
  - **Market order** — fills right now at the going price. Simple, but on a thin or fast-moving stock you might pay a bit more than the last quote you saw.
  - **Limit order** — fills only at your price or better. You control the price; it may not fill if the market never reaches it. For a beginner buying a liquid ETF, a limit order a cent or two above the current ask gives control without much risk of missing the fill.
- **Time in force** — usually **DAY** (cancels if unfilled by close) is fine to start.

Then **review the confirmation screen** — quantity, price, estimated cost, and fees — before you submit. Read it. This one habit prevents the most expensive beginner errors.

<Callout type="warning">
**The bid-ask spread is a hidden cost.** The price to buy (ask) is always a touch above the price to sell (bid); the gap is the **spread**, and it's wide on illiquid securities. On a huge, liquid ETF it's negligible; on a tiny stock it can quietly cost you real money on every round trip. Prefer liquid instruments, and a **limit order** when the spread looks wide.
</Callout>

### A worked example

Say you decided to invest ₩1,000,000 into a broad market ETF trading at ₩10,000, and you want price control. You place a **limit buy** for **100 shares at ₩10,010**. If the market is at or below ₩10,010, it fills for about ₩1,001,000 (plus any small fee); if the price runs away above your limit, it simply doesn't fill and you reassess — you were never forced to chase.

## Step 5 — After you buy: the part that actually determines returns

The trade is the easy part. What you do *after* decides your outcome:

- **Automate contributions** — set a recurring transfer/purchase (the dollar-cost-averaging lesson). Consistency beats timing for almost everyone.
- **Rebalance on a schedule, not on emotion** — once or twice a year, nudge your mix back to your target (the asset-allocation lesson). This enforces "sell high, buy low" mechanically.
- **Mind taxes on selling** — in a taxable/general account, **selling at a gain can trigger tax**; holding periods and account type change the bill. In Korea, domestic listed stock and overseas stock are taxed differently (and rules change) — check current rules. This is exactly where tax-advantaged accounts (Step 3) earn their keep.
- **Keep costs visible** — periodically re-check the expense ratios you're paying; the cost-drag lab showed how a small fee compounds into a large sum over decades.

<Callout type="key">
The whole site funnels to a simple, unglamorous routine: **decide your mix and horizon → buy low-cost instruments in the right account → automate contributions → rebalance occasionally → leave it alone.** The excitement is in the learning; the results come from the boring consistency.
</Callout>

## The honest caveats

- **This is education, not advice.** Your situation — income, debt, taxes, goals, country — is specific, and none of it is personalized here. For tax and retirement-account decisions especially, confirm current rules and consider a qualified professional.
- **No trade is a "sure thing."** Everything you buy can fall; the lessons on risk management and drawdowns are what keep a bad year from becoming a permanent loss.
- **Start small and deliberate.** Your first order is as much about learning the mechanics calmly as about the money. A tiny, careful first trade beats a large, rushed one.

## Key takeaways

- **Broker choice** for a long-term investor is about cost, the account types offered, and trust — not app flash. Stock/ETF commissions are usually $0; the **fund expense ratio** is the fee that matters.
- **Fund** with money that already has a horizon you set beforehand — never the emergency fund.
- **Account type often beats security selection:** capture an employer match, use tax-advantaged wrappers (KR: ISA / 연금저축 / IRP; US: 401(k) / IRA), then a taxable/general account.
- **Placing the order:** check the ticker, pick **market** (fast) vs **limit** (price control), watch the **bid-ask spread**, and always read the confirmation.
- **After buying** is what matters: automate contributions, rebalance on a schedule, and mind taxes and fees. Educational only — verify current rules and get professional advice for your own situation.
