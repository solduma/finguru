## The one-sentence version

Day trading — buying and selling the same security within a single day to profit from short-term price moves — is one of the few activities where decades of data on entire national markets agree: the large majority of people who try it lose money after costs, and only a tiny sliver are durably profitable.

This is not a motivational warning or an opinion. It is the finding of researchers who got their hands on the *complete* trading records of whole stock exchanges and followed every account for years. This lesson is deliberately placed *before* any day-trading technique, because the most important thing a beginner can know about day trading is the base rate: what happens to people like you, on average, when they do this. If you understand that first, every subsequent lesson lands differently.

<Callout type="warning">

This is educational material, not financial advice or encouragement. Most of the evidence below describes how badly things go for typical day traders. Read it as a sober risk briefing — the kind a responsible mentor gives *before* handing over the keys, not after.

</Callout>

## What "the odds" actually look like

The strongest evidence comes not from surveys or broker marketing but from **regulatory-grade transaction data** — every trade, every account, no self-selection.

### Taiwan: the entire market, 15 years

The canonical work is by Brad Barber, Yi-Tsung Lee, Yu-Jane Liu, and Terrance Odean. In *"Do Day Traders Rationally Learn About Their Ability?"* they analyzed the **complete transaction records of the entire Taiwan Stock Exchange over 15 years, from 1992 to 2006.** That is the whole population of traders, not a sample — which is why it carries unusual weight.

Their headline findings, stated plainly:

- Aggregate day-trader performance, **net of fees, was negative in every one of the 15 years** studied. Not most years — *every* year.
- **"The vast majority of day traders are unprofitable."**
- **Less than 1%** of day traders could **predictably** earn positive abnormal returns net of fees. Even with a generous definition of "skilled," predictably-profitable traders were **less than 3%** of all day traders.

A related Barber–Odean paper, *"The Cross-Section of Speculator Skill"* (*Journal of Financial Markets*, 2014), reaches the same conclusion from the same Taiwan data: a tiny minority show genuine, persistent skill, and the rest lose. Skill exists — it is just rare, and you cannot assume you are in that group before you have years of net-of-cost results proving it.

### Brazil: those who really committed

A natural objection is "those people just quit too early." The Brazilian study answers it directly. In *"Day Trading for a Living?"* (Chague, De-Losso & Giovannetti, 2019), researchers studied Brazilian **equity-index futures (mini-index) day traders from 2012 to 2017**, and zoomed in on the *committed* ones — people who day-traded for **more than 300 days.**

Among those persisters:

- **97% lost money.**
- Only **1.1%** earned more than the Brazilian minimum wage (about **US$16 a day**).
- Only **0.5%** earned more than the starting salary of a bank teller (about **US$54 a day**).
- The single best performer in the whole group averaged about **US$310 a day** — one person, out of thousands.

Persistence did not rescue them. The people who treated it most seriously, who showed up for hundreds of days, overwhelmingly still lost.

<Callout type="key">

The two hardest-to-dispute anchors, both from complete-market data: in Taiwan, **fewer than 1% of day traders are predictably profitable net of fees**; in Brazil, **97% of those who persisted more than 300 days lost money**. Memorize these two, because everything else in the marketing world has to be reconciled against them.

</Callout>

### A note on the "90% / 10% / 1%" numbers

You will constantly see round numbers like "90% of day traders lose," "10% succeed," "1% get rich." These are *commonly cited* and broadly consistent with the academic picture — but the precise "10%" and "1%" figures usually trace back to trading-education websites and popular press (a frequently-shared Forbes piece from 2017 repeated an education site's claim), **not** peer-reviewed studies. So treat the round numbers as folklore that happens to point the right way, and anchor your beliefs to the defensible academic facts: "&lt;1% predictably profitable" (Taiwan) and "97% lost money among persisters" (Brazil).

## Why the math is stacked against you: the cost drag

Why do the odds look like this? Start with costs, because day trading maximizes the one variable that destroys returns: **turnover** (how often you trade). Every round trip — one buy and one sell — pays a toll, and a day trader pays it dozens or hundreds of times where a long-term investor pays it once.

- **The bid/ask spread.** There is a slightly higher price you can buy at (the *ask*) and a slightly lower price you can sell at (the *bid*); the gap is the *spread*. With market orders you buy at the ask and sell at the bid, so a round trip costs the spread *even if the price never moved.*
- **Commissions and payment for order flow.** Even at "zero-commission" US brokers the cost has not vanished — it reappears as **payment for order flow** (the broker is paid to route your order to a market maker, who earns it back on the spread) and in worse fills. Many markets still charge explicit fees outright.
- **Slippage.** The price you actually get is often worse than intended, especially in fast or thinly-traded markets — and the faster you must act, which is the whole premise of day trading, the more slippage you eat.
- **Transaction taxes and tools.** Many jurisdictions levy transaction taxes that scale with how much you trade (the Taiwan study nets one out), and real-time data feeds plus charting software can run tens to hundreds of dollars a month — a fixed cost you must out-earn first.

In the Taiwan data this shows up directly: **unprofitable traders accounted for about 72% of all day-trading volume.** The people losing money are, collectively, doing most of the trading — and paying most of the costs.

## Leverage: the accelerant

Day trading is usually done with **leverage** — borrowing from your broker so you can control a position larger than your cash. Leverage is symmetric in theory (it multiplies gains and losses alike) but asymmetric in practice, because a wipeout ends the game permanently.

- Under the US **Regulation T**, the initial *overnight* margin leverage is capped at **2:1** — a $25,000 account can hold up to ~$50,000 of stock overnight.
- For *intraday* trading, brokers commonly extend **"day-trading buying power" up to 4:1**, which must be reduced back toward 2:1 by the close. So that same $25,000 account might command **~$100,000 of intraday exposure.**

At 4:1, a mere **1% adverse move** on the full position is a **4% hit to your equity**, and a 5% move against you wipes out 20%. Recall the brutal arithmetic of recovery — a deep loss needs a far larger gain to break even, and leverage drags you there fast.

<Callout type="warning">

Leverage does not improve your edge — it only enlarges the consequences of having one or not. If your unleveraged strategy loses money after costs (which the evidence says is the common case), leverage simply helps you lose it faster.

</Callout>

## The US rulebook: the Pattern Day Trader rule

If you day-trade US stocks in a margin account, you will run into FINRA's **Pattern Day Trader (PDT)** rule. Get the details right, because they are widely misstated.

- **Who it applies to:** you're flagged as a pattern day trader if you make **four or more day trades within five business days**, *and* those day trades make up **more than 6% of your total trades** in that window, in a **margin account.**
- **The money requirement:** a flagged pattern day trader must keep **minimum equity of $25,000**, and that money **must already be in the account before** you day-trade on any given day.
- **The legal basis:** it sits under **FINRA Rule 4210** (formerly NASD Rule 2520); the SEC approved the $25,000 minimum on **February 27, 2001.**

<Callout type="warning">

**Time-sensitive — verify before relying on this.** FINRA has adopted **new intraday margin requirements**, effective **June 4, 2026, with a transition period running to October 20, 2027,** which replace the legacy day-trading margin framework. The $25,000 / pattern-day-trader concept persists, but specifics are changing. This rule is also **US/FINRA-specific** — it is not a timeless global law. Always confirm the *current* rules with your broker before acting.

</Callout>

## Time, effort, and the tax bite

Even setting losses aside, day trading is a demanding full-time job, and it is taxed unfavorably. In the US, **short-term capital gains** (on assets held under a year — and day-trade gains are held for minutes) are taxed as **ordinary income**, generally at higher rates than **long-term capital gains**, so even rare winning years are skimmed harder than a patient investor's. And it is screen-bound work performed in direct competition with **high-frequency trading (HFT)** firms (automated systems trading in microseconds) and institutions with faster connections, lower costs, and better information than you. You are the slowest, most expensive participant at the table.

## Survivorship bias: why the gurus look so good

If 97% lose and most quit, why does your feed overflow with people who clearly *won*? Because of **survivorship bias** — the systematic error of judging a process only by the visible survivors while the failures disappear from view.

The Taiwan data supplies the silent denominator the marketing hides: **more than 75% of day traders quit within two years**, and — strikingly — **unprofitable traders quit at nearly the same rate as profitable ones.** Losing money does not reliably make people stop. So the population is constantly churning, and the few who happen to be up at any moment become the loudest voices, while the vastly larger group who lost and left say nothing.

When you evaluate a "guru," watch for the tells of survivorship marketing:

- **Cherry-picked winning trades** — screenshots of big wins, never a full ledger.
- **No audited, net-of-cost returns** over a long period. "Up 300%!" with no time frame, no starting capital, and no costs is not a track record.
- **The course, signal subscription, or affiliate link is the *actual* business.** If someone could reliably compound money trading, selling a $499 course to strangers would be a strange use of their time. Follow where the revenue really comes from.

<Callout type="note">

This is not a claim that *nobody* can day-trade profitably — the data clearly shows a small minority do. It is a claim about **base rates and proof**: the prior probability is heavily against any given beginner, and a credible track record means audited, net-of-cost, multi-year results, not a montage of green candles.

</Callout>

## So what should a beginner actually do with this?

Not necessarily "never trade." The honest takeaways are about *sequencing* and *evidence*:

1. Treat the base rate as your starting assumption: until proven otherwise, you are in the ~97%, not the &lt;1%.
2. If you still want to learn, study **risk management and trading psychology first**, then **paper-trade** (practice with fake money) long enough to measure your real, net-of-cost results.
3. Demand the same proof from anyone selling you a method — audited, net-of-fee, long-horizon returns — that you'd demand of yourself, and compare honestly against the low-effort alternative: a diversified index fund held for years, which beats the *average* day trader in the data without the screen time, leverage, or stress.

## Key takeaways

- **The odds are not folklore — they're measured.** Using complete-market data, fewer than 1% of Taiwanese day traders were predictably profitable net of fees (1992–2006), and 97% of committed Brazilian day traders (those persisting 300+ days) lost money (2012–2017).
- **Aggregate day-trader returns net of fees were negative in every single year** of the 15-year Taiwan study.
- **Costs are the silent killer.** Spreads, commissions/payment-for-order-flow, slippage, transaction taxes, and data fees all scale with turnover — and day trading maximizes turnover. Unprofitable traders did ~72% of Taiwan's day-trading volume.
- **Leverage amplifies outcomes, not edge.** US rules cap overnight leverage at 2:1 but allow ~4:1 intraday; a small adverse move can erase a large share of equity.
- **The US Pattern Day Trader rule requires $25,000 in a margin account** (FINRA Rule 4210; SEC-approved 2001) — but it's US-specific and the underlying margin framework is changing in **2026–2027**, so verify current rules with your broker.
- **Short-term gains are taxed as ordinary income**, skimming even the rare winning years harder than long-term investing.
- **Beware survivorship bias.** Over 75% of day traders quit within two years, and losers quit at nearly the same rate as winners — so the survivors you see online are an unrepresentative, self-promoting minority.
- **The round "90% lose / 1% win" numbers are commonly cited but not peer-reviewed** — anchor to the academic facts instead.
