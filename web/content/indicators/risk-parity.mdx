## The one-sentence version

Most portfolios split your **money** — say 60% in stocks, 40% in bonds. Risk
parity says that's the wrong yardstick: what you should split is **risk**, so
that every holding pulls its own weight in the portfolio's ups and downs.

That one shift turns out to change almost everything about how you build a
portfolio.

## The idea has more than one parent

Risk parity is a *concept*, not one person's invention — and it arrived from
three directions at once: a practitioner who built it, a quant who named it, and
academics who explained *why* it works.

- **Ray Dalio built it first.** In **1996**, Dalio and colleagues at
  **Bridgewater Associates** (Bob Prince among them) launched the **All Weather**
  strategy, originally just to manage Dalio's own family trust. It is recognized
  as the **first commercial risk-parity strategy** — though nobody called it that
  yet. Dalio had founded Bridgewater in 1975 in his New York apartment; it grew
  into the world's largest hedge fund. (His macro philosophy gets its own lesson;
  see the [Ray Dalio](/gurus/ray-dalio) page.)
- **Edward Qian named and formalized it.** In a **2005** white paper, *"Risk
  Parity Portfolios: Efficient Portfolios Through True Diversification,"* Qian — a
  quantitative researcher at **PanAgora Asset Management** — coined the term
  **"risk parity"** and introduced the **risk-contribution accounting** at the
  heart of this lesson. He famously showed that a 60/40 portfolio's risk is
  dominated by equities. He later wrote the textbook *Risk Parity Fundamentals*
  (2016).
- **AQR explained why it should work.** Cliff Asness, Andrea Frazzini, and Lasse
  Pedersen, in *"Leverage Aversion and Risk Parity"* (*Financial Analysts
  Journal*, **2012**), gave the strategy a theory: because many investors **avoid
  borrowing**, they overpay for high-risk assets — so a *leveraged* portfolio of
  safer assets earns superior risk-adjusted returns. That is precisely the edge
  risk parity tries to harvest.

<Callout type="key">
Qian *named* risk parity (2005). Dalio *built* it nearly a decade earlier
(1996). AQR later explained *why* it works (2012). The practice came first; the
theory caught up. That is why this is a lesson about an **idea**, not a single
person.
</Callout>

## Why "60/40" is a lie your eyes tell you

Picture the classic "balanced" portfolio: 60% of your dollars in stocks, 40% in
high-quality bonds. It *looks* diversified. It isn't — not in the way that
matters.

The reason is **volatility** — how much an asset bounces around. Volatility is
measured as the **standard deviation** of returns: a rough yardstick for "how
big are this asset's typical swings?" And stocks swing **3 to 4 times harder**
than high-quality government bonds.

So even though bonds get 40% of your *money*, they contribute almost none of
your *risk*. The stock sleeve dominates the ride. Run the numbers (we will,
below) and a real-world 60/40 portfolio gets **roughly 90% of its risk from
stocks alone** — the figure most commonly cited by practitioners and by Qian's
original paper.

<Callout type="note">
Where does "~90%" come from, when the simple math below gives ~82%? Two things
push it higher in the real world: (1) stocks have historically swung closer to
**4×** as hard as high-quality bonds, not just 3×; and (2) stocks and bonds carry
a **low but positive** correlation on average, which adds more to the stock
sleeve's share. We'll show the simple ~82% calculation first because it's easy to
follow by hand, then explain how those two effects carry it toward ~90%.
</Callout>

## The core move: split risk, not dollars

Risk parity flips the question. Instead of *"How do I split my money?"* it asks:

> *"How do I split my **risk** so each piece contributes equally?"*

This is called **equal risk contribution (ERC)**: every asset class supplies the
same slice of the portfolio's total volatility. To get there you do something
that feels backwards at first — you hold **more** of the calm assets (bonds) and
**less** of the wild ones (stocks).

The result is a portfolio that is genuinely balanced across its return engines.
But it has a side effect: because it's now bond-heavy, its expected **return** is
low. Risk parity fixes that with a second move — **leverage** — which we'll get
to in Step 4.

## The full method, step by step

### Step 1 — Pick a few genuinely different return drivers

Classic risk-parity sleeves:

- **Equities** (stocks) — win when growth surprises to the upside.
- **Long-duration government (nominal) bonds** — win when growth disappoints and
  rates fall.
- **Inflation hedges** — commodities and/or **inflation-linked bonds (TIPS)** —
  win when inflation surprises upward.
- Sometimes **credit** (corporate bonds, EM debt).

The point isn't to own *many* things. It's to own things that win in
**different** conditions, so something in the portfolio is always working.

### Step 2 — Measure each asset's risk (volatility *and* correlation)

Volatility (annualized standard deviation) is your risk yardstick. Rough
beginner numbers to anchor on:

| Asset | Typical annual volatility |
|---|---|
| Stocks | ~15% |
| Long government bonds | ~5% |
| Commodities | ~15% |

Stocks at 15% vs. bonds at 5% is the **3:1 ratio** that drives everything. You
also need the **correlations** between assets — how they move together — which we
fold in at Step 5.

### Step 3 — Compute each asset's *risk contribution*, then equalize it

Instead of asking how many *dollars* go into each asset, compute how much *risk*
each one adds. An asset's total risk contribution is roughly **its weight × its
volatility** (more precisely, weight × its marginal contribution to portfolio
volatility — and by a result from calculus, **Euler's theorem**, those
contributions sum *exactly* to total portfolio volatility).

**Equal Risk Contribution (ERC)** sets the weights so every asset contributes the
same share — `1/N` of total risk. The simple rule that captures the intuition:
**weight each asset in proportion to 1 ÷ its volatility.** The calmer the asset,
the more of it you hold. With two assets it means: if bonds are 1/3 as volatile
as stocks, you hold roughly 3× as many bond-dollars as stock-dollars.

### Step 4 — Pick a risk target, then lever (or de-lever) to hit it

The balanced portfolio from Step 3 is bond-heavy and therefore **low-volatility
and low-return.** To restore return potential you scale the **whole balanced
mix** up with **leverage** (borrowing at the cash rate, or via futures) until its
volatility equals your chosen target — say 10% or 12%. This is called
**volatility targeting.**

<Callout type="warning">
The crucial detail: you lever the **balanced** portfolio, not just the stocks.
Leverage here is a *dial to set the risk level*, applied evenly so the equal-risk
split is preserved. It is **not** a way to juice returns by piling into
equities. Bridgewater's framing: "a moderately-levered, highly-diversified
portfolio is less risky than an unleveraged, un-diversified portfolio." Getting
this backwards is how people blow up.
</Callout>

### Step 5 — (The real version) use correlations, not just standalone vols

The 1/volatility rule in Step 3 is an approximation that is exactly right *only*
when assets are uncorrelated (or all equally correlated). The proper method uses
the full **covariance matrix** (every asset's volatility *and* every pair's
correlation). Formally, portfolio volatility is:

```
σ(w) = √( wᵀ Σ w )
```

where `w` is the vector of weights and `Σ` is the covariance matrix. You break
that total down (via Euler's theorem) into each asset's **marginal risk
contribution**, then solve so all contributions are equal. It's a well-behaved
(convex) optimization — typically solved by cyclical coordinate descent — with
open-source solvers in Python, R, and JavaScript.

For understanding the *idea*, the 1/vol approximation is plenty. Just know the
real thing also accounts for correlations — and that ignoring them gives the
wrong weights.

### Step 6 — Rebalance on a schedule, and keep cash for margin

Volatilities and correlations drift over time, so weights and leverage are
re-estimated and reset periodically (e.g., monthly or quarterly). Keep
**unencumbered cash** on hand to meet margin calls. Risk parity is a maintained
process, not a set-and-forget allocation.

## A worked example: stocks vs. long bonds

*(Illustrative numbers, built straight from the rules. Stock vol = 15%, bond vol
= 5% — a clean 3:1 ratio. We ignore correlation on the first pass.)*

**First, see the problem in the 60/40 baseline.** Each sleeve's risk
contribution is roughly weight × volatility:

- Stock risk = 0.60 × 15% = **9.0**
- Bond risk = 0.40 × 5% = **2.0**
- Equity share of risk = 9.0 ÷ 11.0 ≈ **82%**

So on the simplest possible pass — a 3:1 vol ratio, correlation ignored — stocks
already supply **~82%** of the risk. Two real-world adjustments push that higher:
swap the 3:1 ratio for a more historically typical **4:1** (stock risk = 0.60 ×
20% = 12.0, bond risk = 0.40 × 5% = 2.0 → **~86%**), then fold in the low but
**positive** stock–bond correlation in a full covariance calculation, and it
lands near the commonly cited **~90%**. The progression is consistent — 82% → 86%
→ ~90% — and the lesson is the same at every step: a "60/40" portfolio is really
a **~90% risk bet on stocks.**

**Now fix it with equal risk contribution.** Weight each asset ∝ 1/vol:

- Stock weight ∝ 1/15 = 0.0667
- Bond weight ∝ 1/5 = 0.20
- Normalize (divide by their sum, 0.2667):
  - Stocks = 0.0667 / 0.2667 = **25%**
  - Bonds = 0.20 / 0.2667 = **75%**

**Check the risk split:**

- Stock risk = 0.25 × 15% = **3.75**
- Bond risk = 0.75 × 5% = **3.75**
- Each contributes **50% of risk.** Balanced. ✓

**Finally, add leverage to restore return.** With the two sleeves uncorrelated,
that 25/75 mix has a portfolio volatility of only:

```
√( 3.75² + 3.75² ) = √28.1 ≈ 5.3%
```

— far too tame to deliver equity-like returns, and well below the ~9% volatility
of the 60/40 baseline (`√(9.0² + 2.0²) ≈ 9.2%`). To raise the balanced mix to the
60/40 risk level you apply leverage of:

```
9.2% ÷ 5.3% ≈ 1.7×
```

You borrow cash to scale **both** sleeves up by ~1.7×, keeping their 50/50 risk
balance intact. Now you have a portfolio with the *same* risk level as 60/40 —
but whose risk is genuinely diversified across **two** engines instead of riding
entirely on stocks. The thesis (per AQR) is that it earns a better return per
unit of risk.

## The All Weather variant: balance across *environments*

Dalio's All Weather applies the same logic but balances across **economic
environments** instead of asset labels. The origin story is instructive: Dalio
was shaped by the 1971 "Nixon shock" (the end of dollar-gold convertibility),
which taught him to *expect surprises he couldn't forecast.* So instead of
betting on a forecast, you balance across all the scenarios — and collect the
long-run risk premium.

Bridgewater's insight: asset values are driven by two things — **growth** and
**inflation** — and each can come in **above or below** what the market expected.
That makes a **2×2 grid of four environments**, and each box has assets that tend
to win in it:

| | **Growth surprises up** | **Growth surprises down** |
|---|---|---|
| **Inflation surprises up** | Commodities, inflation-linked bonds | Commodities, inflation-linked bonds |
| **Inflation surprises down** | Equities, corporate credit, EM debt | Nominal government bonds |

Roughly: **equities** win when growth surprises up; **nominal bonds** win in
disinflationary recessions; **commodities** win in 1970s-style inflation;
**inflation-linked bonds** win when inflation surprises up. All Weather holds four
sub-portfolios, one tuned to each box, with **equal risk on each box** — so
whatever the future brings, ~25% of the risk is positioned to do well.

In Bridgewater's own words: *"The key was to put equal risk on each scenario to
achieve balance,"* because investors *"have equal odds of being right about any
one scenario."* When the environmental exposures offset, *"that leaves just the
risk premium to collect."*

## Honest criticisms and limitations

Risk parity is elegant, but it is not free money. The skeptics have real points.

<Callout type="warning">
**1. It depends on leverage.** To earn competitive returns you *must* borrow.
Leverage adds funding costs, margin-call risk, and the danger of forced selling
in a crunch. Anderson, Bianchi & Goldberg (*"Will My Risk Parity Strategy
Outperform?"*, 2012) argue the levered portfolio may offer **slight or no
advantage over plain 60/40** once realistic borrowing costs and turnover are
included.

**2. It's a big bet on bonds — built in a bond bull market.** Risk parity is
bond-heavy and was largely backtested across the 30+ year falling-rate era
(1980s–2020). Ben Inker of **GMO** (in *"The Hidden Risk of Risk Parity
Portfolios,"* 2010) argues bonds have **"negative skew"** — a small chance of
large losses — which makes levering them dangerous, especially when yields are
low.

**3. It relies on stable correlations.** Diversification breaks down when
everything falls together. **2022** is the canonical failure case: as rates rose
disorderly, stocks *and* bonds dropped at once and many risk-parity funds had
sharp losses. The Q1 2020 COVID sell-off stung too.

**4. Volatility ≠ true risk, and it ignores valuation.** The method uses *past*
volatility as the risk yardstick — backward-looking, and it can understate tail
and regime risk. It also deliberately ignores valuations, so it will hold
expensive bonds simply because they're calm.
</Callout>

**The academic defense.** Asness, Frazzini & Pedersen's *"Leverage Aversion and
Risk Parity"* (AQR, 2012) argues that because many investors *avoid* leverage,
the safe assets you *can* lever up are systematically underpriced — which is
exactly the edge risk parity harvests. The honest verdict on the live record is
mixed: strong relative results in some windows, weak in others, and a clear
warning in 2022.

## Common beginner pitfalls

<Callout type="warning">
1. **Confusing capital weights with risk weights.** "I'm 60/40, so I'm balanced"
   — no, you're ~90% equity *risk*. This single confusion is the entire reason
   the strategy exists.
2. **Skipping the leverage step, then being disappointed.** Unlevered risk
   parity is bond-heavy and low-return *by design*. If you refuse leverage,
   don't expect equity-like returns.
3. **Over-levering, or levering without a vol target.** Leverage is a dial to
   reach a chosen risk level — not a way to blindly amplify gains. 2022 punished
   the careless.
4. **Treating it as "can't lose."** The stock–bond correlation is not reliably
   negative; in inflation shocks (2022) both fall together. Risk parity is not
   crash-proof.
5. **Using the 1/vol shortcut blindly.** It only equals true ERC when
   correlations are equal. Ignoring correlations gives the wrong weights.
6. **Treating historical volatility/correlation as permanent.** They drift and
   must be re-estimated; the portfolio needs rebalancing and cash for margin.
7. **Conflating Dalio with the whole idea.** All Weather is *one*
   implementation. Qian's ERC, AQR's version, and others all differ.
</Callout>

## Putting it to work

You don't need a Bloomberg terminal to use the core insight. A motivated
beginner can:

1. **Audit your real risk split.** For any portfolio you hold, multiply each
   sleeve's weight by its rough volatility (stocks ~15%, bonds ~5%). You'll
   almost certainly find your "balanced" portfolio is a stock bet in disguise.
2. **Rebalance toward equal risk contribution** — using the 1/vol rule — if you
   want a genuinely diversified ride. Expect a calmer, lower-returning portfolio.
3. **Decide consciously about leverage.** Many individuals reasonably choose to
   run risk parity *unlevered* and simply accept the lower, steadier return —
   that's a valid trade. If you do lever (e.g., via a fund or modest margin),
   size it to a **risk target**, never to a return wish, and keep cash for margin.
4. **Diversify the drivers, not just the tickers.** Add a real inflation hedge
   (commodities or TIPS) so you're covered in the environment that crushes both
   stocks and nominal bonds.
5. **Rebalance on a schedule** and re-check your vol and correlation estimates.

The mindset is the lasting lesson: **diversification is about balancing risk
contributions, not dollar amounts.** Once you see that, you can never look at a
"60/40" the same way again.
