## Why this matters

Here is the single most useful idea in global macro: **markets don't trade the
economy — they trade the central bank's expected response to the economy.**

A currency rallies not because growth is good, but because good growth makes the
central bank more likely to *raise rates*. A bond rallies not because inflation
fell, but because falling inflation makes the bank more likely to *cut*. The
economy is the input; the **policy regime** — the set of rules and tools a
central bank is currently using — is the machine that turns that input into the
prices you actually trade.

So before you take a view on the dollar, the euro, or a 10-year government bond,
you have to answer one question: *what regime is the central bank in, and what is
it expected to do next?* This lesson builds that map.

<Callout type="key">

The professional shortcut: **the level of rates matters less than the *surprise*
versus what was already expected.** Central banks tell you their plan in advance
(forward guidance), markets price that plan, and the big moves happen when
reality forces the bank *off* its stated path.

</Callout>

## The policy rate: the one lever everything hangs on

The Federal Reserve's main tool is the **federal funds rate** — the interest
rate banks charge each other to borrow reserves (the cash banks park at the Fed)
overnight. Since **December 2008** the Fed doesn't pick a single number; it sets
a **25-basis-point-wide target range** — a "basis point" is one-hundredth of a
percent, so a range looks like "5.25%–5.50%."

A common beginner misconception is that the Fed hits this target by buying and
selling small amounts of bonds each day. It doesn't. In today's system there are
trillions of reserves sloshing around, so the Fed instead steers the rate with
two administered rates that act like a **floor**:

- **Interest on Reserve Balances (IORB)** — the rate the Fed *pays* banks on
  their reserves. No bank will lend to another for less than it can earn risk-free
  at the Fed, so this sets the floor.
- **The overnight reverse repo (ON RRP) rate** — a slightly lower floor for money
  market funds and others that can't earn IORB.

### How a rate change reaches your life

The Fed's own framing describes a chain of transmission. A rate change ripples
outward with **"long and variable lags"** — a phrase popularly attributed to
economist Milton Friedman (worth flagging that the attribution is conventional
rather than a precise quote). Central banks generally reckon it takes **roughly
one to two years** for a rate change to fully show up in inflation.

The plain-English version: cheaper money makes borrowing, houses, and stocks
more attractive, so people and firms spend more, which lifts employment and —
eventually — inflation. Tighter money does the reverse. As the Fed puts it, when
demand slows, unemployment tends to rise and inflation tends to decline.

## The dual mandate and the 2% target

The **Federal Reserve Act** instructs the Fed to pursue "maximum employment,
stable prices, and moderate long-term interest rates." That's technically three
goals, but the third is treated as a by-product of the first two, so it's
universally called the **dual mandate** — jobs and prices.

What is "stable prices"? Since **January 25, 2012** (under Chair Ben Bernanke),
the Fed has defined it as **2% inflation**. Two details that catch people out:

- The target is measured by the **PCE price index** (Personal Consumption
  Expenditures), *not* the more famous CPI (Consumer Price Index). News headlines
  quote CPI; the Fed steers by PCE, which usually runs a touch lower.
- 2% is not "as low as possible." A small, predictable positive number gives the
  economy room to adjust and keeps a safety buffer above zero, where deflation —
  a damaging spiral of falling prices — becomes a risk.

<Callout type="note">

Inflation targeting itself was a foreign import. **New Zealand's** Reserve Bank
pioneered an explicit numerical inflation target around **1990** (sources
disagree on whether to date it to the 1989 legislation or the March 1990 Policy
Targets Agreement). The idea spread worldwide: the **UK targets 2% on CPI**, the
**ECB** targets **2% symmetrically** on its HICP measure.

</Callout>

### When the regime itself changes: FAIT and its reversal

This is where "regime" stops being abstract. On **August 27, 2020**, at the
Jackson Hole conference, Chair Jerome Powell announced **Flexible Average
Inflation Targeting (FAIT)**. Two changes mattered:

1. The Fed would aim for inflation that **"averages 2 percent over time"** —
   meaning after a period *below* 2%, it would deliberately allow inflation
   **"moderately above 2 percent for some time"** to make up the shortfall.
2. On employment it would respond only to **"shortfalls"** from maximum
   employment (too few jobs), not to a hot labor market on its own — an
   intentionally asymmetric stance, more tolerant of running the economy hot.

Then on **August 22, 2025**, the FOMC **unanimously** updated the framework
again. The Fed's press release confirms it reaffirmed maximum employment and
stable prices "across a broad range of economic conditions," and that the 2%
goal itself was *not* under review (the target is unchanged).

<Callout type="warning">

**A flag on the 2025 change.** It is *widely reported* that the 2025 review
**dropped FAIT's "averages 2 percent" makeup language and the asymmetric
"shortfalls" wording**, reverting to a more traditional, symmetric approach. The
press release confirms a unanimous update but does not spell out that substance
in plain words. Treat the "FAIT was scrapped" framing as the strong consensus
read, and verify the exact wording against the revised framework document and
Powell's 2025 Jackson Hole remarks before stating it as settled fact.

</Callout>

The lesson for a trader: a framework change is a *regime* change. After 2020,
markets had to price a Fed that would sit on its hands longer. The 2025 reversal
flips that intuition back. Same economy, different machine.

## Forward guidance: talking the market into doing your work

Because long-term rates are really the market's *average expectation* of future
short rates, a central bank can move long rates today simply by changing
expectations — without touching the policy rate at all. That tool is **forward
guidance**: communicating the likely future path of policy on purpose.

It comes in flavors worth knowing:

- **Delphic** guidance is a soft *forecast* ("we expect rates to stay low") — the
  bank reserves the right to change its mind.
- **Odyssean** guidance is a *commitment* to a path, named for Odysseus tying
  himself to the mast — harder to renege on, and therefore more powerful when
  believed.
- **Time-based** guidance pins to a date ("low through mid-2015"); **state- or
  threshold-based** guidance pins to data. The canonical example is the **2012
  "Evans rule,"** which committed to low rates at least until unemployment fell
  below `6.5%` (subject to an inflation ceiling). *(These textbook examples are
  standard; flagged here because they came from general knowledge, not a single
  cited page.)*

Guidance only works if the bank is credible — which is recent. The Fed only
began **announcing its rate decisions publicly in 1994**; before that, markets
literally had to guess from open-market operations.

<Callout type="warning">

**The failure mode: the 2013 "taper tantrum."** When Bernanke merely *hinted*
the Fed might slow its bond buying, long-term yields spiked violently and
emerging-market currencies sold off. Nothing had actually changed in policy — a
*communication* moved markets more than an *action*. Guidance is a loaded
weapon; it can fire when you only meant to clear your throat.

</Callout>

## QE and QT: the balance sheet as a second lever

When the policy rate is already near zero — the **zero lower bound (ZLB)** —
cutting further isn't possible. So central banks reach for a second tool:
**quantitative easing (QE)**, buying large quantities of longer-term bonds
(government bonds and mortgage-backed securities) to push *long-term* yields down
directly and inject reserves into the system. The reverse — letting those
holdings mature without replacing them, or selling them — is **quantitative
tightening (QT)**, which shrinks the balance sheet.

The scale is the story. The Fed's balance sheet went from roughly **$870–900
billion before 2008** to about **$4.5 trillion** by 2014 after the financial
crisis, then COVID-era buying pushed it to a **peak of about $8.9 trillion in
April 2022**.

**QT began in June 2022**, with the Fed initially capping the monthly runoff at
**$95 billion** (the standard reported split is **$60bn of Treasuries + $35bn of
mortgage-backed securities**, though the single source checked confirmed only the
$95bn total). Those caps were tapered to roughly **$50 billion** a month by
September 2024. Other banks tightened too: the **ECB** accelerated its QT through
2023 (over €30bn/month of non-reinvestment), and the **Bank of England** began
winding down its holdings in early 2022 *(BoE/ECB start dates flagged for direct
verification)*.

<Callout type="note">

**Why a macro trader watches the balance sheet.** QE tends to lift asset prices
and weaken the currency (more of it sloshing around); QT does the reverse, and —
crucially — it drains the system's plumbing of reserves, which can break things
when it goes too far. The balance sheet is a slow-moving regime variable that
sets the *backdrop* against which rate moves land.

</Callout>

## Monetary vs. fiscal: two hands, ideally separate

It's easy to lump "government policy" together. Don't. Two distinct institutions
are at work:

- **Monetary policy** is run by the **central bank** — ideally *independent* of
  day-to-day politics. Its tools are the policy rate, the balance sheet, and
  guidance; its targets are inflation and employment.
- **Fiscal policy** is run by the **government and legislature** (treasury +
  parliament/congress). Its tools are **taxes and spending**, which affect demand
  directly — a tax cut or stimulus check puts money in pockets immediately.

The key teaching point is **independence**. When a central bank can set rates
free of political pressure, its inflation promise is credible and guidance works.
The dangerous failure case is **fiscal dominance** — when government debt and
deficits grow so large that the central bank effectively *can't* raise rates (it
would bankrupt the treasury), so it's forced to keep money loose and let
inflation run. Spotting the early signs of fiscal dominance is one of the highest
forms of the macro craft.

## Why the regime drives FX and rate trades

Now we can close the loop from the opening. Currency and bond prices hinge on
**expected policy paths and the rate differential between countries** — the
intuition behind *uncovered interest parity*, which says money should flow toward
where it expects higher returns. Markets price the *anticipated* regime in
advance, so the biggest moves come from **surprises versus guidance**.

You can see it in the tape: the dollar's strength in 2014–15 and again its surge
in 2022 both tracked expectations of Fed *tightening*. The currency moved with
the expected regime, often ahead of the actual rate hikes.

## The honest part: where central banking falls short

This is education, not a sales pitch for the omnipotence of central banks. The
tools have real, documented limits and failure modes.

- **QE can inflate assets and worsen inequality.** A **2012 Bank of England**
  study estimated that around **40% of the wealth gains** from QE accrued to the
  richest 5% of households — because QE works by lifting asset prices, and the
  wealthy own the assets. And **Alan Greenspan (July 2012)** flatly said QE had
  "very little impact on the economy." QE can simply fail to reach the real
  economy if banks won't lend the new reserves out.
- **Lags make fine-tuning impossible.** With effects arriving over one to two
  *years*, a bank is always steering by a rear-view mirror — hence the constant
  risk of over-tightening into a recession or under-tightening into runaway
  inflation.
- **Guidance can backfire and faces a credibility trap.** The taper tantrum
  showed words can overshoot; more deeply, **time-inconsistency** means a bank
  may *promise* to stay easy, then break the promise when it's tempting to —
  which markets anticipate, blunting the tool.
- **QT can break market plumbing.** Two episodes are sobering: the **September
  2019 repo spike**, when reserves got so scarce that overnight funding rates
  jumped and the Fed had to inject emergency liquidity; and the **September 2022
  UK gilt crisis**, when a pension-fund ("LDI") fire-sale forced the Bank of
  England to *buy* gilts in the middle of its own tightening campaign. Shrinking
  a balance sheet is not the clean mirror-image of growing it. *(These episodes
  are well documented but were not in the cited primary pages — verify framing.)*

<Callout type="key">

The takeaway is not "central banks are useless" — they are the single most
important force in macro. It's that they are powerful, lagged, and fallible
*all at once.* That combination is exactly why expected-vs-actual policy is where
the trading edge lives.

</Callout>

## Key takeaways

- Markets trade the **central bank's expected reaction** to the economy, not the
  economy itself. Regime first, data second.
- The **policy rate** (a 25bp-wide target range since 2008) is steered via
  **IORB** and **ON RRP** in a floor system, and transmits to the real economy
  with **long and variable lags** of roughly **1–2 years**.
- The Fed's **dual mandate** is maximum employment and stable prices, defined as
  **2% on PCE** (not CPI) since 2012. The **FAIT** regime of 2020 was, by strong
  consensus, **reverted in 2025** — but verify the exact 2025 wording.
- **Forward guidance** moves long rates by moving expectations; it only works
  with credibility, and the **2013 taper tantrum** shows it can backfire.
- **QE/QT** is the second lever — the balance sheet rose from `~$0.9T` pre-2008
  to a peak `~$8.9T` in April 2022, with **QT** running it down from June 2022.
- **Monetary policy** (independent central bank, rates/balance sheet/guidance) is
  distinct from **fiscal policy** (government, taxes/spending); the failure case
  is **fiscal dominance**.
- Honest limits: QE skews wealth upward and can fail to reach the economy, lags
  prevent fine-tuning, guidance faces credibility traps, and QT can break market
  plumbing (2019 repo spike, 2022 UK gilt crisis).
- This is educational material, not financial advice.
