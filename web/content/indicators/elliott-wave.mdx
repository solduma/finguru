## The one-sentence version

Elliott Wave is the claim that crowd psychology moves markets in a repeating, fractal pattern of **five waves forward and three waves back** — and the practical skill is learning to label those waves on a real chart, attach probabilities to competing interpretations, and place trades where being wrong is cheap.

This lesson builds on the [Ralph Elliott guru lesson](#). There you met the man (Ralph Nelson Elliott, 1871–1948) and the big idea. Here we get our hands dirty: how to actually count waves, the rules you cannot break, the guidelines that tip the odds, the Fibonacci math that turns a count into price targets, and — crucially — how to survive the theory's biggest weakness, which is that two skilled analysts can look at the same chart and see two different futures.

<Callout type="key">

Elliott Wave is not a signal generator. It is a framework for organizing what you see into a **set of scenarios with a clear invalidation point**. Used that way, its main value is risk definition, not prediction.

</Callout>

## The basic building block: 5 up, 3 down

The core pattern is a **motive phase** of five waves that moves in the direction of the larger trend, followed by a **corrective phase** of three waves that moves against it.

```
Motive (with trend):   1 - 2 - 3 - 4 - 5
Corrective (counter):  A - B - C
```

- **Motive waves** make net progress in the trend direction. The five-wave version is called an **impulse**.
- **Corrective waves** partially undo that progress, usually in three legs labeled A, B, C.

Inside an impulse, waves 1, 3, and 5 are the **action waves** (with trend) and waves 2 and 4 are the **reaction waves** (counter-trend pullbacks).

The pattern is **fractal**: each wave is itself made of smaller waves of the same form. Wave 1 of a large move is itself a five-wave impulse on a smaller timeframe; wave 2 is itself a three-wave correction. Elliott catalogued these scale levels as **degrees** — from Grand Supercycle (multi-century) down through Supercycle, Cycle, Primary, Intermediate, Minor, Minute, Minuette, and Subminuette ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliott_wave_principle)). In practice you only need two or three adjacent degrees: the one you trade, the one above it (context), and the one below (entry timing).

## The three inviolable rules

If a count breaks any of these, the count is **wrong** — not "unlikely," wrong. This is what makes Elliott falsifiable in practice.

1. **Wave 2 never retraces more than 100% of wave 1.** It cannot make a new low below the start of wave 1 (in an up-move).
2. **Wave 3 is never the shortest** of waves 1, 3, and 5. (It need not be the longest — it just can't be the shortest.)
3. **Wave 4 never enters the price territory of wave 1.** In a standard impulse, wave 4's low stays above wave 1's high.

([Source: Wikipedia, Elliott Wave Principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliott_wave_principle))

<Callout type="warning">

Rule 3 has one official exception: in a **diagonal** (described below), wave 4 *can* overlap wave 1. If you see overlap, you are not allowed to call it a standard impulse — you must either re-label or switch to the diagonal pattern. Forcing an impulse label onto overlapping waves is the single most common beginner error.

</Callout>

## The key guidelines (not rules — tendencies)

Guidelines bend; rules don't. But guidelines are where most of the predictive edge lives.

- **Alternation:** waves 2 and 4 tend to differ. If wave 2 was a sharp, deep, quick pullback, expect wave 4 to be a shallow, sideways, time-consuming one — and vice versa. This stops you from expecting a carbon copy.
- **Extension:** one of the three action waves is usually noticeably longer than the others, most often **wave 3**. When wave 3 extends, it is frequently about **1.618×** the length of wave 1.
- **Channeling:** draw a trendline from the end of wave 1 to the (eventual) end of wave 3, then a parallel line off the end of wave 2. Wave 5 often ends near that upper parallel. This is a practical way to project where a move is "running out."
- **Wave equality:** when wave 3 is the extended wave, waves 1 and 5 tend toward equality (or wave 5 ≈ 0.618× wave 1).

## Wave personalities — reading the crowd

Elliott Wave is fundamentally a model of crowd psychology, and each wave has a recognizable emotional fingerprint (described here for a bull market; invert for bear):

| Wave | Personality |
|------|-------------|
| 1 | Tentative. News is still bad; few believe the move. |
| 2 | Fear of relapse; sharp pullback; sentiment says "the bounce is over." |
| 3 | The strongest, broadest leg. News turns good, volume surges, often extends 1.618×. |
| 4 | Complacent, choppy, sideways consolidation; frustrating to trade. |
| 5 | Euphoria, but narrowing participation; often shows **momentum divergence** (price makes a new high, an oscillator like RSI does not). |
| A | First crack; widely dismissed as a dip. |
| B | "Bull trap" rally; sucks people back in. |
| C | Broad, decisive decline; capitulation. |

<Callout type="note">

Momentum divergence on wave 5 is one of the most useful real-world confirmations. If your count says "this should be wave 5" and your oscillator is diverging, the two independent tools agree — that is a higher-confidence setup than either alone.

</Callout>

## The pattern catalogue

### Motive patterns
- **Impulse:** the standard 5-wave move obeying all three rules. The default.
- **Diagonal:** a wedge-shaped 5-wave move where wave 4 overlaps wave 1. **Ending diagonals** appear in wave 5 or wave C and warn that the move is exhausting; **leading diagonals** appear in wave 1 or wave A.

### Corrective patterns
- **Zigzag (5-3-5):** a sharp A-B-C where B is a shallow retrace of A. Steep and trending-looking.
- **Flat (3-3-5):** a sideways A-B-C where B retraces most of A. Common in wave 4 or wave B.
- **Triangle (3-3-3-3-3):** five overlapping, converging legs labeled A-B-C-D-E. Per Elliott, a triangle "is formed usually in wave 4, but very rarely in wave 2" — i.e., it precedes the *final* move of the larger sequence ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliott_wave_principle)). Spotting a triangle is a strong hint that one last thrust is coming.

Corrections can also chain together (combinations), which is part of why corrective counting is the hardest part of the discipline.

## Fibonacci wave relationships

Fibonacci ratios convert a wave count into actual price levels. The common relationships (illustrative defaults, not guarantees):

```
Wave 2  retraces  50%–61.8% of wave 1   (usually no deeper than 61.8%)
Wave 3  =          1.618× wave 1          (or 2.618× when strongly extended)
Wave 4  retraces  less than 38.2% of wave 3
Wave 5  =          wave 1, or 0.618× wave 1, or 0.618× (waves 1+3)
Wave B  retraces  varies by correction type
Wave C  =          at least wave A, often 1.618× wave A
```

### A worked example (illustrative numbers)

Suppose a stock bottoms at **$100** and rallies to **$120** — you label that wave 1 (length **$20**). It pulls back; Fibonacci says wave 2 likely ends near 50%–61.8%, i.e. **$110–$112.4**. It stops at **$112** and turns up. Now you have a plan:

- **Wave 3 target:** 1.618 × $20 = $32.36, added to the $112 low → about **$144**.
- **Risk definition:** by Rule 1, wave 2 cannot go below $100. If price breaks $100, the count is dead. So a long entry near $112 with a stop just under $100 risks ~$12 to target ~$32 — a roughly 2.7:1 reward-to-risk, *defined by the rules themselves*.

<Callout type="key">

This is the practical payoff of Elliott Wave: the **rules hand you a stop-loss level**. Your invalidation point is not arbitrary — it's the price at which the entire scenario becomes impossible under the rules. That is a genuinely useful discipline even if you're skeptical of the broader theory.

</Callout>

## The hard problem: subjectivity and multiple counts

Here is the honest part. Elliott Wave's rules are tight, but its **labeling is not unique**. The same squiggle can often be counted as "wave 3 of a new impulse" (bullish) or "wave B of a correction" (bearish). Critics have hit this hard:

- Mathematician **Benoit Mandelbrot** wrote that "wave prediction is a very uncertain business," adding that the forecasting record "is at best mixed."
- **David Aronson** argued the principle "is not a legitimate theory, but a story," persuasive only because it has "the seemingly remarkable ability to fit any segment of market history" after the fact.
- Researchers **Robert Batchelor and Richard Ramyar** found the idea that prices retrace to a Fibonacci ratio "clearly lacks any scientific rationale," reporting "no significant difference between the frequencies" of the claimed ratios and random expectation in the Dow.

([All from Wikipedia, Elliott Wave Principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliott_wave_principle); the [Fibonacci retracement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fibonacci_retracement) article similarly notes the levels' significance "could not be confirmed by examining the data.")

The professional response is not to deny this but to manage it:

1. **Maintain two counts** — a preferred count and an alternate. Know the price level at which the preferred dies and the alternate takes over.
2. **Trade only high-confidence setups.** The best is the **third-wave entry after a clean wave 1 and a Fibonacci wave 2**, because Rule 1 gives a tight stop and wave 3 is the strongest leg.
3. **Let invalidation, not opinion, exit you.** When a rule breaks, you don't argue — you flip to the alternate count and re-plan.

<Callout type="warning">

Beware **hindsight counting**: any completed chart can be labeled perfectly after the fact. The test of skill is whether your count made a *forward* prediction with a *pre-defined* invalidation. If you only ever know the count once the move is over, you have a story, not a method.

</Callout>

## Common beginner mistakes

- Forcing a 5-wave label onto a 3-wave (corrective) move because you *want* a trend.
- Ignoring Rule 3 overlap and calling a diagonal an impulse.
- Counting noise: trying to label every tiny wiggle instead of stepping up a degree.
- Treating Fibonacci targets as certainties rather than *zones* to watch for confirmation.
- Adjusting the count to fit a position you already hold (the cardinal sin).

## How to apply it today

1. **Start at a higher timeframe** to find the dominant trend and the current wave at a larger degree.
2. **Look for clean impulses** — moves where all three rules visibly hold. Skip ambiguous charts; there will always be another.
3. **Wait for a wave-2 pullback into a Fibonacci zone** to position for wave 3.
4. **Define the stop at the rule-based invalidation** (e.g., the start of wave 1).
5. **Project the target** with Fibonacci extensions and confirm with an independent tool (RSI divergence, channel touch, volume).
6. **Journal both counts** and review which one the market validated. Over time this is how you calibrate, not memorize.

Elliott Wave rewards the patient and punishes the eager. Treat it as a probability map with hard edges, pair it with momentum and structure tools, and let its own rules tell you when you're wrong.
